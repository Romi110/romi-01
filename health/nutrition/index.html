    Nutrition¶

    The fundamentals of eating to fuel your body — macros, food quality, and how to hit your targets consistently as a vegetarian.

    What Is It¶

    Nutrition is how you use food to build, fuel, and maintain your body. Three macronutrients — protein, carbohydrates, fat — make up almost all the calories you eat. Each has a specific job. Getting the ratio and total amount right determines whether your body performs and recovers, or just runs.

    For most people with health and training goals, the main challenge isn't knowing what's healthy — it's hitting consistent targets without overthinking every meal.

    Why It Matters¶

    Food is the most direct lever you have on body composition, energy, and how you feel. Training without dialing in nutrition is like optimizing code without fixing the infrastructure — you'll hit a ceiling fast. For Romi specifically: the vegetarian constraint makes protein the hardest macro to hit, which is why it gets the most attention here.

    Core Concepts¶

    Protein — The Priority Macro¶

    Protein builds and repairs muscle. It's also the most satiating macro — it keeps you full longer and has the highest thermic effect (your body burns more calories digesting it than it does with carbs or fat).

    Target: 128–160g/day (~0.8–1g per lb bodyweight at 160 lbs) Split across 3–4 meals: ~35–45g per eating window.

    Protein is the hardest macro for vegetarians to hit in volume. You have to actively build meals around protein sources — it won't happen by accident.

    Primary Veg Sources (Tier 1 — 15g+ per serving) | Food | Protein | Serving | Notes | |------|---------|---------|-------| | Soy chunks (dry) | ~50g/100g dry | 50g dry = ~25g | Rehydrate, use in curry or stir-fry | | Tofu | ~15g/100g | 150g = ~22g | Stir-fry, bhurji-style, grilled | | Paneer | ~18g/100g | 150g = ~27g | Tikka, bhurji, palak paneer | | Greek yogurt | ~10g/100g | 200g = ~20g | Breakfast, smoothies, raita | | Lentils/dal (cooked) | ~9g/100g | 1 cup = ~18g | Dal, khichdi, soups | | Chickpeas (cooked) | ~8g/100g | 1 cup = ~15g | Chole, hummus, roasted snack | | Rajma (cooked) | ~8g/100g | 1 cup = ~15g | Rajma chawal | | Eggs | ~6g each | 3 eggs = ~18g | Omelette, boiled, bhurji | | Whey protein | ~24g/scoop | 1 scoop | Post-workout or gap-filler |

    Supporting Sources (Tier 2 — 5–15g per serving) | Food | Protein | Serving | Notes | |------|---------|---------|-------| | Cottage cheese | ~11g/100g | 150g = ~16g | Snack, salads | | Peanut butter | ~25g/100g | 2 tbsp = ~8g | Toast, smoothies | | Peanuts | ~26g/100g | 30g = ~8g | Snack | | Almonds | ~21g/100g | 30g = ~6g | Snack, topping | | Edamame | ~11g/100g | 100g = ~11g | Snack, salads | | Sprouts (moong) | ~9g/100g | 100g = ~9g | Salads, sautéed with spices | | Milk | ~3.5g/100ml | 300ml = ~10g | Plain, with shake | | Quinoa (cooked) | ~4g/100g | 1 cup = ~8g | Rice substitute | | Besan/chickpea flour | ~22g/100g | — | Cheela, pakora base |

    Add-Ons (Tier 3 — every gram counts) | Food | Protein | Use | |------|---------|-----| | Hemp seeds | ~10g/30g | Sprinkle on anything | | Pumpkin seeds | ~7g/30g | Snack, salad topping | | Chia seeds | ~5g/30g | Smoothies, overnight oats | | Makhana (fox nuts) | ~9g/100g | Roasted snack | | Green peas | ~5g/100g | Add to curries, rice |

    Non-veg options (occasional): - Chicken breast: ~31g/100g cooked — 150g = ~46g, highest per-gram density of any whole food - Canned tuna: ~25g/100g — fast, no prep - Eggs already listed above (technically veg for lacto-ovo vegetarians)

    Amino acid completeness: Plant proteins are often incomplete individually. Combine across the day (no need in same meal): - Dal + rice — the classic complete combo - Roti + hummus — wheat + chickpea - Tofu + quinoa — both nearly complete on their own

    Carbohydrates — Fuel¶

    Carbs are your body's primary fuel source, especially for training. Don't cut them — just choose them well.

    Target: ~40–50% of total calories (~200–250g/day at a moderate intake)

    Quality matters more than quantity: - Whole grain roti, brown rice, oats, quinoa, sweet potato — digest slowly, sustain energy - White rice, white bread — fine around workouts when fast digestion is useful - Vegetables and fruit — count toward carbs but also provide fiber and micronutrients

    Practical approach: Build your carb base around roti, rice, oats, and vegetables. Don't chase a number — just avoid eating mostly refined carbs (bread, packaged food, sugar) at every meal.

    Fat — Necessary, Not the Enemy¶

    Fat supports hormones, joint health, brain function, and absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). You need it.

    Target: ~25–30% of calories (~60–80g/day)

    Good sources: - Nuts and nut butters (almonds, peanuts, cashews) - Seeds (chia, hemp, flax, pumpkin) - Ghee — small amounts, high quality - Coconut and olive oil — for cooking - Full-fat dairy (paneer, Greek yogurt, milk) - Avocado (where accessible)

    Avoid: seed oils in excess (sunflower, soybean), trans fats, anything heavily processed.

    Micronutrients — The Gaps Vegetarians Have¶

    You won't notice deficiencies immediately — they accumulate. These are the ones to watch:

    Nutrient Why it matters Sources
    Vitamin B12 Nerve and blood cell health — almost zero plant sources Dairy, eggs, fortified milk, or supplement
    Iron Energy, oxygen transport — plant iron absorbs less efficiently Lentils, spinach, fortified cereals — pair with vitamin C to increase absorption
    Zinc Immunity, testosterone, wound healing Pumpkin seeds, chickpeas, cashews, dairy
    Omega-3 (ALA/EPA/DHA) Brain, inflammation, heart Flaxseed, chia, hemp, walnuts — or algae supplement for DHA
    Vitamin D Bone health, immunity, mood Sun exposure, fortified milk, supplement in winter (NJ winters are real)
    Calcium Bones, muscle function Dairy, tofu (calcium-set), dark leafy greens

    Practical minimum: If you eat dairy and eggs, you're mostly covered. B12 and Vitamin D are the two most likely gaps — supplement both.

    Calories — The Override¶

    Macros matter, but total calories determine weight direction: - Maintenance: ~15 × bodyweight in lbs = ~2,400 cal/day at 160 lbs (rough baseline) - Cut: 300–500 cal below maintenance - Build: 250–300 cal above maintenance

    You don't need to count calories obsessively. But if nothing is changing when you expect it to, the calories aren't where you think they are.

    Sample Day — 140g+ Protein¶

    Breakfast (~35g protein) - 200g Greek yogurt + 1 scoop whey mixed in + chia seeds (2g) - OR: 3-egg omelette + 2 toast with peanut butter + glass of milk

    Lunch (~40g protein) - 1 cup dal + 150g paneer sabzi + roti - OR: Chickpea curry + quinoa + raita

    Snack (~20g protein) - Roasted chana + almonds + glass of milk - OR: Peanut butter banana smoothie with milk

    Dinner (~40g protein) - Rajma + tofu stir-fry + rice - OR: Soy chunk curry + dal + roti

    Post-workout if needed (~24g) - Whey shake with milk

    Key Considerations¶

    Protein first, then fill the rest. At every meal, decide the protein source first. Then add carbs and fat around it. If you start with the carbs (roti, rice), protein gets squeezed out.

    Vegetarian protein requires volume. You can't get 40g from one ingredient the way you can with chicken. Plan for combinations — dal + paneer, yogurt + whey, eggs + cottage cheese.

    Meal prep isn't optional. If there's no ready-to-eat protein in the fridge, you default to carbs. See meal-prep.md for the weekly system.

    Don't count protein from grains. Roti and rice have protein but it's incidental — 3–5g per serving. Build your targets from dedicated protein sources.

    Common Mistakes¶

    1. Relying only on dal — good base but 1 cup = ~18g. You need 3–4 sources per day.
    2. Skipping dairy — paneer, Greek yogurt, and milk are vegetarian protein gold. Use them.
    3. Fat fear — cutting fat drops hormones and satiety. Ghee, nuts, and full-fat dairy are not the enemy.
    4. Eating the same meal every day — leads to micronutrient gaps and burnout. Rotate protein sources.
    5. Ignoring B12 and D — you will not notice the deficiency until it's months in. Supplement.
    6. Not prepping — the biggest real-world failure mode. Without prep, nutrition breaks down mid-week.

    Getting Started¶

    1. Pick a protein target (128g is minimum, 160g is ideal) and track for one week — just to see where you actually land.
    2. Identify which meals are protein-weak and fix one at a time.
    3. Set up Sunday prep for the two biggest protein staples in your rotation (e.g., dal + paneer or dal + tofu).
    4. Add a whey shake on days you're short — no shame in using it as a gap-filler.
    5. Pick a B12 supplement and take it consistently (daily or weekly high-dose).

    Resources¶

    • Precision Nutrition Level 1 Textbook — Berardi & Andrews. Best systematic breakdown of how macros, meal timing, and food quality interact. Dense but worth reading selectively.
    • The Omnivore's Dilemma — Michael Pollan. Not a macro guide — but changes how you think about food quality and sourcing, which matters for the long game.
    • Examine.com — Evidence-based supplement and nutrient database. Use it to check anything before supplementing.
