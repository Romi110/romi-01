    Hydroponic Systems¶

    The six system types — how they work, what they cost, and which one to start with.

    What Is It¶

    A hydroponic system is the physical infrastructure that delivers nutrient-rich water to plant roots in a controlled way. There are six main system designs. They differ in how water contacts roots, how much active equipment is involved, and what crops they suit.

    Choosing the right system is the first decision in hydroponics. The wrong system for your space, crops, or skill level causes failure that has nothing to do with your nutrient knowledge or plant care.

    The Six Systems¶

    1. Kratky Method¶

    How it works: Plants sit in net pots over a static reservoir of nutrient solution. Roots grow down into the water. As the plant absorbs water, the solution level drops — creating an air gap between the water surface and the net pot. Upper roots stay in this humid air gap (providing oxygen); lower roots stay submerged (providing water and nutrients). No pump, no electricity beyond optional lighting.

    The air gap is critical: it's what makes passive Kratky work. If you keep topping off the reservoir to the original level, roots stay fully submerged, oxygen-starve, and rot. Let the level drop naturally.

    Equipment needed: opaque container (bucket, tote, mason jar), net pots, growing medium (clay pebbles or rockwool), nutrient solution, pH meter.

    Cost: $20–50 to get started. A mason jar Kratky system costs under $5.

    Best crops: lettuce, herbs (basil, cilantro, mint), spinach, arugula, kale. All high-water-use, fast-growing leafy plants that suit a single-fill cycle.

    Not suited for: tomatoes, peppers, large fruiting plants (they need more water than a static reservoir provides, and benefit from active oxygenation).

    Why start here: zero mechanical failure. No pump to burn out. No timer to misconfigure. Your only variables are nutrient concentration and pH. Perfect for learning the fundamentals before adding complexity.

    Limitation: not truly "set it and forget it" — pH still drifts, and you may need to top off large plants in hot conditions. But far more forgiving than any active system.

    2. Deep Water Culture (DWC)¶

    How it works: Plants in net pots are suspended in holes cut into a lid over a reservoir. Roots hang directly in aerated nutrient solution. An air pump runs continuously through air stones to keep dissolved oxygen levels high. Roots are always submerged.

    Why oxygenation matters: without oxygen, submerged roots rot quickly (pythium). The air pump is what makes always-submerged roots viable. This is the fundamental DWC trade-off vs. Kratky — you add electricity and a pump in exchange for higher oxygen levels and faster growth.

    Equipment needed: opaque reservoir (5–20 gallon), net pots, air pump, air tubing, air stones, growing medium, nutrients, pH/EC meters.

    Cost: single-bucket DWC $30–60; 4-site system $60–100; 8-site $100–200.

    Best crops: lettuce, herbs, and with a large reservoir + strong lighting, tomatoes and peppers. DWC is the system most people graduate to after Kratky.

    Variant — RDWC (Recirculating DWC): multiple buckets connected to a central reservoir; nutrient solution circulates between them. More consistent chemistry across many plants. For growing at scale indoors.

    Key maintenance: check pH and EC every 1–2 days; change full reservoir every 7–14 days; keep water temperature 65–72°F (warm water holds less oxygen and promotes root rot).

    3. Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)¶

    How it works: A thin, continuous film of nutrient solution flows down a slight slope through channels (usually square or round PVC tubes). Plant roots sit on the channel floor; the film passes over them. Upper portions of the roots are exposed to air in the channel. A pump circulates solution from a reservoir continuously.

    Why it works: roots get both moisture from the film and oxygen from the air in the channel simultaneously. High oxygen + consistent nutrition = fast growth.

    Equipment needed: grow channels (PVC or purpose-built), pump, reservoir, tubing, timer (optional — many systems run continuously), net pots.

    Cost: $100–300 for a basic home setup; commercial channels can be made from 2-inch PVC pipe.

    Best crops: lettuce, spinach, herbs, strawberries. Plants with smaller root masses. Avoid heavy feeders with large root systems — they block the channel.

    Limitation: pump failure = plants die within hours. The thin film dries out fast. NFT requires reliable power and backup planning.

    Common in: commercial lettuce and herb production; vertical wall systems.

    4. Ebb and Flow (Flood and Drain)¶

    How it works: A grow bed (tray or table) sits above a reservoir. A pump periodically floods the grow bed with nutrient solution to a set depth, then a drain returns the solution to the reservoir. Between floods, roots sit in the growing medium (clay pebbles, rockwool, coco) with good oxygen exposure.

    Flood frequency: depends on medium and crop. Clay pebbles drain fast and need more frequent floods (3–8x per day). Rockwool holds moisture longer (2–4x per day). Timers control the pump schedule.

    Equipment needed: grow tray with overflow drain, reservoir below, pump, timer, tubing, growing medium, net pots or solo cups.

    Cost: $100–250 for a starter tray setup.

    Best crops: lettuce, herbs, tomatoes, peppers — more versatile than NFT because you can use different growing mediums and adjust flood schedule for different plant needs.

    Advantage over DWC: supports larger plants and different media; more flexible.

    Key risk: if the drain clogs and the tray stays flooded, roots suffocate. Inspect drain screens regularly.

    5. Drip Systems¶

    How it works: nutrient solution drips through emitters onto the base of each plant (in growing medium) at timed intervals. Excess solution either drains to waste (run-to-waste) or returns to the reservoir (recirculating). Roots sit in coco coir, rockwool, or clay pebbles.

    Two types: - Recovery/recirculating: drain collects and returns to reservoir; efficient but reservoir chemistry fluctuates as plants uptake selectively - Run-to-waste: drain goes to waste; fresh solution each cycle; simpler chemistry management; more nutrient use

    Equipment needed: reservoir, pump, timer, drip emitters, tubing manifold, growing medium in containers or slabs.

    Cost: $150–400+ depending on scale; more infrastructure than other systems.

    Best crops: tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, cannabis — large fruiting plants that need precise, adjustable nutrient delivery across multiple growth stages.

    Why commercial: scalable, automatable, adaptable to many plant types. Most commercial hydroponic vegetable and herb production uses drip.

    For home use: overkill for lettuce and herbs; worth it if you're serious about fruiting plants indoors.

    6. Aeroponics¶

    How it works: Plant roots hang in the air in a dark chamber. High-pressure nozzles mist roots with nutrient solution at short intervals (every 30–60 seconds for high-pressure; every few minutes for low-pressure). Roots are never submerged; they exist entirely in oxygenated air between misting cycles.

    Why it's fast: maximum oxygen exposure at the root zone + frequent nutrient delivery = fastest possible growth. NASA research on aeroponics showed substantially accelerated plant development compared to all other methods.

    Equipment needed: pressure chamber, high-pressure pump, misting nozzles, timer, reservoir. High-pressure aeroponics requires a pressure accumulator tank and mist nozzles that don't clog.

    Cost: $300–800+ for a functional home setup; commercial systems cost thousands.

    Best crops: any fast-growing crop benefits, but aeroponic systems are used most for lettuce, herbs, and potato seed stock production (roots form tubers in air more efficiently).

    Limitation: nozzle clogging is the primary failure mode. A clogged nozzle means roots dry out in minutes. High-pressure systems require regular maintenance. Not a beginner system.

    System Comparison¶

    System Complexity Cost (starter) Best For Failure Risk
    Kratky Very low $20–50 Lettuce, herbs Very low
    DWC Low $60–100 Lettuce, herbs, tomatoes Low-medium
    NFT Medium $100–300 Lettuce, herbs, strawberries Medium (pump dependent)
    Ebb & Flow Medium $100–250 Most crops Medium
    Drip Medium-high $150–400+ Tomatoes, peppers, scale Low (with good setup)
    Aeroponics High $300–800+ Advanced, R&D High (nozzle clog)

    Which System to Start With¶

    Zero experience → Kratky. One-time setup. Grow lettuce. Learn pH and EC management without the added complexity of pumps, timers, and equipment failure. When you've grown two successful Kratky crops, you understand the fundamentals.

    Ready for active systems → DWC. Add a pump and air stone to your Kratky knowledge. Faster growth, more oxygen, handles larger plants. Still simple. 4-site DWC is a natural second system.

    Want fruiting plants indoors → Drip into coco. Tomatoes and peppers need more nutrient delivery precision than DWC handles easily. Coco coir drip is closer to soil growing in feel and is more forgiving for the transition.
