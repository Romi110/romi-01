    Companion Planting¶

    Placing plants together for mutual benefit — support, nutrition, pest pressure, and yield

    What Is It¶

    Companion planting is the deliberate placement of plants in proximity for functional reasons: one plant fixes nitrogen that feeds another, one confuses pests that target its neighbor, one attracts the predatory insects that keep a third plant clean. It's the bed-level expression of the same logic that drives permaculture guilds — a diverse planting outperforms a monoculture because the plants are doing work for each other.

    The benefits fall into four categories: pest management, soil improvement, physical support, and pollination/beneficial insect attraction. Not every claimed companion relationship is well-supported — some traditional pairings are real, some are folklore. This file focuses on the ones with strong practical evidence.

    Core Concepts¶

    Three Sisters¶

    The most well-documented companion planting system, developed by Indigenous North American farmers:

    • Corn — grows tall, provides a living trellis for beans
    • Beans — climb the corn, fix atmospheric nitrogen into the soil, feeding the corn and squash
    • Squash — sprawls at ground level, shading out weeds, retaining moisture, and deterring pests with its rough leaf texture

    The three work as a system: structural support, soil fertility, and ground management, each provided by one plant for the others. Plant corn first, allow it to establish 6–8 inches, then plant beans at its base, then squash between hills. Direct-sow all three — transplanting disrupts squash roots.

    In NJ: works well with dent corn (not sweet corn, which doesn't get tall enough to support beans reliably), pole beans, and any winter squash variety.

    Nitrogen Fixers¶

    Legumes (beans, peas, clover, vetch) host rhizobium bacteria in root nodules that fix atmospheric nitrogen into plant-available form in the soil. This nitrogen benefits nearby plants during the season and remains in the soil after the legume is turned under.

    Practical applications: - Interplant clover in paths and around fruit trees — it fixes nitrogen and provides habitat for beneficial insects - Follow heavy-feeding crops (corn, squash, brassicas) with a legume crop the next season — rotation, not just interplanting - White clover as living mulch in orchard zones — mows down underfoot, fixes nitrogen, attracts pollinators

    Pest Confusion¶

    Many pests locate host plants by volatile chemical scent. Interplanting with strongly scented herbs and alliums disrupts this:

    • Alliums with carrots — onion/garlic scent confuses carrot fly; carrot scent confuses onion fly. Classic reciprocal pairing.
    • Basil with tomatoes — repels thrips and aphids; some evidence for improved tomato flavor
    • Aromatic herbs throughout beds — sage, rosemary, thyme, and mint (contained) all reduce pest pressure broadly by masking crop scents

    For pest-specific companion planting (marigolds, nasturtiums, trap cropping), see pest-management.md — that file covers strategic plant combinations in the context of the full pest management system.

    Allelopathy¶

    Some plants release chemicals that suppress the growth of nearby plants — beneficial when those plants are weeds, harmful when they suppress crops.

    Useful allelopathy: - Buckwheat — smothers weeds when used as a cover crop; also attracts beneficial insects - Rye (as cover crop) — strong allelopathic effect on weed seeds when terminated and left as mulch

    Harmful allelopathy to know: - Black walnut (Juglans nigra) — releases juglone, which kills or stunts tomatoes, peppers, apples, and many other plants within root range (up to 60 feet). In NJ, black walnuts are common. Map them on any property before planting. - Sunflowers — mild allelopathic effect; don't plant in the same spot year after year, and rotate away from sensitive crops

    Beneficial Insect Attractors¶

    Predatory and parasitoid insects (lacewings, parasitic wasps, hoverflies, ground beetles) are among the most effective pest controls available — but they need habitat and nectar sources to persist in your garden. Flowering plants in the carrot family (Apiaceae) are particularly effective:

    • Dill — attracts parasitic wasps and lacewings; allow some to flower and go to seed
    • Fennel — similar effect; keep away from tomatoes and beans (mild allelopathy toward them)
    • Yarrow — long-blooming, attracts a wide range of beneficials
    • Phacelia — fast-growing, lavender flowers, excellent beneficial insect plant
    • Sweet alyssum — low-growing, long-blooming, attracts hoverflies whose larvae eat aphids

    The rule: if your vegetable beds have no flowering plants, you're relying on luck for beneficial insects. Plant at least one flowering companion per 4×8 bed section and allow some crops (dill, cilantro, carrot) to bolt.

    Physical Support¶

    • Corn as bean trellis — Three Sisters logic; any tall, sturdy plant can serve as a living support
    • Tall crops as windbreaks — sunflowers or corn on the north side of a bed protect shorter crops from cold winds
    • Dense planting as weed suppression — filling horizontal space with low-growing companions (lettuce, nasturtium) reduces weed germination without the need for mulch

    Key Considerations¶

    Most companion planting claims are not scientifically validated. The Three Sisters, nitrogen fixers, and allelopathy are well-documented. Many traditional companion planting pairings (e.g., "marigolds repel everything") are highly context-dependent or simply untrue. Apply what has clear mechanism or track record; treat the rest as folklore until you test it yourself.

    Spacing matters. Companions need to be close enough to interact — the standard recommendation is within 12–18 inches. A single basil plant at the edge of a 4×8 tomato bed does almost nothing. Interplant, don't just co-locate.

    Some companions compete. Fennel is famously problematic near most vegetables. Strongly allelopathic plants near sensitive crops can reduce yields. Know the exceptions as well as the pairings.

    Succession matters for beneficials. You need flowering plants available throughout the season, not just a burst in June. Stagger plantings of dill, phacelia, and sweet alyssum so something is always blooming.

    Getting Started¶

    1. Add one flowering companion to every bed. Dill, sweet alyssum, or phacelia — pick one, plant it, let it bloom. This is the highest-ROI first move.
    2. Plant the Three Sisters as one experiment. One 4×8 bed or a 6-foot-diameter hill. Direct sow in late May after last frost. Observe how the system functions through the season.
    3. Interplant alliums throughout. Garlic planted in fall, onion sets in spring — scatter them throughout your beds. Cheap, easy, meaningful pest confusion effect.
    4. Map any black walnuts on the property. Do this before planting anything permanent nearby.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Planting companions too far apart. They need proximity to interact. Edge-of-bed placement doesn't count.

    Trusting all claimed pairings equally. The internet is full of companion planting charts with no mechanism behind them. Prioritize pairings with a known reason they work.

    Ignoring the blooming calendar. Planting one round of dill that flowers and dies in July leaves you with no beneficial insect support for August and September — often the highest-pressure months.

    Forgetting to let herbs bolt. Dill, cilantro, and parsley only attract beneficials when flowering. The instinct to cut them back before they flower removes the beneficial insect function.
