    Cognitive Psychology¶

    How the mind perceives, stores, and processes information — and where it goes wrong

    What Is It¶

    Cognitive psychology studies the internal mental processes that mediate between stimulus and response: perception, attention, memory, language, reasoning, and problem-solving. It emerged in the 1960s as a reaction to behaviorism's refusal to study anything not directly observable — the cognitive revolution restored the mind to the center of psychology.

    The core finding: the mind doesn't record reality neutrally. It constructs it. Your perception, memory, and reasoning are active processes shaped by existing beliefs, current goals, emotional state, and cognitive shortcuts. What you think you're seeing or remembering is often a reconstruction, not a recording.

    Why It Matters¶

    Understanding how cognition works tells you: - Why you and someone else can watch the same event and come away with completely different accounts of it - Why memory is unreliable in ways that feel certain - How your brain makes fast judgments about people and situations — and where those judgments go wrong - What "thinking clearly" actually requires

    For reading others: most of the time, people aren't lying to you — they genuinely believe what they're saying. But what they believe is shaped by cognitive processes that distort systematically. Understanding those processes lets you see what's actually happening instead of arguing over "the facts."

    Core Concepts¶

    Dual Process Theory — System 1 and System 2¶

    Daniel Kahneman's framework (built on earlier work by Keith Stanovich and others) describes two modes of thinking:

    System 1: Fast, automatic, effortless, associative, emotional. Pattern matching. Runs continuously. Produces immediate judgments about faces, situations, and threats. Can't be turned off. Prone to predictable errors.

    System 2: Slow, deliberate, effortful, logical. Used for calculating, analyzing, evaluating. Limited in capacity — you can only hold so much in working memory at once. Gets hijacked when tired, stressed, or distracted (System 1 takes over).

    Most of your daily mental activity is System 1. System 2 is the exception, not the rule.

    The key insight: System 1 doesn't announce its conclusions as guesses — they feel like perceptions. When you see someone and immediately feel they're untrustworthy, that feels like a fact, not a guess. It's actually a fast pattern-match running against prior experience, primed by current emotional state, and potentially very wrong.

    This is the mechanism under almost every cognitive bias. The bias isn't stupidity — it's System 1 running unchecked in a context that requires System 2.

    Schemas — The Templates That Shape Perception¶

    A schema is a mental framework — a structure of prior knowledge and expectations that shapes how new information is interpreted. You have schemas for objects (chair), situations (job interview), people (types), social roles (doctor, student), and abstract concepts.

    When new information arrives, you don't process it raw. You fit it into existing schemas. What doesn't fit tends to be: - Distorted to fit (you remember it as more consistent with the schema than it was) - Ignored entirely - Remembered as a vague anomaly

    Stereotype is a schema applied to people. Not all stereotypes are wrong, but the mechanism creates errors: - Attention is selectively drawn to schema-confirming information - Schema-disconfirming information is down-weighted or explained away - Memory for the interaction later is schema-consistent even if the actual events were mixed

    This is why implicit bias research doesn't primarily measure malice — it measures schematic processing. The schema runs automatically. It doesn't require endorsement.

    How this helps you read people: when you have a strong first impression of someone, you are running a schema based on limited data. The impression then shapes how you interpret subsequent behavior. To read someone accurately, you have to catch the schema operating and hold your first impression loosely until you have behavioral data across multiple contexts.

    Memory Is Reconstructive, Not Reproductive¶

    The common mental model of memory is a recording: events are stored and retrieved intact. The research shows memory is more like reconstruction: when you remember something, you rebuild it from fragments, filling gaps with what makes sense given current knowledge, expectations, and emotional state.

    Elizabeth Loftus's research: Leading questions alter what people "remember." In one experiment, people shown a car crash were asked either "how fast were the cars going when they smashed?" or "when they contacted?" Those asked "smashed" later remembered seeing broken glass that wasn't in the video. The question language changed the memory, not just the answer.

    Source monitoring errors: People sometimes remember a fact without remembering where they learned it — and attribute it to the wrong source. A rumor heard once becomes a "fact I read somewhere."

    Memory is confidence-independent. People are reliably more confident about false memories than about true ones in some conditions. Confidence in a memory is not correlated with its accuracy.

    For practical use: when someone insists with certainty on a version of events that differs from yours, the explanation is usually not lying — it's that you both reconstructed different memories from the same incomplete information. Neither version is necessarily accurate.

    Cognitive Biases — The Systematic Errors¶

    These are covered in depth in mind/mental-models.md. The most important ones to understand from a cognitive psychology standpoint:

    Confirmation bias — The tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information in a way that confirms existing beliefs. The default mode of belief updating. The mind is not a truth-seeking machine; it's a belief-defending machine that uses evidence selectively.

    Availability heuristic — Estimating frequency or probability based on how easily examples come to mind. Plane crashes are overestimated as causes of death (vivid, memorable) while car accidents are underestimated (mundane, not memorable). News coverage inflates the availability of dramatic events.

    Anchoring — The first number or piece of information you receive disproportionately influences subsequent judgments. In negotiations, the opening offer anchors the entire discussion. In performance reviews, the first thing mentioned anchors how subsequent feedback is interpreted.

    Halo and horn effects — A single positive trait (attractiveness, confidence, articulateness) creates a positive halo over unrelated traits. A single negative trait creates a horn effect. This is why first impressions have outsized influence: one early observation generates a schema that colors all subsequent perception.

    Dunning-Kruger effect — People with low competence in a domain tend to overestimate their ability (because they lack the metacognitive skills to know what they don't know). People with high competence tend to underestimate it (because what's easy for them seems like it should be easy for everyone). Neither side has an accurate self-assessment.

    Attention — What the Mind Notices and What It Filters¶

    Attention is selective. The brain receives enormous amounts of sensory input; conscious awareness receives a tiny slice, selected by: - Novelty (new things grab attention automatically) - Threat (emotional salience — things that could harm you) - Relevance to current goals - Deliberate focus

    Inattentional blindness: When focused on one task, people completely miss highly visible events in their visual field. The famous "invisible gorilla" experiment: people counting basketball passes missed a person in a gorilla suit walking through the scene. About 50% of participants across replications. The gorilla was not subtle.

    The practical implication: people notice what they're looking for and miss everything else. Someone focused on whether they're liked in a conversation will miss most of the content. Someone focused on closing a deal will miss signals that the relationship is fracturing.

    Change blindness: People fail to notice significant changes in a visual scene if the change happens during a brief interruption. Even large changes go undetected when attention isn't specifically directed.

    For reading others: what someone pays attention to tells you what matters to them. What they consistently fail to notice tells you their attention is elsewhere.

    Working Memory — The Cognitive Bottleneck¶

    Working memory is the system that holds and manipulates information in conscious awareness. Capacity: roughly 4 chunks of information simultaneously, though the "chunk" size can be expanded through expertise (a chess grandmaster holds a whole position as one chunk; a novice holds individual pieces).

    Working memory is the bottleneck for deliberate, System 2 thinking. When it's full: - System 1 takes over - Decision quality drops - People resort to heuristics and emotional defaults

    What loads working memory: stress, emotional distress, multitasking, time pressure, sleep deprivation, trying to suppress thoughts. This is why people make worse decisions when under pressure — not because they're different people, but because cognitive capacity is genuinely reduced.

    For reading situations: a person under significant stress or emotional load is cognitively impaired even if they appear functional. Their reasoning capacity is literally reduced. Decisions made in this state are lower quality. This is a mechanical reality, not a moral failing.

    Key Considerations¶

    The mind optimizes for speed and efficiency, not accuracy. Most of the time, fast heuristics are good enough — they evolved to work in typical environments. They fail in novel, high-stakes, or statistically unusual situations: financial decisions, medical decisions, evaluating strangers, understanding large-scale risks.

    You cannot think your way out of cognitive bias. Awareness raises the threshold slightly; it doesn't eliminate the bias. The reliable interventions are structural: checklists, decision rules made in advance, outside perspectives, slowing down, separating data collection from evaluation.

    Getting Started¶

    1. Practice noticing when you're in System 1 mode on a decision that warrants System 2. What's the felt quality of the thought? It feels like seeing, not like reasoning.
    2. Pick one belief you hold confidently and deliberately search for evidence against it. Notice the resistance — that's confirmation bias in action.
    3. When you have a strong first impression of someone, write it down. Revisit in a month and see how it held up across more data.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Trusting introspection about your own reasoning. People construct post-hoc explanations for decisions made on System 1 intuition — and the explanations feel like the actual reasons. They usually aren't. Behavioral data tells you more about your thinking than self-report does.

    Thinking intelligence protects you from bias. It doesn't — it mainly improves your ability to construct convincing rationalizations for biased conclusions. High-IQ people are no less susceptible to confirmation bias; they're often better at defending the conclusion.

    Treating disagreements as dishonesty. Two people who watched the same meeting and came away with completely different accounts are almost always both describing their genuine experience — constructed by different schemas, primed differently, attending to different things.

    Resources¶

    • Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman. The accessible summary of decades of cognitive bias research. Best single overview.
    • The Invisible Gorilla — Christopher Chabris & Daniel Simons. The researchers behind the attention experiments. Shows how badly we misjudge our own cognitive abilities.
    • Memory: A Very Short Introduction — Jonathan Foster. Compact, rigorous overview of how memory actually works.
    • Predictably Irrational — Dan Ariely. Behavioral economics lens on cognitive biases in decision-making and consumption. More applied than Kahneman, slightly less rigorous.
