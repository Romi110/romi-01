    Repair Over Replace¶

    When fixing something is smarter than buying new — and how to actually do it

    What Is It¶

    Repair-over-replace is the practice of extending the life of something you own through maintenance and repair rather than discarding it at the first sign of wear. It involves knowing which things are worth repairing (and when), which tradespeople and resources can do it, and which products are designed to be repaired vs. designed to be discarded.

    It connects directly to buy-it-for-life thinking: there's no point buying quality goods if you throw them away when something breaks. Repair is the operational half of that philosophy.

    Why It Matters¶

    The economics are often straightforward — a $50 cobbler repair on $400 boots beats a $400 replacement. But more than cost, repair builds a different relationship with objects. You understand them. You maintain them. You don't throw things away at the first inconvenience. That's a Stoic discipline applied to material life: don't waste what still has value.

    The right-to-repair movement also matters for long-term self-reliance. As manufacturers make products harder to repair (proprietary screws, glued components, software locks), the ability to fix what you own is a form of independence.

    Core Concepts¶

    The Repair Decision Framework¶

    Before sending something out (or throwing it away), run the calculation:

    1. Repair cost — what does the fix actually cost in money + time?
    2. Replacement cost — what does it cost to buy the same quality new?
    3. Remaining useful life — if repaired, how many more years of use?
    4. Attachment premium — is there value in keeping this item (fit, familiarity, sentimental)?

    Rule of thumb: repair if the cost is less than 50% of replacement AND the item has at least 5 more years of useful life if fixed. For BIFL-quality goods, the threshold is even more generous — a $500 item repaired for $100 costs $200 over 20 years if you repeat the repair twice. The same $500 item replaced every 5 years costs $2,000.

    This is the cost-per-use calculation from buy-it-for-life.md applied to maintenance, not purchase.

    Shoes and Leather Goods¶

    This is the highest-value repair category. Quality leather footwear is designed to be resoled. The upper (the leather body of the shoe) on a well-made pair can last decades. The sole wears out. A resole costs $50-100 and restores the shoe to functional condition.

    When to resole: when the heel or ball of the sole is worn through to the midsole, or when you can see the welt (the stitching between upper and sole) is intact. If the welt is intact and the upper leather is sound, the shoe is worth resoling.

    Finding a cobbler: - Independent cobblers (not mall shoe repair chains) do better work. Look for one that's been around 10+ years. - Ask if they do Goodyear welt resoling — this is the benchmark skill. If yes, they can handle most work. - Good cobblers are worth traveling for. A bad resole with wrong-hardness rubber damages the shoe.

    What cobblers can do: heel replacement, full resoles, leather conditioning and restoration, zipper replacement on boots, stretching leather, patching scuffs and tears.

    Brands designed for resoling: Alden, Tricker's, Viberg, White's, Chippewa (Goodyear welt construction). Brands that cannot be resoled: most cemented-sole sneakers and cheap shoes — the upper isn't worth saving anyway.

    Leather bags and belts: cleaning, conditioning, edge redressing, strap replacement, hardware swap. A quality leather bag conditioned and stored properly lasts decades; a cobbler can restore dried-out or cracked leather.

    Clothing and Tailoring¶

    Simple repairs (DIY possible): - Button replacement — 5 minutes with a needle and thread - Hem shortening — basic sewing skill - Small tears and holes on non-visible seams — hand stitch or iron-on patch

    When to use a tailor: - Structural repairs (side seams, inseams, crotch blowouts on jeans) - Taking in or letting out waist/chest - Shortening sleeves with button intact (harder than it looks) - Replacing zippers on jackets and pants

    Finding a tailor: independent tailors do better work than dry-cleaning chain alterations. Ask specifically about the repair you need — a tailor who does custom suiting may not want to hem $40 jeans. Find the right shop for the task.

    Denim specifically: raw denim enthusiasts have a culture around repairs — sashiko darning (Japanese boro technique) for crotch and knee repairs. Visible repair as aesthetic feature. Worth learning if you wear quality denim.

    The economics: a $250 pair of jeans with a blown crotch seam can be repaired for $15-25. If the denim itself is still good, repair is obvious.

    Appliances¶

    The repair vs. replace inflection point for appliances: - Under 5 years old: repair unless catastrophic failure - 5-10 years: repair if parts are available and cost < 50% of replacement - Over 10 years: evaluate efficiency gains from replacement (an old refrigerator running inefficiently may cost $50/year extra in electricity — that's real money over 10 years)

    Brands designed to be repaired: - Miele — German engineering, spare parts available for 20+ years post-manufacture, modular design - Speed Queen — American-made washers designed for commercial use, simple mechanical design, decades of parts support - Sub-Zero/Wolf — expensive, but designed for long service life with independent repair technician support

    Brands difficult to repair: - Most Samsung and LG appliances use proprietary boards and increasingly software-locked components - Bosch mid-range: parts availability is inconsistent - Whirlpool (and Maytag, Amana — same company): hit or miss depending on model era

    DIY appliance repair: more accessible than most people think. Appliance repair YouTube channels (Appliance Tech Nick, LG/Samsung/Whirlpool-specific channels) cover 80% of common failures. Parts are available at AppliancePartsPros.com for most major brands. A $10 door seal or $25 control board is more dignified than a $1,200 replacement.

    Electronics and Right-to-Repair¶

    The right-to-repair fight: Apple, John Deere, Samsung, and others have lobbied against right-to-repair laws that would require them to provide spare parts, repair manuals, and diagnostic tools to independent repair shops and consumers. The argument from manufacturers: quality control, safety. The actual motive: parts sales and planned obsolescence.

    As of 2024-2025: 20+ US states have passed or considered right-to-repair legislation. The EU has passed right-to-repair directives covering smartphones, tablets, and appliances. This trend is in your favor over the next decade.

    iFixit: the central resource for electronics repair. Free repair guides for thousands of devices, proprietary repair tools, and parts sourcing. Before buying a new phone or laptop, check iFixit's repairability score for the model you're considering.

    Where electronics repair makes sense: - Phone screen replacement: iPhone 13 screen replacement ~$70-120 at an indie shop vs. $1,000+ for a new phone. The economics are obvious unless you want a new phone anyway. - Laptop RAM and SSD upgrades: on non-soldered machines (Lenovo ThinkPads, many Dell Latitudes), a $80 SSD upgrade extends useful life 5 years. Check before buying whether the storage is soldered. - Battery replacement: phones, laptops, wireless headphones all degrade over 2-3 years. A $50-80 battery replacement is rational before spending $300+ on replacement headphones.

    What to avoid repairing: - Modern ultra-slim laptops with glued displays and soldered everything — if it fails, it's often not worth repairing - Cheap electronics under $100 — the labor cost of repair exceeds replacement - Devices where software updates are no longer available — a repaired phone that can't run secure software is a security liability

    The Right-to-Repair Principle¶

    Buy products where repair is possible by design: - Standardized fasteners: avoid products with pentalobe or proprietary screws used to prevent self-repair (a telltale sign) - Modular design: can you replace the battery? The screen? The motor? - Parts availability: is a 10-year-old version still supported with parts? - Independent repair ecosystem: are there independent shops that service it, or only manufacturer authorized centers?

    Framework works applies to cars too — Toyota and Honda have far better independent repair support than German luxury brands. A vehicle that can only be serviced at a dealer under software lock is a long-term cost liability.

    Key Considerations¶

    Your time is a real cost. "Repair cost $50" understates the true cost if you spent two hours sourcing a cobbler, dropping off the shoes, and picking them up. For low-value items, that time math tips toward replacement. For high-quality items, the math usually still favors repair.

    Learn basic repairs yourself. Sewing a button, patching a seam, replacing a faucet washer, swapping a phone battery — these are skills with high return on time invested. The first repair takes 30 minutes; the tenth takes 5.

    Condition prevents repair need. Most leather, textile, and wood deterioration is preventable with basic maintenance. Condition leather before it cracks. Wash woollens properly before they felt. Sharpen knives before they're damaged. Prevention is cheaper and easier than repair.

    Not everything is worth saving. A $30 fast-fashion shirt isn't worth $25 in tailoring. A $300 quality shirt is. The decision to repair starts at the time of purchase: buy things worth repairing.

    Getting Started¶

    1. Audit your footwear — do you own leather-soled shoes or boots that need a heel or sole? Find a cobbler and get a quote. Do the math.
    2. Set up a basic sewing kit — needles, thread in 3-4 neutral colors, small scissors, a few spare buttons. Costs under $20.
    3. Download iFixit on your phone — before your next electronics replacement, search for your device model and see what the repair options are.
    4. Check your appliances: for any appliance under 10 years old that's having problems, look up the specific error code or symptom on YouTube before calling for service or buying new.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Sending quality items to bad repairers. A cheap cobbler with the wrong rubber compound will ruin a Goodyear-welted sole. A tailor who doesn't understand quality fabric will damage it. Invest time finding good craftspeople.

    Waiting too long. Leather that's cracked beyond conditioning costs more to restore than leather that's just dried. A worn heel repaired before it damages the upper is a $20 job; a worn heel that damages the welt is a $100+ job. Maintain before repair is needed.

    Keeping sentimental junk. Repair calculus should be clear-eyed. If you haven't worn something in two years, repairing it to not wear it in two more years is waste, not wisdom.

    Assuming new is better. Older appliances (pre-2010 especially) were often more repairable and more durable than current equivalents. A 15-year-old Speed Queen washer can be worth repairing even at high cost.
