    Fit and Tailoring¶

    What good fit actually looks like, how to evaluate off-the-rack, and when to tailor

    What Is It¶

    Fit is the single highest-leverage variable in how clothes look. A $50 shirt that fits well looks better than a $300 shirt that doesn't. Most people know this in theory and ignore it in practice — they buy in their stated size, accept clothes that roughly cover their body, and never look as good in their clothes as they could.

    This file covers what proper fit looks like for each major garment category, how to evaluate fit at purchase, and what tailoring can and cannot fix. It's the foundation that makes wardrobe-building.md actually work.

    Why It Matters¶

    Fit is why someone in a $40 thrifted blazer can look better than someone in a $600 designer jacket. The difference is almost never brand or price — it's whether the fabric drapes where it's supposed to and sits where it's supposed to.

    For a tech professional who wants to look put-together without thinking about clothes constantly: if your clothes fit, you look pulled-together in any context. If they don't, no amount of quality fabric or brand names fixes it.

    The other side: most off-the-rack clothes don't fit perfectly. That's expected. Knowing what's worth tailoring and what's a structural mismatch saves you from buying things that will never work.

    Core Concepts¶

    Shirts and T-Shirts¶

    Shoulder seam: This is the most important fit point on any top. The seam where the sleeve meets the body should sit exactly at the shoulder bone — the end of the bony ridge, not the top of the arm and not hanging down the bicep. If the shoulder seam is off, the whole garment drapes incorrectly and tailoring cannot fix it without a very expensive rebuild.

    Chest: You should be able to fit two fingers (not a fist) between the shirt and your chest when buttoned. Too tight = straining at buttons, pulling across the back. Too loose = fabric bunching, shapeless silhouette.

    Body length: A tucked shirt should stay tucked when you raise your arms. An untucked casual shirt should end at the top of your back pocket — no lower. A shirt that hits mid-thigh is designed for a different body or a different era.

    Sleeves: The sleeve hem should hit just below the wrist bone when your arm hangs naturally. For dress shirts, you want about half an inch of cuff visible below a jacket sleeve. Too long is easy to tailor; too short cannot be fixed.

    Collar: For dress shirts, you should be able to slip two fingers under the collar when buttoned. If it's tight, it will be uncomfortable all day. If it gaps away from the neck, it's too large and the silhouette looks sloppy.

    T-shirts specifically: The shoulder seam rule applies. For fit: slim enough to suggest there's a body inside, not so tight it reads as athletic wear. If you can see the outline of your stomach through the fabric, it's too tight for most contexts.

    Trousers and Chinos¶

    Waist: Should sit without a belt and without digging into your midsection. One finger of room at the waistband. If you need a belt to hold trousers up or if you can't comfortably sit down, the waist is wrong.

    Seat: The trousers should cover your seat without pulling tight (horizontal creases across the back) or bagging (excess fabric bunching below the seat). Seat fit is hard to tailor and usually requires buying the right cut from the start.

    Thigh: Enough room to walk normally with no pulling at the inner thigh. For slim cuts, this is tight but not restrictive. For straight cuts, this is relaxed. You shouldn't feel fabric resistance when walking.

    Break: The "break" is how much the trouser hem touches the shoe. Options: - No break — hem hits right at the ankle or slightly above. Clean, modern, shows some sock. Current consensus for most contexts. - Slight break — hem touches the top of the shoe with just a small fold. Classic, versatile, works in most contexts. - Full break — significant fold at the hem, fabric pools on the shoe. Traditional, more formal, reads as dated in casual contexts.

    For chinos and most modern trousers, aim for no break or a slight break. This is easily adjusted by a tailor — the most common and cheapest alteration anyone gets.

    Knee and calf: Trouser leg should taper evenly from thigh to calf without billowing or clinging. Most slim-cut trousers do this correctly; classic-cut trousers often need the leg tapered slightly for a modern silhouette.

    Jeans¶

    All trouser fit rules apply. Additional considerations:

    Rise: The rise is the distance from the crotch seam to the waistband. Low-rise sits below the hips; mid-rise sits at the natural waist; high-rise sits at or above. For most men, mid-rise (the default for most jeans) is most flattering — it eliminates the visual break between top and bottom and avoids the crotch-drop effect of low-rise.

    Stretch vs. non-stretch: Raw (non-stretch) denim has no elastane. It's stiffer initially but molds to your body over 2–4 months of wear, creates unique fades, and lasts significantly longer than stretch denim. Stretch denim (1–2% elastane) is more comfortable from day one but loses shape faster and the elastane degrades after 2–3 years, causing bagginess in the knees and seat.

    Shrinkage: Raw denim shrinks — typically 1–2 sizes in waist and inseam on first wash. Buy accounts for this (some brands suggest soaking before first wear to pre-shrink). Washed denim has already been pre-shrunk.

    Jackets and Blazers¶

    This is the most complex fit category and the one where tailoring matters most.

    Shoulder: Same rule as shirts — seam exactly at the end of the shoulder bone. On a jacket, this is even more critical because the shoulder determines the entire drape of the garment. A jacket with a shoulder seam hanging off the end of your shoulder cannot be properly tailored without essentially rebuilding it. A jacket with shoulders too small creates pulling across the back and restriction in arm movement. When trying on a jacket, confirm the shoulder fit before anything else.

    Chest: When buttoned, you should be able to place a flat hand between the jacket and your chest with no resistance, but the jacket should not open or gap. A chest that's too tight creates an X-shaped pulling at the button; too loose creates shapeless fabric and the jacket falls away from the body.

    Button stance: The button (or top button on a two-button jacket) should sit at or just below your natural waist — the narrowest part of your torso. Buttons that sit too high or too low visually distort the waist.

    Lapels: The lapels should lie flat against the chest without curling or gaping away from the shirt. Lapels that bow outward indicate the chest is too small.

    Sleeve length: The jacket sleeve should expose approximately 1/4 to 1/2 inch of shirt cuff below it. This is easily adjusted and one of the most common tailoring alterations.

    Jacket length: The hem should cover the seat and end roughly where your fingers meet your hand when your arm hangs naturally. Too short reads as tight; too long reads as oversized.

    Back: A clean, smooth back with no horizontal creases (too tight) or vertical draping fabric (too loose). If the jacket pulls across the shoulder blades when you extend your arms forward, the back is too tight.

    What Tailoring Can Fix¶

    This is the practical list — what's worth taking to a tailor versus what's a structural mismatch you should not buy:

    Alteration Ease Cost Range Worth It?
    Hem trousers/jeans Easy $10–20 Always
    Take in trouser waist Easy $15–25 Yes
    Taper trouser leg Easy $15–30 Yes
    Shorten jacket sleeves (no buttons) Easy $15–25 Yes
    Shorten jacket sleeves (functional buttons) Moderate $40–80 Yes if you love the jacket
    Take in jacket sides (suppressing waist) Moderate $30–60 Yes
    Shorten jacket hem Moderate $30–50 Usually yes
    Shorten shirt sleeves Moderate $20–35 Yes for dress shirts
    Take in shirt body Moderate $25–40 Yes
    Replace suit lining Complex $100–200 Only for high-value items
    Fix shoulder seam position Complex/Expensive $100–300+ Usually not worth it
    Fix seat fit on trousers Complex $60–100 Depends on value
    Add a size to a small garment Rarely possible N/A Don't buy it

    The shoulder rule bears repeating: shoulders cannot be meaningfully fixed without rebuilding the garment from the top. This is the one fit point where "I'll get it tailored" is almost never the right answer. Walk away if the shoulders are wrong, even if everything else is perfect.

    Reading Off-the-Rack Sizing¶

    Off-the-rack sizing assumes an average body that doesn't match most people's actual proportions. What this means practically:

    Buy for your largest dimension and tailor down. If you have broad shoulders and a narrower waist, buy for the shoulders and take in the waist. Taking fabric in is always easier than letting it out. Most garments have limited seam allowance for letting out anyway.

    European sizing runs smaller than US sizing for the same number. Size M from a US brand and size M from an Italian or Swedish brand are different garments. Know which brands run true, which run large, and which run small — this is learned by trying things on or reading reviews.

    Fit varies by cut within a brand. "Slim fit" vs. "regular fit" vs. "relaxed fit" are the three cut families most brands offer. Don't assume your size from the slim fit line transfers to the regular fit line — measure and try.

    Key Considerations¶

    Find one tailor and stick with them. Tailoring quality varies enormously. A good tailor who knows your preferences and proportions is more valuable than hunting for the cheapest alterations. The relationship matters — a tailor who's done three pairs of your trousers knows exactly what "no break" means to you.

    Tailor before wearing, not after. Getting trousers hemmed before the first wear is the right habit. Wearing them unhemmed once creates a crease at the wrong length that a hem can't fully erase.

    Budget for tailoring at purchase. If you buy a $60 pair of chinos knowing they'll need hemming and waist suppression ($35 in alterations), the real cost is $95. That's still worth it — and it beats spending $200 on a premium brand hoping the fit is right.

    Fit changes with weight. A wardrobe that fits perfectly at one weight is wrong at another. This isn't a problem to solve — it's a reality to plan for. Quality pieces worth tailoring can be taken in or let out within a range (usually 1–2 sizes). This is part of the value of quality.

    Getting Started¶

    1. Try on everything you own with honest eyes. Shoulder seam position, sleeve length, trouser break. Identify what's wrong. Most people find 30–50% of what they own has fit issues they've been ignoring.
    2. Find a local tailor. Ask for a hem on an inexpensive pair of trousers as a test. Evaluate the result. A good tailor is one of the highest-value recurring costs in your wardrobe.
    3. On your next purchase, check shoulders first. Stand in front of a mirror with the jacket or shirt on. Look at the shoulder seam — is it on the bone? Everything else is fixable; this isn't.
    4. Hem your trousers. This single alteration changes the silhouette of every bottom you own. If you've been wearing trousers with a full break because you never bothered, one $15 hem per pair will immediately upgrade your look.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Buying for potential. "It'll fit when I lose 10 pounds" is not a fit strategy. Buy for your body now. Clothes that fit you today will look better than clothes waiting for a body you don't have yet.

    Trusting the mirror in-store. Store lighting and mirrors are designed to make you feel good about purchases. Do a basic shoulder check, sit down, and move around. Real-world fit is different from standing still under flattering light.

    Assuming "it'll stretch." Some fabrics do (raw denim molds to your body). Most don't. A tight shirt is a tight shirt.

    Skipping the tailor because the item was cheap. A $20 hem on a $40 pair of trousers creates a garment you'll actually wear. The cost is almost always worth it relative to the cost of clothes that sit unworn.

    Not knowing a garment's limits. Taking a jacket in two sizes or shortening a trouser rise significantly are major alterations that introduce new problems. Not everything can be tailored into something that works.
