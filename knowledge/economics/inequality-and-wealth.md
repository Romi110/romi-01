# Inequality and Wealth
*How wealth concentrates, how it's measured, why it compounds, and what actually determines economic mobility*

---

## What Is It

Inequality economics studies the distribution of income and wealth across households — why some end up with vastly more than others, how that distribution has changed over time, what mechanisms drive concentration, and what (if anything) can interrupt it.

It's not primarily about fairness, though fairness debates surround it. It's about understanding the mechanisms: why capital accumulates, why mobility differs across generations and countries, and what structural forces produce different distributional outcomes.

---

## Why It Matters

Wealth inequality isn't abstract — it determines who can afford to start a business, who has a buffer against a job loss, who can compound investment returns, and who must liquidate at the worst moments. Understanding the mechanisms is useful whether you're trying to build wealth (what allows compounding to work in your favor?) or understand the broader economy (does inequality cause or reflect economic conditions?).

For a self-reliant frame: the data on intergenerational mobility is the most practically relevant piece. It tells you how real the barriers are, which helps calibrate whether your strategy for building wealth is fighting the current or working with it.

## Core Concepts

### Income vs. Wealth: The Fundamental Distinction

**Income** is a flow: what you earn per year from wages, capital, business income.  
**Wealth** is a stock: the accumulated balance — assets minus liabilities.

This distinction is consistently confused in public debate. High income does not mean high wealth — a doctor earning $400,000 who spends $380,000 has no wealth. Moderate income compounded over decades into index funds can produce substantial wealth. This is covered in [economics/economics-overview.md](economics-overview.md) under Common Mistakes.

**Why wealth inequality exceeds income inequality**: wealth compounds. If you have $1 million earning 7%/year, you gain $70,000 in wealth from doing nothing. If you have $0, you gain $0. Starting wealth produces more wealth, which is why the distribution widens even when income distributions are relatively stable.

### Measuring Inequality: Gini and Beyond

The **Gini coefficient** is the standard summary measure:
- 0 = perfect equality (everyone has the same income/wealth)
- 1 = perfect inequality (one person has everything)
- US income Gini ≈ 0.41 (among the highest in developed countries; comparable to China, lower than South Africa and Brazil)
- US wealth Gini ≈ 0.85 — dramatically higher than income Gini, reflecting the compounding dynamics above

**Gini's limitations**: it's a single number summarizing the entire distribution. Two economies with the same Gini can have different shapes — one might have high middle-class concentration, another might have extreme top-end concentration. The Gini can't distinguish these.

**Top shares** complement the Gini: what fraction of total income/wealth is held by the top 1%, 0.1%, 0.01%?
- US: top 1% holds ~35% of wealth; top 10% holds ~70%
- The bottom 50% holds approximately 2% of wealth
- These numbers have worsened since the 1980s — the top 1% share in the US was ~25% in 1980

**Income vs. wealth shares diverge at the top**: the very wealthy derive most of their income from capital (dividends, capital gains, business income), not wages. This is why income tax increases that exempt capital gains have limited redistributive effect at the top — you're not taxing the primary income source of the ultra-wealthy.

### Piketty's r > g

Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2013) made one central empirical claim and one theoretical prediction:

**Empirical**: historically (pre-WWII), the wealthiest 10% owned 60–70% of total wealth in Europe and 45% in the US. Inequality declined from roughly 1930–1980 (WWII destruction, progressive taxation, strong unions, regulated capital) and has been rising since.

**Theoretical**: when the return on capital (r) exceeds the economy's growth rate (g), wealth concentration naturally increases. Those who own capital earn more than the economy grows — their share of the total expands. Those who rely on wages (whose growth is bounded by economic growth) fall behind relative to capital owners.

**r > g**: Piketty estimated r ≈ 4–5% historically (return to capital) and g ≈ 1–2% (long-run economic growth in mature economies). The gap means capital owners compound faster than the economy expands.

**The critical reception:**
- The r > g observation is widely accepted as roughly correct
- Critics (especially Larry Summers) argue the return to *net* capital (after depreciation) is not as consistently above g as Piketty claims
- The mechanism doesn't account for why wealthy dynasties have often dissipated wealth over generations (estate fragmentation, consumption, poor investment decisions) — if r > g were the complete story, families from the Gilded Age would still be dominant
- Piketty's policy prescription (global wealth tax) is politically implausible and technically difficult

**The most actionable implication**: owning capital (equity in businesses, real estate, indexed broad market funds) gives access to the r > g compounding that wealth inequality produces. Relying only on labor income means growing at g. This is the economic logic for investing rather than holding cash — you're accessing the capital return side of the r > g equation.

### The Mechanisms of Wealth Concentration

Several distinct mechanisms compound each other:

**Returns to scale in investment**: wealthier investors access asset classes unavailable to others (private equity, venture capital, real estate syndications). The returns to these asset classes have historically exceeded public equities. The entry barrier keeps the compounding inside the already-wealthy.

**Tax treatment of capital vs. labor**: in the US, long-term capital gains are taxed at 15–20%; ordinary income (wages) up to 37%. Wealthy individuals who derive income primarily from capital pay lower marginal rates than high-earning workers. This is Warren Buffett's famous observation — he pays a lower tax rate than his secretary.

**Inheritance and intergenerational transfer**: wealth transfers between generations without being taxed as income. The estate tax (applying only above ~$13 million per individual in 2024) captures a small fraction of intergenerational transfers; much of the transfer occurs through lifetime gifts, trusts, and appreciated assets that receive a step-up in basis at death.

**Network and social capital**: wealthy families provide education, connections, and professional networks that increase children's income-earning capacity independent of financial inheritance. This is the "soft" form of wealth transfer that doesn't appear in financial data.

**Debt access asymmetry**: wealthy individuals can borrow at low rates against their assets — a billionaire's stock portfolio secures a loan at 2%. Poor individuals can't secure cheap credit; payday loans at 400% APR represent the cost of capital for those with no assets.

### Intergenerational Mobility

**Intergenerational mobility** measures how much children's economic outcomes are determined by their parents'. Two metrics:

**Intergenerational income elasticity (IGE)**: if your parent earns 10% more than average, how much higher are your earnings predicted to be? IGE of 0 = no correlation (complete mobility). IGE of 1 = perfect correlation (no mobility). US IGE ≈ 0.45 — moderately persistent.

**Raj Chetty's absolute mobility measure**: what fraction of children earn more in real terms than their parents did? This has fallen dramatically:
- Children born in 1940: ~92% earned more than their parents
- Children born in 1980: ~50% earned more than their parents
- The decline reflects not increased inequality per se, but slower economic growth (the pie grew slower, so fewer children could exceed their parents absolutely)

**Geographic variation in mobility (Chetty's "Opportunity Atlas")**: mobility varies enormously by location. Salt Lake City, San Jose, and Seattle have high mobility (children from poor families do significantly better than expected). Atlanta, Detroit, and Charlotte have low mobility. The factors associated with high mobility: good schools, lower residential segregation, stronger family stability, higher social capital and civic engagement, lower income inequality.

**The mechanism that matters for policy**: mobility is not purely about financial transfers. The conditions in which children develop — neighborhood, school quality, family stability, peer networks — have large and durable effects on outcomes independent of parental income. This is why housing policy (who can afford to live near good schools) and education policy are inextricably linked to mobility.

### What Inequality Does to the Economy

The distributional consequences of inequality are better documented than the macroeconomic effects, which are genuinely debated:

**Aggregate demand effects (Keynesian argument)**: lower-income households spend a higher fraction of marginal income (higher marginal propensity to consume). As income concentrates at the top (where the marginal propensity to consume is lower), aggregate demand falls relative to income — leading to slower growth, which may be offset by debt accumulation (households borrow to maintain consumption). This is one explanation for the 2000s credit boom.

**Growth and investment effects (the contested part)**: some argue inequality incentivizes effort and risk-taking (returns to success are high — so more people try). Others argue it concentrates resources in the hands of those who invest in rent-seeking (capturing existing value) rather than innovation (creating new value). The empirical relationship between inequality and growth is U-shaped in theory but difficult to isolate in practice.

**Political economy effects**: high wealth inequality concentrates political influence — via campaign finance, lobbying, and media ownership. This allows wealthy interests to shape tax policy, regulatory policy, and redistribution in ways that further entrench their position. The feedback between economic and political power is one reason inequality can be self-reinforcing in democratic systems.

## Key Considerations

**Inequality at what level?** Global inequality has fallen dramatically — living standards in China, India, and Sub-Saharan Africa have risen faster than in rich countries since 1990. Within-country inequality in the US and Europe has risen. Whether you're describing a trend as good or bad depends on which lens you use.

**Income vs. consumption inequality**: measured by consumption rather than income, inequality is lower — because lower-income households receive transfers (Medicaid, food stamps, housing vouchers) that don't show in pre-tax income. Neither measure is "right"; they answer different questions.

**Mobility vs. inequality are distinct policy targets.** You can have a highly unequal society with high mobility (people move through the distribution) or a more equal society with low mobility (the distribution is compressed but sticky). Nordic countries have lower inequality *and* higher mobility than the US. The US has higher inequality *and* lower mobility than most comparators — a particularly unfavorable combination.

## Getting Started

1. **Use Chetty's Opportunity Atlas** (opportunityatlas.org) — enter any zip code in NJ and see the actual mobility outcomes for children from different income levels who grew up there. Concrete and local.
2. **Read the introduction and conclusion of *Capital in the Twenty-First Century*** (Piketty) — skip the 600-page middle unless you want the full empirical treatment. The core argument is in the bookends.
3. **Distinguish your income from your wealth.** Calculate your actual net worth (assets minus liabilities). What fraction is in compounding assets (equities, business equity, real estate equity) vs. depreciating assets (cars, consumer goods) vs. cash? That ratio determines which side of r > g you're on.

## Common Mistakes

**Conflating income inequality with wealth inequality.** They're correlated but different. Income redistribution (via progressive taxes and transfers) can reduce income inequality without touching wealth concentration, which is driven by different mechanisms.

**Assuming all inequality is bad or all is fine.** Some inequality reflects different choices, skills, and risk-taking. Extreme inequality — especially intergenerational — reflects structural barriers more than earned differences. The distinction matters for both policy and personal moral reasoning.

**Treating r > g as deterministic.** Piketty's framework describes tendencies under current conditions, not iron laws. Tax policy, inheritance law, and economic growth rates can all change the dynamic. The postwar compression (1945–1980) happened; the current reconcentration is not inevitable.

## Resources

- *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* — Thomas Piketty. Read the introduction and Chapter 10 ("Inequality of Capital Ownership"). The empirical data is the contribution; don't get lost in the full book.
- *The Fading American Dream* — Raj Chetty et al. (2017 paper, available free). The definitive data on declining absolute mobility. 30 pages; the graphs are the content.
- *Wealth of Nations vs. Winner-Take-All* — the policy debate between Emmanuel Saez (pro-redistribution) and Greg Mankiw (anti-redistribution) is well-summarized in their respective papers, both available freely. Read both to see the shape of the actual disagreement.
- *The Price of Inequality* — Joseph Stiglitz. The case that inequality damages economic growth and political democracy. Readable and direct on mechanisms.
