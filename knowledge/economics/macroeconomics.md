# Macroeconomics
*How aggregate economies behave — business cycles, policy tools, and why the whole is not the sum of its parts*

---

## What Is It

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole: total output, employment, price levels, and the interaction of monetary and fiscal policy. Where microeconomics asks "why does this firm charge this price?", macroeconomics asks "why did the US economy contract 10% in a quarter?" or "why does Japan have low growth and low inflation despite running enormous deficits?"

The field was born from the Great Depression. Before Keynes, economists largely believed markets would self-correct — unemployment was temporary, wages would fall until workers were re-hired. The Depression persisted for a decade and killed that confidence. Macroeconomics developed as the attempt to explain why, and what governments could do about it.

It's a harder science than micro. You can't run controlled experiments on national economies. The feedback loops are long, the data is aggregate, and the politics of policy create motivated reasoning on all sides.

---

## Why It Matters

Macroeconomic conditions set the backdrop for everything else. Inflation erodes savings. Recessions destroy businesses and livelihoods. Interest rate decisions by the Fed affect the cost of every mortgage, business loan, and bond in the country. Understanding macro means you're not surprised when rates rise and your home equity line becomes expensive, or when quantitative tightening hits stock valuations. You also develop immunity to confident macroeconomic predictions — the forecasting record is poor.

## Core Concepts

### Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

The macro-level equivalent of supply and demand.

**Aggregate Demand (AD)**: total demand for goods and services across the entire economy at different price levels. Components: C (consumption) + I (investment) + G (government spending) + NX (net exports). AD slopes downward: at lower price levels, real wealth is higher, interest rates tend to be lower, and exports are cheaper — all of which increase spending.

**Aggregate Supply (AS)**: total output the economy can produce at different price levels.
- **Short-run AS (SRAS)**: slopes upward. At higher price levels, firms expand production because input costs (especially wages) adjust slowly. In the short run, firms can increase output by running harder — overtime, drawing down inventory.
- **Long-run AS (LRAS)**: vertical. In the long run, the economy produces at its **potential output** (also called full-employment output) regardless of the price level. Wages and prices have had time to adjust. Potential output is set by real factors — technology, capital stock, labor force size and skills, institutions.

**Equilibrium**: where AD meets SRAS. When output exceeds potential (above LRAS), wages rise, costs rise, SRAS shifts left → price level rises (inflation). When output is below potential (recession), wages and prices eventually fall, SRAS shifts right → recovery. This automatic adjustment is the classical argument against intervention. Keynesians argue it's too slow — the long run may be years away, and people suffer in the interim.

**Demand shocks** shift AD. COVID shutdowns crashed consumer spending — massive negative demand shock. CARES Act stimulus was a positive demand shock. Supply chain disruptions that reduce what the economy can produce are supply shocks.

**Supply shocks** shift SRAS. The 1973 oil embargo was a negative supply shock — input costs rose, output fell. The effect: **stagflation** (falling output + rising prices). Stagflation is the nightmare because the normal policy response to falling output (stimulate demand) makes inflation worse, while the response to inflation (raise rates) makes the recession worse.

### Business Cycles

Economies don't grow smoothly. They cycle through expansion, peak, contraction, and trough — then repeat.

**Expansion**: output rising, unemployment falling, consumer confidence high, business investment increasing. Credit growth typically accelerates. Inflation may begin to build.

**Peak**: maximum output. Often characterized by overconfidence, leverage, and asset price inflation. The peak isn't visible in real time — it's only identified in retrospect.

**Contraction/Recession**: technically, two consecutive quarters of falling GDP. Investment collapses first (firms cut capital expenditure quickly). Consumer spending follows. Unemployment rises. Credit tightens (banks pull back, firms can't borrow). Inventory drawdowns.

**Trough**: lowest point. Unemployment highest. Eventually, inventories draw down enough that firms restart production. Interest rate cuts (if there's room) make borrowing cheap. Recovery begins.

**Why cycles happen**: several competing theories.
- **Demand-side (Keynesian)**: animal spirits. Sudden collapse in business confidence (investment dries up) or consumer confidence (spending falls) creates a self-fulfilling contraction. The solution: government stimulus to fill the demand gap.
- **Real Business Cycle (RBC)**: cycles are efficient responses to real shocks (technology changes, resource availability). Recessions are optimal responses to productivity shocks. Controversial and empirically contested, but influential in academic macro.
- **Credit/financial cycles (Minsky)**: economies go through phases of stability → overconfidence → leverage → fragility → collapse. "Stability breeds instability." The 2008 crisis is the canonical Minsky moment. Long dismissed; now mainstream.
- **Austrian business cycle theory**: artificially low interest rates from central banks distort investment toward capital-intensive projects that wouldn't otherwise be profitable. When rates normalize, these "malinvestments" fail — the boom becomes a bust. Connected to [economics/austrian-school.md](austrian-school.md).

Most serious economists use elements of multiple frameworks. The honest answer is that cycles are multi-causal and the relative weight of causes varies by episode.

**Leading vs. lagging indicators**: Macro data is lagged (GDP reported weeks after the quarter ends). Practitioners use leading indicators to anticipate turns: yield curve (inverted yield curve has preceded most US recessions), housing permits, consumer sentiment, stock prices, PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index). Unemployment is a lagging indicator — it peaks after the recession has ended.

### IS-LM Framework

Developed by John Hicks as a formalization of Keynes. Describes equilibrium in both the goods market and the money market simultaneously. Used for decades as the standard macro teaching tool.

**IS curve (Investment-Savings)**: combinations of interest rates and output where the goods market is in equilibrium. When interest rates fall, investment rises, output rises — IS slopes downward.

**LM curve (Liquidity-Money)**: combinations of interest rates and output where the money market is in equilibrium. Higher output → more money demand → higher interest rates — LM slopes upward.

**Equilibrium**: where IS meets LM — the combination of interest rate and output where both markets clear simultaneously.

**Policy analysis:**
- Fiscal expansion (more government spending) shifts IS right → higher output AND higher interest rates. The higher interest rate "crowds out" some private investment — government borrowing competes for the same pool of savings.
- Monetary expansion (lower interest rates from the Fed) shifts LM right → lower interest rates AND higher output.
- **Liquidity trap**: when interest rates hit zero, the LM curve becomes flat. Monetary policy loses traction — you can't push rates below zero (or can you? — see negative interest rates). The IS-LM model explains why QE was invented: normal rate cuts were exhausted in 2008.

**Limits of IS-LM**: it's a static, short-run model. It doesn't model inflation expectations (added later by Friedman's critique), it treats money supply as directly controllable (not accurate for modern banking), and it ignores financial sector dynamics entirely. It's useful as a teaching device and intuition pump, not as a forecasting tool.

### Monetary vs. Fiscal Policy

These are the two levers governments use to stabilize the economy. They interact and conflict.

**Monetary policy** is controlled by the central bank (the Fed in the US). Primary tool: the federal funds rate. Secondary tools: quantitative easing, forward guidance, reserve requirements. See [economics/monetary-policy.md](monetary-policy.md) for full depth.

**Fiscal policy** is controlled by Congress and the President. Tools: government spending and taxation. Budget surplus (tax > spend) is contractionary. Budget deficit (spend > tax) is expansionary.

**Keynesian argument for fiscal policy**: in a liquidity trap (interest rates at zero), monetary policy is ineffective. Fiscal stimulus — direct government spending — bypasses the financial sector and injects demand directly. The multiplier effect: $1 of government spending generates more than $1 of economic activity because recipients spend the money, which becomes income for others, and so on.

**The crowding out counterargument**: government borrowing to fund deficits raises interest rates, reducing private investment. The stimulus is partially offset. The empirical magnitude of crowding out is debated; at low interest rates and high unemployment, evidence suggests it's small.

**Ricardian equivalence** (Robert Barro): if consumers are rational and forward-looking, they'll save more when the government deficit-spends, expecting future tax increases to pay the debt. The fiscal stimulus is thus fully offset by reduced private spending. Theoretically elegant; empirically rejected — consumers don't behave this way in practice.

**Automatic stabilizers**: programs that automatically expand spending (or reduce taxes) during recessions without requiring new legislation. Unemployment insurance, food stamps, progressive income taxes (as income falls, you pay less tax automatically). These dampen the cycle without requiring political action.

**Coordination problem**: fiscal and monetary policy can work at cross-purposes. The Fed tightening (raising rates) while Congress stimulates (deficit spending) creates conflict — as happened in the early 1980s when Volcker's Fed tightened aggressively against Reagan's tax cuts.

### Macroeconomic Schools of Thought in Practice

The [economics-overview.md](economics-overview.md) covers the intellectual history of the schools. Here's where they disagree in practice:

**On recessions**: Keynesians say stimulate demand (spend, cut taxes). Neoclassicals/monetarists say stabilize money supply and let markets clear. Austrians say the recession is necessary — let the malinvestments fail, don't extend the boom.

**On inflation**: monetarists (Friedman) say it's always a monetary phenomenon — control money supply. Keynesians say inflation depends on output gaps and expectations. Post-Keynesians say cost-push and supply factors matter as much as demand.

**On deficits**: mainstream view: deficits are sustainable if growth > interest rate (r < g). Austrians: deficits always crowd out private investment and distort resource allocation. MMT: currency-issuing governments don't face a hard budget constraint — the limit is inflation, not solvency.

**On growth**: mainstream: growth comes from technology and human capital (Solow model). Institutionalists: quality of institutions (rule of law, property rights, governance) matters as much as inputs. Endogenous growth theorists: innovation itself is partly a function of economic conditions — it can be encouraged by policy.

## Key Considerations

**The fallacy of composition** is central to macro. What's rational for an individual may be irrational at the aggregate. If everyone saves more in a recession (individually rational), total spending falls, output falls, and everyone's income falls — meaning they actually save less. The "paradox of thrift." Macro is full of these aggregate-level paradoxes.

**Expectations matter enormously.** Modern macro (New Keynesian) emphasizes that what people expect to happen shapes what actually happens. If people expect high inflation, they demand higher wages, which causes higher inflation. If the Fed can credibly commit to a 2% target, expectations stay anchored — which itself helps keep inflation at 2%. Central bank communication is partly about managing expectations.

**The Lucas critique** (Robert Lucas, 1976): when governments implement policy, private actors change their behavior in response. Historical relationships between variables (like inflation and unemployment) may not hold post-policy because behavior changes. This is why macro is hard — your policy changes the system you're trying to steer.

**Data revisions**: GDP numbers are revised multiple times. What looks like strong growth in real time may be revised down significantly. Macro data is imprecise and backward-looking. Never treat an initial GDP print as definitive.

## Getting Started

1. **Read *A Monetary History of the United States*** (Friedman & Schwartz) or just the chapter on the Great Depression — it will make the mechanisms concrete and explain the origin of modern monetary policy.
2. **Follow one business cycle through** — pick 2008 or 2020 and reconstruct the sequence: what broke first, what policy response happened when, what the debates were. The [economic-history.md](../history/economic-history.md) file covers this.
3. **Track the yield curve** — it's the single most legible leading indicator. When the 2-year Treasury yield exceeds the 10-year, historically recession follows within 12–18 months (though with the usual caveats about forecasting).

## Common Mistakes

**Applying household budget logic to national economies.** A household that spends more than it earns goes broke. A currency-issuing government is not a household — it has tools (money creation, debt issuance, taxation) unavailable to individuals. The analogy is actively misleading in most macro debates.

**Treating any macro school as settled science.** None of them is. The 1970s stagflation broke Keynesian confidence. The 2008 crisis and post-2008 low inflation broke monetarist confidence. The 2021–23 inflation surprised almost every school. Humility is warranted.

**Confusing correlation with policy causation.** The stock market goes up during recoveries. That doesn't mean the stock market causes recoveries or that policies targeting the stock market cause economic health.

## Resources

- *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* — Keynes. Dense. Read Chapter 12 ("The State of Long-Term Expectation") — it's the most relevant and readable chapter on animal spirits.
- *A Monetary History of the United States* — Friedman & Schwartz. Long; the Great Depression chapters are the essential read.
- *Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis* — original paper (1992). Short; explains the logic of why financial stability breeds instability.
- *Macroeconomics* — Gregory Mankiw. The standard textbook if you want rigorous treatment of IS-LM, AS-AD, and the models.
