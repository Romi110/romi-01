# Trade and Globalization
*Why countries trade, what it actually produces, and what's changing as the global order fragments*

---

## What Is It

International trade is the exchange of goods, services, and capital across borders. Globalization is the process by which trade, investment, and economic activity have increasingly crossed national boundaries over the past 200 years — accelerating dramatically from the 1980s onward.

The economics of trade is one area where the professional consensus has been unusually clear and unusually challenged. Economists nearly universally support free trade on efficiency grounds. The political backlash of the 2010s — Brexit, Trump tariffs, reshoring, the China decoupling drive — reflects a real tension: trade generates aggregate gains while producing concentrated losses. The aggregate wins; specific communities lose. That gap is at the center of every current trade debate.

---

## Why It Matters

Trade policy is now geopolitical policy. Tariffs are the primary tool in the US-China confrontation. Supply chain resilience has become a national security issue. The semiconductor, pharmaceutical, and rare earth supply chains are now officially strategic assets. For someone tracking global risk and making long-term preparations (see [geopolitics/supply-chain-resilience.md](../geopolitics/supply-chain-resilience.md)), understanding the economics behind these political decisions matters.

## Core Concepts

### Comparative Advantage: The Theoretical Foundation

The [economics-overview.md](economics-overview.md) introduces comparative advantage. The deeper version is more interesting.

**Ricardo's model (1817)**: Portugal can produce both wine and cloth more efficiently than England. Despite Portugal's absolute advantage in both, both countries gain from trade if Portugal specializes in wine (where its relative advantage is greater) and England specializes in cloth (where its relative disadvantage is smaller). Each country's opportunity cost determines what it should specialize in.

**The terms of trade**: the relative price at which countries exchange goods. If Portugal gets 1.5 yards of cloth per bottle of wine (the terms of trade), and its domestic opportunity cost was 1 yard per bottle, Portugal gains from trade. The terms of trade must fall between both countries' domestic opportunity costs for both to benefit. Bargaining power, market structure, and demand elasticities shape where in this range the terms land.

**Factor proportions (Heckscher-Ohlin)**: countries export goods that intensively use their abundant factors. Labor-abundant countries (China in the 1990s) export labor-intensive goods. Capital-abundant countries (US, Germany) export capital-intensive goods. Land-abundant countries (Canada, Australia, Brazil) export agricultural goods. This explains more trade patterns than Ricardian comparative advantage alone.

**The Stolper-Samuelson theorem** (the political economy consequence of H-O): trade benefits the abundant factor and hurts the scarce factor. In the US, trade with labor-abundant countries benefits capital owners and hurts unskilled labor. This is not just political rhetoric — it's embedded in the theory. The aggregate GDP gain hides a real distributional shift. The workers who lost manufacturing jobs to China weren't wrong about what happened to them; they just couldn't be easily compensated from aggregate gains.

**What the model misses**: comparative advantage is static. It explains current trade patterns but doesn't capture dynamic effects — the ability to develop new comparative advantages through industrial policy, learning-by-doing, and scale effects. Japan and South Korea didn't have comparative advantages in electronics and automobiles in 1960; they built them through active industrial policy. This is the theoretical basis for infant industry protection — controversial but with some empirical support in specific historical cases.

### The WTO and the Postwar Trade Order

**Bretton Woods (1944)** created the postwar economic architecture: IMF (monetary stability), World Bank (development lending), and GATT (tariff reduction). The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade ran from 1947 until it became the **World Trade Organization (WTO)** in 1995.

**WTO principles:**
- **Most Favored Nation (MFN)**: any trade advantage granted to one member must be extended to all. You can't give Japan a sweetheart deal that China doesn't get.
- **National Treatment**: imported goods must be treated the same as domestic goods once inside a border. No discriminatory regulations or standards.
- **Reciprocal concessions**: tariff reductions are negotiated in rounds (Uruguay Round 1986–1994 was the last major one). Members offer reductions in exchange for reciprocal reductions.
- **Dispute settlement**: the WTO's Appellate Body adjudicates trade disputes between members. The US has blocked appointments to the Appellate Body since 2019, effectively paralysing WTO dispute settlement.

**The China accession (2001)**: China joined the WTO in 2001 under terms that assumed it would liberalize over time. It didn't — state subsidies, intellectual property theft, currency management, and market access restrictions continued. The WTO lacks enforcement mechanisms when a major power doesn't comply. The "China shock" research (Autor, Dorn, Hanson) documented that Chinese import competition eliminated 2–2.4 million US manufacturing jobs 1999–2011, with concentrated regional devastation (the Midwest manufacturing belt) and no effective policy response.

**Regional trade agreements**: the WTO allows bilateral and regional free trade agreements as exceptions to MFN. NAFTA (1994, renegotiated as USMCA 2020), EU single market, RCEP (Asia-Pacific, 2020 — the world's largest FTA by GDP). These shape trade at least as much as WTO rules.

### Trade Deficits: What They Actually Mean

Trade deficits are chronically misunderstood in political debate.

**Trade deficit** = imports > exports. The US runs a persistent trade deficit — most recently around $800B–$1T/year in goods, partially offset by a services surplus (~$250B).

**What a trade deficit IS NOT**: it's not evidence that foreign countries are "beating" the US, that jobs are being destroyed on net, or that the country is losing. These framings conflate trade balances with economic performance.

**What a trade deficit actually reflects**: a **current account deficit** must be offset by a **capital account surplus** (accounting identity — the balance of payments must balance). When the US imports more than it exports, foreigners receive dollars. They use those dollars to invest in US assets — Treasuries, stocks, real estate, direct investment. A trade deficit means the US is a capital importer — foreigners want to hold US assets.

**Why the US runs a persistent deficit**: the dollar is the global reserve currency. Foreign governments and central banks hold dollars as reserves. To hold dollars, they must run trade surpluses with the US, sending goods in exchange for dollar assets. US trade deficits are, in part, the price of dollar reserve status. This is the "exorbitant privilege" (Valéry Giscard d'Estaing's phrase) — the US borrows cheaply in its own currency because the world needs dollars.

**When trade deficits matter**: if the deficit reflects excess consumption funded by debt (not investment), it can be a vulnerability. If it reflects productive capital inflows (foreign investment in US productive capacity), it's less concerning. The composition matters; the headline number doesn't tell you enough.

**Bilateral trade balances** (US-China, US-Germany) are mostly meaningless for policy. Global supply chains mean components cross multiple borders before final assembly. An iPhone assembled in China contains components from Japan, South Korea, Germany, and Taiwan — counting it as a Chinese export to the US overstates Chinese value added and the bilateral "imbalance."

### Tariffs: Mechanics and Effects

A **tariff** is a tax on imports. A **specific tariff** is a fixed dollar amount per unit ($5/ton). An **ad valorem tariff** is a percentage of the value (25% on steel).

**Who pays tariffs?**: the importing country's consumers and firms. When the US places a 25% tariff on Chinese steel, US steel buyers (manufacturers, construction) pay 25% more for that steel, whether they buy the now-more-expensive Chinese steel or switch to domestic producers who've been able to raise prices. The tariff is not "paid by China." This is the most common public misunderstanding in trade policy.

**Terms of trade argument**: there's one legitimate theoretical case for tariffs — a large country (like the US) can use tariffs to worsen the terms of trade for exporters, forcing them to lower prices to maintain market access. The US pays less per unit of Chinese goods; China's net export revenue falls. Optimal tariff theory says this can improve the large country's welfare — at the expense of the exporting country. In practice, retaliation limits this benefit; the net effect of the US-China tariff war was estimated at $316B in costs to US consumers and firms (US-China Business Council estimates).

**Infant industry protection**: tariffs to protect emerging industries until they reach competitive scale. The US steel industry in the 19th century, South Korean electronics in the 1970s–80s. The standard critique: the protected industry rarely becomes competitive on its own; protection becomes permanent (US sugar tariffs have been in place for 80 years). The rejoinder: some industries did develop under protection. The empirical record is mixed but the theory has merit in specific cases with defined timelines.

**National security tariffs**: Section 232 (US law) allows tariffs on national security grounds. Trump used this for steel (2018) and semiconductors. The category is increasingly elastic — advanced manufacturing broadly is now categorized as "national security" in US policy. This is a real shift from efficiency-based to security-based trade logic.

**Non-tariff barriers (NTBs)**: regulations, standards, licensing requirements, and procedures that restrict trade without explicit tariffs. Japan's agricultural standards, EU's regulatory standards for food and chemicals (often more restrictive than US), "buy American" procurement requirements. Often harder to negotiate away than tariffs because they're embedded in domestic regulatory systems.

### The China Question

The US-China trade relationship is now explicitly decoupling — not into full autarky but into a managed separation of strategic supply chains.

**The phases:**
- **Pre-2001**: some trade, significant restrictions on Chinese goods
- **2001–2016**: China WTO integration, massive growth in bilateral trade, enormous US consumer benefit from low-cost Chinese goods, manufacturing displacement in the US
- **2017–2020**: Trump tariffs (Section 301: 25% on $250B of Chinese goods, 7.5% on $120B more), Chinese retaliation, "trade war"
- **2020–present**: bipartisan hardening. Biden maintained Trump tariffs, added restrictions on semiconductor equipment exports (October 2022), AI chips (October 2023), and imposed additional tariffs on EVs, solar panels, and critical goods in 2024.

**Semiconductor decoupling**: the October 2022 export controls restricted not just chips but the equipment and software needed to make advanced chips, and any non-US entity that uses US technology to make such equipment. Designed to freeze China at its current capability level and prevent advancement. The most aggressive extraterritorial application of US trade controls since the Cold War. See [geopolitics/technology-cold-war.md](../geopolitics/technology-cold-war.md) for the geopolitical dimension.

**Supply chain diversification (China+1)**: companies building production alternatives in Vietnam, India, Mexico, and Indonesia. Not elimination of China exposure but reduction of single-point dependence. This is genuinely happening: Apple, Samsung, and others have moved significant production out of China. The scale is still modest relative to total China integration — full decoupling is not occurring and may be impossible for many goods.

**Costs of decoupling**: McKinsey estimates rebuilding semiconductor supply chains in the US would cost $1T+ and take 10+ years, with chips costing 35–65% more than Asian-manufactured equivalents. There are no free lunches in supply chain localization.

### Reshoring Economics

**Why companies offshored**: labor cost arbitrage (Chinese manufacturing wages in 2000 were 3–5% of US wages), scale (Chinese suppliers had enormous scale), supply chain density (entire ecosystems of specialized suppliers concentrated in specific regions), and logistics improvement (container shipping made long-distance supply chains viable).

**Why reshoring is happening**: labor cost convergence (Chinese wages 5–10x 2000 levels), supply chain risk made visible by COVID disruptions, political/policy pressure (IRA, CHIPS Act subsidies), tariffs making Chinese imports more expensive, automation reducing the labor cost differential.

**The CHIPS and Science Act (2022)**: $52B in direct subsidies for semiconductor manufacturing in the US. Intel, TSMC, Samsung committed to US fab construction. First new US semiconductor fabs in decades. The chips themselves will still be significantly more expensive — the economic case is national security, not efficiency.

**The Inflation Reduction Act (2022)**: massive subsidies for domestic clean energy manufacturing, explicitly conditional on domestic content requirements. EV tax credits require battery components from the US or free trade agreement partners. Industrial policy on a scale not seen since WWII. Triggered significant European resentment (EU industries said it would divert investment from Europe to the US — they weren't wrong).

**Where reshoring works**: 
- High-value goods where labor cost is a small fraction of total cost (semiconductors, aircraft engines, medical devices)
- Security-critical supply chains (pharmaceuticals, munitions)
- Automation-heavy manufacturing where robots eliminate labor cost differentials
- Perishable or time-sensitive goods where logistics costs offset labor savings

**Where it doesn't**: labor-intensive consumer goods (clothing, simple electronics, toys). US consumers would face significantly higher prices. Full re-shoring of consumer goods is not economically viable without accepting permanent inflation in those categories or massive subsidies.

## Key Considerations

**The gains from trade are real but unequally distributed.** The aggregate efficiency gain from comparative advantage is not a myth — it's measurable. So is the distributional harm to workers displaced by import competition. Policy failure was not in trade liberalization itself but in the absence of effective adjustment assistance, retraining programs, and regional economic support. The politics turned because the economic harm was concentrated and visible while the consumer benefits were diffuse and invisible.

**Trade and geopolitics are now inseparable.** The WTO-centric rule-based trade order assumed member states would subordinate economic self-interest to mutual benefit via rules. China's rise and the US-China confrontation have broken that assumption. Trade policy is now explicitly used as geopolitical leverage: tariffs, export controls, investment restrictions, and sanctions are tools in a strategic competition, not just economic optimization.

**Currency manipulation is as powerful as tariffs.** If China keeps its currency undervalued relative to the dollar, Chinese exports are artificially cheap and US exports to China are artificially expensive. The US and China have accused each other of currency manipulation repeatedly. The mechanism: China's central bank buys dollar assets (US Treasuries), keeping yuan demand low and dollar demand high → yuan stays undervalued. Since 2015, China has allowed the yuan to gradually appreciate.

**Services trade is growing and under-measured.** Most trade debate focuses on goods. But US services exports (finance, professional services, education, IP licensing, cloud computing, entertainment) exceed $1T annually. The US runs a large services trade surplus. Services are harder to tariff (you can't stop a software download at the border in the same way) but are increasingly subject to market access restrictions (China blocks US tech companies) and data localization requirements.

## Getting Started

1. **Read the Autor, Dorn, Hanson "China Shock" paper** — it's the empirical foundation for understanding why free trade theory and political backlash coexist. Accessible summary: NBER Working Paper 21906. Changes how you evaluate pro-trade arguments.
2. **Follow the US-China trade war tariff tracker** (Peterson Institute for International Economics maintains one) — seeing the sequence of escalation, retaliation, and exceptions makes the mechanics concrete.
3. **Look at your own supply chain exposure** — pick any electronic device you own and map where the components are made. The complexity of actual supply chains is invisible until you trace it.

## Common Mistakes

**"We have a trade deficit so we're losing."** No. Trade deficits reflect capital flows, not economic failure. The US ran a trade deficit for most of its strongest economic decades.

**"Tariffs protect jobs."** Sometimes, in specific sectors, temporarily. But they protect those jobs at the expense of consumers (higher prices) and other industries (downstream manufacturers pay more for inputs). The net job effect of tariffs is typically close to zero or negative in aggregate.

**Assuming comparative advantage is static.** Countries can change what they're good at. Industrial policy that builds new capabilities can shift comparative advantage — South Korea and Taiwan did exactly this. The static model of free trade misses this dynamic.

**Treating reshoring as a simple reversal of offshoring.** Supply chain complexity built over 40 years cannot be unwound in 4 years. The costs, timelines, and feasibility vary enormously by industry. Not all reshoring is economically viable without permanent subsidy.
