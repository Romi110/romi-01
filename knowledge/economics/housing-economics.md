# Housing Economics
*Why housing is so expensive, what zoning does, and the actual math behind rent vs. buy*

---

## What Is It

Housing economics studies why homes cost what they do, how housing markets fail to work like other markets, and what happens when policy intervenes. It applies microeconomic tools — supply and demand, elasticity, market failure — to a good that is uniquely fixed in location, takes years to build, commands enormous household expenditures, and serves simultaneously as shelter and financial asset.

Housing costs in major US metro areas are not a mystery — they're the predictable result of restricted supply meeting inelastic demand. The mechanisms are well understood. The politics of the solution are the obstacle.

---

## Why It Matters

Housing is your largest expense and (if you own) likely your largest asset. Understanding the market means:
- Knowing when rent vs. buy is economically rational (the answer depends on how long you stay and local price-to-rent ratios, not on generalities)
- Understanding why NJ property is expensive and what drives those prices
- Seeing through narratives ("it's speculators" / "it's immigrants") to the actual supply-demand dynamics
- Making better decisions about where to live and when to buy

## Core Concepts

### Why Housing Supply Is Inelastic

In most markets, high prices attract new supply — factories build more units when the good's price rises. Housing supply is notoriously slow and constrained because:

**Land is fixed in location.** You can't move land to where demand is highest. San Francisco has enormous demand for housing, but you can't ship in plots from Nevada.

**Zoning restricts density.** Zoning laws determine what can be built where: single-family only (SFZ), minimum lot sizes, height limits, setback requirements, parking minimums. In most desirable US cities, large portions of land are zoned exclusively for single-family homes — meaning an apartment building cannot be built there at any price. This is a legal cap on supply that operates independently of economics.

**Construction is slow.** Permitting, environmental review (CEQA in California is the infamous example), design, financing, construction — a project can take 5–7 years from concept to occupancy. The supply response to a demand shock is measured in years, not months.

**Existing homeowners oppose new supply.** Homeowners benefit from scarcity — it increases property values. They have strong incentives to attend city planning meetings and oppose new development. Renters (who would benefit from more supply) are diffuse and less organized. This **political economy** produces systematically restricted supply even in cities with explicit housing shortage emergencies.

**The result**: supply elasticity in most major US metros is low. When demand for housing rises (job growth, population growth, falling interest rates), prices rise rather than supply expanding to absorb the shock. This is why places like San Francisco, NYC, and NJ have housing costs that have compounded for decades.

### Supply Elasticity Across Cities

Cities vary enormously in how responsive their housing supply is to price signals. **Houston** is the canonical example of an elastic supply city — permissive zoning, fast permitting, flat land, no geographic constraints. When housing prices rise, Houston builds. This is why Houston is one of the few major US cities with relatively affordable housing despite strong growth.

**Inelastic cities** (San Francisco, NYC, Boston, coastal NJ): geographic constraints (ocean, bay, mountains), strict zoning, strong homeowner political power, and complex permitting. Demand shocks become price shocks because supply can't respond.

This elasticity difference is the core explanation for why some cities have unaffordable housing and others don't — it's not primarily about incomes, taxes, or speculation. It's about whether the supply side can respond.

### Why Cities Are So Expensive

Several compounding forces:

**Agglomeration economies**: productivity is higher in dense urban areas. People and firms clustered together generate knowledge spillovers, thicker labor markets (specialists find each other more easily), and better matching between buyers and sellers. This means firms pay more to be in cities, which means workers are worth more in cities, which means workers bid more for housing in cities. The demand side reflects genuinely higher economic productivity in dense locations.

**Supply constraints**: the agglomeration premium can't be arbitraged away by building more because supply is restricted. If San Francisco could build enough housing to accommodate everyone who wants to live there, prices would fall until the wage premium approximately offset the cost of living premium. Instead, supply restriction means the productivity premium accrues as land value, not as accessible living for workers.

**Interest rates and the asset price channel**: housing is a financial asset. When interest rates fall, the present value of future housing services rises — you can borrow more for the same monthly payment, so buyers bid up prices. The 2010–2022 period of historically low rates contributed significantly to housing price appreciation. The 2022–2023 rate rises cooled markets that had become extremely overvalued relative to rental income.

**Financialization and short-term rentals**: institutional investors (single-family rental REITs) and Airbnb conversions reduce housing available to long-term residents. The magnitude of this effect is real but often overstated in popular narratives — it's not the primary driver of housing costs (supply restriction is), but it's a meaningful marginal factor in specific markets.

### Rent Control

Rent control caps how much landlords can raise rents for existing tenants. It's the most politically durable but economically contested housing intervention.

**What it does for current tenants**: keeps rents below market for people who have been in a unit — genuine protection, especially for low-income renters who would otherwise be displaced.

**What it does to the market:**
- Reduces housing supply: landlords convert to condos, let units decay, or remove from rental market (rather than accept below-market returns indefinitely)
- Reduces mobility: tenants stay in rent-controlled units even when the unit no longer fits their needs (empty nesters in 3-bedrooms), because moving means losing the subsidy
- Misallocates housing: the wrong people end up in the wrong units based on who got there first, not who values or needs the space
- Shifts the cost to newcomers: new and market-rate tenants subsidize existing tenants; rents for uncontrolled units rise to compensate

A 2019 Stanford study found San Francisco's rent control reduced rental housing supply by 15% as landlords converted units — partially offsetting the benefit to protected tenants by raising market rents.

**The honest summary**: rent control protects current tenants in the short run at the cost of housing supply and market efficiency in the long run. It's a transfer, not a solution. If the goal is affordable housing, supply expansion is more durable.

### Zoning Reform as the Lever

If restricted supply is the core problem, zoning reform is the core solution.

**Upzoning**: allowing higher density (multi-family, mixed-use, taller buildings) in areas currently zoned single-family or low-density. This is the supply side fix. Research suggests upzoning near transit, job centers, and in high-demand neighborhoods materially increases housing supply and moderates prices — though the effect takes years to materialize as projects get built.

**Exclusionary zoning's history**: single-family zoning was explicitly used in the 20th century to segregate neighborhoods by race (when racial covenants were ruled illegal, zoning achieved the same result). This history is directly relevant to current policy debates — the beneficiaries of restrictive zoning are predominantly white homeowners; the people locked out of high-opportunity neighborhoods are disproportionately Black and Hispanic.

**Recent reforms**: Minneapolis eliminated single-family zoning citywide (2020). Oregon followed. California passed laws allowing duplexes on any single-family lot (SB9) and streamlining ADU (accessory dwelling unit) permits. New Jersey's Mount Laurel doctrine requires municipalities to provide their "fair share" of regional affordable housing — one of the most aggressive state-level interventions in the country, though compliance and enforcement have been uneven.

### Rent vs. Buy: The Actual Math

The rent vs. buy decision is often presented as a lifestyle preference. It's an economic calculation that depends on local market conditions and your specific timeline.

**The price-to-rent ratio** is the key local market indicator:
- Annual rent / Home price (or equivalently: Home price / Annual rent)
- A price-to-rent ratio of 20 means a $400,000 home rents for $20,000/year ($1,667/month)
- **Rule of thumb**: P/R < 15 → buying tends to be advantageous; P/R 15–20 → ambiguous; P/R > 20 → renting often cheaper

NJ varies enormously by area: suburban North Jersey near NYC corridors often runs P/R ratios of 25–35+ in desirable areas, making buying economically costly relative to renting — unless you expect sustained price appreciation to compensate.

**The full cost of ownership (often underestimated):**
- Mortgage interest (especially in years 1–5, where the vast majority of payment is interest)
- Property taxes (NJ has the highest effective property tax rate in the US — median ~2.2%)
- Insurance (~0.5–1% of home value/year)
- Maintenance (the 1% rule: budget 1% of home value annually; 1.5–2% for older homes)
- Transaction costs (~6–10% to buy and sell combined — a huge drag on short holds)
- Opportunity cost of down payment (the down payment invested in index funds compounds)

**The break-even horizon**: given transaction costs, buying typically doesn't make financial sense unless you stay 5–7 years minimum. In high-appreciation markets, this threshold shrinks. In flat markets with high property taxes (much of NJ outside the NYC commuter corridor), it extends.

**When buying wins financially:**
- Long time horizon (7+ years)
- Below-market purchase (motivated seller, distressed property)
- Low-rate mortgage locked in before rate increases
- High-appreciation area where price growth outpaces the total cost of ownership

**When renting wins financially:**
- High P/R ratio (renting capital is cheap relative to owning it)
- Short or uncertain horizon
- High-interest environment where mortgage costs dominate
- Mobile career where geographic flexibility has real value

The emotional value of ownership (stability, customization, roots) is real and doesn't appear in the math. Just don't pretend the math is favorable when it's not.

## Key Considerations

**Property taxes change the rent vs. buy calculation dramatically.** NJ's property tax burden means a $600,000 home may carry $13,000–15,000/year in property taxes alone — nearly as much as renting a comparable property in some markets. This is a fixed ownership cost that doesn't appear in the mortgage payment but is inescapable.

**Location within a metro matters more than the metro itself.** The supply constraints, appreciation rates, school district quality (which is capitalized into home prices), and property tax rates vary enormously within a 20-mile radius in NJ. The metro-level average obscures the variation that matters for any specific decision.

**Housing is illiquid.** You cannot sell half your house when you need cash. The illiquidity premium is real and cuts both ways — it protects you from panic-selling (which is good for equity accumulation) and traps you in situations where the market is down and you need to move.

## Getting Started

1. **Calculate the P/R ratio for neighborhoods you're considering.** Find comparable rentals (Zillow, apartments.com) and comparable for-sale prices. Divide. This tells you how expensive ownership is relative to renting in that specific market right now.
2. **Calculate total cost of ownership** using the components above, not just the mortgage payment. Include property taxes, insurance, and maintenance in your monthly cost comparison.
3. **Set your minimum time horizon before buying.** If you can't commit to 5+ years, renting is almost certainly cheaper once transaction costs are factored in.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating mortgage payment as the total cost of ownership.** Property taxes, insurance, and maintenance add 50–100% to the effective monthly cost in many NJ markets. Buyers who don't model this are in for a shock.

**Assuming home prices always appreciate.** The 2006–2012 period in NJ showed that home prices can fall 20–30% and stay depressed for years. Real home prices (inflation-adjusted) have been roughly flat nationally over very long periods; the 2012–2024 run was not the historical norm.

**Blaming speculators for housing costs.** Investors and short-term rentals are real factors but secondary to supply restriction. Eliminating all investor-owned housing would lower prices modestly in most markets; eliminating restrictive zoning would lower them substantially.

## Resources

- *The Color of Law* — Richard Rothstein. The legal and policy history of how zoning created residential segregation. Essential context for any housing policy discussion.
- *Order Without Design* — Alain Bertaud. Urban economist's framework: cities as labor markets, how zoning distorts urban form and housing supply. Dense but authoritative.
- Upjohn Institute research on minimum wage and local housing — for the empirical work on rent control specifically, look for the Diamond et al. 2019 Stanford study on San Francisco.
- New York Times "Why Is Rent So High?" series (2022–2023) — good journalistic treatment connecting supply, zoning, and political economy.
