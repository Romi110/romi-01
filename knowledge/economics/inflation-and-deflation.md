# Inflation and Deflation
*Why the price level rises and falls — measurement, causes, and what history says about the extremes*

---

## What Is It

Inflation is a sustained, general increase in the price level — meaning money buys less over time. Deflation is the reverse. These aren't about one product getting expensive or cheap; they're about the overall level of prices across the economy.

A 2% annual inflation rate sounds mild. Over 35 years, it cuts the purchasing power of a dollar in half. A 10% inflation rate does that in 7 years. Germany's hyperinflation in 1923 destroyed the value of the Reichsmark entirely — workers were paid twice daily so they could spend the money before it lost value at lunch. Understanding inflation is understanding one of the fundamental forces that determines what your savings are worth.

---

## Why It Matters

Inflation is the silent tax on stored wealth. It hits hardest anyone holding cash or fixed-rate instruments. It benefits debtors (you repay in cheaper dollars). It distorts investment decisions, erodes pensions, and — at high enough levels — destabilizes economies and governments. For a self-reliant person building financial resilience: understanding inflation is central to knowing where to hold savings, how to structure debt, and which assets preserve purchasing power. See [finance/index-fund-investing.md](../finance/index-fund-investing.md) and [finance/investing-beyond-index-funds.md](../finance/investing-beyond-index-funds.md) for how this shapes asset allocation.

## Core Concepts

### Measurement

**Consumer Price Index (CPI)**: the most widely cited measure. The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) tracks the price of a fixed "basket" of goods and services representative of typical consumer spending — food, housing, transportation, medical care, apparel, education. Weight reflects average spending: housing is ~33% of the CPI basket.

- **Headline CPI**: includes all items, including food and energy (which are volatile).
- **Core CPI**: excludes food and energy. Less volatile, better signal of underlying trend. The Fed watches core more than headline.

**Limitations of CPI:**
- **Substitution bias**: if beef gets expensive and people buy more chicken, the fixed basket doesn't capture that substitution. CPI slightly overstates inflation.
- **Quality adjustment**: a 2026 laptop is far more powerful than a 2016 laptop at the same price — is that inflation or improvement? The BLS makes hedonic quality adjustments, which can reduce measured inflation.
- **Housing (OER)**: CPI uses "owners' equivalent rent" (what homeowners would pay to rent their home) rather than actual home prices. This created a bizarre situation in 2021–22: actual home prices surged 20%+, but OER — which lags by design — only gradually reflected that, keeping measured CPI artificially low while real housing costs exploded.

**PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures)**: the Fed's preferred inflation measure, published by the Bureau of Economic Analysis. Key differences from CPI:
- Uses chain-weighted basket (adjusts for substitution in real time)
- Weights medical care higher (more of healthcare is employer/government-paid)
- Broader scope: covers all personal consumption, not just out-of-pocket
- Typically runs 0.3–0.5% lower than CPI

**PPI (Producer Price Index)**: measures price changes at the producer/wholesale level before they reach consumers. PPI leads CPI — input cost increases work their way forward into consumer prices.

**GDP Deflator**: measures price changes for all goods and services in GDP (broader than CPI). Used to convert nominal GDP to real GDP.

### Causes of Inflation

**Demand-pull**: too much spending chasing too few goods. The classic "too much money chasing too few goods." Causes: fiscal stimulus overshoot, monetary easing held too long, rapid credit expansion. The US 2021–22 inflation had a strong demand-pull component — $5T+ in COVID stimulus hit an economy with supply still constrained.

**Cost-push**: rising input costs passed through to consumer prices. Energy price spikes (1973 oil embargo, 2022 Russia/Ukraine gas cutoff), commodity shocks, supply chain disruptions. Cost-push is stagflationary — it raises prices while reducing output, creating the worst of both worlds.

**Built-in (wage-price spiral)**: inflation expectations become self-fulfilling. If workers expect 8% inflation, they demand 8%+ wage increases. Firms pass higher labor costs into prices. Prices rise, confirming the expectation. Repeat. The 1970s spiral is the textbook case. This is why central bank credibility matters — if workers believe the Fed will hold inflation at 2%, they don't demand 8% wage hikes, so 8% inflation never materializes.

**Monetary inflation**: Milton Friedman: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." In the long run, if money supply grows faster than output, prices rise proportionally. **MV = PQ** (quantity theory of money): Money Supply × Velocity = Price Level × Output. If M grows faster than Q (real output), P (price level) must rise. The relationship is real in the long run and unreliable in the short run — velocity (V) is unstable, especially in financial crises when people hoard money.

**Asset inflation vs. consumer inflation**: these can diverge. QE 2009–2021 created enormous bank reserves but little consumer inflation — the money flowed into financial assets (stocks, bonds, real estate) rather than consumer goods. "Inflation" is measured at the consumer level, but asset price inflation is a real redistribution of wealth (owners gain; non-owners are priced out). The political consequences of sustained asset inflation + low CPI are significant.

**Structural inflation** (supply-side): insufficient housing supply creates persistent rent inflation despite low demand growth. Healthcare structural inflation is driven by supply restrictions (doctor licensing, hospital certificate-of-need laws), insurance complexity, and information asymmetry — not primarily by monetary factors.

### Historical Episodes

**Weimar Germany, 1921–1923**

The defining hyperinflation case. Germany lost WWI, owed reparations under the Versailles Treaty in foreign currency, had a devastated economy. When Germany defaulted on reparations in 1922, France occupied the Ruhr (industrial heartland). The German government funded passive resistance by printing money. By November 1923, the exchange rate was 4.2 trillion Reichsmarks per US dollar.

**Mechanism**: not simply "printing money" — the sequence was: reparations demand → foreign currency needed → foreign reserves depleted → mark falls → imported goods inflate → more marks needed to buy foreign currency → accelerating spiral. Each step required more money printing, which accelerated the collapse.

**Social consequences**: wiped out the German middle class's savings entirely. A generation learned that holding financial assets (bonds, savings accounts, cash) was dangerous — fixed claims lost everything while real assets (farmland, factories, gold) survived. This informed German ordoliberalism and the ECB's single mandate: price stability above all else.

**Lesson**: hyperinflation is not a monetary policy mistake — it's a fiscal collapse that monetary authorities are forced to accommodate. You can't have hyperinflation without a government that needs to spend more than it can tax or borrow.

**The 1970s United States: Stagflation**

Inflation built gradually in the late 1960s (LBJ's "guns and butter" — Vietnam War + Great Society spending without tax increases). The 1971 Nixon Shock (ending dollar-gold convertibility under Bretton Woods) removed the external anchor. Then two oil price shocks: 1973 OPEC embargo (price quadrupled) and 1979 Iranian Revolution (second spike).

CPI peaked at 14.8% in March 1980. Unemployment also rose — the combination (stagflation) broke the Keynesian Phillips Curve assumption that inflation and unemployment trade off predictably.

**The Fed's failure**: Arthur Burns repeatedly relented on rate hikes under pressure from Nixon and Ford administrations. Each relenting re-entrenched inflationary expectations. G. William Miller (Burns' successor) was similarly ineffective. By the time Volcker arrived in 1979, expectations were so entrenched that breaking them required extreme measures.

**The Volcker Shock (1979–1983)**: Fed funds rate raised to 20% in June 1980. Two recessions. Unemployment hit 10.8% in 1982. Farmers defaulted on loans; small businesses collapsed; Latin American debt crisis erupted (EM borrowers couldn't service dollar debt). But it worked: by 1983, inflation was back to 3%. The "Great Moderation" — low inflation, moderate growth — followed for 25 years.

**Lesson**: the longer inflation runs entrenched, the more painful the cure. Early action is far less costly than late action.

**2021–2023: Post-COVID Inflation**

CPI peaked at 9.1% in June 2022. Multiple causes stacked:
1. **Demand shock**: $5T+ in COVID stimulus created massive consumer demand
2. **Supply shock**: supply chains had been optimized for efficiency (just-in-time), not resilience. Simultaneous disruption across multiple sectors (semiconductors, shipping, labor markets) reduced supply exactly as demand surged.
3. **Energy shock**: Russia/Ukraine war in February 2022 → European energy market disruption → global commodity prices spike
4. **Housing**: OER lag underreported 2021 housing inflation, then overreported 2023 (catching up to a market that had already peaked)

**The Fed's miscalculation**: called inflation "transitory" through 2021, based on the assumption that supply chain disruptions would resolve quickly. By March 2022, it was clear this was wrong. The Fed began hiking from 0.25%; by July 2023 the rate was 5.25–5.5%. Inflation did fall — to ~3% by end 2023 — without a deep recession. The "soft landing" debate: how much credit does the Fed deserve vs. supply normalization?

**Lesson**: supply chain concentration creates fragility. When supply and demand shocks compound, inflation can be severe and fast. The Fed's communication errors (persistent "transitory" messaging) damaged credibility and required more aggressive tightening to restore it.

**Argentina: Chronic High Inflation**

Argentina has had persistent inflation above 20% for most of the past 30 years, peaking above 100% in 2023. The mechanism: fiscal deficits too large to finance through borrowing → monetization (central bank buys government debt with printed money) → chronic inflation → capital flight → currency depreciation → import inflation → more monetization. Each cycle erodes institutional credibility, making the next episode worse.

Argentines hold dollars as a store of value, have multiple exchange rates, and treat peso savings as temporary. A society where the local currency isn't trusted forces everyone into defensive strategies — this is what monetary instability actually looks like day-to-day.

**Lesson**: inflation is ultimately a fiscal problem. A government with a credible commitment to fiscal balance and an independent central bank avoids chronic inflation. Erosion of those institutions causes cascading cycles.

### Deflation and Deflationary Traps

Deflation (falling prices) sounds appealing — things get cheaper. In practice, persistent deflation is economically devastating.

**The deflationary trap mechanism**: if prices are falling, why buy today what will be cheaper tomorrow? Consumer and business spending defers. Demand falls. Producers cut prices further to sell inventory. Demand falls more. Output and employment fall. Wages fall or workers get laid off. They spend less. Prices fall more. A self-reinforcing spiral.

**Japan's Lost Decade(s)**: the 1990–91 asset bubble collapse triggered deflation and low growth that persisted for 30 years. Japan tried massive fiscal stimulus (accumulated debt to 260% of GDP), zero interest rates, and eventually QE. Nothing broke the deflationary psychology. Why?

- Debt overhang: households and firms prioritized paying down bubble-era debt over new spending
- Demographics: aging, shrinking population → falling demand
- Institutional rigidity: corporations refused to restructure; banks carried zombie loans
- Expectations: once a deflationary mindset is established, extremely hard to dislodge

Japan spent 2013 onward attempting "Abenomics" — massive QE, fiscal stimulus, structural reform. Some success; inflation finally reached 2%+ only after global commodity shocks in 2022. Still has the world's largest debt/GDP ratio.

**Debt deflation** (Irving Fisher, 1933): when nominal debt loads are fixed but prices fall, real debt burdens rise. Borrowers become more indebted in real terms even without borrowing more. This triggers defaults, bank failures, reduced lending, more deflation — a spiral. The Great Depression followed this pattern. Understanding debt deflation explains why falling prices during a debt crisis makes things worse, not better.

**Good vs. bad deflation**: productivity-driven price declines (technology getting cheaper, efficiency gains) are "good deflation" — more output, not less demand. The 1870–1890 deflation in the US coincided with rapid growth. The distinction: good deflation comes from supply growth; bad deflation comes from demand collapse. The former can coexist with prosperity; the latter cannot.

### Inflation Expectations and Anchoring

Modern central banking is largely about managing inflation expectations — because expectations drive actual inflation.

**Anchored expectations**: if households and businesses believe inflation will stay at 2%, they set wages and prices accordingly, and 2% tends to be self-fulfilling. The Fed's 2% target is not based on any fundamental economic theorem — it's a communication device to anchor expectations with a buffer above zero (avoiding deflation risk) and below problematic levels.

**De-anchoring risk**: when inflation surprises to the upside for long enough, expectations shift. Workers demand higher wages "just in case." Firms raise prices preemptively. This is the transition the Fed was managing in 2022: prevent de-anchoring before it became entrenched like the 1970s.

**TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)**: US government bonds that adjust principal with CPI. The spread between TIPS yields and nominal Treasury yields = the market's implied inflation expectation (breakeven inflation rate). Watching the 5-year and 10-year breakevens tells you what the market thinks future inflation will be — more revealing than any individual economist's forecast.

## Key Considerations

**Inflation is distributional.** It's not a uniform tax. Debtors benefit (repay in cheaper dollars). Creditors lose. Owners of real assets (property, equities) are partially hedged. Holders of cash and fixed-income instruments lose purchasing power. Workers whose wages lag inflation see real wage cuts. Understanding who wins and loses from inflation is central to evaluating it politically.

**Core vs. headline disagreement**: when food and energy are spiking, headline inflation can be 9% while core is 6%. Which number matters depends on your situation. A household budget feels headline inflation — you buy food and gas. Financial markets often key on core CPI as the signal for Fed policy.

**Real interest rates**: the rate that matters for economic decisions is the **real interest rate** = nominal rate − expected inflation. In 2021, the fed funds rate was 0.25% and inflation was rising toward 7% — meaning real rates were deeply negative (around −6%). This is extraordinarily stimulative. The 2022–2023 hikes brought nominal rates to 5.5%, and with inflation at 3%, real rates turned positive — genuinely restrictive.

**Hyperinflation vs. high inflation**: these are qualitatively different phenomena. High inflation (10–50% annually) is painful but manageable — the economy adjusts. Hyperinflation (prices doubling monthly or faster) destroys the monetary system itself. The mechanism that tips high inflation into hyperinflation is the loss of confidence in the currency — when people refuse to accept it even for immediate transactions.

## Getting Started

1. **Track monthly CPI releases** — the BLS releases CPI the second or third week of each month. Read the summary: headline, core, and the biggest movers. Builds intuition fast.
2. **Check TIPS breakevens** via FRED — watch the 5-year and 10-year implied inflation expectations over time. This tells you whether the market trusts the Fed's anchor.
3. **Read the Volcker chapter in any good Fed history** — it's the clearest case study in what it takes to break entrenched inflation and why that matters.

## Common Mistakes

**Conflating all price increases with inflation.** A single commodity spiking (avocados due to drought) is a relative price change, not inflation. Inflation requires broad, sustained rises across many categories.

**Assuming deflation is always good.** Cheaper goods sound nice; deflationary spirals are economic catastrophes. The distinction is cause — supply-driven or demand-driven.

**Forgetting real vs. nominal.** A 5% return on savings with 4% inflation is a 1% real return. A 10% mortgage rate with 10% inflation is a 0% real rate. Always deflate nominal figures.

**Treating hyperinflation as just "more inflation."** Hyperinflation is a different phenomenon — a collapse of monetary confidence, not a bad central bank policy. The prerequisites (fiscal chaos, currency destruction) are specific.
