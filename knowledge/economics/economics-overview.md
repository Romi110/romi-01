# Economics Overview
*How economies work — the core frameworks, major schools, and what to actually understand before going deeper*

---

## What Is It

Economics is the study of how people, firms, and governments allocate scarce resources. It asks: who gets what, why, and at what cost? It divides into two domains:

- **Microeconomics** — individual actors: how households make decisions, how firms set prices, how markets allocate goods
- **Macroeconomics** — the aggregate: GDP, inflation, unemployment, money supply, trade, government policy

These are not the same thing studied at different scales. They have different tools, different assumptions, and often different conclusions. Someone who understands microeconomics well can still be lost in macroeconomic policy debates.

Economics sits between a social science and applied mathematics. Its models simplify human behavior to generate predictions — useful for identifying mechanisms, dangerous when mistaken for reality. The best economists know the limits of their models. The worst don't.

---

## Why It Matters

Economic forces shape almost every decision in this wiki — whether to buy vs. rent, how tax policy works, why supply chains broke, what inflation does to savings, why NJ property taxes are what they are, how geopolitical power is really distributed. Understanding economics doesn't make you a forecaster; it makes you harder to mislead.

More directly: for a business owner, investor, and self-reliant person, economic literacy is defensive armor. Inflation erodes savings. Tax structures reward or punish specific behaviors. Monetary policy drives asset prices. You navigate these forces regardless of whether you understand them — better to understand them.

## Core Concepts

### Supply and Demand

The foundation. Price is determined by the intersection of:
- **Supply** — quantity producers will sell at each price
- **Demand** — quantity buyers will purchase at each price

When demand rises faster than supply, price rises. When supply rises faster than demand, price falls. This is not always true in real markets (monopolies, externalities, sticky prices), but it's the starting framework for almost everything else.

**Elasticity:** How sensitive quantity is to price changes. Insulin demand is inelastic (people buy it regardless of price). Luxury travel demand is elastic (small price increase, large demand drop). Businesses exploit this constantly — price discrimination, versioning, dynamic pricing are all elasticity games.

### Opportunity Cost

Every choice forecloses others. Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best alternative foregone. If you spend $100,000 on a business, the opportunity cost isn't just the $100,000 — it's what that $100,000 would have returned invested elsewhere (say, 7%/year in index funds). Ignoring opportunity cost is how people convince themselves of bad investments.

The Stoic frame: you're always trading one thing for another. The question is whether you're making that trade consciously.

### Comparative Advantage

The basis for trade — between individuals, firms, and nations. Even if one party is absolutely better at everything, both parties gain by specializing in what they do relatively best and trading.

Example: Even if a surgeon is faster at typing than their assistant, the surgeon should still operate while the assistant types — because the surgeon's comparative advantage is much larger in surgery. The same logic scales to nations: why the US and China trade despite deep political tension.

This is also why specialization beats being a generalist at the margin — though not at every scale.

### Market Structures

Not all markets work the same way:

- **Perfect competition** — many buyers, many sellers, identical products, free entry. Price = cost. Exists in textbooks and commodity markets (wheat, soybeans). Firms are price-takers.
- **Monopoly** — single seller. Sets price above cost, extracts surplus. Deadweight loss to society.
- **Oligopoly** — few large firms (airlines, telecom). Price-setting power, strategic interdependence. Game theory governs behavior — what one firm does depends on what it expects competitors to do.
- **Monopolistic competition** — many firms, differentiated products. Restaurants, clothing brands, smartphones. Some pricing power from differentiation; competitive pressure from substitutes.

Most real industries are oligopolies or monopolistic competition. "The market will fix it" works best in actual competitive markets, which are rarer than assumed.

### Externalities and Market Failure

Markets fail when prices don't reflect full costs or benefits:

- **Negative externalities** — the cost falls on someone not in the transaction. Pollution is the textbook example. The factory doesn't pay for the downstream environmental damage; society does. Markets under-regulate negative externalities without intervention.
- **Positive externalities** — the benefit accrues to people outside the transaction. Vaccination, education, basic research. Markets under-produce positive externalities because producers can't capture the full benefit.
- **Public goods** — non-excludable (can't prevent use) and non-rivalrous (use by one doesn't reduce availability for others). National defense, basic research, roads. Markets don't produce these at all; government provision or subsidy is required.
- **Information asymmetry** — one party knows more than the other. Insurance markets, used car markets, healthcare. The buyer doesn't know what the seller knows; market outcomes are distorted. George Akerlof's "Market for Lemons" explains this formally.

### GDP and National Accounting

**Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** measures total economic output: the market value of all final goods and services produced in a country in a period.

Components (expenditure approach): **C + I + G + (X - M)**
- C = Consumer spending
- I = Investment (business capital expenditure)
- G = Government spending
- X - M = Net exports (exports minus imports)

GDP is useful for measuring economic size and growth; it's a poor measure of welfare. It counts a car crash (medical bills, repairs) as positive. It doesn't count unpaid care work, environmental degradation, or leisure. It's the best broad measure we have and also systematically misleading when used as a proxy for wellbeing.

**Real vs. nominal GDP:** Nominal GDP is measured in current prices. Real GDP adjusts for inflation, measuring actual output growth. When someone says "the economy grew 2.5%," they mean real GDP grew 2.5%.

### Inflation

**Inflation** is a general, sustained rise in the price level — the purchasing power of money falling over time. Measured by CPI (Consumer Price Index), PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures), and PPI (Producer Price Index).

**Types:**
- **Demand-pull** — too much money chasing too few goods. Stimulus overshoot, supply shocks combined with fiscal expansion. 2021–2022 US inflation was primarily demand-pull post-COVID.
- **Cost-push** — supply-side shocks drive up input costs. Oil price spike in 1973; supply chain disruptions.
- **Built-in (wage-price spiral)** — workers demand higher wages to keep up with inflation; firms raise prices to cover higher wages; repeat. The mechanism the Fed most fears.

**Why the Fed targets 2%:** Zero inflation risks deflation (falling prices → consumers delay purchases → demand collapses → recession). High inflation distorts investment decisions, erodes savings, and functions as a regressive tax (hits lower-income people harder). 2% is the chosen midpoint with a buffer.

### Interest Rates and Monetary Policy

The **Federal Reserve** (and central banks globally) controls the money supply primarily through the **federal funds rate** — the rate at which banks lend to each other overnight. This rate cascades through the economy: it affects mortgage rates, business borrowing costs, bond yields, and asset prices.

**Tight policy (raising rates):** Makes borrowing more expensive. Reduces spending and investment. Slows inflation at the cost of economic growth and employment.

**Loose policy (cutting rates):** Cheapens borrowing. Stimulates spending, investment, asset prices. Risks inflation if done excessively.

**Quantitative Easing (QE):** When rates hit zero, the Fed buys assets (Treasuries, mortgage-backed securities) directly, injecting money into the financial system and lowering long-term rates. Used 2008–2015 and 2020–2022. The mechanism and effects are genuinely debated by serious economists.

### Fiscal Policy

Government spending and taxation. **Expansionary fiscal policy** (spending more, taxing less) stimulates demand. **Contractionary** (spending less, taxing more) slows it.

The key debates:
- **Keynesian:** Government should actively stimulate demand during recessions; private demand is insufficient. Deficit spending is appropriate in downturns.
- **Classical/neoclassical:** Markets self-correct; government intervention creates distortions. Crowding out: government borrowing raises interest rates, displacing private investment.
- **Modern Monetary Theory (MMT):** Governments that issue their own currency cannot "run out of money" — the constraint is inflation, not debt. Controversial, increasingly mainstream in left-leaning policy circles. Not without merit but also not without real limits.

### The Major Schools of Economic Thought

**Classical** (Smith, Ricardo, Mill) — free markets, self-regulating, comparative advantage, labor theory of value. 18th–19th century foundation.

**Neoclassical** — dominant 20th century academic framework. Rational agents maximizing utility, marginal analysis, supply/demand equilibria. The foundation of most introductory economics.

**Keynesian** (Keynes) — markets don't self-correct quickly enough; government must manage aggregate demand. Born from the Great Depression. The intellectual foundation of modern fiscal stimulus.

**Monetarist** (Friedman) — inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon; central banks should control money supply via rules, not discretion. Dominant 1970s–1990s.

**Austrian** (Hayek, Mises) — subjective value, price signals, knowledge problem (no central planner can aggregate dispersed information). Skeptical of government intervention. Strong on business cycle theory; weak on empirical testability.

**Behavioral** (Kahneman, Thaler) — humans are not rational agents. Cognitive biases, loss aversion, anchoring. Integration of psychology into economic models. Increasingly influential.

**Institutional** (Veblen, North) — institutions (laws, culture, norms) shape economic outcomes. Markets are embedded in social structures, not free-floating.

No school is entirely right. Each captures a real mechanism the others underweight.

## Key Considerations

**Economic models are maps, not territory.** The Phillips Curve (inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment) held for decades, then broke. The efficient market hypothesis explains a lot and predicts poorly in specific cases. Use models to identify forces at work, not to make confident predictions.

**Incentives explain more than intentions.** People respond to how they're measured and rewarded, not how they're told to behave. This is the most transferable economic insight across domains — management, policy, medicine, law.

**Correlation vs. causation.** Economics suffers more from this confusion than almost any other field. Two things move together; the policy question is whether one causes the other and in which direction. Natural experiments and randomized controlled trials (rare in economics) are the tools for establishing causation.

**The long run and the short run often point different directions.** A minimum wage increase may reduce employment in the long run but have minimal effect in the short run (firms can't instantly restructure). Low interest rates stimulate growth in the short run but may distort capital allocation over a decade. Always ask: which time horizon?

## Getting Started

1. **Start with microeconomics.** It's more concrete, better empirically grounded, and the foundation for everything else. *The Armchair Economist* (Landsburg) or *Thinking Strategically* (Dixit & Nalebuff) are entry points that don't require math.
2. **Read economic history before economic theory.** Understanding how the Great Depression happened, how Bretton Woods worked, and how the 2008 crisis developed gives you context to evaluate theoretical claims. See [history/economic-history.md](../history/economic-history.md).
3. **Pick one ongoing debate and follow it.** Inflation vs. stagnation, tariffs vs. free trade, MMT vs. orthodox fiscal constraint. Following a live debate teaches more than reading a finished argument.
4. **Read economists you disagree with.** The field has genuine political valence; exposure to both Keynesians and Austrians makes your model more accurate.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing microeconomics and macroeconomics.** What's true for a household budget is not necessarily true for a national economy (the fallacy of composition). "The government should live within its means" sounds right from household logic; it's not obviously right for a currency-issuing sovereign.

**Treating economic models as laws.** Supply and demand is a framework, not gravity. Real markets have frictions, power imbalances, and behavioral deviations that break the clean model.

**Single-cause explanations.** Inflation is never just one thing. Recessions are never caused by a single factor. The economy is a complex adaptive system; monocausal explanations are almost always wrong.

**Confusing wealth with income.** Income is a flow; wealth is a stock. A high earner who spends everything has no wealth. A moderate earner who saves and invests for 30 years has substantial wealth. This distinction matters enormously for policy and for personal finance.

## Resources

- *The Worldly Philosophers* — Robert Heilbroner. History of economic thought from Smith to Keynes. The best starting point for understanding where these ideas came from.
- *Basic Economics* — Thomas Sowell. Clear, non-mathematical micro and macro from a free-market perspective. Excellent on incentives and unintended consequences; read with awareness of his ideological priors.
- *Thinking, Fast and Slow* — Kahneman. Not strictly economics, but behavioral economics is now central to the field. [mind/cognitive-psychology.md](../mind/psychology/cognitive-psychology.md) covers the related concepts.
- *The General Theory* — Keynes. Dense. Read the preface and Chapter 24 first. Understand why it was written (Great Depression) before evaluating it.
