# Labor Economics
*How wages are set, why some work pays more than others, and what automation, unions, and the gig economy actually do to workers*

---

## What Is It

Labor economics studies the market for human work: how wages are determined, what drives productivity, how employment reacts to policy, and how labor market institutions (unions, minimum wage laws, employment contracts) shape outcomes.

It sits at the intersection of microeconomics and policy. The micro foundations — labor supply and demand, marginal productivity — are clean and rigorous. The policy conclusions are genuinely contested because labor markets have features that make simple supply-demand reasoning unreliable: information asymmetry, bargaining power imbalances, contracts, and norms that make wages sticky.

---

## Why It Matters

You are a participant in a labor market. Understanding it means understanding why your time is priced the way it is, how to increase it, what protections and risks come with different work arrangements, and how macro forces (automation, immigration, trade) affect the demand for what you do. For a business owner, it means understanding how to structure compensation to attract, retain, and motivate workers without waste.

## Core Concepts

### Labor Demand: Marginal Product and Derived Demand

Firms hire workers when the additional output a worker produces is worth more than their wage. This is the **marginal product of labor** — the additional output from hiring one more worker, holding everything else constant.

**Labor demand is derived demand**: firms don't want labor for its own sake — they want the output labor produces. If demand for the product falls, labor demand falls. This is why recessions cause unemployment cascades: falling consumer demand reduces product demand, which reduces labor demand.

**Marginal product falls as you hire more** (diminishing returns): the 10th employee adds less than the 5th, assuming fixed capital. This gives the labor demand curve its downward slope — firms hire more workers only at lower wages.

Covered more formally in [economics/microeconomics.md](microeconomics.md) under Factor Markets.

### Labor Supply: The Work-Leisure Tradeoff

Workers supply labor hours in exchange for wages. The decision is a tradeoff between work (income) and leisure (everything else: rest, time with family, personal projects).

**Substitution effect**: when wages rise, work becomes more valuable relative to leisure → work more.

**Income effect**: when wages rise, you can achieve your target income with fewer hours → work less.

For low wages, the substitution effect dominates (higher wage → work more). For high wages, the income effect can dominate (high earner reduces hours when wages increase further). This produces a **backward-bending labor supply curve** — an empirical observation, not a theoretical certainty.

**Reservation wage**: the minimum wage at which a person will accept employment. Below this, the person doesn't enter the market. The reservation wage depends on: available non-work income, childcare costs, commute burden, benefits of staying home. Policy changes that affect these (welfare, childcare subsidies) change reservation wages and labor force participation.

### Wage Determination: Competitive vs. Non-Competitive Markets

In a **perfectly competitive labor market**: workers are paid their marginal product, wages equal until supply meets demand, and neither side has bargaining power. This is the textbook baseline — useful but rarely describing reality.

**Non-competitive cases:**

**Monopsony** — a single (or dominant) employer in the labor market. Amazon in some counties, a hospital in a small city. Monopsonists can pay below marginal product because workers have nowhere else to go. Evidence suggests monopsony is widespread in low-wage labor markets, which changes the analysis of minimum wage policy entirely.

**Employer market power via search frictions**: even with many employers, the cost of job search (time, information, uncertainty) means workers can't instantly switch to the highest-paying employer. This gives all employers some wage-setting power — wages fall below marginal product not because of formal monopsony but because of search friction.

**Bargaining power**: wages reflect the outcome of a negotiation, not just marginal products. Negotiating leverage comes from alternatives (other offers, low unemployment), information (knowing market rates), and credibility (ability to walk away).

### Human Capital: Why Some Work Pays More

Human capital is skill and knowledge that raises a worker's marginal product — and therefore their wage. Investment in human capital includes: formal education, on-the-job training, and deliberate skill development.

**General vs. specific human capital:**
- **General**: skills valuable to many employers (programming, writing, management). Workers bear the cost (tuition, time) because they capture the return through higher wages across firms.
- **Specific**: skills valuable only at the current firm (proprietary systems, firm-specific processes). Firms may share the cost via training programs because the worker can't take the value elsewhere. This is also why firm-specific human capital creates lock-in: high switching costs for both sides.

**The college premium**: the wage gap between college-educated and non-college workers. This premium has grown since the 1980s — not because college is a better credential, but because skill-biased technological change (computers, automation) raised the marginal product of highly skilled work more than low-skilled work. The premium is real but eroding at the margins as credential inflation and rising tuition change the cost-benefit calculation for many degrees.

**Credentialism vs. actual skill**: degrees often function as **signals** (Spence's signaling model) — they tell employers something about underlying ability without directly measuring it. This creates a paradox: if the degree primarily signals ability rather than building it, spending 4 years and $200,000 to get a credential may be wasteful for society even if rational for the individual.

### The Minimum Wage Debate

The standard supply-demand prediction: a minimum wage set above the market wage creates a surplus of workers (unemployment). This is textbook, and for competitive labor markets with elastic demand, it holds.

**The empirical reality is messier:**

David Card and Alan Krueger's famous 1994 study compared fast food employment in New Jersey (raised minimum wage) to Pennsylvania (didn't) — and found no negative employment effect. This launched two decades of research. The current empirical consensus is nuanced:
- **Small minimum wage increases in monopsonistic or low-elasticity markets**: minimal employment effect. Firms absorb via lower profit margins, reduced turnover, or small price increases.
- **Large minimum wage increases relative to prevailing wages** (like a $15 minimum in rural areas where median wage is $14): more likely to produce employment reductions, especially for teenagers and low-skill workers.
- **Effects vary by labor market tightness**: in tight markets with high employer bargaining power (monopsony), minimum wages can raise employment by correcting the market failure.

The key variable is **monopsony power** — how much price-setting power employers have. In genuinely competitive markets, the textbook result applies. In monopsonistic markets, minimum wages can raise both wages and employment.

### Unions: Collective Bargaining Power

Workers individually have weak bargaining power against large employers. **Unions** solve this by aggregating workers' threat — collective refusal to work (strike) is a credible threat that individual refusal is not.

**What unions do:**
- Raise wages for members (the **union wage premium** in the US has historically been 10–20%)
- Compress the wage distribution within firms (narrow the gap between highest and lowest-paid union workers)
- Provide procedural protections (just-cause firing requirements, grievance processes)
- Improve safety — OSHA-mandated improvements often followed union pressure

**The tradeoff:**
- Higher wages for members may reduce employment at the margin (same debate as minimum wage, but within the firm)
- Unions can create rigidities — protecting low-performing workers, resisting productivity-enhancing changes
- Rent-seeking: unions in regulated industries (public sector, utilities) can extract wages above competitive levels because the employer can't exit

US union membership peaked at ~35% of workers in the 1950s; it's ~10% today (mostly public sector). The decline tracks with the rise in earnings inequality — whether unions *caused* the compression or just *correlated* with the post-war economy is genuinely debated.

### Automation and the Future of Work

Automation doesn't destroy jobs — it changes which jobs exist. This has been true since the spinning jenny. The empirical track record: aggregate employment holds up long-term. But the distribution of winners and losers shifts dramatically, and the adjustment period for displaced workers is real suffering, not an abstraction.

**The task-based model** (Acemoglu & Autor): think of jobs as bundles of tasks, not monolithic roles. Automation substitutes for **routine tasks** — both cognitive (bookkeeping, data entry) and physical (assembly line operations). It **complements** non-routine tasks — complex problem-solving, social interaction, physical adaptability (plumbing, surgery).

**Empirical pattern since 1980:** middle-wage routine jobs (manufacturing, clerical) have declined. High-wage non-routine cognitive jobs and low-wage non-routine manual jobs have grown. This is **job polarization** — the hollowing out of the middle.

**AI's current threat profile** (as of 2026): different from prior automation. AI substitutes for *cognitive* non-routine tasks at scale: legal research, coding assistance, writing, radiology. This time, high-skill workers face substitution risk in ways they didn't before. The historical reassurance — "automation creates more jobs than it destroys" — may still hold, but the transition cost falls on workers who trained for jobs now being automated.

### The Gig Economy

Gig work (Uber, Upwork, freelancing platforms) classifies workers as **independent contractors** rather than employees. The legal and economic distinction matters:
- Contractors don't receive employment protections: no minimum wage guarantee, no employer payroll taxes, no workers' compensation, no unemployment insurance
- Contractors can set their own hours — genuine flexibility that many value
- Contractors bear their own equipment and overhead costs

**Who benefits**: high-skill contractors with strong alternatives (programmers, designers, lawyers) gain flexibility and premium pricing. Low-skill contractors with no alternatives (rideshare drivers in areas with no other jobs) bear the risks without the compensating premium.

**The classification debate**: firms benefit enormously from classifying workers as contractors — lower labor costs, reduced liability, avoidance of benefits. This creates incentive to misclassify employees. California's AB5 attempted to restrict this; the political backlash (including from workers who valued flexibility) revealed how contested the tradeoff is.

## Key Considerations

**Wages are sticky downward.** Workers resist nominal wage cuts even when their real wage is falling due to inflation. This is why unemployment spikes in recessions rather than wages just falling to clear the market — firms lay workers off rather than cut wages, because wage cuts destroy morale and productivity.

**Labor markets are local.** National average unemployment obscures enormous geographic variation. Detroit at 12% unemployment and San Francisco at 3% are the same country but effectively different labor markets. Policy needs to address geographic immobility — workers can't easily move to where jobs are.

**Discrimination is expensive but persistent.** Labor market discrimination — paying workers less due to race, gender, or age — is inefficient (you're leaving marginal product on the table), but it persists. The persistence is explained by: information costs, statistical discrimination (using group averages as proxies for individual quality), network effects (who gets referrals), and taste-based discrimination (employers willing to pay to discriminate). The **gender pay gap** at the raw level (~18% in the US) narrows considerably when controlling for occupation, hours, and experience — but the remaining gap is real and partially discrimination, partially occupation sorting driven by caregiving expectations.

## Getting Started

1. **Read *The Race Between Education and Technology*** (Goldin & Katz) — the definitive account of how skill-biased technological change drove wage inequality since 1980. Dense but worth it.
2. **Understand your own marginal product** — what specifically do you produce that a firm would pay for? What alternatives does a firm have for getting that output? The answers tell you your negotiating position.
3. **Track your labor market** — BLS.gov publishes occupational employment and wage data by metro area. Look up your occupation. Is the trend rising or falling? How does local compare to national?

## Common Mistakes

**Thinking the labor market is purely competitive.** Employer power (monopsony, search frictions, information asymmetry) is widespread enough that the competitive model frequently misdirects policy.

**Assuming automation has a fixed net employment effect.** The jobs-created vs. jobs-destroyed calculation depends heavily on how wages and prices adjust, how fast new industries emerge, and which workers are positioned to benefit from new tasks. History is reassuring; the current transition isn't guaranteed to follow history's path.

**Confusing correlation with causation in union research.** Unionized industries may have had higher wages before unionization for other reasons (capital intensity, market power of the industry). Disentangling the union effect from selection effects is genuinely hard.

## Resources

- *The Race Between Education and Technology* — Goldin & Katz. The most rigorous account of skill-biased technological change and the college premium. Start with the introduction and conclusion if the full book is too much.
- *Card & Krueger, "Minimum Wages and Employment" (1994)* — the paper that changed the minimum wage debate. Short, readable, available free. The methodology matters as much as the result.
- *The Work of Nations* — Robert Reich. Dated (1991) but still useful frame: symbolic analysts vs. routine production workers vs. in-person service workers as the basic labor market divide.
- *The Second Machine Age* — Brynjolfsson & McAfee. Optimistic but clear-eyed on automation. Best on the task-based decomposition of labor.
