# Ecology Basics
*How energy flows and matter cycles through living systems — the science behind why farms, forests, and soils work the way they do*

---

## What Is It

Ecology is the study of how organisms interact with each other and their environment. It operates at scales from the local (a single pond, a garden bed) up through regions (a watershed, a biome) to the global (the carbon cycle, climate). The central questions: where does energy come from and where does it go, how do nutrients circulate, and what determines which species live where?

This is not taxonomy (classifying species) or natural history (describing what exists). Ecology is about mechanisms — the flows, cycles, and feedbacks that make living systems stable or unstable.

## Why It Matters

A farm is an ecosystem being managed. So is a garden. So is a soil. The same principles that govern a forest — energy flow, decomposition, nutrient cycling, succession — govern your plot. Farmers who understand ecology don't fight their system; they work with it. Concepts like nitrogen cycling, soil food webs, and succession planning show up constantly in the farming files in this wiki. This file is the underlying science behind all of them.

Beyond farming: climate change, land use decisions, water management, and invasive species problems are all ecological problems. Understanding the mechanism makes the news and the stakes legible.

## Core Concepts

### Producers, Consumers, and Decomposers

All organisms play one of three roles in an ecosystem:

**Producers (autotrophs)**: organisms that make their own food from inorganic inputs. Plants, algae, and cyanobacteria use photosynthesis: CO₂ + water + sunlight → glucose + oxygen. A few organisms use chemosynthesis (in deep-sea vents, using chemical energy instead of sunlight). Producers are the energy entry point for every ecosystem.

**Consumers (heterotrophs)**: organisms that eat other organisms. Primary consumers eat producers (herbivores: deer, caterpillars, cows). Secondary consumers eat primary consumers (a frog eating insects). Tertiary consumers eat secondary consumers (a hawk eating a frog). The feeding levels are called **trophic levels** — first (producers), second (herbivores), third (primary predators), and so on.

**Decomposers**: organisms that break down dead organic matter back into inorganic compounds — fungi, bacteria, detritivores (earthworms, millipedes). This is the most important group for soil fertility and is the least glamorous. Without decomposers, dead organisms and waste would pile up indefinitely and nutrients would stay locked in organic form. Decomposers complete every nutrient cycle.

In a healthy soil, decomposers are the dominant biomass by weight — more bacteria, fungi, and invertebrates than anything visible above ground. See [farming/soil-building.md](../farming/soil-building.md) for how this translates to agricultural practice.

### Energy Flow and Trophic Levels

**The 10% rule**: on average, only about 10% of the energy stored at one trophic level transfers to the next. A field of grass contains 10,000 kcal of stored energy; rabbits eating that grass accumulate roughly 1,000 kcal in their bodies; foxes eating the rabbits accumulate roughly 100 kcal. 90% is lost at each step — primarily as heat (cellular respiration) and unusable tissue.

**Why this matters**:
- It explains why animal products are energy-intensive: you need roughly 10× the plant calories to produce the same caloric value in meat.
- It explains why food chains rarely exceed 4–5 levels — there simply isn't enough energy left to support top predators beyond that.
- It directly applies to farm efficiency: growing vegetables is more land-efficient per calorie than raising animals.

**Gross vs. net production**: gross primary production (GPP) is total photosynthesis. Net primary production (NPP) is GPP minus what the plant uses for its own respiration. NPP is what's actually available to consumers — it's what we're measuring when we talk about a field's productivity. Tropical rainforests have the highest NPP; deserts and tundra the lowest.

### Nutrient Cycles

Unlike energy (which flows through an ecosystem and is ultimately lost as heat), nutrients cycle. The same nitrogen atom that was in a dinosaur may be in your food today.

**The Nitrogen Cycle** (most important for farming):

Nitrogen (N₂) makes up 78% of air but is unavailable to most organisms in its gaseous form. It must be "fixed" — converted to ammonia (NH₃) or nitrate (NO₃⁻).

- **Nitrogen fixation**: specialized bacteria (Rhizobium in legume root nodules, free-living Azotobacter in soil) convert N₂ to ammonia. This is the entry point for biologically available nitrogen. Growing legumes (beans, peas, clover) is how you fix nitrogen without synthetic fertilizer.
- **Nitrification**: soil bacteria (Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter) convert ammonia → nitrite → nitrate. Nitrate is water-soluble and plant-available, but also leaches easily — which is why nitrogen-heavy soils near waterways cause algal blooms.
- **Denitrification**: bacteria in waterlogged, anaerobic soils convert nitrate back to N₂ gas, returning nitrogen to the atmosphere. This is nitrogen lost from the system — one reason waterlogged fields are low-fertility.
- **Decomposition**: when organisms die, decomposers break proteins and nucleic acids down to ammonia, re-entering the nitrification cycle.

**The Carbon Cycle**:

Carbon cycles between the atmosphere (CO₂), living organisms, soil organic matter, and ocean. Photosynthesis removes CO₂ from air; respiration and combustion return it. In a balanced ecosystem, carbon inputs (photosynthesis) roughly equal outputs (respiration, decomposition). Burning fossil fuels adds carbon that was stored underground for millions of years — that's the source of rising atmospheric CO₂.

Soil organic matter (SOM) is a massive carbon store. Degraded agricultural soils have lost 50–70% of their original organic carbon. Rebuilding SOM sequesters carbon and improves fertility simultaneously. This is why regenerative farming advocates claim it as a climate tool — both claims are true at the same time. See [farming/soil-building.md](../farming/soil-building.md).

**The Phosphorus Cycle** (no atmospheric phase):

Phosphorus cycles from rocks (via weathering) → soil → organisms → back to soil via decomposition. Unlike nitrogen and carbon, there's no significant atmospheric reservoir. Phosphorus is often the limiting nutrient in aquatic ecosystems — when fertilizer runoff loads it into lakes and rivers, it triggers explosive algal growth (eutrophication) that depletes oxygen and kills aquatic life.

### Ecological Succession

Succession is the predictable sequence of changes in species composition over time, from bare ground to a stable mature state.

**Primary succession**: starts from bare rock or newly formed land (volcanic island, glacial retreat). Lichens and mosses colonize first, weathering rock into proto-soil. Grasses and small plants follow. Eventually shrubs, then trees if climate allows. This takes centuries to millennia.

**Secondary succession**: starts from disturbed land that still has soil — after fire, clear-cutting, or field abandonment. Much faster: pioneer species (weeds, fast-growing annuals) establish quickly, then perennials outcompete them, then shrubs, then forest. An abandoned New Jersey field returns to forest in 50–100 years without intervention.

**Climax community**: the self-sustaining end state for a given climate and soil. In much of the northeastern US, this is mixed hardwood forest. It's "stable" in that it can perpetuate itself indefinitely without disturbance — but it's not static; individual trees die and are replaced, gaps open and close.

**Application to farming**: a garden left unattended is undergoing succession — weeds (pioneers) colonize first, then woody plants try to establish. Farming is essentially arresting succession at the annual-plant stage. Permaculture embraces succession differently — deliberately accelerating toward a diverse, self-sustaining productive "climax food forest."

### Biomes

A biome is a major ecological region defined by climate (temperature + precipitation) and the vegetation it supports. The same biome type can appear on different continents if the climate matches.

**Key terrestrial biomes**:
- **Tropical rainforest**: high rainfall year-round, high temperature. Highest biodiversity and NPP. Soil is actually nutrient-poor (nutrients locked in living biomass, not soil).
- **Temperate deciduous forest**: four distinct seasons, moderate rainfall. NJ is here — oak, maple, hickory. High NPP with good soil-building from leaf litter.
- **Grassland/Prairie**: too dry for forest, enough rain for grass. Most productive agricultural soils on Earth (Great Plains, Pampas, Eurasian Steppe) formed under grasslands.
- **Desert**: low rainfall, high temperature variation. Low NPP, little soil organic matter.
- **Tundra**: Arctic and alpine. Frozen subsoil (permafrost), very low NPP, short growing season.
- **Boreal forest (Taiga)**: cold climate, conifer-dominated. Large carbon store in permafrost and peat.

**NJ context**: the Pinelands is an anomaly — a globally rare temperate pine barrens, maintained by fire, with extremely acidic, sandy, nutrient-poor soil. The rest of NJ is temperate deciduous forest transitioning to coastal maritime ecosystems. Understanding your biome predicts what grows naturally, what soil improvements are needed, and what pests and diseases are endemic.

### Ecosystem Services

Ecosystems produce value beyond the organisms themselves:

- **Provisioning**: food, water, timber, medicinal plants
- **Regulating**: carbon sequestration, flood control, water filtration, pollination
- **Supporting**: soil formation, nutrient cycling, primary production
- **Cultural**: recreation, aesthetic value, traditional knowledge

These aren't abstract — forest cover upstream reduces flood risk downstream. Wetlands filter nutrients before they reach rivers. Native pollinators provide free crop pollination. When ecosystems are degraded, these services are replaced by expensive human alternatives (water treatment plants, flood walls, shipped-in pollinators) or lost entirely.

## Key Considerations

**Scale matters.** Ecological principles are scale-dependent. What's true for a small garden may not be true for a watershed; what's true for a temperate forest doesn't apply to a tropical one. Always ask: what scale is this principle operating at?

**Stability ≠ equilibrium.** Healthy ecosystems aren't in a fixed state — they're cycling, fluctuating, and recovering from disturbances. "Balance of nature" is a useful shorthand but shouldn't be taken literally. Ecosystems resist disturbance up to a threshold, then shift to a new state. Some of those shifts are irreversible at human timescales.

**Keystone species can have outsized effect.** A keystone species has disproportionate impact on its ecosystem relative to its biomass. Wolves in Yellowstone (by controlling elk grazing patterns) had cascading effects on riverbank vegetation, stream flow, and beaver habitat. Removing or restoring one species can change an entire system. In farm ecosystems: earthworms, mycorrhizal fungi, and specific soil bacteria can be keystone decomposers.

## Getting Started

1. **Watch succession in action.** Find an abandoned field, roadside, or disturbed area and observe what's growing. What are the pioneers? Is woody vegetation establishing yet? You're watching succession.
2. **Test your soil food web.** A jar of healthy soil from a productive garden vs. depleted soil looks identical visually. The biology is invisible. A jar test (add water, watch settlment) distinguishes organic content. Better: get a soil test and interpret the organic matter percentage.
3. **Plant nitrogen-fixers.** Grow legumes in a section of your garden. The nitrogen fixation is happening underground in the root nodules — invisible but real, verifiable by testing that section's soil the following year.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating soil as chemistry, not ecology.** Adding nutrients addresses deficiencies but doesn't build the biological system that makes nutrients continuously available. Healthy soil biology is an ecological system — diverse, self-regulating, slow to build. See [farming/soil-building.md](../farming/soil-building.md).

**Ignoring trophic cascades.** Removing top predators (wolves, large raptors) cascades down trophic levels in ways that aren't obvious. In a garden context: killing all spiders and beneficial insects while targeting one pest species often makes the pest problem worse.

**Underestimating decomposers.** The carbon cycle, the nitrogen cycle, and soil fertility all flow through decomposer communities. Any practice that kills soil fungi and bacteria (heavy tilling, chemical fumigation, leaving soil bare) disrupts these cycles at their base.

## Resources

- *The Ecology of a Changing Planet* — Mark Bush. Clear undergraduate-level intro to ecology. Covers all major concepts without requiring a science background.
- *Entangled Life* — Merlin Sheldrake. Specifically on fungal networks and their role in soil and forest ecology. Concrete and recent — not a textbook but excellent on the decomposer/mycorrhizal side.
- *Braiding Sweetgrass* — Robin Wall Kimmerer. A botanist who writes about plant ecology through the lens of reciprocity and long-term observation. Not a textbook — a perspective on how to think about ecosystems.
