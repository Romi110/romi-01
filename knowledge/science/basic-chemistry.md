# Basic Chemistry
*The rules that govern how matter is built, how it changes, and why — the foundation behind soil pH, fermentation, nutrition, and anything you actually make or grow*

---

## What Is It

Chemistry is the study of matter — what it's made of, how it's structured, and how it transforms. At its core: all physical substances are made of atoms, atoms bond to form molecules, and molecules interact through chemical reactions. Those reactions either release or absorb energy, change structure, and produce new substances.

This isn't abstract. Soil pH is acid-base chemistry. Fermentation is redox chemistry. Cooking is protein and starch chemistry. Your body runs entirely on enzymatic reactions. Chemistry isn't a subject — it's the mechanism underneath everything physical.

## Why It Matters

For Romi's work: fermentation (sourdough, yogurt, kimchi) depends on understanding lactic acid chemistry. Soil building depends on pH, nitrogen cycles, and mineral solubility. Nutrition is literally metabolic chemistry. The more concrete and self-reliant your life becomes, the more chemistry you're actually doing — you just may not have the vocabulary for it. This file gives you that vocabulary so the other files in this wiki aren't opaque.

## Core Concepts

### Atoms and the Periodic Table

Everything is made of atoms. An atom is a nucleus of protons and neutrons surrounded by electrons in shells. The number of protons defines the element — 1 proton is hydrogen, 6 is carbon, 8 is oxygen, 7 is nitrogen. The periodic table organizes elements by this proton count (atomic number) and by electron shell structure.

The outer electrons (valence electrons) determine how an element reacts. Elements with one valence electron (sodium, potassium) react very easily — they want to give that electron away. Elements with a full outer shell (helium, neon) don't react at all. This predictability is the point: the table tells you reactivity before you've seen the element.

**Practical touchpoints**: Nitrogen (N) is essential to plants. Phosphorus (P) and potassium (K) are the other two in the NPK of any fertilizer bag. Carbon (C) is in all organic matter. These elements and their behavior explain all of soil science.

### Chemical Bonds

Atoms combine into molecules via bonds. The type of bond determines a molecule's properties.

**Covalent bonds**: atoms share electrons. This is how most organic molecules are built — carbon is the master of covalent bonding, forming up to 4 bonds per atom, which gives it the structural flexibility to build proteins, fats, carbohydrates, and DNA. Covalent bonds are strong and give molecules stable shapes.

**Ionic bonds**: one atom gives an electron to another, creating ions with opposite charges that attract. Table salt (NaCl) is sodium (Na⁺) and chloride (Cl⁻) held by ionic attraction. Ionic compounds dissolve well in water because water molecules pull the ions apart.

**Hydrogen bonds**: weak attraction between a slightly positive hydrogen atom on one molecule and a slightly negative atom (usually oxygen or nitrogen) on another. Individually weak — collectively powerful. Water's high boiling point, its surface tension, and DNA's double helix all depend on hydrogen bonds. Protein shapes (which determine function) are maintained by hydrogen bonds.

**Why this matters for cooking**: protein denaturation — when heat unfolds proteins — is breaking hydrogen bonds. When egg white sets, proteins refold into new stable structures. See [cooking/food-science.md](../cooking/food-science.md) for this applied directly to cooking.

### Chemical Reactions

A chemical reaction converts reactants into products. Atoms are rearranged; no atoms are created or destroyed. Reactions can be:

- **Synthesis**: A + B → AB (building something larger, e.g., plants building glucose from CO₂ and water)
- **Decomposition**: AB → A + B (breaking something apart, e.g., starch breaking into glucose during digestion)
- **Combustion**: fuel + oxygen → CO₂ + water + heat (burning wood, metabolizing food)
- **Acid-base**: acid + base → salt + water

**Activation energy**: reactions need an energy input to start, called activation energy. Catalysts lower the activation energy, speeding up reactions without being consumed. Enzymes are biological catalysts — they are why your body can run complex chemistry at 98°F instead of requiring extreme temperatures. Soil bacteria, yeast, and every cell in your body run on enzyme-driven chemistry.

**Exothermic vs. endothermic**: some reactions release energy (combustion, cellular respiration), others absorb it (photosynthesis). This matters for soil — decomposition of organic matter is exothermic, which is why a hot compost pile generates heat.

### Solutions and Concentration

A solution is a homogeneous mixture where one substance (solute) is dissolved in another (solvent). Water is the universal solvent for biological systems — its polar structure pulls ionic and polar compounds apart and surrounds them.

**Concentration**: how much solute is dissolved. Usually measured as molarity (moles per liter), but in practical contexts you'll see percentages (5% acetic acid in vinegar), parts per million (water quality), or pH.

**Solubility rules**:
- Ionic compounds (salts, minerals) dissolve well in water
- Nonpolar compounds (fats, oils) don't dissolve in water — they dissolve in other nonpolar solvents
- "Like dissolves like" is the rule

This explains: why fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) need fat in a meal to absorb (they're nonpolar). Why minerals (calcium, magnesium, iron — ionic) are water-soluble and interact with pH in soil. Why an oil-water emulsion separates unless an emulsifier bridges the polarity gap.

### Acids, Bases, and pH

An acid releases hydrogen ions (H⁺) in solution. A base accepts hydrogen ions (or releases hydroxide ions, OH⁻). The pH scale measures hydrogen ion concentration logarithmically from 0 to 14: pH 7 is neutral, below 7 is acidic, above 7 is alkaline.

**Logarithmic**: each unit represents a 10× change. pH 5 is 10× more acidic than pH 6. pH 4 is 100× more acidic than pH 6. This is why small soil pH changes have large effects on nutrient availability.

**Common pH values**:
- Stomach acid: pH 1–2
- Lemon juice: pH 2–3
- Black coffee: pH 5
- Blood: pH 7.35–7.45 (tightly regulated)
- Seawater: pH 8.1
- Baking soda solution: pH 9

**Buffers**: systems that resist pH change when acid or base is added. Blood has a bicarbonate buffer system. Good soil has buffering capacity from organic matter and clay minerals — it resists large pH swings. Poor sandy soil (like much of NJ's Pinelands) has low buffering capacity and goes acidic easily.

**Soil pH and nutrient availability**: most nutrients are available to plants at pH 6–7. Outside that range, minerals chemically bind to soil particles in forms plants can't access. Iron becomes unavailable above pH 7. Aluminum becomes toxic below pH 5.5. Lime (calcium carbonate) raises pH; sulfur lowers it. This is the practical application of acid-base chemistry for farming. See [farming/soil-building.md](../farming/soil-building.md) for the farming side.

For the cooking side — how pH affects browning, color, and leavening — see [cooking/food-science.md](../cooking/food-science.md).

### Oxidation and Reduction (Redox)

Oxidation is the loss of electrons; reduction is the gain of electrons. They always happen together (one atom loses, another gains), which is why they're called redox reactions.

**Intuition**: "oxidation" originally referred to combining with oxygen — iron rusting, food browning, fuel burning. These all involve electrons being pulled away by oxygen. Reduction is the reverse — adding electrons, which often means adding hydrogen or removing oxygen.

**Why it matters**:
- **Cellular respiration**: glucose is oxidized (electrons removed), releasing energy. Oxygen is the final electron acceptor (gets reduced to water). This is literally how your body and every aerobic organism extracts energy from food.
- **Fermentation**: when no oxygen is available, cells oxidize glucose by passing electrons to organic molecules instead. Lactic acid fermentation (yogurt, kimchi) and alcoholic fermentation (bread yeast, beer) are anaerobic redox reactions.
- **Antioxidants**: compounds that donate electrons to neutralize oxidizing molecules (free radicals) that would otherwise damage cells. Vitamin C, Vitamin E, polyphenols — all electron donors.
- **Soil**: decomposition is aerobic oxidation. Compost heaps need oxygen. Anaerobic (oxygen-free) soil produces different chemistry — often toxic gases, reduced iron (which stains soil grey-blue), and slow decomposition.

## Key Considerations

**pH interacts with everything else.** Nutrient availability, reaction rates, enzyme function, flavor development — all are pH-sensitive. Getting pH right in soil, fermentation, or baking is often the prerequisite for everything else working.

**Concentration thresholds matter.** Many compounds are helpful at low concentrations and harmful at high ones. Fluoride in water is a useful example: trace amounts prevent cavities; high concentrations are toxic. This applies to soil minerals, salt in fermentation, acids in recipes. "How much" often matters as much as "what."

**Temperature drives reaction rates.** As a rule, a 10°C (18°F) temperature increase roughly doubles the rate of a chemical reaction. This is why fermentation runs faster in summer, why cold slows bread rising, and why cooking chemistry is fundamentally about managing temperature.

## Getting Started

1. **Soil test your garden plot** — it costs $20 and is an immediate chemistry experiment. The report tells you pH, N/P/K levels, and organic matter %. Interpreting it requires understanding pH and nutrient availability.
2. **Make sourdough or yogurt** — both are lactic acid fermentation. You're dropping pH through microbial acid production, which both preserves the food and changes its texture and flavor.
3. **Read the acids/bases section in food-science.md** — it shows the same chemistry applied immediately to cooking decisions.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating pH as linear.** People hear "add lime to raise pH from 5 to 6" and think "more lime raises it more." Because the scale is logarithmic, the amount of lime needed isn't linear — and over-liming to pH 8 creates its own problems (magnesium and phosphorus become unavailable).

**Ignoring buffers.** Sandy soil and clay soil both test at pH 6.5 but respond very differently to lime — sandy soil has almost no buffering capacity and swings easily; clay buffers strongly and needs much more amendment to change. The number alone isn't the full picture.

**Conflating "natural" with "safe chemistry."** Fermentation, smoke, and curing all involve chemical reactions that produce preservative compounds (acids, antimicrobials). They work because the chemistry is sound, not because they're ancient. Understanding the mechanism is what lets you troubleshoot when a batch fails.
