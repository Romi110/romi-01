# Biology Fundamentals
*The logic of living systems — how cells work, how life reproduces and changes, and the shared mechanisms behind all organisms from bacteria to humans*

---

## What Is It

Biology is the study of living organisms — their structure, function, reproduction, evolution, and interactions. At its foundation, all of biology is unified by a few core ideas: all life is made of cells, life uses DNA to encode and transmit information, evolution by natural selection explains the diversity and fit of organisms to their environments, and energy flows through living systems via the same basic chemistry.

These aren't separate topics — they're interlocking. A bacterium in your soil and a cell in your liver run the same fundamental chemistry, use the same genetic code, and are distantly related by evolution. Once you have the core framework, a huge amount of biology becomes legible without memorizing details.

## Why It Matters

Biology is the foundation beneath the health, farming, and ecology files in this wiki. Understanding cell biology makes nutrition mechanistic instead of rule-based. Understanding evolution explains why antibiotic resistance happens, why your immune system is the way it is, and why organisms in different climates have predictable adaptations. ## Core Concepts

### The Cell — Life's Basic Unit

Every living organism is made of cells. Bacteria are single cells. You are approximately 37 trillion cells. The cell is the smallest unit that can carry out all the functions of life: taking in energy, using it for work, reproducing, and responding to the environment.

**Two fundamental types**:

**Prokaryotes** (bacteria and archaea): no nucleus, no membrane-bound organelles. DNA floats in the cytoplasm. Simple structure, but enormously diverse in metabolism. Prokaryotes can live in boiling hot springs, acidic mine drainage, and the deep ocean floor. They appeared roughly 3.8 billion years ago and ran the biosphere alone for 2 billion years.

**Eukaryotes** (plants, animals, fungi, protists): DNA enclosed in a nucleus. Complex internal compartments called organelles, each with a specific function. All multicellular organisms are eukaryotes. Eukaryotes evolved from an ancient merger: an early cell engulfed a bacterium that became the mitochondrion. This endosymbiotic event is why mitochondria have their own DNA — they were once independent organisms.

**Key organelles in eukaryotic cells**:
- **Nucleus**: stores DNA, controls gene expression, receives signals from the cell's environment
- **Mitochondria**: generate ATP via cellular respiration (the cell's power plants). More active cells (muscle, liver) have more mitochondria.
- **Ribosomes**: assemble proteins from amino acids according to genetic instructions — present in all cells, prokaryotic and eukaryotic
- **Endoplasmic reticulum (ER)**: rough ER (with ribosomes) makes and processes proteins; smooth ER makes fats and processes toxins
- **Golgi apparatus**: packages and ships proteins and lipids to their destinations inside or outside the cell
- **Lysosomes**: contain digestive enzymes; break down damaged organelles and cellular debris — the cell's recycling system
- **Cell wall** (plants, fungi, bacteria): rigid outer layer that gives shape and structural support; absent in animal cells
- **Chloroplasts** (plants, algae): capture light energy and run photosynthesis; also endosymbiotic in origin — once free-living cyanobacteria

### Energy in Cells: Photosynthesis and Cellular Respiration

All life runs on ATP (adenosine triphosphate) — the universal energy currency. Every cellular process that requires energy (muscle contraction, protein synthesis, active transport) is paid for in ATP. Two processes produce the majority of ATP in living systems.

**Photosynthesis** (in plants, algae, cyanobacteria):

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose) + 6O₂

Takes place in chloroplasts. Light is absorbed by chlorophyll (the green pigment), energizes electrons, and that energy is used to convert CO₂ and water into glucose and oxygen. Two stages: light-dependent reactions (capture light, produce ATP and NADPH) and the Calvin cycle (use that ATP to build glucose from CO₂).

The oxygen in Earth's atmosphere was produced by photosynthesis — cyanobacteria started pumping it out ~2.5 billion years ago in the Great Oxidation Event. Every breath you take is a product of photosynthesis.

**Cellular respiration** (in virtually all organisms):

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP

Essentially photosynthesis in reverse. Takes place in mitochondria. Glucose is broken down, releasing energy captured as ATP. Three stages: glycolysis (breaks glucose to pyruvate, in the cytoplasm), the Krebs cycle (extracts electrons from pyruvate, in the mitochondrial matrix), and oxidative phosphorylation (uses those electrons to produce most of the ATP, in the inner mitochondrial membrane).

**Fermentation** is cellular respiration without oxygen — anaerobic. Less efficient (2 ATP per glucose vs. ~36 in aerobic respiration) but works when oxygen is absent. Yeast runs alcoholic fermentation (glucose → ethanol + CO₂). Many bacteria run lactic acid fermentation (glucose → lactic acid). Both are how living organisms extract energy when oxygen isn't available. Applied: your muscles switch to lactic acid fermentation during intense exercise, producing the burning sensation and eventual fatigue.

### DNA, Genes, and Heredity

**DNA structure**: deoxyribonucleic acid is a double helix — two strands of nucleotides wound around each other. Each nucleotide has one of four bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), cytosine (C). A pairs with T; G pairs with C. These base-pair rules mean each strand is a template for the other — the mechanism for copying DNA during cell division.

**The central dogma**: DNA → RNA → Protein. This is the flow of genetic information in all known life:
1. **Transcription**: a section of DNA (a gene) is copied into messenger RNA (mRNA) in the nucleus
2. **Translation**: ribosomes read the mRNA sequence and assemble a chain of amino acids — a protein — using a three-nucleotide code (each codon specifies one amino acid)
3. **Protein function**: proteins do almost everything — enzymes catalyze reactions, structural proteins build tissue, receptors receive signals, antibodies fight pathogens

Every cell in your body contains the same DNA. What makes a liver cell different from a neuron is which genes are expressed — which DNA sections are transcribed into protein. Gene regulation is the difference between cell types, between a healthy cell and a cancerous one, and between an organism at different life stages.

**Chromosomes and cell division**: DNA is packaged into chromosomes. Humans have 46 (23 pairs). During cell division (mitosis), chromosomes are duplicated and divided equally between two daughter cells — genetic copies. During sexual reproduction, specialized division (meiosis) halves the chromosome number; fertilization restores it, mixing genetic information from two parents.

**Mutations**: changes in DNA sequence. Mutations happen through copying errors, radiation, chemical damage. Most mutations are neutral or harmful; occasionally one improves fitness. Mutations are the source of genetic variation, which is the raw material for evolution. The vast majority of mutations don't change protein function — they occur in non-coding DNA, or change one amino acid in a protein without affecting its shape.

### Evolution by Natural Selection

Darwin's mechanism remains the central organizing principle of all biology. Four conditions produce evolution:

1. **Variation**: individuals in a population differ in heritable traits
2. **Heritability**: traits are passed from parents to offspring
3. **Differential reproduction**: some variants survive and reproduce more than others
4. **Struggle for resources**: populations consistently produce more offspring than the environment can support

When these conditions are met, traits that improve survival and reproduction accumulate in the population over generations. This is not progressive improvement toward some goal — it's local, relative adaptation to a specific environment. An organism doesn't need to be fast or intelligent; it needs to produce more surviving offspring than its competitors in its current environment.

**Common misconceptions**:
- Evolution is not "survival of the fittest" in the colloquial sense. "Fittest" means best-adapted to reproduce in that environment — which might mean being the most docile, the best camouflaged, or the most cooperative.
- Organisms don't evolve in response to need. A bacterium doesn't develop antibiotic resistance because it needs to — resistance mutations exist before antibiotic exposure, and the antibiotic selects for the pre-existing variants that happen to survive.
- Evolution doesn't have a direction. There is no endpoint. A bacterium today is not "more evolved" than a bacterium from 1 billion years ago — it's differently adapted.

**Why this matters practically**: antibiotic resistance, pesticide resistance, vaccine-resistant strains — these are all natural selection operating on short generational timescales. Understanding the mechanism tells you why using antibiotics at sub-therapeutic doses is dangerous (it selects for resistance), why rotating pesticides slows resistance, and why eliminating the reservoir of susceptible insects is sometimes the point.

### Classification of Life

Life is organized hierarchically: Domain → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. The domains are: **Bacteria**, **Archaea**, and **Eukarya**. All plants, animals, fungi, and protists are eukaryotes — one domain.

The classification reflects evolutionary relationships (phylogeny). A snake and a human are more closely related (both amniote vertebrates, same phylum) than either is to an earthworm. Fungi are more closely related to animals than to plants — they lost their photosynthetic ancestors and evolved a different strategy (absorptive digestion).

This matters for microbiology and fermentation: bacteria and fungi are entirely different domains of life. Antibiotic drugs exploit differences between bacterial and human cell biology — they work because bacteria are different enough that you can kill them without killing your cells. Antifungal drugs are much harder to design because fungi (eukaryotes, like us) share more cell biology with human cells.

## Key Considerations

**Context determines which level of biology is relevant.** For nutrition, biochemistry matters (what does the metabolic pathway do with this nutrient?). For farming, ecology and microbiology matter (what are the soil organisms doing?). For disease and health decisions, cell biology and evolutionary biology matter (what is the pathogen, and how do drugs/vaccines interact with its biology?). Use the right level for the question.

**Structure follows function.** In biology, form and function are inseparable. Why do mitochondria have elaborate folded inner membranes? Because more membrane surface = more ATP-producing complexes = more capacity. Why do neurons have long axons? Because they need to transmit signals over long distances fast. Why are plant cells rigid? Cell walls allow trees to grow tall without collapsing under gravity. This principle — structure matches function — applies at every scale from protein to organism.

**Evolution is happening now.** It's not ancient history. The gut microbiome you have evolved over millions of years of host-microbe coevolution. The crops you eat were shaped by 10,000 years of artificial selection. SARS-CoV-2 variants are evolution happening in real time. This timescale is in your awareness.

## Getting Started

1. **Get a basic mental model of the cell.** Draw (or find a diagram of) a eukaryotic cell and label the organelles with their functions. The spatial relationships matter — things in the nucleus vs. the mitochondria vs. the membrane are doing different things.
2. **Understand central dogma concretely.** Trace one protein from gene to function: insulin is a good example — a gene on chromosome 11 → mRNA → protein → signaling → cellular glucose uptake. The pathway is documented and concrete.
3. **Watch evolution in a context you care about.** If you ferment food, you're running selection experiments: the bacteria that thrive in your acidic, salty, cold environment are selected for. Changing conditions changes the population composition over batches.

## Common Mistakes

**Thinking "natural" is a meaningful category in biology.** Viruses are natural. Poisons are natural. Evolution produces enormous amounts of suffering and inefficiency. Natural selection doesn't optimize for health or happiness — it optimizes for reproduction. The naturalistic fallacy is especially dangerous in health decisions.

**Assuming complexity = better.** Simple organisms (bacteria) are not inferior to complex ones. They are exquisitely adapted to their niches. E. coli has been replicating successfully for hundreds of millions of years. Evolutionary "success" means surviving to reproduce — not being complex.

**Conflating genetics with determinism.** Most traits — disease risk, behavior, height — are polygenic (many genes) and highly influenced by environment. "It's genetic" doesn't mean "it's fixed." Gene expression is regulated; environment shapes which genes are expressed. This is the preview for the planned [genetics-and-epigenetics.md](genetics-and-epigenetics.md) file.

## Resources

- *The Selfish Gene* — Richard Dawkins. Evolution from the gene's-eye view. Builds precise intuition for natural selection with no math and lots of counterintuitive examples. Read Chapters 1–5 for the essential framework.
- *The Cell: A Very Short Introduction* — Terence Allen & Graham Cowling. Exactly what the title says — short, accurate, accessible. The right starting reference for cell biology.
- *Life: The Science of Biology* — Sadava et al. Standard intro textbook. Don't read it cover to cover — use it as a reference for the specific concepts you want to go deeper on.
