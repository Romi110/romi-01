# How Government Works
*From local to federal — who actually has power, how decisions get made, and how to engage when it matters*

---

## What Is It

Government is the set of institutions that hold a legal monopoly on authority and coercive power in a territory. In the United States, this is structured across three levels (local, state, federal), three branches at each level (legislative, executive, judicial), and a web of agencies, courts, and appointed bodies between them.

The civics textbook version — Congress passes laws, the President signs them, courts interpret them — is accurate but incomplete. What it leaves out: where the real leverage is, how the bureaucracy actually shapes policy, what happens between passage and enforcement, and what ordinary people can actually do. This file covers both.

## Why It Matters

Self-reliance requires understanding the systems you live inside. Property rights, zoning laws, tax codes, regulatory requirements for a small farm, building permits for solar panels — all of these are government functions. The person who understands how local government actually works has far more practical power over their own life than someone who only follows national politics.

For Romi specifically: NJ has its own zoning laws, agricultural exemptions, and net metering rules that will matter for farming and energy goals. Knowing how local government works is a practical self-reliance skill, not an abstract civics lesson.

## Core Concepts

### The Three Levels — and Where Power Actually Sits

**Local government** (municipal, county) is the layer that affects daily life most directly. Zoning determines what you can build and grow on your land. Property taxes fund local schools. Building permits govern home modification. Ordinances regulate noise, animals, parking, and short-term rentals. Local government is also the most accessible — you can actually talk to the people making decisions.

*NJ structure*: NJ has 564 municipalities (cities, boroughs, townships) and 21 counties. Each has its own zoning ordinances, tax rates, and planning boards. What's allowed in one town may be prohibited 5 miles away.

**State government** handles: education standards, driver licensing, criminal law, professional licensing, Medicaid, and any area not explicitly federal or local. States have enormous authority in practice. Many issues that feel national (abortion law, gun restrictions, minimum wage) are primarily state-level.

**Federal government** handles: national defense, currency, immigration, interstate commerce, and anything where uniform rules across states matter. It also sets baseline regulatory floors (EPA standards, OSHA, FDIC) that states can exceed but not fall below.

The common misconception: most people focus almost entirely on federal politics — the President, Congress, Supreme Court. But the laws that govern what you can do with your land, whether your building permit gets approved, and whether local schools are funded well are made by people you could meet in person.

### How Laws Actually Get Made (Federal)

The textbook version: bill introduced → committee review → floor vote → Senate → President signs.

What actually happens:

1. **Interest groups and lobbyists** draft significant portions of major legislation. Industry associations, unions, and advocacy organizations have full-time staff who write bill language and brief Congressional staff who often have no independent expertise.

2. **Congressional staff** do most of the actual work. Representatives and Senators manage hundreds of issues; their staff specialists run the day-to-day. Relationships with staff often matter as much as relationships with the member.

3. **Committee chairs** control what gets a hearing. The majority of bills die in committee — they're never voted on at all. A hostile committee chair can kill almost anything.

4. **Floor votes** are often on bills that have been significantly altered in committee or through amendments. What passes may look little like what was introduced.

5. **Implementation** happens through federal agencies (EPA, FDA, OSHA, FTC, etc.) writing regulations. Congress passes broad authority; agencies fill in the specifics through rulemaking. This is where an enormous amount of practical policy is actually determined — often with less public attention than the original legislation.

6. **Courts** then interpret both the law and the regulations when they're challenged. Major policy swings often happen through court decisions, not new legislation.

### How Local Decisions Actually Get Made

Local decisions — zoning changes, permit approvals, development projects — often move through:

- **Planning boards / zoning boards of adjustment**: hear applications, grant variances, and make recommendations. Meetings are open to the public and public comment is often meaningful here, especially for contested applications.
- **Town councils / boards of commissioners**: vote on ordinances, set budgets, hire administrators. These are elected positions, often won with a few hundred votes in small municipalities.
- **County freeholder/commissioner boards**: county-level equivalents. Handle county roads, county courts, county parks.

Key insight: at the local level, showing up to a meeting and speaking at public comment actually matters. A planning board that hears organized opposition is less likely to approve a variance than one that hears silence. At the federal level, constituent contact has marginal impact; at the local level, it can be decisive.

### The Bureaucracy and Its Power

The permanent bureaucracy — federal agencies, state departments, local administrators — outlasts any administration and holds enormous practical power:

- **Regulatory agencies** write the rules that govern industries, workplaces, and the environment. They operate with delegated congressional authority and are only loosely controlled by the President.
- **Enforcement discretion**: agencies choose what to enforce and how strictly. The law on paper and the law in practice can be quite different depending on the administration and local culture.
- **Rulemaking**: agencies must go through a formal notice-and-comment process to issue regulations. This is a real public participation mechanism, particularly used by advocacy groups and industries. Individual comments rarely matter; coordinated campaigns sometimes do.
- **FOIA (Freedom of Information Act)**: you can request government records at the federal level (and equivalents at state/local). Used by journalists and researchers to surface what agencies are actually doing.

### Courts and Their Role

Three layers: trial courts (facts), appellate courts (law), Supreme Court (constitutional interpretation).

In practice: most meaningful legal change happens in federal circuit courts and state supreme courts, not the US Supreme Court. Circuit courts hear many more cases and their decisions govern large regions without SCOTUS review.

Key mechanisms:
- **Judicial review**: courts can strike down laws and regulations as unconstitutional. Established in *Marbury v. Madison* (1803) and the foundation of judicial power ever since.
- **Standing**: to sue, you generally need to show you've been specifically harmed. This limits who can challenge laws and regulations in court.
- **Class actions**: when many individuals have small but similar claims, they can sue collectively. Often the only mechanism that makes civil rights and consumer protection cases financially viable.

### Political Parties and Their Actual Function

Parties are private organizations that control ballot access in most states. Their practical functions:

- Recruit and fund candidates
- Write party platforms (which candidates don't have to follow)
- Run primaries that determine general election candidates
- Organize legislative caucuses that control committee assignments and floor scheduling

**The primary system** is where most elections are actually decided. In heavily partisan districts (most), winning the primary essentially means winning the seat. Primaries typically have lower turnout, which means more ideologically committed voters have disproportionate influence. Understanding this explains why Congress is more polarized than the general public.

### How to Actually Engage

**Most effective (ranked by impact)**:
1. Local government: attend meetings, speak at public comment, build relationships with council members and planning board members.
2. State legislature: constituent contact (phone calls, letters) to your specific rep and senator on bills in committee.
3. Voting in primaries: higher per-vote impact than general elections due to low turnout.
4. Organizing: joining or forming advocacy coalitions amplifies individual impact significantly.
5. Federal contacts: constituent casework (passport problems, VA benefits) gets results; policy contact is lower impact.

**Least effective**: signing online petitions, posting on social media, contacting representatives outside your district.

## Key Considerations

**Local government is the highest-leverage entry point.** National politics is what most people pay attention to; local government is where most decisions that directly affect your life are made, by people who are far more accessible. If you want to do something about zoning on your property, your town's planning board meeting matters more than a federal election.

**Agencies are not just implementers.** The gap between a law's stated intent and its operational effect is often determined by rulemaking and enforcement decisions made inside agencies with little public visibility. Following policy means tracking agency activity, not just legislation.

**Incumbency is strong but not unbeatable at the local level.** Many local officials run unopposed or win with tiny margins.

**NJ specifics**: Right to Farm Act (protects agricultural operations from nuisance lawsuits); Highlands Act and Pinelands regulations (land use restrictions that vary by zone); net metering law (requires utilities to credit solar generation); 564 municipalities each with independent zoning codes.

## Getting Started

1. **Know your representatives**: Find your US Senator, US Rep, state senator, state assembly members, county commissioner, and town council member. Write them down.
2. **Find your local zoning board**: Look up your town's planning board and zoning board of adjustment meeting schedule. Attend one meeting. Just watch.
3. **Get your town's zoning ordinance**: Available on your municipality's website. The section covering your zone tells you what you're allowed to build, keep, or operate on your property.
4. **Sign up for local alerts**: Most municipalities send email or text notices for meetings and hearings. Subscribe.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing media coverage for importance.** Federal politics generates 90% of news coverage and affects daily life less directly than local government for most people.

**Voting only in general elections.** Primaries are often where the real choice is made. Low-turnout primaries are highly responsive to organized groups.

**Assuming nothing can be changed.** Local variances, ordinance changes, and planning decisions are regularly overturned by organized public opposition. The bar for influence at the local level is actually reachable.

**Conflating the law with enforcement.** Many regulations exist on paper but aren't enforced without complaints. Understanding which rules are actively enforced and which aren't matters practically.

## Resources

- *The Federalist Papers* — Hamilton, Madison, Jay. The original arguments for the constitutional structure and separation of powers. Start with #10 (Madison on factionalism) and #51 (checks and balances). Still the most rigorous explanation of why the structure exists.
- *The Power Broker* — Robert Caro. Biography of Robert Moses. Shows how one unelected official shaped New York for 40 years through bureaucratic power and political leverage. The definitive text on how power actually operates inside government.
- NJ Legislature website (njleg.state.nj.us) — bill tracking, committee assignments, your representatives' contact info.
- Your municipality's website — zoning ordinances, planning board minutes, tax records, meeting schedules.
