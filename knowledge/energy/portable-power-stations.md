# Portable Power Stations
*All-in-one units — battery, inverter, and charge controller in a single box. The lowest-barrier entry to energy independence.*

---

## What Is It

A portable power station (PPS) is a self-contained unit that combines a battery bank, a pure sine wave inverter, a charge controller, and a display into one box. You charge it from a wall outlet, your car, or solar panels. You plug devices directly into its AC outlets, USB ports, and DC outputs.

No wiring. No installation. No permits. No electrical knowledge required.

The best units are genuinely capable — not toys. A 2 kWh portable power station with two 200W solar panels can power a mini-fridge for 24 hours, charge all your devices, run lighting throughout a house, and handle a CPAP machine. That's real emergency capability.

---

## Why It Matters

This is where most people should start with renewable energy. You learn what energy storage actually means in practice, discover which loads matter most in an outage, and get real backup capability — all for $500–1,500 with zero installation complexity.

You also avoid the classic first-timer mistake: designing a permanent solar system without any lived experience of what 1 kWh of storage actually gets you.

---

## Core Concepts

### What the Numbers Mean

**Capacity (Wh or kWh)**: how much energy the battery stores. 1,000 Wh = 1 kWh. To estimate runtime: divide capacity by the wattage of what you're running. A 1,000 Wh unit running a 100W device = 10 hours. Accounting for inverter efficiency (~90%), more like 9 hours.

**Continuous AC output (W)**: how much power it can deliver continuously. A 2,000W unit can run anything up to 2,000W continuously. Important for high-draw devices.

**Peak/surge output (W)**: how much it can handle for a few seconds at startup. Motors (refrigerators, power tools) draw 3–6x their running wattage at startup. A fridge that runs at 150W might surge to 600W at startup. Check that the surge rating exceeds the surge draw of your largest load.

**Solar input (W)**: how fast it charges from panels. A unit with 600W solar input + 600W of panels = full charge in 3–4 hours on a clear day. More solar input means faster charging.

**AC charging time**: how fast it charges from a wall outlet. Most units charge in 1–2 hours at full speed.

## What Portable Power Stations Can and Cannot Handle

### Can Handle (at appropriate capacity)
- Phone and laptop charging — always
- LED lighting — minimal draw, runs for days
- CPAP machine — 30–60W; a 1 kWh unit handles 10–16 nights
- Mini-fridge (compact) — 40–80W running; 1 kWh unit handles 12–24 hours
- Full-size refrigerator — 150–200W running; 2 kWh unit handles ~10 hours
- Power tools (drill, circular saw, jigsaw) — check watt rating; most are fine on a 1,800W+ unit
- Window AC unit (small) — 500–1,200W; 2+ kWh unit handles several hours
- Electric blanket — 50–150W; runs all night on 1 kWh
- Coffee maker — 800–1,200W; fine for brewing, not continuous

### Cannot Handle
- Central AC (compressor is 2,000–5,000W running, 6,000–15,000W surge)
- Electric dryer (5,000–6,000W)
- Electric water heater (4,000–5,500W)
- Electric stove/oven (2,000–5,000W)
- EV charging (Level 2: 7,200W)

These require a whole-home solar + battery system or generator.

## Pairing with Solar Panels

A portable power station without solar is a backup battery — it depletes and must be recharged from the wall. With solar panels, it becomes a renewable system.

**EcoFlow's own panels** pair cleanly (single connector) but are expensive per watt. **Third-party panels** work fine — most PPS units accept any panel via MC4 connectors; check max input voltage (Voc) and wattage of the unit before buying panels.

**Sizing solar input**:
- Match your total panel wattage to the unit's max solar input (don't exceed it)
- Size panels to recharge the battery in a reasonable time on a typical day
- Rule of thumb: panel wattage ≈ battery capacity in Wh ÷ 3 (charges in ~4–5 peak sun hours)
- Example: 1,000 Wh unit → 300–400W of panels → full charge in a day of good sun

**For NJ emergency backup**: a 2 kWh unit with 400–500W of panels will recharge in a full sunny day. In winter (2.5 peak sun hours), plan for partial recharge; supplement with wall charging when available.

## Connecting to Your Home (Without a Permanent Install)

**Method 1: Extension cord** — run a heavy-duty extension cord from the PPS to individual appliances. Simple, completely legal, zero installation.

**Method 2: Transfer switch / interlock kit** — a licensed electrician installs a manual transfer switch or interlock kit on your main panel (cost: $200–500 labor). You then plug the PPS into a dedicated inlet outside. Flip the interlock, and the PPS powers selected circuits. Legal, safe, works for refrigerator + lights + outlets.

**Method 3: EcoFlow Smart Home Panel** — EcoFlow's own product ($700+) connects the Delta Pro to up to 10 home circuits. Automatic switching during outages. Permits likely required depending on jurisdiction.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying too small** — a 500 Wh unit feels limiting immediately; 1–2 kWh is the useful minimum for emergency home use

**Not accounting for surge draw** — a refrigerator that surges 600W at startup will trip a 600W continuous inverter; check surge specs vs running watt specs of your devices

**Expecting it to run everything** — portable power stations don't replace a generator for whole-home power; they replace a generator for essential loads

**Letting it sit at full charge** — lithium batteries stored at 100% charge degrade faster; store at 50–80% charge if not using for extended periods

**Ignoring warranty and customer service** — EcoFlow and Bluetti both have US-based support; some no-name brands don't; matters when a unit fails in year 2

## When to Upgrade to a Permanent System

A portable power station is the right tool until:
- You want to offset your utility bill (requires permanent grid-tied installation)
- You want to power loads a PPS can't (central AC, dryer, water heater)
- You want battery capacity above 5–10 kWh without spending $3,000+ on expandable units
- You've confirmed through using a PPS that solar is worth the permanent investment

The PPS experience tells you exactly what you use, what matters during an outage, and what a permanent system needs to handle. It is not wasted money — it's the research phase.
