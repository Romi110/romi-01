# Economic History
*How economies actually developed — mercantilism to capitalism, the industrial revolution, Bretton Woods, the petrodollar, and the financialized world we inherited*

---

## What Is It

Economic history is the study of how humans have organized the production, distribution, and exchange of resources across time. It's not economic theory — it's the actual sequence of events, decisions, and accidents that produced the system we live in now. The models in [history/history-mental-models.md](history-mental-models.md) describe cycles; this file grounds those cycles in what specifically happened.

Understanding this history answers questions like: why is the US dollar the world's reserve currency? Why did manufacturing leave America? What was the gold standard actually and why did it end? What is a petrodollar? The answers matter because the financial structures you navigate today — inflation, debt, currency risk, global trade — are the direct product of decisions made in 1944, 1971, and 1980.

## Why It Matters

The economy isn't a natural phenomenon — it's a set of rules, agreements, and institutions that were constructed by specific people at specific times, often under duress. Those rules can change. They have changed dramatically within living memory. Understanding the sequence makes the current system legible instead of mysterious — and makes the failure modes identifiable rather than surprising.

For Romi: self-reliance, property ownership, farming, and energy independence are all economic decisions. The context for those decisions — inflation, dollar strength, debt cycles, the relationship between productive and financial capital — only makes sense against this history. Also: the tax system you optimize (Roth IRA, 401k, HSA) was built in response to historical crises and policy choices, not discovered from first principles.

## Core Concepts

### Pre-Capitalism: Mercantilism and the Colonial System (1500–1800)

Before capitalism, the dominant economic doctrine was **mercantilism**: the idea that national wealth is measured in gold and silver, and that trade is zero-sum — one nation's gain is another's loss. The goal was to export more than you import, accumulate bullion, and use colonies as captive markets and raw material suppliers.

This produced the Atlantic slave trade, plantation agriculture, and the forced destruction of local industry in colonized territories to create markets for manufactured goods (famously in India under British rule — India went from a major textile exporter to a massive importer of British cloth).

**What mercantilism got wrong**: David Hume's price-specie-flow mechanism (1752) showed that accumulating gold just caused inflation, which made exports more expensive, which eroded the trade advantage. Adam Smith's *Wealth of Nations* (1776) made the case that specialization and voluntary trade are positive-sum, not zero-sum. This shifted elite opinion but colonial practice continued well into the 20th century.

**The legacy**: the current global economic hierarchy — which countries are rich, which are poor, which make things, which extract raw materials — is largely the product of this period. The "developing world" is mostly the product of deliberate underdevelopment.

### The Industrial Revolution (1760–1850, UK; then globally)

The first industrial revolution was not primarily a technological event — it was an energy event. Shifting from human and animal muscle (and wind and water) to steam power broke the fundamental constraint on economic output: the biological limit of what living things can do.

**Why Britain first**: convergence of factors — coal deposits near navigable water (cheap transport), a legal system that protected property and contract (making investment safe), an empirical scientific culture, an agricultural revolution that freed labor from subsistence farming, and accumulated colonial capital. No single factor; all of them together.

**What changed**:
- Production moved from craft (skilled artisans, small scale) to factory (semi-skilled workers, large scale, division of labor)
- Population urbanized rapidly — London grew from ~1M (1800) to ~6.5M (1900)
- Real wages fell for workers initially despite massive productivity gains, then rose as competition for labor intensified — this pattern (gains accrue to capital first, labor later) has repeated in every major technology wave

**The second industrial revolution (1870–1914)**: electrification, steel, chemicals, internal combustion. This wave saw the US surpass Britain in industrial output by 1890. Germany became Europe's industrial power. The pattern of US economic dominance in the 20th century was set here.

### The Gold Standard Era (1870–1914, and briefly 1919–1931)

The gold standard: currencies had fixed exchange rates against gold, and gold moved between countries to settle trade imbalances. If a country ran a trade deficit, gold flowed out, its money supply contracted, prices fell, exports became cheaper, and the deficit corrected. Automatic adjustment.

**What it got right**: price stability over long periods, no inflation, clear anchor for monetary policy.

**What it got wrong**: deflation during downturns was brutal. When credit contracted (panics of 1873, 1893, 1907), the gold constraint prevented governments from expanding money supply to stabilize. Depressions ran their full course without intervention. Labor suffered catastrophically.

**The interwar experiment (1919–1931)**: after WWI, countries returned to gold at pre-war parities, which was deflationary (the money supply didn't match the expanded post-war economy). Britain especially suffered. When the Great Depression hit in 1929, countries stuck on gold were forced into austerity that deepened the slump. Britain abandoned gold in 1931; the US in 1933. Countries that left gold early recovered faster — the data on this is unambiguous.

### Bretton Woods (1944): Building the Post-War System

In July 1944, with WWII not yet over but the outcome clear, 44 Allied nations met at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire to design the post-war international monetary system.

**The deal**: all currencies pegged to the US dollar at fixed rates; the dollar pegged to gold at $35/ounce. Only the US would maintain the gold peg. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and World Bank were created to manage the system.

**Why the dollar**: the US held ~75% of the world's monetary gold in 1944. Its industrial economy was intact (unlike Europe, Japan, UK). It was the largest creditor nation. The dollar was the obvious anchor.

**What the system did**: enabled post-war reconstruction, stable international trade, and the longest sustained economic expansion in US history (1945–1971). The Marshall Plan flowed dollars into Europe; those dollars were used to buy US goods, recycling back. The system worked as long as the US ran trade surpluses and maintained gold convertibility.

**The Triffin Dilemma**: Robert Triffin identified the internal contradiction in 1960. For the world to have enough dollars to conduct global trade, the US had to run trade deficits (exporting dollars). But running persistent deficits would erode confidence in dollar/gold convertibility. The system was self-undermining. This played out exactly as Triffin predicted.

### The Nixon Shock (1971): The End of Bretton Woods

By the late 1960s, the US had spent heavily on Vietnam and the Great Society programs. Inflation rose. More dollars were circulating than the US had gold to back at $35/ounce. France, suspecting this, began systematically exchanging dollars for gold. Britain tried to do the same for $3 billion in 1971.

On August 15, 1971, Nixon announced the US would no longer convert dollars to gold. The gold window was closed. Exchange rates floated. Bretton Woods was effectively over.

**What this created**: a pure fiat currency system. The dollar is backed by nothing except the credibility of the US government and the fact that global trade — especially oil — is priced in dollars. Every currency in the world is now, at bottom, backed by faith in its issuing government.

**The consequence**: freed from the gold constraint, central banks can expand money supply in response to downturns, which enables smoother downturns but also enables inflation. The inflation of the 1970s was the first major test of this new system.

### The Petrodollar System (1974–present)

After Nixon closed the gold window, the dollar needed a new anchor. The arrangement emerged through 1973–1974 negotiations between the Nixon administration and Saudi Arabia:

**The deal**: Saudi Arabia (and OPEC) would price oil exclusively in US dollars. In exchange, the US would provide military protection for Saudi Arabia and support for the House of Saud's rule. Saudi dollar surpluses would be recycled into US Treasury bonds.

**The effect**: global demand for dollars became structural. Every country that imports oil needs dollars to buy it. This creates permanent demand for the dollar regardless of US trade deficits — the world needs dollars to function. This is the "exorbitant privilege" French finance minister Valéry Giscard d'Estaing complained about: the US can run persistent deficits, fund itself cheaply, and export inflation, because dollar demand is captive.

**The vulnerability**: BRICS nations and others are actively trying to reduce dollar dependence in oil trade. Whether this erodes petrodollar dominance is the central question in international monetary economics right now. History suggests reserve currency transitions take decades and happen when the dominant power's relative economic and military position declines significantly.

### The Volcker Shock and Financialization (1980–2008)

The 1970s were stagflation — high inflation combined with slow growth, which the Keynesian models of the time said shouldn't be possible. The Fed, under Carter appointee Paul Volcker, crushed inflation by raising the federal funds rate to 20% in 1981. This caused a severe recession (unemployment peaked at 10.8%), but it broke inflation expectations and restored dollar credibility.

**What followed**: the Reagan era brought deregulation of financial markets, reduction of top marginal tax rates (70% → 28%), and weakening of labor unions. The share of GDP going to financial services rose from ~15% in 1970 to ~25%+ by 2007. Manufacturing's share fell. This is **financialization**: an economy oriented around financial transactions and asset appreciation rather than production.

**Consequences of financialization**:
- Wages for non-elite workers stagnated in real terms from ~1980 onward while productivity continued growing (the gains went to capital)
- Asset prices (stocks, real estate) rose consistently — those who owned assets got richer; those who didn't fell behind
- Corporate short-termism: CEO pay tied to stock prices created incentives to manage quarterly earnings over long-term investment
- The FIRE economy (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate) became dominant in many US cities

### The 2008 Crisis and Its Aftermath

The 2008 financial crisis was not a random event — it was the predictable result of a 30-year debt expansion in the US housing and financial markets, combined with regulatory failures that allowed excessive risk to accumulate invisibly.

**The mechanics**: US housing prices had risen continuously since ~1990, backed by expanding mortgage credit, including subprime loans made to borrowers who couldn't sustain them. These mortgages were packaged into mortgage-backed securities (MBS) and collateralized debt obligations (CDOs), rated AAA by conflicted rating agencies, and sold globally. When housing prices fell (~2006), the underlying loans defaulted. The securities' values collapsed. Banks holding them were insolvent. Credit froze.

**The response**: the Fed cut rates to zero and initiated quantitative easing (QE) — buying bonds with newly created money to inject liquidity. The government bailed out major financial institutions. The recovery was slow and uneven: asset prices recovered quickly (benefiting owners); employment and wages recovered slowly (affecting workers).

**The long-tail consequence**: rates near zero for 2009–2022 drove investors into riskier assets (stocks, real estate, private equity), inflating asset prices further. Anyone who owned assets in 2009 and held through 2022 saw enormous gains. Anyone who didn't own assets saw their cost of living rise without commensurate wage increases. Wealth inequality reached levels not seen since the 1920s.

### Post-2020: The Pandemic, Inflation, and the New Regime

COVID-19 triggered the fastest economic contraction in US history (Q2 2020 GDP fell 31% annualized), followed by massive fiscal stimulus (~$5-6T over 2020–2021) and monetary expansion. The combination — demand stimulus hitting supply-constrained goods markets — produced the highest inflation in 40 years (CPI peaked at ~9% in mid-2022).

The Fed raised rates from near zero to 5.25-5.5% (2022–2024) in the fastest hiking cycle since Volcker. This caused significant pain in rate-sensitive sectors (housing, venture capital, crypto) but achieved a "soft landing" — inflation fell without a severe recession.

**Where we are**: US federal debt-to-GDP ~125% (historically high). Interest payments on debt now exceed defense spending. Real rates are positive for the first time in 15 years. AI is the next potential GPT (general purpose technology), with capital concentrated in a small number of infrastructure providers.

## Key Considerations

**The system is constructed, not natural.** Every feature of the current economic system — fiat currency, central banking, fractional reserve banking, limited liability corporations, capital gains tax treatment — was created by specific people responding to specific crises. These features can change.

**Timing matters enormously in long-cycle economic history.** Being born in 1980 vs. 1950 vs. 2000 in America produces radically different economic outcomes even with identical behavior, because asset prices, wage growth, and policy environments differ so dramatically across decades.

**Debt is not inherently bad — but the rate of accumulation matters.** Every major economic era has involved credit expansion. The question is always: is the debt financing productive investment (railroads, factories, education) or consumption and asset speculation? The former compounds; the latter creates bubbles.

**Reserve currency transitions are real but slow.** The British pound's decline from dominance to secondary status took roughly 50 years (1920s–1970s). Dollar dominance may be declining, but "the dollar is dying" has been predicted every decade since 1971 without occurring. Don't mistake direction for speed.

## Getting Started

1. **Read [history/history-mental-models.md](history-mental-models.md) first** if you haven't — this file grounds those abstract cycles in specific events.
2. **Pick one era and understand it cold**: Bretton Woods and its end is probably the highest-yield, most currently relevant event. Understand the 1944 deal, the Triffin dilemma, and why 1971 happened.
3. **Read one primary source**: Dalio's *Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises* (free PDF) applies his cycle model to the Great Depression, 2008, and the Weimar inflation with historical data. More useful than any textbook.
4. **Connect it to your financial decisions**: how does the current debt/inflation environment affect whether to hold bonds, what real estate does in an inflationary vs. deflationary scenario, and what dollar weakness would mean for VTI vs. VXUS weighting?

## Common Mistakes

**Treating any single theory as the complete explanation.** Marxist analysis, Austrian economics, and mainstream Keynesian economics each capture real dynamics and each has significant blind spots. The actual history fits none of them cleanly.

**Anachronism** — judging historical economic decisions by today's knowledge. The gold standard looked rational in 1870 given what was known. The failures of Bretton Woods were understood in theory (Triffin) but not in practice until they materialized.

**Assuming the current system is permanent.** The dollar's reserve status, the dollar-denominated oil trade, and the US-led international financial institutions are all products of the specific post-WWII power distribution. That distribution is shifting.

**Conflating long-run cycles with investment calls.** Knowing that the US debt level is historically high doesn't tell you when or how it resolves. Structural forces take decades to play out. Don't make 20-year bets based on a 10-year timeline.

## Resources

- *A Monetary History of the United States* — Friedman and Schwartz. The definitive account of US monetary policy 1867–1960, including the Fed's role in deepening the Depression. Dense, but the Depression chapters are accessible.
- *The Ascent of Money* — Niall Ferguson. Readable history of financial innovation from Mesopotamian credit to modern derivatives. Better on historical narrative than on analytical depth.
- *Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises* — Ray Dalio. Free from Bridgewater. Applies the debt cycle framework with historical case studies. The most practically useful resource in this list.
- *Lords of Finance* — Liaquat Ahamed. The four central bank governors of the 1920s–30s and how their decisions produced the Great Depression. Best narrative account of how monetary policy mistakes compound.
