# Human History: A Global Timeline
*Every major civilization on the planet, from the first humans to the present day*

---

## What Is It

Human history is the ~300,000-year story of Homo sapiens: how we spread across every continent, built cities, empires, religions, and economies, collapsed and rebuilt, and arrived at the world we now live in. This file covers that story chronologically — era by era, civilization by civilization — at enough depth to understand who they were, what they built, and why they fell.

Most people know fragments: Rome, World War II, the American Revolution. This file gives you the full map so those fragments have context. When you understand the whole arc, you stop being surprised by things like imperial decline, debt crises, or civilizational collapse — you've seen the pattern dozens of times before.

## Why It Matters

History is the field where Stoic principles get tested across thousands of years. Every empire that didn't understand impermanence was destroyed by it. Every civilization that confused its present dominance with permanence was eventually humbled. History teaches the same things Stoicism teaches — it just uses blood and centuries instead of philosophy.

For someone building self-reliance: most of what you need was already solved by people with far fewer resources. For understanding the present: the US-China rivalry, dollar decline, and geopolitical realignments all have direct historical precedents. You cannot understand what's happening now without the map of what happened before.

## Core Concepts

### Era 1 — Prehistory (300,000 BCE – 3,500 BCE)

**Origins in Africa**

Homo sapiens emerged in Africa roughly 300,000 years ago, distinguished by a larger brain, language capability, and complex social behavior. For the first 250,000 years, all humans lived as hunter-gatherers: small nomadic bands of 20–150 people, following game and seasonal plant food. No permanent settlements, no writing, no formal government.

The critical fact about this period: human biology and psychology were shaped here. The pattern-recognition, status-seeking, tribalism, loss aversion, and short-term thinking that drive human behavior today are adaptations for a world that ended 10,000 years ago.

**Out of Africa (70,000 – 12,000 BCE)**

Around 70,000 BCE a small population — perhaps as few as 10,000 individuals — migrated out of Africa. Their descendants populated the entire planet. By 40,000 BCE they reached Australia; by 30,000 BCE they were in Europe; by 15,000 BCE they had crossed the Bering land bridge into North America and reached South America shortly after.

This is the origin of every civilization that follows. Every human alive today traces ancestry to this migration.

**The Agricultural Revolution (10,000 – 5,000 BCE)**

The single most consequential event in human history. Around 10,000 BCE, humans in the Fertile Crescent (modern Iraq/Syria/Turkey) began deliberately planting crops — emmer wheat, einkorn, barley — and herding animals. This happened independently and nearly simultaneously in several locations:
- Fertile Crescent (~9500 BCE): wheat, barley, goats, sheep
- China (~7500 BCE): rice, millet, pigs
- New Guinea (~7000 BCE): taro, yam
- Sub-Saharan Africa (~5000 BCE): sorghum, millet
- Mexico (~6000 BCE): maize, squash, beans
- Andes (~5000 BCE): potato, quinoa, llama

The tradeoff was not obvious: early farmers were shorter, sicker, and worked harder than hunter-gatherers. But agriculture enabled population density that hunter-gathering could not. More people on less land meant cities, specialization, armies, and eventually empires. Agriculture made everything else possible — including war, plague, and mass slavery.

**The Rise of Villages and Proto-Cities (5,000 – 3,500 BCE)**

With agriculture came permanent settlement. Çatalhöyük in Anatolia (~7500 BCE) housed thousands of people. By 4000 BCE the Ubaid culture in Mesopotamia had proto-cities with temples and administrative centers. The technology stack emerged: pottery, the wheel, bronze metallurgy, and eventually writing — developed to track grain, not for poetry.

### Era 2 — The First Civilizations (3,500 BCE – 500 BCE)

The pattern repeats in every first civilization: agriculture surplus → population density → administrative need → writing → hierarchy → state religion → military → empire.

**Mesopotamia (3500 – 539 BCE)**

The first civilization on record. Sumer, in southern Iraq, built the first cities (Uruk, Ur, Eridu) around 3500 BCE. They invented the first writing system — cuneiform, on clay tablets — to track grain and trade. Sumerian city-states were each ruled by a king who was also a priest; the temple was the economic center.

Key sequence:
- **Sumer (3500–2300 BCE)**: City-states, Epic of Gilgamesh (oldest surviving literature), Code of Ur-Nammu (first written law code)
- **Akkadian Empire (2334–2154 BCE)**: Sargon of Akkad conquered the Sumerian city-states, built the first multi-ethnic empire. First empire in history.
- **Ur III / Neo-Sumerian revival (2112–2004 BCE)**: Sumerian culture reasserted; sophisticated bureaucracy
- **Babylonian Empire (1894–1595 BCE)**: Hammurabi (~1754 BCE) wrote the Code of Hammurabi — 282 laws covering commerce, property, family, crime. The first comprehensive legal system.
- **Assyrian Empire (900–612 BCE)**: Military machine; the first state to use iron weapons systematically. Known for brutal conquest tactics and also for sophisticated library-keeping (Ashurbanipal's library at Nineveh held 30,000 clay tablets).
- **Neo-Babylonian Empire (626–539 BCE)**: Nebuchadnezzar II built the Hanging Gardens (possibly legendary) and destroyed Jerusalem in 586 BCE, exiling the Jews. Fell to Persia in 539 BCE.

Mesopotamia's recurring pattern: city-states consolidate under a strong ruler, build an empire, overextend, collapse, fragment, then repeat.

**Ancient Egypt (3100 – 30 BCE)**

Egypt's geography — the Nile cutting through desert — created a civilization uniquely protected and uniquely centralized. The Nile flooded annually and predictably, depositing rich silt. No flood management required, just harvest timing. This reliability produced a stable, conservative culture that lasted longer than almost any other.

Key periods:
- **Early Dynastic / Old Kingdom (3100–2181 BCE)**: Unification under Narmer; pyramids built (~2560 BCE for Khufu); pharaoh as god-king
- **Middle Kingdom (2055–1650 BCE)**: Expansion into Nubia; trade with Levant; literature flourishes
- **New Kingdom (1550–1070 BCE)**: Egypt at its greatest extent. Thutmose III fought 17 campaigns; Ramesses II fought the Hittites at Kadesh (~1274 BCE) — the first recorded peace treaty. Akhenaten's monotheistic revolution (briefly replacing all gods with Aten) — possibly influential on later Hebrew monotheism.
- **Late Period (664–332 BCE)**: Increasingly dominated by Assyrians, then Persians
- **Ptolemaic Egypt (332–30 BCE)**: Alexander the Great conquered Egypt; his general Ptolemy established a Greek dynasty. Alexandria became the intellectual capital of the Mediterranean (Library of Alexandria). Cleopatra VII was the last Ptolemaic ruler; Egypt fell to Rome in 30 BCE.

Egypt's survival secret: geographic isolation, annual agricultural renewal, and a state religion that made the pharaoh divinely necessary. When that belief collapsed, so did the civilization's coherence.

**Indus Valley Civilization (2600 – 1900 BCE)**

One of the most sophisticated early civilizations, almost entirely forgotten until the 1920s when archaeologists found Harappa and Mohenjo-daro in modern Pakistan. At its peak (~2500 BCE), these cities housed 50,000+ people with planned street grids, covered sewage systems, standardized brick sizes, and public baths — infrastructure more advanced than contemporary Mesopotamia and Egypt.

What makes it mysterious: no deciphered writing, no clearly identified rulers or palaces, and an entirely unclear collapse. Around 1900 BCE the cities were abandoned. Likely causes: climate shift, Indus River rerouting, drought, or migration. The people dispersed into smaller settlements; some migrated toward the Ganges plain.

The Vedic culture that emerged in India after 1500 BCE — bringing the Sanskrit texts and early Hinduism — may represent descendants of these people plus incoming Indo-Aryan groups from Central Asia.

**Ancient China: Shang and Zhou Dynasties (1600 – 221 BCE)**

Chinese civilization developed independently in the Yellow River valley. The Shang dynasty (1600–1046 BCE) had bronze casting, a writing system (oracle bone script, ancestor of modern Chinese characters), ancestor veneration, and human sacrifice in royal burials.

The Zhou dynasty (1046–256 BCE) overthrew the Shang and introduced the **Mandate of Heaven** — the idea that rulers governed legitimately only while virtuous, and heaven would withdraw the mandate from corrupt rulers. This concept structured Chinese political philosophy for 3,000 years. When a dynasty fell, the successor always claimed they had received the mandate from a collapsing predecessor.

The Zhou period fragmented into the **Warring States period** (475–221 BCE) — 250 years of constant warfare between rival kingdoms. This crucible produced China's greatest intellectual output: Confucius (~551–479 BCE), Laozi (Taoism), Sun Tzu (*The Art of War*), Mencius, Legalism. All the schools of Chinese thought emerged from trying to answer the same question: how do you create order out of chaos?

**The Phoenicians and Early Mediterranean Trade (1200 – 300 BCE)**

The Phoenicians (modern Lebanon) were the great maritime traders of the ancient world. They established trade colonies across the Mediterranean — Carthage (modern Tunisia) was the most powerful. Most critically, they developed the first alphabet (~1050 BCE): 22 consonants, no vowels. The Greeks adapted it, added vowels, and it became the ancestor of every Western alphabet in use today.

**The Persian Empire (550 – 330 BCE)**

Cyrus the Great (559–530 BCE) conquered Media, Lydia, and Babylon to create the first true world empire — one governing dozens of different peoples. Persian administration was unusual for its time: conquered peoples kept their religions and local customs. Cyrus freed the Jewish exiles from Babylon, making him the only non-Jew called a messiah in the Hebrew Bible.

At its peak under Darius I and Xerxes (~500–480 BCE), the Achaemenid Empire stretched from Greece to India — 50 million people, roughly 44% of the world's population. Infrastructure included the Royal Road (2,700 km from Sardis to Susa) and a postal relay system.

The Persian Wars against Greece (490–479 BCE) are famous from the Greek side, but from Persia's view, Greece was a minor frontier rebellon — the wars cost relatively little and were geopolitically peripheral. The empire fell to Alexander the Great in 330 BCE.

**The Hebrew World (1000 – 63 BCE)**

The Israelites developed the world's first strict monotheism — one God, universal, creator of everything, with an exclusive covenant with one people. This idea was radical: other ancient peoples had national gods that were parochial and polytheistic. The Hebrew insistence that their God was the only real God was not obviously compelling to neighbors, and the Israelites spent most of their history as a small people caught between larger empires.

Key moments: United kingdom under Saul, David, and Solomon (~1000–930 BCE); split into Israel and Judah; conquest of Israel by Assyria (722 BCE); Babylonian exile (586 BCE); return and Second Temple period. The trauma of exile and persecution deepened Jewish theological reflection and produced much of the Hebrew Bible as we have it. Jewish monotheism was the direct ancestor of Christianity and Islam.

### Era 3 — The Classical Age (500 BCE – 500 CE)

The civilizations of this period set the templates for most of what followed: Greek philosophy and political theory, Roman law and governance, Indian and Chinese philosophical traditions, and the birth of the world's major religions.

**Classical Greece (500 – 323 BCE)**

Greece was never politically unified — it was a collection of independent city-states (poleis) that shared a language and culture but frequently warred with each other. The geography enforced this: mountains isolated valleys, creating natural political units.

Athens under Pericles (~450 BCE) was the center of Greek intellectual life:
- **Democracy**: Direct democracy for male citizens (excluding women and slaves, who were the majority). The first system where ordinary people governed themselves.
- **Philosophy**: Socrates (470–399 BCE) — the method of questioning that reveals ignorance; Plato (428–348 BCE) — the Forms, the ideal state; Aristotle (384–322 BCE) — logic, biology, ethics, politics. These three set the trajectory of Western philosophy.
- **Theater**: Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides — drama as a tool for examining moral questions publicly
- **History**: Herodotus and Thucydides invented the discipline of historical inquiry

Sparta — Athens's great rival — was the opposite model: a militarized oligarchy built on the backs of enslaved Helots (who outnumbered Spartans 7:1). Spartan boys were taken from families at age 7 for military training. The system worked for military dominance but produced nothing intellectually.

The **Peloponnesian War** (431–404 BCE) between Athens and Sparta destroyed Athenian power. Then Philip II of Macedon and his son Alexander unified Greece by force.

**Alexander the Great (356 – 323 BCE)**

Alexander conquered the Persian Empire, Egypt, Mesopotamia, and continued east through Central Asia into India — all by age 32. More important than the conquest was what happened after: **Hellenism**, the fusion of Greek culture with Persian, Egyptian, Babylonian, and Indian cultures. Greek became the common language of the educated world from Egypt to Afghanistan. The city of Alexandria (Egypt) became the intellectual center of the world for three centuries.

When Alexander died with no clear succession plan, his empire immediately fractured among his generals. But Greek culture — language, philosophy, art — continued spreading through those successor kingdoms.

**The Roman Republic and Empire (509 BCE – 476 CE)**

Rome is the most studied civilization in Western history, and for good reason: its trajectory from small city-state to world empire, followed by institutional decay and collapse, is the most thoroughly documented case study in how civilizations rise and fall.

**Republic (509–27 BCE)**: Founded after expelling their Etruscan king, Rome built a republican system: Senate (aristocratic council), two Consuls (elected annually), and gradually more democratic institutions under plebeian pressure. The Roman innovation was **law** — a body of rules applying to all citizens regardless of status, enforceable and predictable. Roman law became the foundation of every European legal system.

Rome's military system was also innovative: the legionary, a professional soldier fighting in disciplined units with standardized equipment. Rome conquered Italy by 265 BCE, then Carthage (after three Punic Wars, 264–146 BCE), then Greece, Spain, Gaul, and eventually much of Europe and the Middle East.

The Republic's internal contradictions destroyed it: the wealth of empire flowed to elites while small farmers were displaced by slave-worked estates; generals accumulated private armies loyal to them personally rather than to Rome; political violence became normal. Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon in 49 BCE, seized power, and was assassinated in 44 BCE. His successor Augustus (27 BCE – 14 CE) established the Principate — maintaining republican forms while holding real autocratic power. The Empire began.

**Empire at Peak (27 BCE – 180 CE)**: The "Five Good Emperors" period (Nerva, Trajan, Hadrian, Antoninus Pius, Marcus Aurelius) was Rome's apex. Population: ~75 million. Roads: 400,000 km of paved roads. Architecture: the Pantheon, the Colosseum, aqueducts delivering clean water to cities. Marcus Aurelius (161–180 CE), the philosopher-emperor, wrote *Meditations* — still read 1,800 years later.

**Decline and Fall (180 – 476 CE)**: Rome's problems compounded for three centuries. The Crisis of the Third Century (235–284 CE) saw 50 emperors in 50 years, many assassinated. The economy contracted; hyperinflation from currency debasement; plague; the borders could not be defended at their full extent. Diocletian and then Constantine reorganized the empire — Constantine also legalized Christianity in 313 CE and moved the capital to Constantinople (modern Istanbul) in 330 CE. The Western Empire collapsed entirely in 476 CE when the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed the last emperor, Romulus Augustulus. The Eastern half (Byzantine Empire) continued for another 1,000 years.

Rome's collapse pattern is the template: overextension, currency debasement, elite corruption, loss of civic virtue, and eventually inability to defend borders. Read [history-mental-models.md](history-mental-models.md) for the recurring pattern this represents.

**The Maurya and Gupta Empires of India (321 BCE – 550 CE)**

India's first empire: Chandragupta Maurya (321–297 BCE) unified most of the subcontinent with advice from the strategist Kautilya, who wrote the *Arthashastra* — a manual of statecraft as ruthlessly pragmatic as Machiavelli's *Prince*.

His grandson **Ashoka** (268–232 BCE) was arguably the greatest ruler in Indian history. After a bloody conquest of Kalinga that killed ~100,000 people, he converted to Buddhism, renounced further conquest, and established the first example of a state based on non-violence and welfare. He built hospitals for humans and animals, maintained roads and water systems, and spread Buddhism across Asia through missionary networks. The dhamma wheel on the Indian flag is Ashoka's.

The Maurya Empire collapsed after Ashoka (small successors, invasions). Then the **Gupta Empire** (320–550 CE) was India's classical golden age: algebra invented here (Aryabhata calculated pi to 4 decimal places), the decimal system and zero developed (transmitted to the Arab world and then Europe), Sanskrit literature flourished. The Gupta fell to the White Huns (~550 CE).

**Han Dynasty China (206 BCE – 220 CE)**

The Qin dynasty (221–206 BCE) briefly unified China for the first time under the Legalist emperor Qin Shi Huang — who standardized weights, measures, currency, and writing, but ruled so harshly that his dynasty collapsed within 15 years of his death.

The Han dynasty took over and established the template for Chinese governance that lasted 2,000 years:
- **Confucian meritocracy**: Officials selected through civil service examinations based on Confucian texts — the world's first merit-based bureaucracy
- **Silk Road**: Han traders and diplomats established trade routes west through Central Asia to Persia and Rome. China exported silk; received gold and horses.
- **Technology**: Paper invented (~105 CE), iron casting, seismograph, advanced agriculture
- **Population**: ~60 million, comparable to Rome

The Han fell to internal fragmentation, warlordism, and peasant rebellions in 220 CE — the same collapse pattern as Rome, at roughly the same time, for roughly the same reasons.

**The Birth of the Major Religions**

This period produced the religious traditions that still dominate the world:
- **Buddhism** (~500 BCE): Siddhartha Gautama in northern India. Spreads east through Ashoka's missionaries; becomes dominant in Southeast Asia, China, Japan, Korea.
- **Zoroastrianism** (~600 BCE): Persian religion with good vs. evil cosmology; likely influenced Jewish, Christian, and Muslim theology on heaven, hell, devil, and apocalypse.
- **Christianity** (~30 CE): Jesus of Nazareth in Roman-occupied Judea. Spreads through the Roman Empire; legalized by Constantine (313 CE); becomes state religion (380 CE). Split into Eastern (Orthodox) and Western (Catholic) branches in 1054 CE.
- **Rabbinic Judaism** (~70–200 CE): After Rome destroyed the Second Temple in 70 CE, Jewish religious leadership shifted from priests to rabbis; the Talmud compiled. Judaism as practiced today dates from this transformation.

### Era 4 — The Post-Classical World (500 – 1450 CE)

Rome's fall didn't end civilization — it redistributed power. While Western Europe fragmented into feudal kingdoms, the Islamic world, China, India, and Africa built some of history's most sophisticated civilizations.

**The Byzantine Empire (476 – 1453 CE)**

The Eastern Roman Empire never fell — it just continued in Constantinople for a thousand years after Rome. It preserved Roman law, Greek language, and Christian theology while Western Europe lost literacy for centuries. Justinian I (527–565 CE) recodified Roman law (*Corpus Juris Civilis*) — still the basis of civil law in much of Europe.

Byzantium acted as a buffer between Western Europe and Islamic expansion. It fell to the Ottoman Turks in 1453 CE — the event that triggered the Renaissance partly by sending Greek scholars with ancient manuscripts fleeing into Italy.

**The Rise of Islam and the Islamic Golden Age (610 – 1258 CE)**

Muhammad (570–632 CE) received the Quran in Mecca. Within a century of his death, Arab armies had conquered the Persian Empire, Egypt, North Africa, Spain, and Central Asia — the fastest territorial expansion in history. The unifying force was not just military but ideological: one God, one community (*umma*) regardless of tribe, and a legal-moral code (*sharia*) that applied universally.

The **Abbasid Caliphate** (750–1258 CE), centered in Baghdad, presided over the Islamic Golden Age:
- **Baghdad's House of Wisdom**: Systematic translation of Greek, Persian, and Indian texts into Arabic — saving classical knowledge that Western Europe had lost
- **Mathematics**: Al-Khwarizmi invented algebra (algorithm is his name, latinized); trigonometry developed
- **Medicine**: Ibn Sina (Avicenna, 980–1037 CE) wrote the *Canon of Medicine*, the standard medical text in both the Islamic world and Europe for 500 years
- **Astronomy, optics, chemistry, geography**: The Islamic world was the leading scientific civilization from 800–1200 CE
- **Philosophy**: Ibn Rushd (Averroes) wrote commentaries on Aristotle that were how medieval Europeans learned Aristotelian thought

This period ended when Mongol armies sacked Baghdad in 1258 CE, destroying the House of Wisdom and killing the Caliph.

**Tang and Song Dynasty China (618 – 1279 CE)**

The Tang dynasty (618–907 CE) was China's most cosmopolitan era: the Silk Road brought Persian, Arab, Central Asian, and Buddhist influences to Chang'an (modern Xi'an), then the world's largest city (~1 million people). Poetry flourished; Buddhism reached its peak in China; gunpowder invented.

The Song dynasty (960–1279 CE) produced what historians call the "first modern economy": paper money, merchant credit, sophisticated markets, a massive iron industry, and urbanization unseen in Europe until the 19th century. Population reached ~100 million. The Mongols conquered the Song in 1279.

Key Chinese inventions from this period that changed world history: **printing** (movable type, ~1040 CE, 400 years before Gutenberg), **gunpowder** (weaponized for rockets and bombs by Tang period), **compass** (magnetized needle, applied to navigation by Song), **paper money**.

**The Mongol Empire (1206 – 1368 CE)**

Genghis Khan unified the Mongol tribes in 1206 and built the largest contiguous land empire in history. At its peak, the Mongol Empire stretched from Korea to Poland and from Siberia to Vietnam — roughly 24 million km².

The conquest was catastrophic: Baghdad destroyed (1258), populations massacred, the Islamic heartland's agricultural infrastructure collapsed and never fully recovered. Central Asian cities like Samarkand and Merv were obliterated. Some historians estimate 40 million people killed — a significant fraction of the world population at the time.

But the Pax Mongolica (Mongol Peace) that followed for ~100 years connected Eurasia as never before. The Silk Road was safe; trade and ideas moved from China to Europe. Marco Polo traveled the route (~1271–1295 CE). The Black Death also traveled it — moving from Central Asia to Europe in the 1340s.

The empire fragmented into four successor states after Genghis's grandsons divided it: the Yuan dynasty in China, the Ilkhanate in Persia, the Golden Horde in Russia, and the Chagatai Khanate in Central Asia. Each eventually assimilated into local cultures.

**The Mali, Songhai, and Other African Empires (700 – 1600 CE)**

Sub-Saharan Africa had sophisticated civilizations that European colonialism deliberately erased from world history textbooks.

- **Kingdom of Ghana (700–1200 CE)**: Controlled the gold and salt trade across the Sahara; funded by taxing trans-Saharan commerce
- **Mali Empire (1235–1600 CE)**: Mansa Musa (r. 1312–1337 CE) was possibly the wealthiest individual in human history. His 1324 pilgrimage to Mecca with 60,000 people and 12,000 kg of gold crashed the price of gold in Cairo and the Mediterranean for 20 years. Timbuktu was a major center of Islamic scholarship — the Sankore mosque was effectively a university housing 25,000 students.
- **Songhai Empire (1430–1591 CE)**: Larger than Mali; Timbuktu continued as an intellectual center
- **Great Zimbabwe (1100–1450 CE)**: A stone-walled city complex in modern Zimbabwe, capital of a trading state dealing in gold and ivory with Swahili Coast merchants who traded with Arabia and India
- **Axum/Ethiopia (100–940 CE)**: One of the world's great early powers; controlled Red Sea trade; adopted Christianity in 330 CE; preserved the Ark of the Covenant (in their own tradition); never colonized (defeated Italy in 1896 at the Battle of Adwa)

**Japan: Nara to Warring States (710 – 1600 CE)**

Japan borrowed heavily from Tang China (writing, Buddhism, government structure) while developing distinct cultural forms. Key phases:
- **Heian Period (794–1185 CE)**: Aristocratic court culture in Kyoto; *The Tale of Genji* (~1010 CE, often called the first novel) written by Murasaki Shikibu; refined aesthetic culture (*mono no aware* — the bittersweet awareness of impermanence)
- **Kamakura Period (1185–1333 CE)**: Military government (shogunate) replaces court aristocracy; samurai code begins forming; survived two Mongol invasion attempts (1274, 1281) only because typhoons (*kamikaze*, divine winds) destroyed the Mongol fleets
- **Sengoku / Warring States (1467–1615 CE)**: 150 years of warfare between feudal lords (daimyo); produced the final shogunate (Tokugawa) that unified Japan in 1615

**The Americas: Maya, Aztec, Inca (250 – 1532 CE)**

The Americas developed entirely separately from Eurasia — no direct contact until 1492. Three major civilizations reached remarkable sophistication:

- **Maya (250–900 CE Classical period, continued until Spanish conquest)**: City-states in modern Mexico/Guatemala. Writing system (one of only five independently invented writing systems in human history), accurate astronomical calendars, zero, ball courts, monumental architecture. The Classic Maya collapse (~900 CE) remains debated — drought, warfare, deforestation, or a combination.

- **Aztec / Mexica (1300–1521 CE)**: Built Tenochtitlan (modern Mexico City) on a lake with chinampas (floating gardens) feeding a population of ~200,000 — larger than any European city at the time. A tribute empire extracting goods and captives from conquered peoples; captives sacrificed in large numbers to feed the sun god. The Aztec were defeated by Hernán Cortés's 600 Spaniards partly through devastating smallpox epidemics (local populations had zero immunity to European diseases) and partly by allying with peoples the Aztec had long oppressed.

- **Inca (1438–1532 CE)**: The largest empire in the pre-Columbian Americas — 10 million people along 4,000 km of Andes coast. Roads, relay runners (*chasqui*), a quipu system (knotted strings encoding numerical records). No writing system but an extraordinary administrative apparatus. Toppled by Francisco Pizarro with 168 men, again through smallpox plus internal succession conflict.

The absence of wheels (for transport), iron metallurgy, and above all domesticable large animals (horses, cattle, etc.) shaped why these civilizations couldn't close the technological gap with Eurasia. Their isolation was the critical variable.

**Medieval Europe and the Feudal System (500 – 1350 CE)**

After Rome's fall, Western Europe fragmented into hundreds of small kingdoms. The organizing institutions were the Catholic Church (which preserved literacy and Roman administrative traditions) and feudalism (a hierarchy of obligation: serfs served knights who served lords who served kings who served God).

Key structures:
- **Carolingian Empire**: Charlemagne (742–814 CE) briefly reunified much of Western Europe, promoted literacy and Christianity. His empire split among his grandsons into France, Germany, and a middle kingdom — the origin of the modern map of Western Europe.
- **The Holy Roman Empire** (800–1806 CE): German and Italian kingdoms loosely unified; famously described by Voltaire as "neither holy, nor Roman, nor an empire." Constant struggle between emperors and popes over authority.
- **The Crusades** (1095–1291 CE): Eight major campaigns to recover Jerusalem from Muslim control. Militarily largely failed; but they opened trade routes to the Middle East, brought Islamic knowledge back to Europe (Arab translations of Greek philosophy, mathematics, medicine), and devastated Jewish communities in Europe (pogroms during Crusade preparations). The Fourth Crusade (1204) sacked Constantinople, accelerating Byzantine decline.
- **The Black Death** (1347–1351 CE): Bubonic plague killed ~50% of Europe's population — 25–50 million people. Collapsed the feudal labor system (dead peasants meant surviving ones could demand wages), weakened the Church's authority (prayers didn't stop it), and set conditions for the Renaissance and eventually the Reformation.

### Era 5 — The Early Modern World (1450 – 1800)

**The Ottoman Empire (1299 – 1922 CE)**

The Ottomans were Turkic Muslims who conquered Constantinople in 1453, ending the Byzantine Empire, and built a multi-ethnic empire stretching from Algeria to Iran, from Hungary to Yemen. At its peak under Suleiman the Magnificent (r. 1520–1566), the Ottoman Empire was the most powerful in the world.

The Ottoman system (*millet*) allowed non-Muslim communities (Christians, Jews) to govern themselves under their own religious law within the empire. This relative tolerance made it a refuge — Jews expelled from Spain in 1492 were welcomed in Ottoman cities.

The empire began declining in the late 17th century — failed siege of Vienna (1683 was the high-water mark), military and economic stagnation, provincial governors gaining autonomy — and slowly contracted over the next 200 years, earning the label "the sick man of Europe." Final collapse came in 1922 after World War I, replaced by Turkey under Kemal Atatürk.

**The Mughal Empire (1526 – 1857 CE)**

Babur (a Timurid prince claiming descent from Genghis Khan and Timur) invaded India in 1526 and founded the Mughal Empire. His grandson Akbar (r. 1556–1605 CE) was the greatest Mughal ruler — he controlled ~100 million people (a quarter of world population), abolished the *jizya* tax on non-Muslims, convened interfaith dialogues between Hindus, Muslims, Christians, and Zoroastrians, and oversaw a cultural flowering that produced Mughal architecture (including the Taj Mahal, built by Shah Jahan in 1632–53 CE for his wife).

The Mughals extracted enormous wealth from India's textile industry — Indian cotton and silk textiles were the luxury goods of the early modern world. When the British East India Company arrived, it first came to trade, not conquer. The Mughal collapse in the early 18th century (Aurangzeb's religious persecution alienated Hindu allies; provincial governors fragmented the empire) created the power vacuum the British exploited.

**Ming and Qing Dynasty China (1368 – 1912 CE)**

The **Ming dynasty** (1368–1644 CE) drove out the Mongols and rebuilt Chinese civilization. Admiral Zheng He's voyages (1405–1433 CE) reached East Africa with a fleet of 317 ships — by comparison, Columbus's fleet was 3 ships. China could have built a maritime empire but chose not to, withdrawing to focused internal governance after Zheng He's death.

The Ming also built the current Great Wall, restored agricultural infrastructure, and had a sophisticated porcelain export industry. Internal decline from fiscal problems, eunuch factions, and Manchu pressure from the north led to collapse in 1644.

The **Qing dynasty** (1644–1912 CE) was Manchu (non-Han Chinese) but adopted Han governance systems. Kangxi and Qianlong emperors oversaw China at its greatest territorial extent (~13 million km²). By the early 19th century, China produced ~30% of world GDP. The Qing then stagnated while Europe industrialized — a catastrophic relative decline.

**The Age of Exploration and Colonialism (1450 – 1800)**

Portugal began the age of European maritime expansion. Under Prince Henry the Navigator, Portuguese sailors systematically explored the African coast, reaching India in 1498 (Vasco da Gama) and Brazil in 1500.

Spain funded Columbus's 1492 voyage (intending to reach Asia; reached the Caribbean instead). The Spanish conquest of the Americas — Cortés defeating the Aztec (1521), Pizarro defeating the Inca (1532) — transferred extraordinary wealth to Spain through silver mining (Potosí, Peru).

The **Columbian Exchange** reshaped the world's agriculture and demography:
- From Americas to Europe: maize, potatoes, tomatoes, cacao, tobacco, chili peppers, sweet potatoes. The potato alone is credited with enabling the European population explosion.
- From Europe to Americas: wheat, sugarcane, horses, cattle, pigs, and catastrophically — smallpox, measles, influenza. Indigenous American population collapsed by 50–90% from disease. The Caribbean Taíno were essentially extinct within 50 years of Columbus.

The slave trade was the financial engine of early modern colonialism. An estimated 12.5 million Africans were transported to the Americas as enslaved people between 1500–1900 CE. They built the sugar, cotton, and tobacco economies of the New World. The wealth created funded European industrialization. This is the direct economic root of the Atlantic world's present structure.

**The Protestant Reformation (1517 – 1648 CE)**

Martin Luther's 95 Theses (1517) challenged the Catholic Church's practice of selling indulgences — payments that reduced time in purgatory. But the deeper challenge was epistemic: Luther argued individuals could interpret scripture for themselves, without Church mediation. This shattered Western Christianity's unity.

Within decades: Calvinist reform in Switzerland, Henry VIII's creation of the Church of England (motivated by divorce politics, not theology), and eventually a century of religious warfare. The **Thirty Years' War** (1618–1648 CE) killed ~8 million people in Central Europe, roughly one-third of the German population. The Peace of Westphalia (1648 CE) ended it — and established the modern concept of state sovereignty (governments decide their own affairs without external religious authority).

The Reformation's indirect effects mattered more than its theology: it promoted literacy (reading scripture required reading), decentralized authority, and created the skeptical-individualist intellectual culture that produced the Scientific Revolution.

**The Scientific Revolution (1543 – 1700 CE)**

A sequence of conceptual breakthroughs that replaced Aristotelian natural philosophy with empirical science:
- Copernicus (1543): heliocentric solar system
- Vesalius (1543): human anatomy through dissection
- Galileo (1610): telescope confirms Copernicus; mechanics experiments; under house arrest by Inquisition
- Kepler: laws of planetary motion
- Newton (1687): *Principia Mathematica* — universal law of gravitation, laws of motion. The single most powerful scientific framework ever published.

This was not a clean revolution from darkness to light. Newton was deeply religious and spent more time on alchemy and biblical prophecy than physics. But the *method* — hypothesis, experiment, quantification, peer review — was transformative. It created the epistemological framework for everything that followed.

**The Enlightenment and Atlantic Revolutions (1680 – 1815 CE)**

The Enlightenment (roughly 1680–1789 CE) was the application of reason and scientific method to human affairs: government, economics, society. Key thinkers:
- **Locke**: Natural rights (life, liberty, property); government by consent; influenced American founders
- **Montesquieu**: Separation of powers (inspired the US Constitution)
- **Voltaire**: Anti-clericalism, toleration, free speech
- **Rousseau**: Popular sovereignty; the social contract
- **Adam Smith**: *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) — free markets, division of labor, the invisible hand

These ideas produced revolutions:
- **American Revolution (1776)**: 13 British colonies declared independence; new constitution the first secular governing document based on Enlightenment principles
- **French Revolution (1789)**: Overthrew monarchy; Declaration of Rights of Man; devolved into the Terror (mass executions); then Napoleon
- **Haitian Revolution (1791–1804)**: The only successful slave revolt in history. Enslaved Africans defeated French, Spanish, and British armies and established Haiti — the first Black republic. Largely ignored in mainstream history.

**Napoleon (1799 – 1815)**

Napoleon Bonaparte seized power in France in 1799 and between 1803–1812 dominated Europe. He codified French law (*Code Napoléon*), still the basis of civil law in France, Quebec, and Louisiana. His invasions spread Enlightenment legal principles across Europe while also triggering nationalist reactions. His invasion of Russia (1812) with 685,000 soldiers — fewer than 100,000 returned — was one of history's greatest military catastrophes. Defeated at Waterloo (1815).

The Congress of Vienna (1815) tried to restore the pre-revolutionary order, but you can't un-invent ideas. Nationalism and liberalism would drive European politics for the rest of the 19th century.

### Era 6 — The Age of Industry, Empire, and Global War (1800 – 1945)

**The Industrial Revolution (1760 – 1900 CE)**

The most transformative economic shift since the agricultural revolution. It happened first in Britain (~1760–1840), then spread to Europe, North America, and Japan.

Key elements:
- Steam engine (Watt's improvement, 1769): replaced animal and water power; enabled factories, locomotives, steamships
- Textile mechanization: hand-spinning and weaving replaced by machines; first factories
- Iron and steel: railways, bridges, ships, weapons
- Coal: energy source for steam; also enabled chemicals, gas lighting

Why Britain first? Coal and iron deposits near each other and near water transport; a legal system protecting property and patents; a culture valuing practical invention; a colonial empire providing raw materials and markets; the Enclosure movement had displaced rural labor, creating an industrial workforce.

The social consequences were brutal for the first generation: child labor, 14-hour days, industrial accidents, urban slums, no social safety net. The Irish Famine (1845–1852) — 1 million dead, 1 million emigrated — happened partly because British industrial-colonial policy prioritized export during a potato blight.

This period created the modern class structure: industrial capitalists, a middle class of managers and professionals, and an urban proletariat. Karl Marx observed this in Manchester in the 1840s; his analysis produced *Das Kapital* (1867) and the Communist Manifesto — ideas that would reshape the 20th century.

**The Scramble for Africa (1880 – 1914)**

European powers divided Africa at the Berlin Conference (1884–1885) without a single African leader present. In 1880, European nations controlled ~10% of Africa; by 1914, they controlled ~90%. Only Ethiopia and Liberia remained independent.

The consequences for Africa were catastrophic and lasting: borders drawn to suit European convenience split ethnic groups and forced rivals together; traditional governance structures destroyed; resources extracted; education structured to produce clerks not engineers. The underdevelopment of much of Africa today traces directly to this period.

Belgian Congo under King Leopold II (1885–1908) was particularly extreme: rubber quotas enforced by cutting off workers' hands; population fell by ~10 million through killing, starvation, and disease.

**The Meiji Restoration and Japanese Modernization (1868 – 1945)**

Japan opened to the West under Commodore Perry's "black ships" (1853) and made a choice that China did not: rapid, deliberate modernization. The Meiji government (1868) abolished feudalism, built railroads, created a modern army and navy, established public education, and adopted Western legal and governmental forms — all within 30 years.

By 1905 Japan defeated Russia in the Russo-Japanese War — the first time an Asian nation defeated a European power in modern war. By the 1930s, Japan had its own imperial ambitions in Asia (invasion of Manchuria 1931, China 1937, Southeast Asia 1941).

**World War I (1914 – 1918)**

A conflict triggered by the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand but driven by decades of imperial rivalry, arms races, entangling alliance systems, and a military doctrine (Germany's Schlieffen Plan) that required immediate mobilization once war began.

The Western Front: 400 miles of trenches from Belgium to Switzerland. Industrial-scale slaughter: machine guns, artillery, poison gas, barbed wire. The Battle of the Somme (1916) — 60,000 British casualties on the first day; 1 million total casualties over 5 months.

Outcomes: ~20 million dead; the Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, and German empires dissolved; the Bolshevik Revolution (1917) created the Soviet Union; the Treaty of Versailles (1919) punished Germany so harshly it set conditions for World War II (Adolf Hitler explicitly referenced Versailles constantly). The mandate system handed Middle Eastern territories to Britain and France, drawing the borders of modern Iraq, Syria, Jordan, Palestine.

**The Russian Revolution (1917)**

The Tsarist regime — already weakened by military defeat in WWI and the 1905 revolution — collapsed in February 1917. A liberal provisional government took over; Lenin's Bolsheviks seized power in October 1917. Civil war (1917–1922) ended with Bolshevik victory and creation of the USSR.

Stalin (in power from ~1924) collectivized agriculture (killing ~6 million in the Ukraine famine/Holodomor), executed or imprisoned millions in the Great Purge, and industrialized the Soviet Union at enormous human cost. By 1941 the USSR was the second-largest industrial economy. It was essential to defeating Germany in WWII (27 million Soviet dead, compared to ~400,000 American).

**World War II (1939 – 1945)**

The deadliest conflict in human history: ~70–85 million dead, including ~50 million civilians. Fought across Europe, North Africa, the Middle East, East Asia, and the Pacific.

The European theater was fundamentally the clash between Nazi Germany (Hitler's racial ideology + industrial war machine) and the Soviet Union, with the Western Allies in a supporting role. The Holocaust: systematic murder of ~6 million Jews, ~500,000 Roma, 200,000 disabled people, 3 million Soviet POWs — the industrialization of genocide.

The Pacific theater: Japan's imperial expansion across East Asia collided with US interests after Pearl Harbor (December 1941). Island-hopping campaign; atomic bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki (August 1945) — the only wartime use of nuclear weapons.

Outcomes: US and Soviet Union as superpowers; European empires mortally weakened; UN created; Nuremberg Trials established international law criminalizing genocide and crimes against humanity; Cold War began immediately.

### Era 7 — The Contemporary World (1945 – Present)

**The Cold War (1947 – 1991)**

The US and Soviet Union never fought directly but engaged in 44 years of ideological, economic, and proxy warfare. Two systems: liberal capitalism vs. Soviet communism. Both armed with nuclear weapons, both knew direct war meant mutual annihilation — so every conflict was fought through proxies.

Key events: Berlin Blockade (1948), Korean War (1950–1953), Cuban Missile Crisis (1962 — the closest the world came to nuclear war), Vietnam War (1955–1975), Soviet invasion of Afghanistan (1979). US backed authoritarian regimes across Latin America and the Middle East if they were anti-communist; USSR backed leftist movements and states.

Both sides spent enormous resources on this competition — the US military-industrial complex, the Soviet command economy — while their competition drove the Space Race, produced the internet (ARPANET, a US defense project), and globalized ideology.

**Decolonization (1945 – 1975)**

European empires could no longer hold their colonies after WWII — morally discredited, economically exhausted, facing organized nationalist movements. India independence (1947), partition creating Pakistan; Israeli independence (1948); wave of African independence in the 1960s. By 1975, virtually all formal colonies were independent.

The independence movements often had to negotiate with colonizers who redrew borders for maximum post-independence instability (especially in Africa and the Middle East), install friendly regimes, and maintain economic extraction through different means (neocolonialism). Patrice Lumumba of Congo was assassinated with Belgian-US complicity 3 months after independence; similar stories repeated across the continent.

**Communist China (1949 – Present)**

Mao Zedong's Communist Party won the Chinese Civil War in 1949. Two disasters:
- **Great Leap Forward** (1958–1962): Forced collectivization and industrial production targets; bureaucrats falsified reports upward; actual crop production collapsed; 15–55 million died in the resulting famine — the largest famine in history
- **Cultural Revolution** (1966–1976): Mao mobilized youth Red Guards to destroy "bourgeois" elements; professors, doctors, intellectuals persecuted; cultural artifacts destroyed; 1–2 million killed directly

After Mao's death (1976), Deng Xiaoping introduced market reforms while maintaining Communist Party control. China's GDP grew ~10% annually for 30 years. 800 million lifted out of poverty — the largest poverty reduction in history. By 2023, China is the world's second-largest economy by GDP, largest by purchasing power parity. See [china-rise.md](../geopolitics/china-rise.md) for current trajectory.

**The Postwar Order (1945 – 2000)**

The US built the post-WWII international system: Bretton Woods (dollar as reserve currency, IMF, World Bank), the Marshall Plan (rebuilding Western Europe), NATO, and a network of military bases. This created extraordinary stability and growth for the developed world — the "Long Boom" from 1945–1973.

The 1971 Nixon Shock ended the gold standard; the dollar became a pure fiat currency backed by confidence and the petrodollar arrangement (OPEC priced oil in dollars in exchange for US security guarantees). This system funded American consumption and debt expansion for the next 50 years. See [reserve-currency-history.md](../geopolitics/reserve-currency-history.md).

**The Post-Cold War and Today (1991 – Present)**

The Soviet collapse in 1991 left the US as the sole superpower. The "unipolar moment." The 1990s saw rapid globalization, the internet revolution, and optimism about liberal democracy spreading globally (Fukuyama's "End of History").

Then: 9/11 (2001) → Afghanistan War → Iraq War → 20 years of "War on Terror" → exhaustion and strategic overreach. Simultaneously: China's rise, Russia's reassertion (2014 Crimea, 2022 Ukraine invasion), populist nationalism globally, COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022), AI revolution beginning (~2022–present).

The unipolar moment lasted roughly 30 years. The multipolar world — with at least China, the US, and possibly others as great powers — is already here. The question is whether the transition involves great-power war (as most historical transitions have) or manages to avoid it.

## Key Considerations

**Geography is destiny — more than most people admit.** Civilizations that controlled river valleys (Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, Yellow, Indus, Ganges) got early advantages through predictable agriculture. Civilizations in defensible geography (islands, mountains) lasted longer. Trade routes shaped who got wealthy. The Mongols only needed a flat steppe highway to conquer half the world. Read Jared Diamond's *Guns, Germs, and Steel* — the geographic argument is overstated but directionally correct.

**Disease has killed more people than war — every time.** The Black Death, smallpox in the Americas, Spanish flu (1918, 50–100 million dead), COVID-19 — pandemics consistently reshape history faster than any army. The civilizations that built urban density and long-distance trade got hit hardest but also developed partial immunity faster.

**Technological advantage is temporary.** China invented printing, gunpowder, and the compass, then failed to exploit them for naval dominance. Europe did. Technological superiority converts to dominance only with the institutional, social, and economic infrastructure to use it. The smartphone is useless without the supply chain, engineers, and capital markets behind it.

**Empires don't die from outside — they die from within.** Rome, Mughal, Qing, Ottoman, British, Soviet — all collapsed primarily from internal decay: currency debasement, elite corruption, institutional dysfunction, overextension, loss of the social contract. External enemies finish what internal rot started. This pattern is so consistent it should be a prior assumption when evaluating any powerful state. See [history-mental-models.md](history-mental-models.md) and [us-empire-decline-preparation.md](../geopolitics/us-empire-decline-preparation.md).

**The speed of change is accelerating.** From agriculture to writing: 5,000 years. From writing to printing: 4,000 years. From printing to industrial revolution: 300 years. From industrial revolution to nuclear weapons: 150 years. From nuclear weapons to the internet: 40 years. From internet to AI: 30 years. Each wave moves faster and disrupts more.

## Getting Started

If you want to actually understand history (not just know facts about it):

1. **Read one narrative history of a single civilization** — not a textbook. Anthony Everitt's *Augustus* or Tom Holland's *Rubicon* for Rome, or *The Fate of Rome* by Kyle Harper. Get the story first; patterns come later.
2. **Read *Guns, Germs, and Steel*** by Jared Diamond — the argument for why civilizations developed differently based on geographic luck. Controversial in the details but forces the big-picture view.
3. **Read *The Lessons of History*** by Will and Ariel Durant — 100 pages summarizing their 11-volume *Story of Civilization*. The densest single book on historical patterns.
4. **Pick one deep sub-topic** from the proposed list below and go deep with a dedicated deep-dive.

The trap to avoid: accumulating historical facts without understanding patterns. History's value is in the recurring themes, not the dates.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating history as a sequence of exceptional individuals.** Napoleon, Caesar, Genghis Khan matter — but they emerge from structural conditions that made their rise possible. Napoleon without the French Revolution's destruction of the old aristocracy is inconceivable. The Great Man theory of history is intellectually convenient and mostly wrong.

**Applying present-day morality anachronistically.** Judging ancient civilizations for slavery or conquest by 21st-century standards produces moral outrage that explains nothing. The relevant question is: what were the actual mechanisms, constraints, and alternatives these people faced? Presentism is intellectually lazy.

**Thinking your civilization is the exception.** Every empire that has ever existed was, to its inhabitants, obviously different from all previous empires and certainly not headed for the same fate. Rome's elites in 150 CE thought the eternal city was, well, eternal. This belief is incompatible with clear thinking.

**Knowing only one civilization's history.** Most Western education covers Greece → Rome → Medieval Europe → Renaissance → Industrial Revolution → WWII — and treats this as "world history." China, India, Africa, and the Americas existed for the whole timeline and were often more advanced. The Eurocentric framing is a perspective, not reality.

**Confusing the written record with what happened.** Most of history was oral; what was written down was written by elites, victors, and literate minorities. The actual experience of ordinary people — the majority of all humans who ever lived — is mostly invisible. Adjust confidence accordingly.

## Resources

*Rumi Vaidya, The Fate of Rome* (Kyle Harper, 2017) — Argues climate and pandemic were central to Rome's fall; outstanding model for how environmental factors drive civilizational change.

*Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind* (Yuval Noah Harari, 2011) — Full sweep of human history through the lens of cognitive, agricultural, and scientific revolutions. Controversial in places but unmatched for big-picture framing.

*The Lessons of History* (Will & Ariel Durant, 1968) — 100 pages of the Durants' synthesis after writing 11 volumes on civilization. Read this first if you want the thesis without the source material.

*Guns, Germs, and Steel* (Jared Diamond, 1997) — Why some civilizations dominated others: geography, domesticable plants/animals, disease. The geographic determinism is overstated but the framework is essential.
