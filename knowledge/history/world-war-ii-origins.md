# World War II Origins
*Versailles, the Great Depression, ideology, and the path to 1939 — how the most destructive war in history became inevitable*

---

## What Is It

World War II (1939–1945) killed 70–85 million people — roughly 3% of the world's population. It was not inevitable, at least not in 1919. A specific sequence of decisions — at the Paris Peace Conference, in Weimar Germany's political crisis, in the boardrooms of industrialists who funded the Nazis, in the cabinets of Britain and France who chose appeasement — made it progressively more likely until it was certain.

This file covers the origins: the failure of the Versailles peace, the rise of fascism, and the specific path from 1919 to September 1, 1939. The war itself — battles, campaigns, military history — is not the focus here. The focus is causation: why this war happened, who made the decisions that led to it, and what a different set of decisions might have produced.

---

## Why It Matters

WWII produced the world's current political order — the UN system, the US as global hegemon, NATO, the EU, decolonization, Israel, the Cold War. Understanding how that order was created requires understanding the catastrophe that made it necessary.

More importantly: the mechanisms that produced WWII — scapegoating, economic crisis, democratic erosion, elite accommodation of extremism, failure of collective security — are not unique to 1930s Germany. They are recurring dynamics. Recognizing them in historical form sharpens recognition of them in current form.

## Core Concepts

### The First World War's Unfinished Business

WWI (1914–1918) didn't resolve the underlying tensions that caused it. It killed 20 million people and left the map of Europe completely redrawn — without answering the questions of German power, Austro-Hungarian succession, or Balkan nationalism that had triggered it.

**The Paris Peace Conference (1919):**
The victors — France, Britain, Italy, and the US — met to determine the post-war settlement. The four key figures:
- **Woodrow Wilson (US):** Idealistic. Proposed the Fourteen Points — self-determination for nationalities, open diplomacy, freedom of the seas, and a League of Nations to adjudicate future disputes. His domestic political position was weak; the US Senate refused to ratify the treaty or join the League.
- **Georges Clemenceau (France):** Realistic and vindictive. France had been invaded twice by Germany in 50 years (1870, 1914); he wanted Germany permanently weakened. "Twenty years of peace," he reportedly said about the Versailles settlement — closer to prediction than satisfaction.
- **David Lloyd George (Britain):** Pragmatic, concerned about punishing Germany too harshly (recognizing the instability this would create) but also facing domestic political pressure for a punitive peace. "Make Germany pay."
- **Vittorio Orlando (Italy):** Largely irrelevant; Italy had been promised territories that weren't delivered.

**The Treaty of Versailles (June 1919):**

*The territorial provisions:*
- Alsace-Lorraine returned to France (taken in 1870)
- West Prussia and Posen given to Poland (creating the "Polish Corridor" — cutting Germany's eastern territory and separating East Prussia)
- The Rhineland occupied by Allied troops for 15 years, then demilitarized
- Germany stripped of all overseas colonies
- German-Austria forbidden to unite with Germany (self-determination applied against German speakers)

*The military provisions:*
- German army limited to 100,000 men (no conscription, no tanks, no aircraft, no submarines)
- German navy severely limited
- Rhineland permanently demilitarized

*The war guilt clause (Article 231):*
Germany and its allies accepted "responsibility... for causing all the loss and damage... as a consequence of the war imposed upon them by the aggression of Germany and her allies." The legal basis for reparations — but also a permanent political wound. Germany's military and political establishment (and most Germans) believed they hadn't started the war through aggression; the clause felt like a lie imposed at gunpoint.

*Reparations:*
Initially set at 132 billion gold marks in 1921 (revised downward repeatedly). The actual payments Germany made before repudiation are disputed — perhaps 20 billion marks — but the psychological and political damage of the reparations obligation was enormous regardless of actual payments.

**John Maynard Keynes's critique:**
Keynes, then a British Treasury official, published *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919) immediately after the conference — resigning in protest. His argument: the Versailles settlement was vindictive and economically incoherent; it would impoverish Germany without making it incapable of war, creating political resentment without eliminating military potential. "The policy of reducing Germany to servitude for a generation, of degrading the lives of millions of human beings, and of depriving a whole nation of happiness should be abhorrent and detestable." He was correct.

### The Weimar Republic (1919–1933)

Germany's attempt at democracy after WWI was established under the worst possible conditions:
- Born in military defeat (the "stab in the back" myth immediately blamed democratic politicians for surrendering)
- Signed an internationally humiliating peace treaty
- Lost territory and population
- Faced immediate left-wing revolution (Spartacist uprising, 1919) and right-wing coup attempts (Kapp Putsch, 1920; Hitler's Beer Hall Putsch, 1923)
- Experienced hyperinflation in 1923 (the German mark became worthless — wheelbarrows of currency for a loaf of bread)

**The stability years (1924–1929):**
The Dawes Plan (1924) restructured reparations; US loans flowed into Germany; the economy stabilized and began to recover. The Weimar Republic's middle years were genuinely creative — Bauhaus design, Berlin's cultural scene, democratic politics functioning. The Nazi Party received 2.6% of the vote in 1928.

**The Depression and the political crisis (1929–1932):**
The Wall Street Crash (October 1929) and the subsequent US bank failures led American creditors to call in their German loans. Germany's credit-dependent economy collapsed. By 1932:
- 30% unemployment (official — real unemployment higher)
- GDP down 25%
- Industrial production down 40%

In this environment, extremist parties flourished. The Communists (KPD) grew. The Nazis grew faster. In the July 1932 elections, the Nazis received 37.4% of the vote — the largest in Weimar history. Neither left nor right was willing to cooperate across the divide; the center collapsed.

### The Rise of Hitler

**Adolf Hitler (1889–1945):**
Austrian-born, failed art student, decorated WWI corporal, who emerged from the Munich beer halls of the early 1920s as a skilled demagogue. The Beer Hall Putsch (November 1923) — an attempt to seize power in Bavaria — failed; Hitler was arrested and sentenced to 5 years (served 9 months). He used the trial as a platform and the imprisonment to write *Mein Kampf* — his autobiography and ideological statement.

*Mein Kampf* was explicit about his program:
- Racial hierarchy, with "Aryans" at the top and Jews as the existential enemy
- Annihilation of Marxism (associated with Jews in Nazi ideology)
- Acquisition of *Lebensraum* ("living space") — conquest of Eastern Europe and Russia for German settlement
- Destruction of the Versailles treaty and restoration of German greatness

None of this was hidden. European leaders who later claimed surprise were not reading the available material.

**The seizure of power:**
The Nazi Party never won a majority in free elections. Their peak was 37.4% (July 1932); by November 1932, they had fallen to 33.1% — their electoral peak had passed. Hitler came to power through appointment, not election.

The decisive figure: **Franz von Papen** (former Chancellor) and **President Paul von Hindenburg**. Papen convinced the 85-year-old Hindenburg that Hitler could be appointed Chancellor and then controlled — manipulated as a useful puppet while conservative elites retained real power. This was a catastrophic miscalculation.

On January 30, 1933, Hitler was appointed Chancellor of Germany. Within two months:
- The Reichstag Fire (February 27, 1933) — blamed on Communists, used to justify suspension of civil liberties under emergency decree
- The Enabling Act (March 23, 1933) — passed by the Reichstag under duress, giving Hitler the power to rule by decree without parliamentary approval. This was the legal end of the Weimar Republic.

**Why ordinary people supported the Nazis:**
- Economic desperation: in 1932, 30% unemployment, the Nazis promised jobs and they delivered (rearmament-driven spending)
- Nationalist grievance: the Versailles humiliation was real and widely felt
- Anti-communism: the German middle and upper classes feared Soviet-style revolution more than fascism
- Anti-Semitism: pre-existing in German (and broader European) culture, exploited by Nazi propaganda
- Charismatic leadership and a media operation more sophisticated than any predecessor

**The role of German industry:**
Major German industrialists (Krupp, Thyssen, IG Farben) provided financial support to the Nazi Party in the belief that a strong government would suppress socialist parties and labor unions. They were correct that the Nazis did this; they didn't anticipate the rest of the program.

### Fascism's Rise Across Europe

Germany was not unique. The interwar period produced fascist or authoritarian governments across Europe:

**Italy (1922):**
Benito Mussolini's Blackshirts marched on Rome; King Victor Emmanuel III appointed him Prime Minister rather than face a constitutional crisis. Mussolini established a one-party state by 1925. Italian fascism — the original, from which the term derives — was nationalist, corporatist (cooperation between state, industry, and labor under state direction), anti-communist, but initially less racially ideological than Nazism.

**Spain (1936–1939):**
The Spanish Civil War — Nationalist forces under Franco (backed by Germany and Italy) against the Republican government (backed by the Soviet Union and international volunteers). 500,000 deaths. Franco won; Spain remained a fascist state until his death in 1975.

**Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, Yugoslavia, Greece:** Various degrees of authoritarian nationalism.

**The common factors:** Economic depression, perceived communist threat, weak democratic institutions, nationalist grievances (real or manufactured), and a political elite willing to accommodate rather than confront extremism.

### Appeasement (1935–1939)

Britain and France faced a difficult choice when Hitler began violating Versailles:

**Why appeasement was chosen:**
- **Military unpreparedness:** Britain and France were not ready to fight in 1935 or 1936. Rearmament was incomplete; public opinion opposed another war.
- **Genuine uncertainty:** Was Hitler's program limited or unlimited? Some Western leaders believed he had legitimate grievances (Versailles was widely acknowledged to have been too harsh) and that satisfying German nationalist demands would stabilize him.
- **Fear of the Soviet Union:** Some British and French conservatives saw Nazi Germany as a bulwark against Soviet communism. Better Hitler than Stalin.
- **The "war guilt" hangover:** WWI had been caused (in the popular narrative) by inflexibility — mobilization timetables that left no room for diplomacy. This generation was determined not to repeat that error. The result was the opposite error.

**The escalation:**
- **Remilitarization of the Rhineland (March 1936):** Hitler marched troops into the demilitarized zone. France and Britain protested but did nothing. Hitler's generals had privately told him to retreat if France moved; France didn't move. Hitler's confidence grew.
- **Anschluss (March 1938):** Germany annexed Austria. Again, protests but no action.
- **Sudetenland crisis (1938):** Hitler demanded the German-speaking Sudetenland of Czechoslovakia, claiming self-determination. Czechoslovakia prepared to fight; its French alliance obligated France to help.

**Munich (September 1938):**
Britain, France, Germany, and Italy met — without Czechoslovakia — and agreed to give Germany the Sudetenland. Chamberlain returned to London with a piece of paper bearing Hitler's signature promising no further territorial demands. "Peace for our time."

Czechoslovakia surrendered the Sudetenland (the fortified mountains that made defense possible) and within six months, Germany occupied all of Czechoslovakia (March 1939) — violating the Munich agreement explicitly.

Churchill's verdict: "You were given the choice between war and dishonor. You chose dishonor and you will have war."

**Why appeasement failed:**
Hitler's demands were not limited. Every concession confirmed that the Western democracies were irresolute, encouraged the next demand, and materially strengthened Germany (Czechoslovakia's Skoda arms factories became German military suppliers). Appeasement of unlimited demands is not diplomacy — it is capitulation on installment.

### The Path to War (1939)

**Soviet-German Non-Aggression Pact (August 23, 1939):**
The surprise of the century — ideological enemies, Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union, signed a non-aggression treaty. The secret protocol divided Eastern Europe into spheres of influence: Germany got western Poland; the Soviets got eastern Poland, the Baltic states, Finland, and Bessarabia.

Stalin's reasoning: buy time for Soviet military preparation; let Germany exhaust itself against the Western powers; then pick up the pieces. It almost worked — except Germany turned east in June 1941.

Hitler's reasoning: eliminate the two-front war risk before attacking Poland, then deal with the Soviets later from a position of strength.

**Poland (September 1, 1939):**
Germany invaded Poland. Britain and France, having guaranteed Polish independence after Czechoslovakia's fall, declared war on Germany September 3. The Second World War began.

Poland was partitioned between Germany and the Soviet Union within weeks. The western front remained inactive ("Phoney War") until Germany invaded France in May 1940.

## Key Considerations

**The lesson of Munich is not "never negotiate."** It's "unlimited demands cannot be satisfied by concessions." The distinction matters: appeasement with a limited aggressor (who has specific achievable goals) can work. Appeasement with an expansionist who interprets each concession as weakness and uses it to strengthen position for the next demand cannot.

**Economic crisis produces political radicalization.** The Weimar Republic was functioning tolerably in 1928; the Depression made it ungovernable. This is not unique to Germany — economic desperation makes extreme solutions attractive. The Depression also produced FDR's New Deal in the US (left-wing expansion of government) and fascism across Europe (right-wing authoritarianism). Both were responses to the same crisis; political culture determined which response.

**Ideology matters.** Hitler wrote down his entire program in 1925. The program was followed. Leaders who claim ideological goals and have the means to pursue them should be taken at their word. This was the intelligence failure of the 1930s — assuming Hitler was a normal politician whose stated goals were bargaining positions.

**Elite accommodation of extremism is one of history's worst recurring mistakes.** Von Papen thought he could control Hitler. German industrialists thought they could use him. The British and French thought they could satisfy him. In each case, the extremist used the accommodation to consolidate power and then pursued his own program.

## Resources

- *The Origins of the Second World War* — A.J.P. Taylor (1961). Controversial and still essential — argues the war wasn't inevitable and Hitler was reacting to circumstances as much as executing a plan. Read it against other accounts.
- *Hitler: 1889–1936 Hubris* and *Hitler: 1936–1945 Nemesis* — Ian Kershaw. The definitive two-volume biography. Comprehensive and exhaustively researched.
- *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* — John Maynard Keynes (1919). A primary source — the best contemporary critique of Versailles.
- *The Gathering Storm* — Winston Churchill (1948). Churchill's own account — self-serving but written by someone who was right when everyone else was wrong.
