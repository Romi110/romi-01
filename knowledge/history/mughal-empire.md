# Mughal Empire
*Babur to Aurangzeb: how a Central Asian conqueror built India's last great empire, how Akbar's genius made it flourish, and how religious hardening destroyed it*

---

## What Is It

The Mughal Empire (1526–1857) ruled most of the Indian subcontinent for nearly three centuries, governing 100–150 million people at its peak — roughly a quarter of humanity. It was founded by a Central Asian prince descended from both Timur and Genghis Khan, developed by a child emperor into one of history's most sophisticated multi-religious states, and eventually hollowed out by overextension and the reversal of the tolerance that held it together.

[Indian history broadly](indian-history.md) covers the Mughals in context. This file goes deep on the empire's internal mechanics, its arc, and what it reveals about governance.

## Why It Matters

The Mughal Empire is the most relevant historical case study for Indian cultural heritage — it shaped the subcontinent's languages (Urdu), architecture (Taj Mahal, Red Fort), cuisine, music, and administrative vocabulary. It's also a study in the governance of diversity: the largest empire in Indian history succeeded by incorporating Hindu and Muslim populations as co-stakeholders, and collapsed when it abandoned that model.

## Core Concepts

### Babur and the Founding (1526–1530)

**Zahir ud-Din Muhammad Babur** was the ruler of Fergana (modern Uzbekistan) — a tiny principality he inherited at age 12 and lost twice. He spent his 20s attempting to recapture Timur's capital of Samarkand, failing repeatedly, before pivoting south to India.

The **First Battle of Panipat (1526)** was the founding moment. Babur's force of 12,000 faced Ibrahim Lodi's 100,000. Babur had two decisive advantages: firearms (matchlock muskets and field artillery, new to Indian warfare) and mobile cavalry tactics — flanking maneuvers called the *tulughma*. Lodi's war elephants panicked under artillery fire. Babur won in hours.

Babur disliked India — the heat, the lack of melons, the flat landscape. He wrote about it honestly in the *Baburnama*, one of the earliest autobiographical works in Islamic literature. He stayed because there was nothing to go back to. He died in 1530 at 47, reportedly after performing a ritual to take his son Humayun's fatal illness upon himself.

### Humayun: Exile and Reconquest (1530–1556)

**Humayun** was an opium addict and an inconsistent ruler who managed to lose the empire and then regain it. The Afghan chieftain **Sher Shah Suri** defeated him twice and expelled him from India entirely in 1540. Humayun spent 15 years in exile — first in Sindh, then as a guest (effectively a dependent) of the Safavid Shah of Persia.

Persian exile was transformative. Humayun absorbed Persian court culture, aesthetics, and administrative practice — importing a Persian sensibility that shaped Mughal art and governance for the next century. The Safavids gave him an army. He reconquered Delhi in 1555. Six months later, he fell down his library stairs and died.

His one significant legacy: he appointed the Persian scholar **Bairam Khan** as regent for his 13-year-old son, Akbar. Bairam Khan stabilized the empire during Akbar's minority, won the **Second Battle of Panipat (1556)** against the Hindu king Hemu (who nearly took Delhi), and then was overthrown when Akbar came into his own authority.

### Akbar the Great (1556–1605): The Governance Peak

**Jalal ud-Din Muhammad Akbar** is the most significant ruler in Indian history after Ashoka, and by most measures the most capable Mughal emperor. He was illiterate (possibly dyslexic) and one of the most intellectually curious rulers who ever lived.

**The mansabdari system:** Akbar's core administrative innovation. Every imperial official held a "mansab" — a numerical rank (from 10 to 10,000) specifying salary, the number of cavalry they were obligated to maintain, and their status in the court hierarchy. Rank was not hereditary; it was assigned by the emperor and revised annually based on performance. This created a meritocratic imperial service in which Hindus, Rajputs, Persians, and Muslims competed on equal terms. At Akbar's death, Hindus held approximately 22% of the highest mansab ranks — unthinkable under the Delhi Sultanate.

**Religious policy:** Akbar abolished the jizya (the tax on non-Muslims that marked their subordinate status), banned the enslavement of prisoners of war, and convened the **Ibadat Khana** — weekly multi-faith debates at his court in Fatehpur Sikri, attended by Muslim scholars, Hindu pandits, Jain monks, Zoroastrian priests, and eventually Jesuit missionaries. He married Rajput princesses (his primary empress, Jodha Bai, was Hindu) and permitted them to practice their religion within the imperial household. He developed **Din-i-Ilahi** — a personal syncretic philosophy drawing from Islam, Hinduism, Zoroastrianism, and Christianity — which was not imposed on anyone and faded after his death.

**The Rajput alliance:** Akbar's political masterstroke was converting Rajputana's warrior clans from enemies into imperial partners. Rajput kingdoms had resisted every previous Delhi ruler. Akbar married into Rajput families, appointed Rajput generals to top military commands, and gave Rajput kings autonomy within their territories in exchange for loyalty and military service. **Man Singh of Amber** became one of the empire's top military commanders. This gave Akbar a military base that was loyal, skilled, and motivated by honor — not merely hired.

**Fatehpur Sikri:** Akbar built an entirely new imperial capital near Agra (1571–1585), abandoned it 14 years later when water ran out, and left behind one of the finest examples of Mughal architecture — a city in sandstone that synthesizes Persian, Hindu, and Jain architectural traditions in a single complex.

### Jahangir and Nur Jahan (1605–1627)

**Jahangir** ("World Seizer") was an aesthete, a naturalist, and a severe alcoholic and opium addict. He wrote the *Tuzk-i-Jahangiri* — a diary recording his observations of animals, plants, and court life with unusual attentiveness. Mughal miniature painting reached its peak under his patronage.

He is most significant for what he delegated. His empress **Nur Jahan** — the only woman to have coins struck in her name in Mughal history — effectively governed for long periods of his reign. She controlled appointments, conducted diplomacy, and managed the court with considerable skill. Her family (father and brothers) dominated administration. Jahangir's son Shah Jahan led a failed rebellion against Nur Jahan's faction.

### Shah Jahan (1628–1658): Grandeur and Overreach

**Shah Jahan** is remembered for the **Taj Mahal** — built over 22 years (1632–1653) as a tomb for his wife Mumtaz Mahal, who died giving birth to their 14th child. It is the finest monument of Mughal aesthetic achievement and one of the most technically accomplished buildings in human history.

His reign was also one of continuous military campaigning in the Deccan (peninsular India) — expensive, mostly inconclusive, and draining. He spent so heavily on architecture and warfare that his treasury, full at accession, was depleted by the time of his illness in 1657.

His illness triggered a war of succession among his four sons. The third son **Aurangzeb** — the most militarily capable and the most religiously orthodox — imprisoned his father in Agra Fort, executed his brothers, and took the throne. Shah Jahan spent his last eight years imprisoned, with a view of the Taj Mahal from his window.

### Aurangzeb (1658–1707): Maximum Extent, Internal Collapse

**Aurangzeb** ruled for 49 years — longer than any other Mughal — and expanded the empire to its greatest geographic extent, taking the Deccan sultanates and pushing south to the tip of the peninsula. He was personally austere: he lived simply, made his own money copying the Quran by hand, and refused to use state funds for personal expenses.

He reversed almost every policy that had made the empire function:

- **Jizya reimposed** (1679) — the non-Muslim tax Akbar had abolished. This was not financial necessity; it was a statement of Islamic supremacy that converted Hindu subjects from stakeholders to subordinates.
- **Temple destruction** — Aurangzeb ordered the demolition of major Hindu temples, including the Kashi Vishwanath temple in Varanasi and the Keshava Deo temple in Mathura, replacing them with mosques.
- **Rajput alienation** — the alliances Akbar had carefully built collapsed under Aurangzeb's religious assertiveness. The Rajputs who had been the empire's military backbone became adversaries.
- **The Deccan quagmire** — Aurangzeb spent the last 26 years of his reign on campaign in the Deccan, personally commanding operations against the Maratha Confederacy under **Shivaji** and his successors. He took territory and bled his treasury and army. The Marathas used guerrilla tactics the Mughal army was not designed to fight — striking, withdrawing, refusing pitched battle.

Aurangzeb died in 1707 on campaign, reportedly saying: "I have sinned terribly, and I do not know what punishment awaits me." He was 88 years old. He had outlived the empire's coherence.

### The Collapse (1707–1857)

After Aurangzeb, the Mughal Empire fragmented faster than any other major Islamic empire in history. The mechanisms:

- **Succession war** — 12 emperors in 12 years after Aurangzeb's death, most installed or removed by competing noble factions
- **The Marathas** — now a confederacy spanning most of central and western India, raiding as far as Delhi
- **Nadir Shah's invasion (1739)** — the Persian emperor sacked Delhi, massacred 20,000–30,000 civilians, and carried off the **Peacock Throne** and the **Koh-i-Noor diamond**. The empire never recovered its treasury or prestige
- **Regional governors (Nawabs and Nizams)** — became independent rulers in all but name: Bengal, Hyderabad, Awadh, Mysore
- **The British East India Company** — after Plassey (1757), controlled Bengal; used Mughal political fragmentation to pick up provinces one by one
- **The 1857 uprising** — called the Indian Mutiny by the British, the First War of Independence by Indian nationalists — was partly a Mughal restoration attempt. After its suppression, the British formally ended the Mughal dynasty and exiled the last emperor, Bahadur Shah Zafar, to Burma, where he died in 1862.

## Key Considerations

The Mughal collapse is a near-perfect case study in the governance of diversity: the empire was held together by making minority populations (Hindus, Rajputs, Sikhs) co-stakeholders in imperial success. When Aurangzeb converted that arrangement into Islamic dominance, he turned stakeholders into subjects and then into enemies. He won every battle and lost the empire.

The administrative genius of the mansabdari system deteriorated because its success depended on imperial supervision. Later emperors lacked Akbar's attention and energy — ranks became hereditary in practice, positions were sold, performance reviews lapsed.

## Resources

- *Akbar: The Great Mughal* — Ira Mukhoty. Best modern account of Akbar's governance and court, written for a general audience without sacrificing depth.
- *The Mughal Empire* — John F. Richards (The New Cambridge History of India). The scholarly standard — dense but comprehensive.
- *Bamber Gascoigne, The Great Mughals*. Classic narrative of the dynasty from Babur to Aurangzeb, readable and well-illustrated.
- *Aurangzeb: The Man and the Myth* — Audrey Truschke. Revisionist but valuable account that complicates the villain-emperor narrative.
