# Indian History
*5,000 years: Indus Valley to Vedic civilization, Maurya to Mughal, British Raj to the world's largest democracy*

---

## What Is It

India's history spans more continuous civilization than almost anywhere on Earth — from the Indus Valley cities of 2500 BCE through the Vedic age, through a succession of empires that alternated between unification and fragmentation, through Islamic sultanates and the Mughal Empire, through 190 years of British colonial rule, to partition in 1947 and the emergence of a democracy of 1.4 billion people. It is not a single story but a layered accumulation of civilizations, religions, languages, and political forms that coexist in one geographic unit.

## Why It Matters

India is the world's largest democracy and its fifth-largest economy (on track to third within a decade). Its history is the context for contemporary geopolitics, the Hindu-Muslim tension that shapes South Asian politics, the caste system's persistent effects on social mobility, and the model of non-violent resistance that Gandhi demonstrated and that influenced every major civil rights movement of the 20th century.

## Core Concepts

### Indus Valley Civilization (2600–1900 BCE)

The Indus Valley (Harappan) civilization was one of the ancient world's three great river valley civilizations alongside Egypt and Mesopotamia — and in some ways the most sophisticated. Cities like **Mohenjo-daro** and **Harappa** had standardized weights and measures, sophisticated drainage and sanitation systems (better than most of Europe for the next 3,000 years), grid-planned streets, and a writing system that has never been deciphered.

The civilization stretched across what is now Pakistan, northwest India, and Afghanistan. It declined by 1900 BCE — likely due to climate change, river shifts, and possibly the first signs of the migrations that would become the Indo-Aryan influx.

### Vedic Age and the Foundations of Hinduism (1500–500 BCE)

The **Indo-Aryan migrations** (debated chronology, roughly 1500–1000 BCE) brought Sanskrit-speaking peoples from Central Asia into the subcontinent. The encounter between these migrants and existing populations produced the **Vedic culture** — the foundation of Hinduism, one of the world's oldest living religious traditions.

The Vedic texts — the **Rigveda**, Upanishads, and later the Mahabharata and Ramayana epics — defined concepts that still organize Indian life: **dharma** (cosmic order, duty, righteousness), **karma** (the moral consequence of actions), **samsara** (the cycle of rebirth), and **moksha** (liberation from the cycle).

The **caste system** (varna: Brahmin, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra) emerged in this period as a social hierarchy that organized labor and ritual purity. It stratified society rigidly in ways that persist today. The Dalits ("untouchables") existed outside the four castes — assigned the most polluting labor and subjected to systematic exclusion that centuries of reform have only partially addressed.

The **Axial Age** (~500 BCE) produced two major reform movements challenging Vedic orthodoxy: **Buddhism** (Siddhartha Gautama, rejecting caste and emphasizing individual path to liberation) and **Jainism** (Mahavira, radical non-violence). Both emerged in the Gangetic plain and shaped India permanently — Buddhism eventually spread across Asia while declining in India; Jainism produced merchants whose business ethics (non-violence, scrupulous honesty) influenced modern Indian commerce.

### Maurya Empire and Ashoka (322–185 BCE)

**Chandragupta Maurya** founded India's first empire in 322 BCE, consolidating the Gangetic plain into a centralized state. His adviser **Kautilya** wrote the **Arthashastra** — a manual of statecraft as coldly realist as Machiavelli, covering administration, economics, warfare, and espionage. Politically sophisticated India, unlike what Western historians assumed.

**Ashoka** (reigned 268–232 BCE), Chandragupta's grandson, fought the Kalinga War (~261 BCE), which killed roughly 100,000 people. Horrified by the destruction he witnessed, Ashoka converted to Buddhism and issued the **Rock Edicts** — carved instructions throughout his empire promoting non-violence, religious tolerance, welfare of subjects, and just governance. It was possibly history's first explicit government policy of toleration. Ashoka sent Buddhist missionaries to Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, and Central Asia — shaping civilizations across the continent.

After Ashoka, the Maurya Empire fragmented. No one matched its territorial scope for centuries.

### Gupta Golden Age (320–550 CE)

The **Gupta Empire** (roughly 320–550 CE) is India's classical age — when Sanskrit literature, mathematics, astronomy, and philosophy reached their peak.

Achievements from this period:
- **Aryabhata** (~476 CE) — calculated π to 4 decimal places, proposed Earth rotates on its axis, developed early trigonometry
- **Brahmagupta** — formalized rules for arithmetic using zero as a number, not just a placeholder
- The concept of **zero** as a mathematical entity was developed in India and transmitted through Islamic scholarship to Europe (the numerals we call "Arabic" were Indian in origin)
- Sanskrit drama, poetry, and the *Kamasutra* (Vatsyayana — a text about how to live well, not merely a sex manual)
- The **decimal numeral system** — still in use everywhere on Earth

The Guptas were followed by regional fragmentation and then the **Harsha** interlude (7th century), before India entered centuries of competing regional kingdoms.

### The Delhi Sultanate and Mughal Empire (1206–1857)

**Turkish and Afghan Muslim invasions** from the northwest brought Islam to India definitively. Mahmud of Ghazni raided India repeatedly in the 11th century; Muhammad of Ghor established the first Muslim sultanate at Delhi in 1206. Over the following three centuries, five dynasties ruled as the **Delhi Sultanate**, drawing on Turko-Persian administrative traditions and fighting off the Mongol invasions that devastated the rest of Asia.

The **Mughal Empire** (1526–1857) was founded by **Babur**, a descendant of both Timur and Genghis Khan, who defeated the last Sultan at the First Battle of Panipat (1526). At peak under **Akbar the Great** (reigned 1556–1605), the Mughals controlled most of the subcontinent and ruled through genuine religious pluralism — Akbar abolished the jizya (tax on non-Muslims), welcomed Hindu, Christian, and Zoroastrian scholars to court, and developed **Din-i-Ilahi**, a syncretic philosophy attempting to merge India's religions.

**Shah Jahan** (1628–1658) built the **Taj Mahal** — the greatest monument of Mughal aesthetic achievement. His son **Aurangzeb** (1658–1707) reversed Akbar's pluralism: reimposed jizya, destroyed temples, and pursued aggressive expansion south. Aurangzeb won territory but generated rebellions (Marathas, Sikhs, Rajputs) that fractured Mughal authority. By 1720, the empire was hollowing out.

### British Raj (1757–1947)

The British **East India Company** arrived in 1600 for trade and stayed for empire. The critical moment was the **Battle of Plassey** (1757) — Company forces under Robert Clive defeated the Bengal Nawab (backed by France) and gained control of Bengal, India's wealthiest province. From this foothold, using Indian soldiers (sepoys) and divide-and-rule politics among fragmented Indian powers, the Company gradually took the entire subcontinent.

Colonial rule restructured India's economy for extraction: deindustrialization of Indian textile manufacturing (which had been globally dominant), forced cultivation of cash crops (opium, cotton, indigo), land revenue systems that created famine vulnerability, and building railroads primarily to extract resources to ports.

The **Sepoy Mutiny/First War of Independence** (1857) — a rebellion triggered in part by cartridges greased with pig and cow fat (offensive to Muslim and Hindu soldiers) — spread across north India before being brutally suppressed. The British Crown then took direct control from the Company, and India became the jewel in the imperial crown.

**Gandhi** and the **Indian National Congress** developed a strategy of **nonviolent non-cooperation** (satyagraha — "truth force") that undermined British legitimacy rather than British military power. The 1930 **Salt March** — Gandhi walking 240 miles to make salt from the sea in defiance of the British salt monopoly — demonstrated the method at scale: civil disobedience that was impossible to suppress without making the suppressor look monstrous.

### Partition and Independence (1947)

WWII left Britain unable to afford empire. Independence came in August 1947 — but the **partition of British India** into India (majority Hindu) and Pakistan (Muslim majority, split into two disconnected parts: West Pakistan and East Pakistan/Bengal) produced one of history's largest forced migrations: 10–20 million people displaced, 200,000–2 million killed in communal violence.

**Jawaharlal Nehru** led India as its first Prime Minister, choosing non-alignment in the Cold War and a mixed economy with strong state direction. India's economy underperformed for decades due to the "License Raj" — an entanglement of regulation and state control that stifled private enterprise. Economic liberalization in 1991 (forced by a balance-of-payments crisis) unleashed growth.

The India-Pakistan conflict over **Kashmir** — divided at partition, claimed by both — has driven three wars (1947, 1965, 1971) and a continuous low-level conflict. Both countries are now nuclear powers.

## Key Considerations

India's scale creates persistent fragmentation. With 22 official languages, hundreds of dialects, four major religious traditions, and caste complexity, the question of what holds India together is real. The answers: the constitution (genuinely respected), the military (apolitical and professional), cricket, Bollywood, and the shared experience of British colonialism.

## Getting Started

- *The Discovery of India* — Jawaharlal Nehru. India's first Prime Minister's history of his own civilization; biased but illuminating.
- *India: A History* — John Keay. The best single-volume overview; comprehensive without being encyclopedic.
- *An Era of Darkness* — Shashi Tharoor. On British colonialism's economic extraction — a corrective to benign-empire narratives.
