# Information Networks
*The evolution of communication infrastructure from postal and telegraph systems through telephone, radio, packet switching, ARPANET, and the modern internet; the architectural and governance choices that shaped information power.*

---

## What Is It
Information networks are the systems, technologies, and institutions that move messages, data, and authority across space. They include physical infrastructure like roads, cables, and radio towers, as well as rules for routing, addressing, and controlling access.

## Why It Matters
Every power structure rests on communications. Empires used postal relays and telegraphs to rule distant lands. Companies use internet protocols to enforce markets. States use surveillance and censorship to preserve stability. Knowing the evolution of information networks is the clearest way to see how technology and governance shape who controls what information and why.

## Core Concepts

### Postal and relay systems
Before electric signals, messages traveled by horses, ships, and human couriers. Empires built relay networks with waystations, official passports, and state monopolies. Speed was limited by terrain and weather, so trust and authority were the real currency: a sealed letter from a sovereign could command obedience far faster than a rumor.

### Telegraph and wired communication
The telegraph changed the game by turning distance into latency. Messages that once took days or weeks could cross continents in minutes. Telegraph networks were expensive and centralized; they required physical lines, switching stations, and standardized codes like Morse. That means the first digital network was also the first example of infrastructure as a geopolitical asset.

### Telephone and radio
Telephone networks moved from point-to-point voice links to exchange-based networks that could connect any two parties. Radio added wireless broadcast and point-to-point signaling, enabling ships, armies, and politicians to reach broad audiences. These technologies introduced the idea that communications could be either direct and private or public and pervasive.

### Packet switching and decentralization
Packet switching broke messages into discrete chunks that could travel independently through a shared network. That design made the network more resilient and efficient than fixed voice circuits. It is the architectural idea behind the internet: intelligence at the edges and a dumb core that routes packets rather than maintaining end-to-end circuits.

### ARPANET and the birth of the internet
ARPANET was a US defense research network built in the late 1960s. It connected universities and labs with packet-switched lines and the first TCP/IP protocols. Its early governance was quasi-military and academic, which shaped the internet’s initial culture of open standards and collaborative research.

### Internet governance and power
The modern internet is layered: physical cables and wireless spectrum, packet protocols, applications, and governance bodies like ICANN and IETF. Control can come from ownership of fiber, control of naming systems, platform moderation policies, national firewalls, or surveillance law. Architecture and governance together determine whether a network favors openness, monopoly, or state control.

## Key Considerations
- Infrastructure is not neutral: a network built for broadcast is different from one built for resilient peer-to-peer communication.
- Physical paths matter. Submarine cables, fiber routes, and satellite footprints shape what is possible and who can cut or control it.
- Network governance is as important as protocols. Standards bodies, policy, and regulation decide who can participate and who is excluded.
- Resilience trades off with efficiency. Centralized control can be fast and cheap, while distributed design is more robust but harder to manage.
- History repeats. Today’s debates about platform power and jurisdiction echo older debates about telegraph monopolies and state postal control.

## Getting Started
1. Learn the core stages: postal relay → telegraph → telephone/radio → packet switching → ARPANET → internet.
2. Understand the architectural shift from circuit-switched telephony to packet-switched networks.
3. Study how governance changed from state postal monopolies to multi-stakeholder internet bodies.
4. Compare modern self-hosted/local networks to the earlier model of centralized carrier systems.

## Common Mistakes
- Calling the web the internet. The web is an application layer; the internet is the packet network underneath.
- Treating the internet as naturally decentralized. Its physical and administrative layers still concentrate power in carriers, cable owners, and platform operators.
- Assuming early ARPANET design was solely military. It was also academic and experimental, which is why it emphasized openness.
- Ignoring the role of regulation and law. Networks are designed by engineers, but their use is shaped by policy and states.

- "The Innovators" by Walter Isaacson — useful for seeing the personalities behind early computing and network ideas.
