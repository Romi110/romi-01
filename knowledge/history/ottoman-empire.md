# The Ottoman Empire
*600 years of Islamic governance across three continents, the fall of Constantinople, and how the empire's collapse created the modern Middle East*

---

## What Is It

The Ottoman Empire (1299–1922) was one of history's longest-running and most consequential states — an Islamic empire that at its peak controlled Anatolia, the Balkans, the Middle East, North Africa, and the Caucasus, ruling over 20–30 million subjects of dozens of ethnicities and religions. It was the last great Islamic caliphate, the successor to Byzantine Rome, and its collapse in WWI directly created the borders, conflicts, and instabilities that still define the Middle East today.

## Why It Matters

Almost every ongoing conflict in the Middle East can be traced to Ottoman collapse: the Palestinian question, Iraqi sectarianism, Syrian fragmentation, Lebanese confessionalism, Kurdish statelessness. The modern map of the region was drawn by Britain and France in 1916 (the Sykes-Picot Agreement) to partition Ottoman territory — lines drawn without regard for ethnic, religious, or tribal realities. Understanding the Ottomans is understanding why that region is structured as it is.

## Core Concepts

### Origins: From Tribal Principality to Regional Power (1299–1453)

The Ottomans began as one of many small **Turkic principalities** (beyliks) in northwestern Anatolia — a frontier zone between the collapsing Byzantine Empire and the Mongol-disrupted Seljuk Sultanate. Their founder **Osman I** (from whom "Ottoman" derives) ruled a small territory in the early 1300s and built a military reputation as a *ghazi* (warrior for Islam) fighting Byzantine frontier towns.

What distinguished the early Ottomans was not their initial power but their **strategic position** (on the Byzantine frontier, where there was always territory to expand into), their pragmatic incorporation of non-Turks into their military and administration, and their legal structure — Osman's successors developed a system that was legitimately Islamic while absorbing Byzantine administrative practices.

By the late 1300s, the Ottomans had crossed into Europe (the Balkans), made the Byzantine Empire a tributary state, and assembled one of the most capable military forces in the world.

### The Fall of Constantinople (1453)

**Mehmed II** ("the Conqueror") laid siege to Constantinople in April 1453 with roughly 80,000 troops against 7,000 defenders. The city's legendary walls, which had resisted sieges for a thousand years, were breached by **massive cannon** — the largest artillery yet built, casting balls that weighed 1,200 pounds. Constantinople fell on May 29, 1453.

The fall ended the Byzantine Empire — the Eastern Roman continuation that had survived 1,000 years after Rome's fall. See [roman-history.md](roman-history.md). Greek scholars fled to Italy, carrying classical manuscripts that fed the Renaissance. The Orthodox Church's administrative center moved to Moscow, which began calling itself the "Third Rome."

For Mehmed, the conquest was theological and political legitimacy: he was now the ruler of the ancient imperial capital, the successor to Rome. He renamed the city Istanbul (though it had been called that informally for years) and made Hagia Sophia a mosque.

### Suleiman the Magnificent: Peak Ottoman Power (1520–1566)

**Suleiman I** reigned 46 years and brought the empire to its geographic and institutional peak. His contemporaries — Henry VIII, Francis I of France, Charles V of the Holy Roman Empire — knew him as the most powerful ruler in the world.

Territorial additions under Suleiman: Hungary (after the Battle of Mohács, 1526), Mesopotamia, most of North Africa, and the Arabian Peninsula including the holy cities of Mecca and Medina (giving the Sultan the title of Caliph — protector of all Sunni Muslims).

The Ottoman siege of Vienna (1529) reached the gates of Central Europe before supply lines and weather forced retreat. Europe feared the Ottomans the way later centuries feared Napoleon.

Suleiman was not just a conqueror. He commissioned one of the greatest architects in history, **Mimar Sinan**, who built the Süleymaniye Mosque in Istanbul and over 400 structures across the empire. He codified Ottoman law (earning the Turkish title *Kanuni*, "the Lawgiver"), patronized poetry and arts, and corresponded with Francis I in alliance against their common enemy, the Habsburgs.

### How the Empire Was Governed

The Ottomans ruled an empire of extraordinary ethnic and religious diversity. Their system for managing it was sophisticated and, by contemporary standards, relatively tolerant.

**The millet system**: religious communities (Greek Orthodox Christians, Armenian Christians, Jews, and later Catholics) were organized as self-governing *millets* — permitted to maintain their own courts, schools, and religious institutions, pay their own community taxes, and be governed by their own religious leadership in civil matters. This was not liberalism — it was administered pluralism with clear hierarchy (Muslims at top, dhimmi communities below). But it produced 400 years of coexistence in cities like Istanbul where Greeks, Armenians, Jews, and Muslims lived in adjacent neighborhoods.

**The devshirme system**: the practice of taking Christian boys from conquered territories (primarily the Balkans), converting them to Islam, giving them elite Ottoman education, and placing the ablest in the highest positions — including Grand Vizier. The Janissary corps (elite infantry) were entirely devshirme recruits. This gave the Sultan a loyal military and administrative class with no ties to hereditary aristocracy that could challenge imperial power. The paradox: some of the empire's most effective rulers and generals were born Christian Albanians or Serbs.

### Decline: The Long Contraction (1600–1914)

Ottoman decline was real but gradual — 300 years of slow contraction. Contributing factors:

**Military stagnation**: the Janissaries, once a merit-based elite corps, became a hereditary interest group resistant to modernization. When the Ottomans failed to adopt European military innovation (drill, professional officer corps, firearms technology improvements), they began losing wars they had previously won.

**Economic peripheralization**: the opening of Atlantic trade routes shifted European wealth away from Mediterranean trade, undercutting Ottoman customs revenues. The empire also failed to develop a productive commercial class — the millet system meant trade was largely in the hands of non-Muslim minorities (Greeks, Armenians, Jews) who lacked political power and didn't reinvest capital into Ottoman development.

**The "Eastern Question"**: as Ottoman power waned, European powers competed to manage — and exploit — the empire's decline. Russia pushed south toward the Bosphorus; Britain and France propped up the empire as a buffer against Russian expansion. The Ottoman Empire became "the sick man of Europe" — too weak to act independently, too large to be allowed to simply collapse.

**The Tanzimat reforms** (1839–1876) attempted modernization: legal equality for non-Muslims, a modern army, new commercial codes, secular courts, and an Ottoman constitution (1876). They were genuine but too limited and too resisted by conservatives to succeed. The Constitutional era was quickly suspended by Sultan Abdulhamid II.

### WWI, Genocide, and Collapse (1914–1922)

The **Young Turks** (Committee of Union and Progress) seized power in 1908 and dominated the empire through WWI, aligning with Germany and the Central Powers. The gamble failed catastrophically.

In 1915, the Ottoman government ordered the deportation of **Armenians** from Anatolia, fearing collaboration with Russia. The deportations — death marches into the Syrian desert without food or water — killed an estimated **600,000–1.5 million Armenians**. This is recognized as genocide by most historians and governments. Turkey's continued denial remains a source of diplomatic tension.

The empire lost WWI and was occupied by Allied forces. The **Sykes-Picot Agreement** (1916, secret) had already divided the Arab portions of the empire between Britain and France — lines drawn on maps without reference to ethnic or tribal realities. The modern borders of Iraq, Syria, Jordan, Lebanon, and Palestine were created here.

**Mustafa Kemal (Atatürk)** led a Turkish nationalist movement that defeated Greek, Armenian, and Allied forces in the Turkish War of Independence (1919–1923), salvaged Anatolia, and established the **Republic of Turkey**. He abolished the Sultanate (1922) and Caliphate (1924), imposed radical Westernization — Latin alphabet, secular law, Western clothing, women's rights — and created the modern state of Turkey.

## Key Considerations

The Ottoman Empire is the clearest example of how **diverse societies can be governed across centuries** — not through enforced uniformity, but through structured pluralism with clear rules. The millet system's coexistence was hierarchical, not egalitarian, but it worked. The contrast with the ethnic nationalism that replaced the Ottomans — which produced WWI, the Armenian Genocide, and the Balkan wars — is uncomfortable.
