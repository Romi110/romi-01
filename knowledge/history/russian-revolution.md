# Russian Revolution
*1917 and the Bolshevik seizure of power: what the Tsar's fall opened, what Lenin built, what Stalin became.*

---

## What Is It
The Russian Revolution refers to the series of events in 1917 when Russia’s centuries-old monarchy collapsed, a provisional government briefly took power, and the Bolshevik Party then seized control in October. It was not a single moment but a sequence of political crises, mass mobilizations, and a brutal civil war that remade Russia into the Soviet Union.

## Why It Matters
The Revolution created the first sustained large-scale alternative to Western liberal capitalism. It reshaped 20th-century geopolitics, drove the Cold War, and provided the model for later revolutionary states. For anyone trying to understand modern authoritarianism, state capacity, or the relationship between ideology and power, 1917 is the clearest case of how a revolutionary vanguard turned crisis into a durable state.

## Core Concepts

### Tsarism’s structural crisis
Russia entered World War I with weak industry, poor infrastructure, and an autocratic system that could not absorb military defeat. The state relied on the loyalty of a narrow elite, and its inability to feed soldiers and cities made political legitimacy fragile. The February revolution was therefore not accidental — it was the collapse of a regime that had already lost its capacity to govern.

### Dual power and the provisional government
After the February uprising, two authorities coexisted: the liberal Provisional Government, which wanted to continue the war and delay land reform, and the Petrograd Soviet, which represented workers and soldiers and demanded immediate change. That split created a political vacuum that the Bolsheviks exploited.

### Bolshevik strategy and October seizure
Lenin returned to Russia in April 1917 with a simple program: end the war, transfer land to the peasants, and put power in the soviets. The Bolsheviks built discipline, a unified command structure, and a readiness to seize the moment. In October, they took key communication centers, bridges, and government buildings in Petrograd and forced the Provisional Government to surrender.

### Civil war and revolutionary state-building
The Bolsheviks consolidated power through the Red Army, War Communism, and repression of opponents. The White armies were diverse and disunited. The civil war let the Bolsheviks convert political victory into institutional control, but it also created the conditions for centralized planning, forced grain requisitioning, and the permanent militarization of society.

### Lenin’s legacy and Stalin’s transformation
Lenin created the Communist Party as the core institution of state power and accepted a one-party dictatorship as a temporary necessity. After Lenin’s death, Stalin used the same party structure to impose a far harsher regime: collectivization, Five-Year Plans, secret police, and a system of terror. The Revolution’s initial promises of democracy and peace were eclipsed by repression.

## Key Considerations
- The revolution was both top-down and bottom-up: popular anger mattered, but so did Bolshevik organization.
- War was the accelerator. Without World War I, the Tsarist regime might have survived longer.
- The Provisional Government failed mostly because it kept the war going and delayed land reform.
- Institutional design mattered: the Bolsheviks built a party-state apparatus that outlived its founders.
- The line between survival and tyranny was thin in a civil war state.

## Getting Started
1. Anchor the timeline: February revolution, April Theses, July Days, Kornilov affair, October revolution, civil war.
2. Read Lenin’s April Theses and Order No. 1 to see the Bolshevik program and the soviet mentality.
3. Compare the Provisional Government’s priorities with Bolshevik demands.
4. Track the transition from War Communism to the New Economic Policy; that’s the clearest evidence of how pragmatic the regime could be when survival required it.

## Common Mistakes
- Treating 1917 as a single, inevitable event instead of a series of contests among rival forces.
- Confusing Lenin with Stalin. Lenin invented the party-state structure; Stalin weaponized it.
- Assuming the Bolsheviks were a mass democratic movement like Western parties. They were a disciplined minority oriented around seizure and consolidation.
- Seeing the Revolution only as ideology. Material shortages, war, and social breakdown were the immediate drivers.

## Resources

- "The Bolsheviks" by Adam Ulam — strong on party organization and the role of Lenin.
- "Russia in Revolution" by S.A. Smith — useful for understanding the broader social and constitutional context.
