# Korea: A History
*Three Kingdoms through Joseon, Japanese colonization, partition, and how South Korea became one of the world's most remarkable development stories*

---

## What Is It

Korean history is the story of a civilization that maintained a distinct cultural identity across 2,000 years of pressure from its much larger neighbors — China, Japan, and Mongol-era Central Asia. Korea invented one of the world's most logical writing systems, sustained long periods of cultural achievement under Confucian governance, endured brutal Japanese colonization, was split by Cold War politics, and then — in the south — executed one of the fastest economic transformations in history, from rubble in 1953 to high-income democracy by 1990.

## Why It Matters

Korea is one of history's clearest lessons in **how small nations survive between great powers**: through deliberate cultural consolidation, tributary diplomacy, and periodic heroic resistance. The contrast between South and North Korea — essentially the same people, same culture, split by ideology in 1945 — is the sharpest natural experiment in the world on what political and economic systems actually produce.

## Core Concepts

### Gojoseon and the Three Kingdoms (2333 BCE–668 CE)

Korean tradition dates its founding to Gojoseon (legendary founding 2333 BCE, historical kingdom ~7th century BCE–108 BCE), centered in what is now North Korea and Manchuria. Its end came when the Han Chinese emperor Wudi destroyed it and established four commanderies — China's first major assertion of control over the peninsula.

The **Three Kingdoms period** (roughly 57 BCE–668 CE) saw three states dominate the peninsula:
- **Goguryeo** — the north, militaristic, occupied most of Manchuria at its peak; successfully repelled massive Sui and Tang Chinese invasions
- **Baekje** — the southwest, cosmopolitan and artistically sophisticated; transmitted Buddhism and Chinese culture to Japan
- **Silla** — the southeast, the eventual unifier; allied with Tang China to defeat Baekje (660 CE) and Goguryeo (668 CE), then turned and pushed the Chinese out

Buddhism and Chinese writing, governance, and philosophy entered Korea during this period — deeply absorbed but never resulting in Korea becoming simply a Chinese satellite.

### Unified Silla and Goryeo (668–1392)

**Unified Silla** (668–935) consolidated the peninsula and produced a genuine cultural flowering — Buddhist art, sculpture, and scholarship centered on Gyeongju. The kingdom weakened through aristocratic factionalism and was replaced by **Goryeo** (918–1392, from which the name "Korea" derives).

Goryeo developed a sophisticated bureaucratic state with a civil service exam system and produced extraordinary achievements:
- The **Tripitaka Koreana** — 80,000 woodblocks carving the complete Buddhist canon, still the most complete version in the world
- **Celadon pottery** — Korean celadon (13th century) is considered technically superior to Chinese celadon of the same period
- **Movable metal type** (1230s) — invented in Korea roughly 200 years before Gutenberg

The Mongol invasions (1231–1259) devastated Goryeo repeatedly. After decades of resistance, Korea became a Mongol vassal state, enduring a century of occupation. See [mongol-empire.md](mongol-empire.md). The Mongols used Korea as a staging base for the failed invasions of Japan.

### Joseon Dynasty: Confucian State (1392–1897)

The **Joseon dynasty** lasted over 500 years — one of the longest-running dynasties in world history. General Yi Seonggye overthrew Goryeo in 1392, replaced Buddhism with Neo-Confucianism as the state ideology, and moved the capital to Hanyang (modern Seoul).

**Key achievements**:
- **Hangul** (1443) — King Sejong created Korea's phonetic alphabet, explicitly designed so common people (not just scholars trained in Chinese characters) could read and write. Linguistics scholars consider it one of the most scientifically designed writing systems ever made.
- A sophisticated meritocratic bureaucracy via civil service exams
- Major advances in astronomy, medicine, and agriculture

Joseon faced two devastating invasions: the **Japanese invasions** (1592–1598) led by Toyotomi Hideyoshi, which killed hundreds of thousands and burned Seoul multiple times before being repelled (partly due to Admiral Yi Sun-sin's famous turtle ships — armored vessels that destroyed Japanese supply lines); and the **Manchu invasions** (1627, 1636) that forced Korea to accept Qing Chinese suzerainty.

Late Joseon became increasingly conservative and resistant to outside contact — earning it the Western label "the Hermit Kingdom," before that label was transferred to North Korea. This isolation proved catastrophic as the 19th century's great-power competition intensified.

### Japanese Colonization (1910–1945)

After Japan's victory in the Russo-Japanese War (1905) made it the dominant power in Northeast Asia, Korea was declared a Japanese protectorate and then **formally annexed in 1910** — stripped of independence and governed directly by Japan until 1945.

Japanese colonization was systematically brutal:
- Korean language, names, and cultural expression were suppressed; Koreans were required to take Japanese names
- Land surveys dispossessed Korean farmers and transferred ownership to Japanese settlers and corporations
- Forced labor conscription sent hundreds of thousands to Japanese mines and factories
- An estimated 200,000 Korean women were coerced into sexual slavery as "comfort women" for the Japanese military — a wound still politically unresolved between the two countries
- Korea's economy was developed but as a resource extraction colony, not for Korean benefit

Japan's defeat in 1945 ended colonization — but liberation came without any Korean agency in determining what came next.

### Division, Korean War, and the Two Koreas (1945–1953)

At the end of WWII, the US and Soviet Union hastily agreed to divide Korea at the 38th parallel: the Soviets would accept Japanese surrender in the north, the Americans in the south. This was meant as a temporary administrative measure. It became permanent.

The Soviets installed **Kim Il-sung** (a Soviet-trained guerrilla fighter who had resisted Japan in Manchuria) in the north. The US eventually installed **Syngman Rhee** (a Princeton-educated independence activist) in the south. Both men claimed to represent all of Korea.

**The Korean War (1950–1953)**: North Korea invaded the south on June 25, 1950, nearly overrunning the entire peninsula before a US-led UN force intervened. General MacArthur's Inchon landing reversed the war's momentum — the UN forces pushed north toward the Chinese border, provoking Chinese intervention (October 1950) with 300,000 troops. The war stabilized roughly at the 38th parallel. An armistice (not a peace treaty) was signed July 27, 1953. The peninsula has been technically at war ever since. See [cold-war-deep-dive.md](cold-war-deep-dive.md) for the Cold War context.

Roughly **3 million Koreans** died in the war. Most Western histories treat it as a sideshow; Koreans call it the "Forgotten War."

### South Korea's Economic Miracle (1960–1990)

South Korea in 1953 was one of the poorest countries on Earth — GDP per capita below Sub-Saharan Africa, infrastructure destroyed, literacy incomplete. By 1990, it was a high-income industrialized democracy. This is one of the fastest economic transformations in history.

The architect was **Park Chung-hee**, a military dictator who seized power in 1961. His strategy: export-led industrialization using Japan's postwar model as a template, directed state credit to chosen conglomerates (**chaebols** — Samsung, Hyundai, LG, Daewoo), investment in education, and suppression of labor costs and political opposition. Brutal but effective.

The chaebols became globally competitive in steel, shipbuilding, electronics, and semiconductors. Samsung today is the world's largest manufacturer of memory chips. Korea's literacy rate reached near-100%. Per capita income went from ~$80/year in 1960 to ~$10,000 by 1990.

Democracy arrived through popular pressure — the 1987 democratic transition followed mass protests. South Korea is now a full democracy with one of the most connected and high-tech societies on Earth.

### North Korea

The other half of the partition experiment. Kim Il-sung established a hereditary communist dynasty (Kim Il-sung → Kim Jong-il → Kim Jong-un) ruling through a combination of ideological control, surveillance, and terror. North Korea has a nuclear weapons program and ballistic missiles capable of reaching the continental US. Its people live in near-total information isolation, with ~40% in food insecurity.

The two Koreas are the same people split 75 years ago. The gap in outcomes is one of history's most vivid illustrations of what institutions and systems produce.

## Key Considerations

Korea's recurring challenge is the "shrimp among whales" problem — a smaller power caught between giants (China, Japan, Mongolia, Russia, the US) and forced to navigate each era's dominant powers. Every major inflection in Korean history involves responding to external pressure. The strategies that worked: cultural consolidation (Hangul, Buddhism, Confucian institutions), diplomatic accommodation with the dominant power, and periodic military resistance when accommodation failed.
