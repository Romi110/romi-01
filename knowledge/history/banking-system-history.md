# The History of Banking and Debt
*From Florentine goldsmiths to the Federal Reserve — how the banking system was built, who built it, and why debt is the permanent condition of the modern economy*

---

## What Is It

Banking is the business of borrowing money at one rate and lending it at a higher one — and, in the modern form, creating that money in the act of lending it. The system we live inside — central banks, fractional reserve lending, sovereign debt, consumer credit — was not discovered. It was constructed, by specific people with specific interests, over several centuries.

This file traces that construction: from the first bankers who realized they could lend money they didn't have, through the Rothschilds who industrialized sovereign debt, through JP Morgan who concentrated American finance, through the Federal Reserve's creation, to the 1980s financialization that produced the debt-saturated world that now exists.

The honest framing: this is not a conspiracy story. But it is a story about power — about who designed the rules, who benefits from them, and why those rules make debt a permanent feature of modern life rather than a temporary condition.

## Why It Matters

You live in a debt-based monetary system. The interest on your mortgage, your car loan, your government's bonds — these are not incidental costs. They are the system operating exactly as designed. Understanding how it was designed tells you: why inflation is a policy goal (not an accident), why sovereign debt never gets paid off (it's not supposed to), why consumer debt is structured to maximize total interest paid, and where your actual leverage points are.

Self-reliance means reducing dependence on debt-based systems where possible. You can't opt out completely — but you can understand the water you're swimming in.

## Core Concepts

### The Origins — Goldsmiths and the Discovery of Fractional Reserve Banking

Before banks, wealthy merchants stored gold and silver with goldsmiths, who had secure vaults. The goldsmith gave the depositor a receipt: "I hold X ounces of gold, payable on demand." These receipts began circulating as money — more convenient than carrying gold.

The goldsmiths noticed something: depositors rarely came for their gold all at once. At any given time, only a fraction of the gold was being claimed. This meant a goldsmith holding 1,000 ounces of gold could issue receipts for 2,000 ounces — lend receipts that had no gold behind them — and as long as fewer than half the depositors demanded gold simultaneously, no one would know.

This was the invention of fractional reserve banking. The goldsmith earned interest on money he created from nothing. The risk: if confidence broke and everyone demanded gold at once — a bank run — the fraud was exposed.

This is not ancient history. The mechanics are identical to modern banking, with the gold replaced by central bank reserves and the receipts replaced by digital account balances.

### The Medici and the Birth of Modern Finance (14th–15th Century)

The Medici family of Florence built the most sophisticated banking network of the medieval world. Their innovations weren't just about money — they were about managing money across borders and circumventing the Catholic Church's prohibition on usury (lending at interest).

**Bills of exchange**: A merchant in Florence could deposit money with the Medici, receive a document (a bill of exchange), and redeem it for local currency at a Medici branch in London or Bruges. The Medici made money on the currency exchange rate — technically not interest, but functionally equivalent. This was how medieval bankers charged for the use of money without calling it usury.

**Double-entry bookkeeping**: Developed in Florence in the 13th–14th century, codified by Luca Pacioli in 1494. Every transaction recorded as both a debit and a credit. This made it possible to track complex financial positions across multiple accounts and geographies — the technical foundation of all modern accounting.

**The lesson the Medici learned**: control of credit is control of power. They financed popes, kings, and wars. When those debtors defaulted (as the English crown repeatedly did), the Medici absorbed catastrophic losses. Credit extended to sovereigns who could not be sued created the first too-big-to-fail dynamic.

### The Bank of England (1694) — The Template for Central Banking

The Bank of England was not created to serve the public. It was created to solve a government borrowing problem.

England needed money to fight Louis XIV's France. The government was broke. A group of private merchants, led by Scottish financier William Paterson, offered a deal: they would lend the government £1.2 million at 8% interest. In exchange, the subscribers would be incorporated as the "Governor and Company of the Bank of England" with the exclusive right to issue bank notes — paper money backed by the government's promise rather than gold.

The bank lent the government money it created by issuing notes. The government paid interest on those notes. The bank's shareholders collected the interest. The government could fight its wars.

This arrangement has never fundamentally changed. The specifics have evolved — the bank was nationalized in 1946, the mechanics are more complex — but the essential relationship remains: the government borrows from banks; banks create the money they lend; the government pays interest; bank shareholders profit.

**What this created**: sovereign debt as a permanent institution. Before central banking, kings borrowed from wealthy individuals and often defaulted spectacularly (the Medici losses; Edward III defaulting to Florentine bankers in 1339–40). After the Bank of England, governments could borrow from a market of investors through standardized bonds — but also became permanently indebted, with interest payments as a perpetual fixture of state finance.

### The Rothschild Dynasty — Industrializing Sovereign Debt

Mayer Amschel Rothschild (1744–1812) started as a coin dealer and antiques merchant in Frankfurt's Jewish ghetto (the Judengasse). He developed relationships with German princes as an agent and financial manager, and built a reputation for discretion and reliability. His five sons — Amschel (Frankfurt), Salomon (Vienna), Nathan (London), Carl (Naples), and James (Paris) — established branches in the five major financial capitals of Europe.

**The actual innovation**: the Rothschilds didn't simply lend money. They created the infrastructure for sovereign debt markets. Before them, a government wanting to borrow had to negotiate individual loans from individual rich men. The Rothschilds syndicated bond issuances — bringing together networks of investors to purchase standardized government debt instruments, managed by the Rothschild house for a commission. They created liquidity for government bonds by acting as market-makers, buying and selling in secondary markets. This dramatically reduced borrowing costs for governments and created a new asset class for wealthy investors.

**The Napoleon era**: The Rothschilds financed the wars of the Napoleonic period, primarily on the British-coalition side — funding subsidies to Prussia, Austria, and others to fight Napoleon. Nathan Mayer Rothschild (London) was the pivotal figure, arranging the transfer of British gold to Wellington's forces in the Peninsula and later managing British war loans.

**The Waterloo story**: The most famous (and most embellished) Rothschild legend. Nathan purportedly learned of Wellington's victory before anyone else through his courier network, spread false news of defeat to crash the bond market, then bought massively at the bottom before the real news arrived — making a fortune in a day. The truth is more mundane: Nathan was at the stock exchange, did receive early news, and did buy consols (government bonds) that rose after Waterloo. The "spreading false news of defeat" element is a fabrication from an 1846 pamphlet, not historical record. What is true: the Rothschilds had superior information networks and profited from them. The legend — exaggerated as it is — captures something real about the power of information asymmetry in financial markets.

**What they built**: By the mid-19th century, the Rothschilds were underwriting loans for Britain, France, Austria, Prussia, Russia, Brazil, and other sovereigns. They financed the Suez Canal purchase (lending the British government £4 million overnight in 1875, when Parliament was in recess). They held together the 1890 Baring Crisis — a British bank nearly collapsed from Argentine debt exposure; the Rothschilds organized the bailout. They were, functionally, the world's crisis managers.

**The legacy**: the Rothschilds built the infrastructure of international sovereign debt — and demonstrated that lending to governments at scale was the most durable and profitable business in finance. The descendants of that observation include every major investment bank today.

### The United States — A Country That Kept Refusing Central Banking

The US had a deeply ambivalent relationship with central banking for its first 125 years. This ambivalence was not irrational.

**First Bank of the United States (1791–1811)**: Alexander Hamilton's creation. A private bank with a federal charter, 20% government-owned, 80% private. It held government deposits, issued bank notes, and provided credit. Thomas Jefferson and James Madison opposed it as unconstitutional and as giving too much power to financial interests. When its charter expired in 1811, Congress let it lapse.

**The War of 1812** was partly financed by chaos — the Treasury had no central bank to coordinate borrowing and was nearly insolvent. This forced a reconsideration.

**Second Bank of the United States (1816–1836)**: Recreated after the war. More powerful than the first. Andrew Jackson ran for president explicitly against it — calling it "a den of vipers and thieves" and vowing to kill it. He vetoed its re-charter in 1832. "The bank is trying to kill me, but I will kill it," he reportedly said. He succeeded. The Second Bank's charter expired in 1836.

**What followed**: A period of wildcat banking. States chartered banks freely; many issued their own notes; the currency was chaotic; financial panics were frequent and severe: 1837, 1857, 1873, 1893, 1907. Without a central institution to act as lender of last resort, panics often cascaded into depressions.

The argument for a central bank was practical: the system was unstable without one. The argument against: private bankers would capture and control it. Both were correct. The creation of the Federal Reserve resolved the instability problem by conceding the capture problem.

### JP Morgan — The Private Central Banker

John Pierpont Morgan (1837–1913) was the dominant figure in American finance from roughly 1880 to his death. He reorganized railroads, created US Steel (the first billion-dollar corporation), and by 1913 his partners held directorships in 47 major corporations.

**The Panic of 1907**: A speculative bubble in copper stocks collapsed, triggering a cascade of bank runs across New York. The US had no central bank; there was no lender of last resort. Morgan personally took charge. He sequestered the leaders of New York's major banks in his Madison Avenue library — literally locked the doors — and refused to let them leave until they committed sufficient capital to stop the panic. He organized which banks were salvageable and which should be allowed to fail. He even propped up New York City's government, which could not meet its debt payments. The panic was contained, but it exposed the absurdity of one private citizen bearing the weight of systemic financial stability.

The Pujo Committee (1912–13) investigated the "money trust" — the concentration of financial power. Morgan testified. The data was stark: a small number of interconnected bankers controlled credit allocation for the entire American economy. This wasn't conspiracy; it was the structural result of having no public institution capable of the coordination function.

### Jekyll Island and the Federal Reserve Act (1913)

In November 1910, a group of men boarded a private railway car in New Jersey, using false names. Their destination: Jekyll Island, a private club off the Georgia coast. Their purpose: to draft a central banking bill in secret.

The participants, later disclosed:
- **Nelson Aldrich** — Senate Republican leader, chair of the National Monetary Commission, father-in-law of John D. Rockefeller Jr.
- **Frank Vanderlip** — President of National City Bank (ancestor of Citibank)
- **Henry Davison** — Senior partner at JP Morgan & Co.
- **Charles Norton** — President of First National Bank of New York
- **Benjamin Strong** — VP of Bankers Trust, Morgan protégé (later became first governor of the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, the most powerful Fed position)
- **Paul Warburg** — Partner at Kuhn, Loeb & Co., the foremost expert on European central banking in America

These men represented the dominant American banking houses. They spent nine days drafting what became the Aldrich Plan, the direct precursor to the Federal Reserve Act.

Why the secrecy? Vanderlip later admitted in a 1916 *Saturday Evening Post* article: "If it were to be exposed publicly that our group had gotten together and written a banking bill, that bill would have no chance whatever of passage by Congress, because the Congressmen would have been right in thinking that we were there to protect our own interests."

The Aldrich Plan was too overtly banking-industry-friendly to pass. The Democrats rewrote it as the Federal Reserve Act, maintaining most of the structure but adding the veneer of public oversight. Woodrow Wilson signed it on December 23, 1913.

**What the Federal Reserve actually is**:
- 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks, each owned by the member commercial banks in its district — private ownership
- The Board of Governors in Washington — a federal government agency, members appointed by the President
- The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) — sets monetary policy; dominated in practice by the New York Fed (whose president is chosen by the New York banking community)
- The Fed creates money, sets interest rates, acts as lender of last resort, and regulates major banks

**The Bank of New York's president** — the most powerful unelected economic position in America — was chosen by the largest private banks until 2010 (Dodd-Frank moved the selection to the Board of Governors). Benjamin Strong, the first holder, was Morgan's man. The structural influence of the banking industry on the institution built to oversee it was baked in from day one.

This is not conspiracy — it is documented history. Whether the Fed serves the public interest alongside banker interests is genuinely debated. That it was designed by bankers to manage banking crises in ways that protected the banking system is not debated.

### How Money Is Created — The Mechanics of Debt-Based Money

This is the most important and least understood part of modern banking. The Bank of England published a landmark paper in 2014 — "Money Creation in the Modern Economy" — explicitly stating what most economics textbooks obscure: **commercial banks create money when they make loans.**

When a bank approves a $300,000 mortgage:
1. The bank does not withdraw $300,000 from a vault
2. The bank does not lend out other depositors' money
3. The bank creates a new $300,000 deposit in the borrower's account — money that did not exist before — and simultaneously records a $300,000 loan asset on its books
4. The borrower now has $300,000 in their account; the bank has a $300,000 loan on its books

The money was created in the act of lending. The only requirement is that the bank maintain sufficient capital ratios and has access to central bank reserves to settle interbank payments.

**What this means**:
- The vast majority of money in the economy is bank credit — it is debt
- For every dollar in existence (in deposits, not physical cash), there is a corresponding dollar of debt somewhere
- When loans are repaid, the principal portion of that money is *destroyed* — it ceases to exist
- Only the interest payment remains — and the interest was never created as money

**The interest problem**: If all money enters the economy as debt, and all debt carries interest, then the total interest owed always exceeds the total money supply. The system as a whole cannot pay off all its debts simultaneously. This means: perpetual new debt must be created to service old debt, or some debtors must default. The system requires continuous expansion of credit to survive. This is why central banks target positive inflation, positive GDP growth, and low interest rates — not as arbitrary preferences, but as structural necessities of the debt-money system.

### The Glass-Steagall Era — Constraints After the Crash (1933–1999)

The Great Depression (1929–1933) discredited the unregulated banking model. Banks had used depositors' money to speculate in equities; when the market crashed, the banks failed; depositors lost savings.

**Glass-Steagall Act (1933)**: Separated commercial banking (taking deposits, making loans) from investment banking (underwriting securities, speculating). A bank could do one or the other, not both. This was designed to prevent banks from speculating with depositors' money.

**FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation)**: Created simultaneously. Insured deposits up to a limit — stopping bank runs by guaranteeing deposits regardless of bank failure. This solved the panic problem but created **moral hazard**: if depositors don't care whether a bank is taking risks (because they're insured), banks have less market discipline.

**The era of boring banking (1933–1980)**: Banks took deposits, made loans, operated with restricted profit potential and restricted risk. Financial sector profits were a small fraction of the economy. Mortgages were 30-year fixed-rate loans held on the bank's books. The bank had to live with the risk it created, which disciplined its lending standards.

### The 1980s — When Finance Took Over

The 1980s produced three simultaneous shifts that transformed the American economy from productive to financial.

**The Volcker Shock (1979–1982)**

Paul Volcker became Fed Chair in August 1979 with one mandate: kill inflation, which had reached 13%. His method: raise the federal funds rate to 20%. This was the highest interest rate in US history and caused a severe recession — unemployment hit 10.8% in 1982, the worst since the Great Depression.

It worked. Inflation was broken. But the consequences:
- Dramatically increased the real cost of existing debt — everyone with variable-rate debt saw payments double or triple
- Increased the dollar's value sharply, devastating US manufacturing exporters
- Established the principle that the Fed would prioritize financial stability (low inflation) over employment — a shift in institutional priorities

**Deregulation and the S&L Crisis**

The Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act (1980) and Garn-St. Germain Act (1982) deregulated savings and loan associations (S&Ls) — allowing them to expand from simple home mortgage lending into commercial real estate, junk bonds, and speculative investments, while still maintaining federal deposit insurance.

The result was predictable: S&Ls took enormous risks with insured deposits. When the bets failed in the late 1980s, roughly 1,000 S&Ls collapsed. The taxpayer bailout cost approximately $132 billion (equivalent to ~$350 billion today). The pattern — privatize profits in good times, socialize losses in bad times — was established as a recurring feature.

**Michael Milken and the Junk Bond Revolution**

Michael Milken, working at Drexel Burnham Lambert, created the modern high-yield ("junk") bond market. He made two observations: (1) below-investment-grade bonds were systematically underpriced because institutional investors were prohibited from holding them; (2) a diversified portfolio of junk bonds could produce better risk-adjusted returns than investment-grade bonds.

He was right on both counts. He also used junk bond financing to enable leveraged buyouts — private equity firms could now take over large companies using the target company's own assets as collateral for the debt used to buy them. The company would then be "restructured" (often meaning: assets sold, workers laid off, debt loaded onto the company's balance sheet) to repay the acquisition debt.

The result: corporate America got loaded with debt as a deliberate strategy. The tax code encouraged this — interest payments are tax-deductible, equity returns are not. Debt financed growth was systematically advantaged by the tax system.

Milken was later convicted of securities fraud (1990) and banned from the securities industry. The market he created survived and grew enormously.

**The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999) — The Repeal of Glass-Steagall**

After decades of lobbying, the banking industry succeeded in repealing Glass-Steagall. Commercial banks could now own investment banks; investment banks could take deposits. The separation that had kept speculation away from depositors' money was gone.

What followed: the mortgage securitization explosion, credit default swaps, collateralized debt obligations — the financial instruments that caused the 2008 crisis. The repeal is not the only cause of 2008, but it is a direct enabling condition.

### Perpetual Debt — The System's Design, Not Its Malfunction

The modern economy is designed around perpetual debt service. This is not a conspiracy — it is the structural consequence of debt-based money creation. But it is worth seeing clearly.

**Sovereign debt**: The US government has not run a budget surplus in a sustained way since Clinton's presidency (1998–2001). Every subsequent administration has run deficits — spending more than it collects in taxes, borrowing the difference. The debt never gets paid off; it gets refinanced. Each year, the Treasury issues new bonds to pay off maturing old bonds plus new deficit spending. Interest on the debt now exceeds the entire defense budget. This is not a crisis — it is the system operating normally.

**The mechanism**: The government spends money into the economy. That spending creates income for businesses and workers, who pay taxes, which partially funds the government. The deficit — the gap between spending and taxes — is funded by issuing bonds. The bonds are purchased by banks, foreign governments, and investors. The interest on those bonds is a permanent transfer from taxpayers to bondholders.

**Consumer debt**: The average American household carries mortgage debt, auto loans, student loans, and credit card balances. For most households, a significant portion of monthly income goes to debt service — interest payments that build no equity, produce nothing, and represent a permanent transfer of wealth to creditors.

- A 30-year $400,000 mortgage at 7% costs approximately $955,000 total — $555,000 in interest
- That interest is pure profit for the lender — created as new money when the loan was made, repaid in money earned through actual labor
- The homeowner spent ~55% of their mortgage payments on interest — on money the bank created by typing numbers into a computer

**The credit card as the purest form**: Credit card debt carries 20-29% interest rates. A $5,000 balance at 24% APR with minimum payments takes 17 years to pay off and costs $6,000 in interest — more than the original balance. This is the system's most extractive instrument — targeted at the people with the least financial literacy and the fewest alternatives.

**Quantitative Easing (QE) and the post-2008 world**:

After 2008, the Fed could not lower interest rates below zero (conventional monetary policy). So it invented a new tool: purchasing financial assets (government bonds, mortgage-backed securities) directly — "printing money" in the colloquial sense. This expanded the Fed's balance sheet from ~$900 billion (2008) to ~$9 trillion (2022).

QE's effect: it inflated asset prices — bonds, stocks, real estate — making existing asset-holders wealthier. It did not proportionally raise wages or productive capacity. The decade of near-zero interest rates (ZIRP) discouraged saving and incentivized borrowing — everyone from corporations to governments took on debt at historic rates. When rates rose again (2022–23), the cost of that debt became visible.

The distribution of QE's benefits was not neutral. Those with assets — stocks, real estate — saw wealth increase. Those without assets — whose wealth is primarily their labor income — saw the purchasing power of that income eroded by inflation without the asset appreciation offset.

## Key Considerations

**The system is not designed against you; it is designed without you.** The banking system was designed by bankers to manage banking crises and generate banking profits. It occasionally serves broader public interests and occasionally doesn't. The useful frame is not anger — it's understanding. You are operating inside a system; knowing the rules of that system is the precondition for playing it well.

**Debt has two sides.** The same system that traps people in credit card debt also enables mortgages that let people build equity, small business loans that fund productive enterprise, and government bonds that fund infrastructure. The question is always: is the debt financing something that generates a return greater than the interest cost? Mortgage debt at 7% on an appreciating asset in a 3% inflation environment is different from credit card debt at 24% on consumption.

**Central banks are not omnipotent.** The Fed can set short-term interest rates and influence long-term rates through QE/QT. It cannot directly control inflation caused by supply-side shocks (it can only reduce demand). It cannot prevent recessions. It can prevent acute financial panics. Its tools are blunt and its effects are distributed unevenly.

**The debt cannot be paid off, and that is the plan.** Sovereign debt was never designed to be retired. It is designed to be permanently rolled over, with interest as a perpetual extraction. Understanding this removes the confusion about why governments "never" pay down their debt — they're not supposed to. The interest payments are the product, not the failure.

## Getting Started

1. Read the Bank of England's 2014 paper "Money Creation in the Modern Economy" — free, 14 pages, and the clearest authoritative description of how money is actually created.
2. Audit your own debt. Separate productive debt (mortgage on an appreciating asset, business loan generating returns above interest cost) from extractive debt (credit cards, high-interest consumer loans). Eliminate the extractive debt aggressively.
3. Understand your mortgage amortization table — look at how much of the first years' payments go to interest vs. principal. The structure is not hidden; most people just haven't looked at it.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating the Rothschild mythology as history.** The Rothschilds were real and powerful and genuinely important to financial history. The legend around them — controlling all governments, starting all wars, the Waterloo fabrication — is largely anti-Semitic conspiracy content attached to a real history. Separate the documented from the invented.

**Thinking the Federal Reserve is the government.** The Fed is a hybrid institution — it has federal statutory authority but its operational decisions are made by an independent board and its regional banks are privately owned. The Fed does not take orders from the Treasury, and the government cannot instruct it to print money. The relationship is complicated, not simple.

**Assuming the system can be reformed easily.** Every element of the current banking system — fractional reserve banking, central banking, fiat currency, bond markets — solves a real problem that existed before it was created. Chaotic wildcat banking killed real businesses and real savers. The gold standard produced its own pathologies (deflation, rigidity). There is no frictionless alternative; every system has failure modes.

## Resources

- *The Creature from Jekyll Island* — G. Edward Griffin. The most-read popular account of the Fed's creation. Detailed on the Jekyll Island meeting and the political maneuvering. Read critically — Griffin has ideological commitments (Austrian economics, gold standard advocacy) that color his analysis, but the historical account of the Fed's formation is accurate and well-sourced.
- *The Ascent of Money* — Niall Ferguson. Academic historian's history of financial innovation from Mesopotamian grain loans through modern derivatives. Rigorous, readable, less ideologically committed.
- *Lords of Finance* — Liaquat Ahamed. The four central bankers who mismanaged the Great Depression — a close study of how central bank decisions produce catastrophic real-world consequences. Pulitzer Prize winner.
- "Money Creation in the Modern Economy" — Bank of England, 2014. The authoritative primary source on how banks actually create money. Free PDF.
- *The Big Short* — Michael Lewis. The 2008 crisis told through the people who saw it coming. The most readable account of what financialization looks like when it blows up.
