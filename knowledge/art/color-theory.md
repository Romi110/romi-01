# Color Theory
*The system behind why colors work together — value, temperature, harmony, and mixing*

---

## What Is It

Color theory is the set of principles that explain how colors relate to each other, how they interact visually, and how to use them deliberately. It's not taste or intuition — it's a learnable system with predictable rules.

The overview (color wheel, hue/value/saturation, temperature, harmonies) is introduced in [visual-art-fundamentals.md](visual-art-fundamentals.md). This file goes deeper: how these principles actually work in practice when you're mixing, choosing, or evaluating color.

## Why It Matters

If you ever do any visual work — drawing, painting, photography, video, design — color theory is the difference between choices that feel right and choices you can *explain and repeat*. More practically: understanding value and temperature in color is what makes a painting feel three-dimensional or a palette feel cohesive. It's a system, not a gift.

## Core Concepts

### The Three Properties, Ranked by Importance

Every color has three properties. Most beginners focus on the wrong one.

**Value** (light/dark) — the most important. Value defines form, creates depth, and determines readability. A painting with wrong hues but correct values still reads. A painting with correct hues but wrong values falls apart. When a color looks "off," check its value first — not its hue.

**Saturation** (pure/muted) — the second most important. High saturation looks artificial, decorative, or intense. Low saturation looks realistic, calm, or aged. Most realistic paintings use mostly desaturated colors with small punches of saturation at focal points. The common beginner mistake is using paint or digital colors straight from the tube/picker — these are usually far too saturated.

**Hue** (the name of the color — red, blue, yellow) — the least important of the three. Hue is the most obvious property and gets the most attention, but it does the least structural work. When you can't figure out what's wrong with a color, it's almost never the hue.

**Rule of thumb**: Before changing a color's hue, try adjusting its value. Before changing value, check whether it reads correctly against its neighbors.

### The Color Wheel in Practice

The wheel is a map of hue relationships. Its practical use is predicting **color harmony** — combinations that feel organized — and **mixing** — what results from combining two colors.

**Mixing on the wheel**: Colors adjacent on the wheel mix cleanly (yellow + orange = yellow-orange). Colors opposite mix toward neutral gray/brown (complements neutralize each other). This is why mixing a complement into a color is the way to desaturate it without adding gray — it's more natural-looking.

**Mixing with pigment vs. light**: Pigment (paint, ink) is subtractive — mixing more colors gets darker. Light (screen, projector) is additive — mixing more light gets lighter. In digital art you work additively; in painting you work subtractively. The color wheel is taught in pigment, but the principles transfer.

### Color Temperature

Temperature is one of the most practical tools in color:

- **Warm**: reds, oranges, yellows, warm yellows — feel close, intense, energetic
- **Cool**: blues, blue-greens, purples, cool grays — feel distant, calm, receding

Temperature creates depth: warm colors visually advance; cool colors recede. In a landscape, the foreground tends warm, the sky and distant hills tend cool.

**The core rule for light and shadow**: The light and shadow on any object have opposite temperatures. If your light source is warm (sunlight, candlelight), your shadows are cool. If your light source is cool (overcast sky, fluorescent light), your shadows are warm. Violating this makes the painting feel lifeless.

This is why painting sunlit scenes: sunlight (warm yellow) hits the top of objects; shadows underneath are filled with sky light (cool blue). The interaction of warm lights and cool shadows is a large part of why impressionist paintings feel alive.

**How to apply it**: When mixing a shadow color, don't just add black (which kills the temperature and produces mud). Instead: shift the color cooler (or warmer, if the light is cool) and reduce the value. That produces a shadow that reads as part of the same object but has life in it.

### Color Harmonies

Harmony is a predictable set of hue relationships that feel visually organized. These are not rules — they're starting points.

**Complementary** (opposite on the wheel — blue/orange, red/green, yellow/violet):
- Maximum contrast and vibration
- Use one as dominant (70–80% of the palette), one as accent
- Both colors look more saturated next to each other than they do alone (simultaneous contrast — see below)
- Most commercial design and illustration uses some form of complementary pairing

**Analogous** (adjacent hues — blue, blue-green, green):
- Low tension, cohesive, natural
- Default for nature scenes, calm moods
- Risk: feels monotonous without a temperature contrast or accent

**Triadic** (three equally spaced hues — red/yellow/blue, orange/green/violet):
- High variety, difficult to balance
- Usually requires one dominant hue and two accents
- Used in illustration and character design more than realism

**Split-complementary** (one hue + the two hues flanking its complement — e.g., blue + red-orange + yellow-orange):
- Softer than complementary, more variety than analogous
- A good default harmony for beginners because it's hard to make it look wrong

**Tetradic/square** (four evenly spaced hues): complex, usually reserved for experienced colorists. Easy to produce visual noise.

### Simultaneous Contrast

This is where color theory gets genuinely surprising: the perceived color of any area depends on what surrounds it.

The same mid-gray square looks lighter on a dark background and darker on a light background. The same orange looks more saturated next to blue and more muted next to yellow. A cool gray on a warm background looks bluish; the same gray on a cool background looks yellowish.

**Practical consequence**: You cannot evaluate a color in isolation. You must look at it in context — surrounded by the other colors in your painting or design. What looks right as a swatch may look completely wrong once placed. This is why experienced colorists mix and test colors in context rather than by instinct.

Simultaneous contrast also explains why complementary colors vibrate against each other — each is making the other appear more saturated.

### Value in Color: Thinking in Grayscale First

The most reliable method for building a color painting is to resolve all value decisions first, then add color.

In practice:
1. Establish your composition and value structure in grayscale (pencil sketch or gray-only underpainting)
2. Once values are correct, introduce color — you're only solving for hue and saturation now, not value
3. Constantly squint at your work. Squinting collapses value contrast — if a color jump disappears when you squint, the values are too close

**Notan**: Japanese concept meaning "dark-light" — the abstract pattern of lights and darks in a composition. Strong paintings have strong notan — clear, readable patterns of light and shadow even when viewed as pure black and white. Weak notan is the most common reason a technically detailed painting doesn't read well at a distance.

## Key Considerations

**Mixing black is usually wrong.** Straight black pigment tends to kill the temperature and life of a color. Instead, mix dark colors from complements or deep hues (ultramarine + burnt umber makes a rich dark that can lean warm or cool). This is a painting rule, not a rigid law — but worth understanding.

**Limit your palette.** Beginners buy many colors and use all of them. Professionals often use 5–7 colors and mix everything. A limited palette forces you to learn mixing and produces more cohesive results because all your colors share the same parents.

**Color temperature shifts across a painting.** It's not that shadows are always cool — it's that shadows are the *opposite temperature* of the light. In moonlit paintings, the light is cool and the shadows are warm.

**Screen vs. print color**: If you work digitally, the same file looks different on different screens. Color calibration is a real concern for professional work but not for learning. Just be aware that RGB on screen is additive, and CMYK for print is subtractive.

## Getting Started

1. **Week 1**: Do a limited-palette study. Pick one warm color, one cool color, and white. Paint or color a simple still life using only those three colors mixed in varying ratios. This forces you to mix temperature rather than reach for a new hue.
2. **Week 2**: Value-only painting. Take a reference photo, desaturate it, and try to match the value structure exactly using a single hue at various saturations and values.
3. **Week 3**: Complementary study. Pick a complementary pair (blue/orange is easiest). Build one painting using only those hues in varying values and saturations. Learn how they interact.
4. **Week 4**: Observational color study. Take a photograph of something with interesting light (outdoor sunlight is ideal) and try to identify: what is the temperature of the light? What is the temperature of the shadows? Paint or render it with that contrast exaggerated.

## Common Mistakes

- **Mixing with black to darken.** Usually produces dead, gray shadows. Use cooler or complementary darks instead.
- **Over-saturating.** Pulling colors directly from bright swatches or tube pigments produces cartoon brightness. Most realistic colors are significantly muted.
- **Fixing hue instead of value.** When a color looks wrong, the first thing to check is its value, not its hue. Most "bad color" problems are actually bad value problems.
- **Evaluating colors in isolation.** A color that looks right on a swatch looks wrong in context. Always judge color surrounded by its neighbors.
- **No temperature contrast in shadows.** Adding black to darken a shadow instead of shifting temperature. The result is a painting that feels flat and lifeless.
- **Using every color in the palette.** Too many hues with no unifying temperature or harmony reads as chaotic. Constrain the palette.

## Resources

- *Color and Light* — James Gurney. The definitive practical guide to color in painting. Strong on temperature, light behavior, and how to observe and mix color from life. Not theory for its own sake — every concept is applied to actual paintings.
- *Josef Albers: Interaction of Color* — Albers. The classic on simultaneous contrast and how perception shapes color. More conceptual than practical but will permanently change how you see color.
- *Color Theory for Designers* — Smashing Magazine series (free online). Strong on harmonies and digital application.
