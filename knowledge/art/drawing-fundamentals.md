# Drawing Fundamentals
*The skill of accurate observation translated to paper — line, proportion, value, and perspective*

---

## What Is It

Drawing fundamentals is the deliberate practice of translating what you see (or imagine) onto a flat surface accurately. It's a skill built from five sub-skills: **mark-making**, **proportion**, **value**, **perspective**, and **gesture**. Each is trainable independently.

The overview of elements (line, shape, value, form, space) is covered in [visual-art-fundamentals.md](visual-art-fundamentals.md). This file goes deeper: how to actually train each one.

## Why It Matters

Drawing is the fastest way to externalize a thought — faster than words for spatial ideas. Sketching a layout, a mechanism, a garden plot, or a sequence of events takes seconds and communicates in ways that prose can't. Beyond utility, it trains accurate perception — the same discipline as Stoic attention to what's actually happening vs. your assumptions about it.

## Core Concepts

### Mark-Making: Control Before Content

Before you draw anything, you need physical control over your lines. This is boring and most people skip it. Don't.

**The exercises:**
- **Ghosting**: hover the pencil over the arc or line you want to draw, make the motion several times without touching paper, then execute in one committed stroke. Fixes chicken-scratch lines.
- **Superimposed lines**: draw a straight line freehand, then draw another on top trying to match it exactly. Repeat 20 times. Reveals and fixes your arc tendencies.
- **Ellipses**: draw circles and ellipses at various angles. Almost no one can draw clean ellipses without practice. They appear constantly (cups, wheels, table tops at angles).

Lines should be drawn from the shoulder, not the wrist. Wrist lines are short and wobbly. Shoulder lines are long and controlled.

### Proportion: Seeing Relationships, Not Sizes

Proportion is not about measuring absolute sizes — it's about **relationships**. The head is X units tall; the torso is 3X. The window is half the width of the wall. You're always comparing one thing to another.

**How to train it:**

**Sighting**: Hold your pencil at arm's length, close one eye, and use it as a measuring tool. Align the top of the pencil to the top of your subject; slide your thumb to where the bottom lands. That unit is now your reference. Measure everything else in multiples of it.

**Comparative drawing**: Draw a box the correct proportions of your subject first (is it wider than tall? By how much?). Build inside the box. Most proportion errors happen before the first mark — at the decision of how big to make the first shape.

**The most common proportion error**: making the head too big and everything below too small. When drawing figures, measure the head and use it as a consistent unit. Most adults are 7–7.5 heads tall.

### Value: Where Form Comes From

Covered as a concept in [visual-art-fundamentals.md](visual-art-fundamentals.md) — this is the training method.

**Value scales**: Draw a row of 5–7 boxes. Fill box 1 with the lightest mark possible; fill the last box solid black. Fill the middle boxes in even gradations between. Do this weekly until you can hit a specific value on command.

**The 5-value system**: Simplify any subject into 5 values — white (highlight), light gray (light), mid-gray (halftone), dark gray (shadow), black (core shadow/occlusion). Most successful drawings can be reduced to this. When a drawing looks muddy, it's usually because values are all crowded in the middle range.

**How light hits form**: Understand the standard lighting sequence on a sphere —
1. **Highlight** — where light hits most directly (lightest)
2. **Light** — surrounding lit area
3. **Halftone** — transitional area between light and shadow
4. **Core shadow** — the darkest dark, at the terminator (edge where light stops)
5. **Reflected light** — light bounced back from the ground or surroundings (lighter than core shadow, darker than halftone — beginners make this too bright)
6. **Cast shadow** — shadow the object throws onto a surface; darkest near the object, softens with distance

This sequence applies to every rounded form: a face, a muscle, a rock, an apple.

### Perspective: Consistent Space

Perspective is the system for making flat drawings read as 3D space. There are three modes:

**One-point perspective**: Objects recede to a single vanishing point on the horizon. Used when you're looking straight at a face of a building or room corner head-on. The horizon line is always at eye level — change where you're standing and the horizon moves.

**Two-point perspective**: Two vanishing points on the horizon. Used when you're looking at the corner of a building (the most common real-world view). All vertical lines stay vertical; all horizontal lines converge to one of the two points.

**Three-point perspective**: Third vanishing point above or below. Used for dramatic high/low angles. Verticals also converge. Most drawings don't need this.

**The practical rule**: Identify your horizon line (eye level) first. Everything above it angles down toward it; everything below angles up toward it. Parallel lines in the same direction converge to the same vanishing point.

Architectural drawing demands precision here. For organic subjects (figures, still life, landscapes), a looser intuitive sense of perspective is usually enough — just ensure consistent scale and convergence.

### Gesture: Fluency Before Accuracy

Gesture drawing is fast, loose, and focused on movement and proportion — not detail. A gesture sketch takes 30–90 seconds. The goal is to capture the action, weight, and feel of a pose before committing to anatomy or clothing.

**Why it matters**: Without gesture, drawings are stiff. You can get every proportion right and still have a figure that looks frozen. Gesture is what makes it feel alive.

**How to practice**: Use a timed reference site (Line of Action, Quickposes, SenshiStock). Set timer to 60 seconds. Draw the flow of the spine first — that C or S curve is the gesture. Then the tilt of shoulders vs. hips (counter-balance). Then rough limb placement. That's it.

Do 10 minutes of gesture drawing before any longer study session. It warms up the hand and eye and makes the longer session more productive.

## Key Considerations

**Mileage vs. quality**: You need both. Pure quantity (sketching fast without attention) builds bad habits. Pure refinement (spending 3 hours on one drawing with constant second-guessing) builds anxiety. Alternating fast gesture sessions with slow, focused studies is the correct mix.

**Subject variety**: If you practice only faces, you get better at faces and nothing else. Rotate: still life, figures, architecture, landscapes, hands. Hands in particular are where most people's drawing ability breaks down — they avoid them, which means they never improve.

**Critique your own work**: After finishing a drawing, flip it upside down or mirror it. Errors in proportion and symmetry that your brain corrects for will jump out. Identify the worst part and fix only that. Don't redraw from scratch — targeted correction is faster.

## Getting Started

**Month 1 — Mark control and proportion:**
- Daily: 10 min ghosting lines and ellipses, 10 min contour drawing of household objects
- Weekly: one proportion study — draw any rectangular object and try to match its exact ratio

**Month 2 — Value:**
- Daily: 10 min gesture drawing (60-second poses)
- Weekly: one full value study from a black-and-white photo — no line, only value shapes

**Month 3 — Perspective:**
- Weekly: one one-point and one two-point perspective sketch of a room or street corner from observation
- Daily: continue gesture drawing; add 1–2 longer figure studies (10–20 min each)

Total: 20–30 min daily. This is sustainable.

## Common Mistakes

- **Drawing from the wrist.** Produces short, scratchy, unconfident lines. Use shoulder for long lines, elbow for medium, wrist only for tiny details.
- **Not committing to lines.** Hairy, overworked lines from repeated light strokes. Ghost the motion, then execute once.
- **Shading without understanding light.** Random scribbling to make things "darker" without thinking about where the light source is. Draw your light source first — even a quick arrow — before shading.
- **Reflected light too bright.** The most common value error. Reflected light is lighter than core shadow but always darker than the halftone. If it looks like another highlight, dial it down.
- **Skipping gesture.** Going straight to detail without first establishing the big shapes and proportions. Build from large to small, always.
- **Avoiding difficult subjects.** You will never get better at hands by drawing only faces. Seek out what you can't draw yet.

## Resources

- *Drawing on the Right Side of the Brain* — Betty Edwards. The contour and negative space methods. Foundational.
- *Keys to Drawing* — Bert Dodson. More practical and less theoretical than Edwards; good on specific techniques for value and proportion.
- *Drawabox* (drawabox.com) — Free structured curriculum. Starts with lines and boxes and builds systematically. No shortcuts, no fluff.
- *Proko* (YouTube) — Best video resource for figure drawing, proportion, and anatomy. Stan Prokopenko teaches clearly and with humor.
