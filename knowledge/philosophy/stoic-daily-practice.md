# Stoic Daily Practice
*The actual tools — not the theory, not the quotes, the practice you run every day*

---

## What Is It

Stoicism without daily practice is just reading. The Stoics themselves were clear: the philosophy is a training regimen, not a belief system. Marcus Aurelius wrote Meditations as a daily workout — reminders to himself, not essays for posterity. Epictetus structured his teaching around *askesis* (deliberate practice). Seneca wrote letters to himself as much as to others.

This file covers the concrete practices — what to do, when to do it, and how long it actually takes. The core concepts (dichotomy of control, four virtues, memento mori) are in [stoicism.md](stoicism.md). This file assumes you've read that. The goal here is execution.

## Why It Matters

Knowing Stoic ideas doesn't make you Stoic. You can read every word Marcus Aurelius wrote and still panic when the project blows up, still waste hours in resentment, still get dragged around by other people's reactions. The gap between knowing and being is closed only by practice — specifically, by doing the exercises repeatedly until they become your default response.

For Romi: this connects directly to the self-reliance goal. The person who can regulate their own mind under pressure doesn't need external circumstances to be favorable. That's a skill worth building.

## Core Concepts

### The Morning Review

Done before the day starts — before email, before the phone, before conversation. 5–10 minutes.

**Purpose**: Set the frame for the day. Identify what's in your control. Pre-load your mind with your values before the world loads it with its agenda.

**What to actually do**:
1. Ask: *What do I want to accomplish today that is fully within my control?* Not outcomes — actions. "Finish the feature spec" is partly outside your control. "Write for 2 focused hours on the feature spec" is not.
2. Ask: *What difficulties are likely today?* Not to dread them — to name them. The commute, the difficult meeting, the temptation to skip training. Name the thing, decide your response in advance.
3. Optional: Read one passage from Meditations or the Enchiridion. One paragraph is enough. The point is to activate the mindset, not to study.

**Time investment**: 5–10 minutes. Can be done while coffee is brewing.

### The Evening Reflection

Done at the end of the day — before sleep, after the noise settles. 10–15 minutes.

Seneca described this in "On Anger": "I examine my entire day and go back over what I've done and said, hiding nothing from myself, passing nothing by." This isn't self-punishment. It's the feedback loop that makes tomorrow better.

**Three questions** (Seneca's original framework, adapted):
1. *What did I do well today?* — Name something specific. This builds self-knowledge of your strengths.
2. *Where did I react instead of respond?* — Lost patience, said something reactive, avoided something hard. Name it without judgment.
3. *What would I do differently?* — One concrete change for tomorrow. Not a vague resolution — a specific action.

**What to do with it**: Write it. A few sentences in a notebook or file is enough. Writing forces precision. Thinking in your head lets you skip the uncomfortable parts.

**Time investment**: 10–15 minutes. Does not need to be long to be effective.

### Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Malorum)

The Stoic practice of deliberately imagining loss — of health, relationships, work, possessions — not to create anxiety but to dissolve the numbness that comes from taking things for granted.

**How to practice**:
- Once a week, take 5 minutes to imagine losing something you value. Your partner. Your health. Your job. Your morning training routine.
- Don't just think about it abstractly — feel the loss concretely. What would the day look like without this? What would you miss?
- Then return to the present. That thing is still here. That's the point.

**Why it works**: Hedonic adaptation — the brain normalizes good things quickly. You stop noticing what's good until it's gone. Negative visualization is the circuit breaker. It reactivates gratitude by temporarily removing it.

This is also how you rehearse for adversity. Marcus Aurelius used premeditatio malorum constantly — not because he expected disaster, but because men who have rehearsed setbacks are not broken by them.

**Frequency**: Weekly is plenty. Daily negative visualization can tip into anxiety. Weekly keeps it functional.

### Memento Mori

*Remember that you will die.*

Not a morbid exercise — a prioritization tool. When you hold your mortality in mind, the question "does this matter?" becomes easier to answer. The petty argument, the status game, the thing you've been procrastinating — the frame of death clarifies them.

**How to use it**:
- When you catch yourself in a prolonged low-value activity (doomscrolling, passive entertainment, circular worry) — use memento mori as a reset. *I have a finite number of days. Is this how I want to spend one?*
- Not as guilt — as redirection. The point isn't to feel bad about wasting time; it's to choose differently now.
- Marcus Aurelius used this constantly. "Think of how many have lived and died before you. Think of how many will after. None of them matter more or less than you."

**A practical form**: Some people keep a small physical object as a reminder — a coin, a stone, something in a pocket. The physical cue triggers the thought. This is optional but surprisingly effective.

### The View From Above

A visualization practice: mentally zoom out from your current problem to the city, then the country, then the planet, then the void of space. Look back down at your specific situation.

**When to use it**: Acute stress. Before reacting to something that feels enormous. After receiving bad news.

**Why it works**: Context collapse. The thing that felt catastrophic at desk-level looks different at planetary scale. This is not about minimizing your problems — it's about accurate perception. Most things that feel career-ending or relationship-ending or life-ruining are not. The view from above helps you see that in the moment, not just in retrospect.

Marcus Aurelius recommended this practice explicitly in Meditations. It appears in modern cognitive behavioral therapy under "cognitive defusion" and "decentering." Same mechanism, different vocabulary.

### The Obstacle Practice (Amor Fati)

The active form of "love of fate" — when something goes wrong, the immediate question is not "why did this happen to me" but "what does this give me to work with?"

**How to build it as a habit**:
1. When an obstacle appears, pause before responding.
2. Ask: *What can I do with this, given that it's now real and can't be undone?*
3. Name one action available to you that is within your control.

This doesn't mean pretending bad things are good. It means extracting what's useful from every situation instead of spending energy on what can't be changed.

Over time, this practice changes your baseline response to difficulty. It stops being a conscious exercise and starts being how you think.

## Key Considerations

**Consistency over intensity.** A 5-minute morning review every day for a year outperforms an hour-long Stoic deep-dive session once a month. The practices are designed to be brief because they're designed to be daily.

**Don't perform it, do it.** The point of journaling is honest self-examination, not a document to be proud of. If you're writing what sounds good rather than what's true, the practice isn't working.

**The practices reinforce each other.** Negative visualization makes memento mori land differently. The morning review gives you material for the evening reflection. The view from above makes the obstacle practice easier to access. Running them together multiplies the effect.

**Expect a lag.** You won't notice changes in week one. You might notice them after 60–90 days. The Stoics trained for years, not weeks. This is a lifetime practice, not a 30-day challenge.

## Getting Started

The smallest viable version: pick one practice only. Start with the evening reflection — three questions, written down, 10 minutes before sleep. Do that for 30 days before adding anything else. Adding all five practices at once is how you end up doing none of them.

After 30 days: add the morning review.
After 60 days: add negative visualization once a week.
The rest will integrate naturally from there.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating reading as practice.** Reading Meditations is preparation, not the practice itself. Knowing what Marcus Aurelius said is not the same as training what he trained.

**Skipping the writing.** The evening reflection done only in your head allows you to be dishonest with yourself without noticing. Write it.

**Over-engineering the system.** A plain notebook works. An Obsidian vault with linked templates is optional and often becomes the obstacle. Don't let the system become a hobby in itself.

**Negative visualization as anxiety.** If the practice leaves you feeling dread rather than gratitude, you're dwelling rather than exercising. The purpose is to appreciate, not to worry. Keep it brief and intentional.

**Using memento mori as a weapon against yourself.** "I'm wasting my life" is not the intended frame. The frame is: *I have time now — use it well.*

## Resources

- *Meditations* — Marcus Aurelius (Gregory Hays translation). The primary text. Read it slowly, one passage at a time, not as a narrative. The Hays translation is the most readable modern version.
- *How to Think Like a Roman Emperor* — Donald Robertson. The most practical modern guide to Stoic practice. Robertson is a cognitive therapist who maps the ancient practices to CBT techniques — useful for understanding why they work.
- *A Guide to the Good Life* — William Irvine. Focuses specifically on the practical application of Stoic exercises. More accessible than Meditations as a starting point if you want the how-to before the primary text.
