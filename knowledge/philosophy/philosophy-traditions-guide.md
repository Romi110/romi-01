# Philosophy Traditions Guide
*How every major school of thought maps to the 7 Core Tenets of Wisdom*

> Reference companion to `core_tenets_of_wisdom.md`.
> For each tradition: what era, what culture, and how it handles each tenet — where it agrees, where it breaks from the others, and what makes it distinct.

---

## How to Read This

The 7 tenets are abbreviated throughout:
1. **T1** — The Mind Is the Source of Most Suffering
2. **T2** — Attachment Is Dangerous
3. **T3** — You Are Not as Separate as You Think
4. **T4** — The Present Moment Is All You Actually Have
5. **T5** — Virtue and Action Matter More Than Belief
6. **T6** — Meaning Requires Engagement, Not Discovery
7. **T7** — Ego Is the Primary Obstacle

Alignment is marked: ✅ Strong | ⚠️ Partial / reframed | ❌ Rejects or inverts

## Part 1 — The Schools by Era and Culture

---

### Ancient East (600–200 BCE)

---

#### Buddhism — India, ~500 BCE (Siddhartha Gautama)

The single most tenet-aligned tradition. The Four Noble Truths are almost a direct encoding of T1–T2: life involves suffering (*dukkha*), suffering arises from craving (*tanha*), the craving can end, and there is a path to end it. The Eightfold Path is pure T5 — a behavioral prescription, not a belief system.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ Central. *Dukkha* is the thesis; mind-training (meditation) is the response. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ The doctrine. *Tanha* (craving/clinging) is THE cause of suffering. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ *Anatta* (no-self) + *Pratītyasamutpāda* (dependent origination) — nothing exists independently. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ *Sati* (mindfulness) is moment-to-moment awareness as a formal practice. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ The Eightfold Path: right speech, right action, right livelihood. Practice is everything. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ⚠️ Buddhism is less about "meaning" and more about liberation from the suffering that the search for meaning creates. The engagement is with the path itself. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ The self is the illusion. *Ahamkara* (I-making) is the root distortion. |

**Unique position:** Buddhism is the only tradition that makes no-self (*anatta*) a metaphysical claim — not just "ego gets in the way" but "the self as a fixed entity does not exist at all." This is stronger than Stoicism's "manage your reactions" and more radical than Taoism's "go with the flow."

---

#### Taoism — China, ~600–300 BCE (Laozi, Zhuangzi)

Taoism resists being pinned down — the first line of the Tao Te Ching says "the Tao that can be named is not the eternal Tao." It is less a philosophy in the Western sense and more a way of perceiving. Its core concept, *wu wei* (effortless action, non-forcing), is a direct answer to T2 and T7: grasping and striving are the problem; flow and release are the solution.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ Resistance to the natural flow (*Tao*) is the source of distress. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ Forcing outcomes is the error. Let go of preference and follow the flow. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ All things arise from and return to the Tao — the undivided source. Separation is illusion. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ *Wu wei* only exists in the now. You cannot plan effortlessness into the future. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ⚠️ Partial. Taoism values action but specifically *non-striving* action. Deliberate practice feels too forceful for a strict Taoist. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ⚠️ Taoism would say meaning is not a problem to be solved — it arises naturally when you stop fighting. Passive in flavor, though not passive in practice. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ The ego that forces, strives, and competes is the core problem. The sage has no fixed agenda. |

**Unique position:** Taoism is the most *anti-effort* of all traditions. Where Stoicism says "train yourself," Taoism says "stop trying so hard." This creates real tension with T5 and T6 — both of which emphasize deliberate engagement. A Taoist would say the Stoic's discipline is still a subtle form of control and grasping.

---

#### Confucianism — China, ~500 BCE (Confucius, Mencius)

The most socially-oriented tradition. Where Buddhism and Taoism tend inward, Confucianism turns outward — toward relationships, ritual, social roles, and the cultivation of virtue through how you treat others. It is practically indifferent to metaphysics and entirely focused on T5.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ⚠️ Not the primary focus. Confucianism is about right conduct, not inner mental states. |
| T2 — Attachment | ⚠️ Confucianism doesn't warn strongly against attachment — it depends on the thing. Attachment to family, duty, and virtue is explicitly *good*. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ Humans are fundamentally relational (*ren*, humaneness, is literally "person + other person"). You cannot be virtuous alone. |
| T4 — Present moment | ⚠️ Less emphasized. Ritual (*li*) and ancestral veneration involve deep engagement with the past as morally formative. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ The *junzi* (exemplary person) is defined entirely by conduct, not creed. Virtue is the habit of right action in relationships. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅ Strong. Meaning comes from full participation in family, community, and the cultivation of character over a lifetime. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ Self-aggrandizement, face-saving, and status-seeking undermine *ren*. But Confucianism has less interest in dissolving the self than in *disciplining* it into proper conduct. |

**Unique position:** Confucianism is the only major tradition that frames virtue primarily through *relationships and social roles*, not through the individual's inner state. A Confucian is a good son, a good friend, a good official — virtue is performed in context, not achieved in solitude. This is a meaningful divergence from Buddhism and Stoicism, both of which emphasize the individual's internal work.

---

#### Vedanta / Upanishadic Hinduism — India, ~800–200 BCE

The Upanishads represent a radical metaphysical claim: *Atman is Brahman* — the individual self is identical with universal consciousness. This is the strongest version of T3 in existence. Unlike Buddhism, which says the self doesn't exist, Vedanta says the self is everything.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ *Avidya* (ignorance) about the true nature of the self is the root of all suffering. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ Attachment to the *unreal* (the ego-self, material things) causes suffering. The Bhagavad Gita gives this its most famous expression: act without attachment to outcome. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅✅ The most extreme version: no separation exists at the deepest level. *Atman = Brahman.* |
| T4 — Present moment | ⚠️ Less central than in Buddhism. Vedanta is more concerned with the nature of consciousness than with moment-to-moment awareness. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ The Bhagavad Gita's core teaching (*karma yoga*): act fully, with skill, without attachment to the fruit. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅ *Dharma* (duty, right action in your role) is the source of meaning — not discovery but fulfillment of your nature. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ The *ego-self* (small self) mistakes itself for the whole. Liberation (*moksha*) is recognizing the ego as the illusion. |

**Unique position:** Vedanta is the most metaphysically ambitious. It doesn't just say "ego is a problem" — it says the individual self is literally a misidentification. The solution is not ego management but *recognition* of what you actually are. This makes it less practical than Stoicism or Buddhism in daily life, but it provides the deepest philosophical foundation for T3.

---

### Ancient West (600 BCE–200 CE)

---

#### Stoicism — Greece/Rome, ~300 BCE–200 CE (Zeno, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, Seneca)

The most practically accessible tenet-aligned tradition in the Western canon. Built around the *dichotomy of control* — some things are "up to us" (our judgments, desires, actions) and some are not (everything external). All suffering comes from misidentifying what's in your control.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ The foundation. It's not events, it's your *judgment* of events (*hegemonikon*). |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ *Preferred indifferents*: health, wealth, relationships worth pursuing, but not worth your peace if lost. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ *Cosmopolitanism*: we are all citizens of the same rational universe (*logos*). Strong communal ethics. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ *Memento mori*, *amor fati* — practices designed to collapse past/future anxiety back into the present. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ *Askesis*: deliberate daily practice. Marcus Aurelius journaled every morning to realign behavior with principles. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅ Meaning comes from fulfilling your role fully — as a human, as a citizen, as whatever you are, right now. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ The *passions* (irrational reactions from false judgments) are the ego in Stoic terms. The goal is the *sage*. |

**Unique position:** Stoicism is the most *actionable* tradition for modern life. It gives concrete daily techniques (journaling, negative visualization, the view from above) and doesn't require metaphysical belief. Where Buddhism asks you to sit in meditation, Stoicism asks you to examine your reactions while doing everything else. It is philosophy-as-engineering.

---

#### Epicureanism — Greece, ~300 BCE (Epicurus)

Widely misunderstood as hedonism. Epicurus's actual teaching was almost the opposite of its reputation: the path to happiness is *subtracting* unnecessary desires, not maximizing pleasure. *Ataraxia* (tranquility) and *aponia* (freedom from pain) were the goals — a quiet, stable contentment, not sensory excess.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ Fear — especially fear of death and divine punishment — is the primary source of unnecessary suffering. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ Prune desires aggressively. Natural and necessary desires (food, shelter, friendship) are fine. Vain desires (fame, luxury, power) are traps. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ⚠️ Epicurus retreated from public life into small communities of friends (*the Garden*). Connection valued, but civic engagement deprioritized. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ Simple present pleasures — conversation, bread, water, friendship — are sufficient. Don't borrow suffering from the future. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ⚠️ More philosophical/contemplative than action-oriented. Epicurus valued *withdrawal* from political life, which is a departure from Aristotle and Confucianism. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ⚠️ Meaning comes from simplicity and friendship, not ambitious engagement. Lower ambition than Aristotle or the Stoics. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ The ego that chases status, fame, and wealth is the source of anxiety. Let it go. |

**Where Epicurus diverges from Stoicism:** Both agree on pruning desires and managing the mind — but Stoics stayed in public life (Marcus Aurelius ran an empire), while Epicurus recommended withdrawal. For Stoics, virtue means engaging with the world under any circumstances. For Epicureans, the wise move is to disengage from most of it.

---

#### Aristotelianism — Greece, ~350 BCE (Aristotle)

The most systematically *practical* ancient Western philosophy. Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* is the original manual for human flourishing — grounded in the idea that humans have a *function* (*telos*) and that the good life is exercising your distinctly human capacities to their fullest.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ⚠️ Aristotle takes external goods more seriously than Stoics or Buddhists — poverty and illness genuinely impede flourishing. Not just a mind problem. |
| T2 — Attachment | ⚠️ Aristotle doesn't advocate detachment. Friendship, family, civic life are *constituents* of the good life, not nice-to-haves. Some attachment is correct. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ *Zōon politikon* — humans are political animals. You cannot flourish without community. This is social interconnection, not metaphysical. |
| T4 — Present moment | ⚠️ Less emphasis. *Eudaimonia* (flourishing) is a feature of an entire life, not a moment. Character built over decades, not a present-moment insight. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ The clearest statement of T5 in Western philosophy: virtue is habit (*ethos* → *ethikos*). You become what you repeatedly do. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅✅ *Eudaimonia* is literally the active exercise of human capacities. Meaning IS engagement — the full use of your reason, relationships, and civic participation. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ⚠️ Aristotle values proper pride (*megalopsychia* — greatness of soul). A man who undersells himself is as vicious as one who is arrogant. Ego management is calibration, not dissolution. |

**Where Aristotle diverges from Eastern traditions:** He takes the external world seriously. Poverty, illness, and bad luck genuinely harm flourishing — not just because of how you interpret them, but in themselves. This is a real break with Stoicism, Buddhism, and Vedanta, which locate the solution entirely inside the mind.

---

#### Platonism — Greece, ~400 BCE (Plato)

Plato's philosophy is built on a split between the world of appearances (*phenomena*) and the world of true Forms (*noumena*). The material world is a shadow; real knowledge is of the eternal, unchanging Forms (Truth, Beauty, Justice, the Good).

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ Ignorance of the Good is the root cause of wrong action and suffering. Philosophy (the examined life) is the cure. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ The *Phaedo*: attachment to the body and material things obscures the soul's access to truth. Philosophy is practice for death (releasing attachments). |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ The soul participates in the universal Forms — a metaphysical interconnection. But less relational than Confucianism or social than Aristotle. |
| T4 — Present moment | ❌ Plato is the least present-focused tradition. His entire direction is *away* from the temporal and toward the eternal. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ⚠️ Knowing the Good will lead to good action — *akrasia* (acting against knowledge) troubled him deeply. More belief-oriented than action-first. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ⚠️ Meaning is *discovered* — the philosopher ascends to truth that already exists. This is the one tradition where meaning genuinely precedes engagement. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ The appetitive part of the soul (desires, status, fear) must be subordinated to reason. The soul has a right order. |

**Unique divergence:** Plato is one of the few traditions that treats meaning as *discovered*, not made. The Forms exist independently of human engagement. This puts him at odds with Existentialism and Pragmatism on T6 — and makes him the ancestor of religious traditions that similarly locate ultimate meaning outside the human.

---

#### Cynicism — Greece, ~400 BCE (Diogenes of Sinope)

The most extreme anti-ego, anti-convention tradition. Cynics rejected *everything* social: status, property, reputation, custom, comfort. Diogenes lived in a barrel, urinated in public, and told Alexander the Great to get out of his sunlight. The point was to demonstrate that virtue is the only good and everything else is *indifferent*.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ Social convention, status-anxiety, and false values create suffering — strip them away. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅✅ The most radical detachment in the Western tradition. Diogenes had literally nothing. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ⚠️ Diogenes called himself a "citizen of the world" (*kosmopolites*) — the first cosmopolitan — but his practice was radical isolation. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ You can't worry about tomorrow if you own nothing and plan nothing. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ Cynicism is pure demonstration. Diogenes didn't write — he *performed* philosophy. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅ Meaning comes from shamelessness and authenticity, not social approval. Engaged with the world through provocation, not withdrawal. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅✅ The most thoroughgoing anti-ego tradition in the West. Every social prop that feeds ego was deliberately destroyed. |

**Unique position:** Cynicism takes T2 and T7 to their logical extreme — not as philosophy but as performance art. Where Stoics manage reactions internally, Cynics eliminate external conditions. Too radical to be practical, but it was the direct ancestor of Stoicism and planted the seed for Diogenes Laertius's formulation: "only virtue is good."

---

### Medieval and Religious (~200–1500 CE)

---

#### Christian Philosophy — Augustine (~400 CE), Aquinas (~1250 CE)

Christianity's philosophical tradition is diverse, but its core commitments on the tenets are relatively consistent. The key move is that the source of suffering, the solution to attachment, and the ground of meaning all converge on *God*.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ *Sin* distorts the mind's capacity to see clearly. Suffering stems from the will turned away from God (*Augustine's restless heart*). |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ Attachment to worldly things over God is *idolatry*. Augustine: "Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless until it repose in Thee." |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ The body of Christ — interconnection through love of neighbor as theological imperative. Agape (unconditional love) is the relational fabric. |
| T4 — Present moment | ⚠️ Christian ethics involves present action, but meaning points forward to eternity. Salvation is a future-oriented frame. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ⚠️ Split: faith (*sola fide* in Protestantism) vs. faith + works (Catholicism via Aquinas). Aquinas aligns with T5; Calvin diverges sharply. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ❌ Meaning is *given* by God — discovered, not created. Christianity is the clearest opposite of Existentialism on this tenet. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ Pride (*superbia*) is the root of all sin in Aquinas's seven deadlies. Humility is the antidote. |

**Where Christianity diverges sharply:** T6. Meaning is not your responsibility to create — it is given by divine design, and your job is to align with it. This is the polar opposite of Camus and Sartre. The metaphysical scaffolding also differs entirely from Buddhist or Stoic traditions, even where the behavioral prescriptions overlap.

---

#### Islamic Philosophy / Sufism — ~800–1300 CE (Al-Ghazali, Ibn Rushd, Rumi)

Islamic philosophy has two streams that relate differently to the tenets: the rational-philosophical tradition (Al-Kindi, Averroes) and the mystical tradition (Sufism). Sufism — particularly Rumi — aligns with the tenets most strongly.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ The *nafs* (lower self) — desire, ego, reactivity — distorts perception. Sufi path is its purification. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ Sufi: attachment to anything other than God is the core problem. *Fana* (annihilation of self) is the goal. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ Sufi mysticism: the soul yearns to return to God, its origin. Rumi's reed flute is the soul separated from the reed bed — longing for reunion. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ Sufi *dhikr* (remembrance of God) is a present-moment practice — continuous attention. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ Islamic jurisprudence (Sharia) is highly action-oriented. The *Five Pillars* are behavioral. Al-Ghazali: spiritual states must be enacted, not just believed. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ⚠️ Meaning is given by God but realized through the *spiritual journey*. Engagement is required, but toward a pre-given end. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅✅ Sufi concept of *nafs* is perhaps the most nuanced ego taxonomy in any tradition — seven stages from the commanding self to the purified self. |

**Unique position:** The Sufi tradition offers the most psychologically sophisticated model of ego in the medieval world. Al-Ghazali's *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences) maps ego states with a granularity that matches modern psychology. Rumi's poetry gives T3 its most emotionally resonant expression.

---

### Early Modern West (1500–1800 CE)

---

#### Rationalism — ~1600–1700 CE (Descartes, Spinoza, Leibniz)

The rationalists are primarily epistemologists — concerned with how we know, not how we live. But Spinoza is the significant exception: his *Ethics* is a comprehensive system grounded in reason that produces tenet-relevant conclusions.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ Spinoza: emotions (*affects*) arise from inadequate ideas. The more you understand the causes of your emotions, the less they control you. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅ Spinoza: striving (*conatus*) is natural, but attachment to contingent goods causes suffering. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ Spinoza's pantheism: God and Nature are the same thing (*Deus sive Natura*). All things are modes of the one substance. Radical interconnection. |
| T4 — Present moment | ⚠️ Rationalists are not particularly present-focused. Understanding sub specie aeternitatis (under the aspect of eternity) is more Spinoza's aim. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ⚠️ Rationalists prioritize understanding. Action follows from correct understanding, not the other way around. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ⚠️ For Spinoza, meaning comes from understanding your place in the whole. Less engagement-driven than Aristotle. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ Spinoza: the illusion of being a free, separate self (rather than a mode of the one substance) is the root confusion. |

---

#### Kantian Ethics — ~1780 CE (Immanuel Kant)

Kant doesn't fit neatly into the tenet framework — his ethics is about *duty* and *rational law*, not suffering and ego. But he's too influential to skip.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ The *phenomenal* world (what we experience) is constructed by the mind. All experience is filtered through mental categories. |
| T2 — Attachment | ⚠️ Kant's morality is independent of outcomes and desires entirely. You act from duty, not from what you're attached to. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ The *categorical imperative* (act only as you could universalize) requires treating all rational beings as ends, never mere means. Radical moral equality. |
| T4 — Present moment | ❌ Not addressed. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅ The moral worth of an action depends on whether it's done from duty — the will behind the act, not its consequences. Action is central, but the emphasis is on *why* you act. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ⚠️ Meaning comes from being a rational, autonomous agent living according to self-given moral law. Engagement, yes, but tightly framed. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ Acting from *inclination* (what you want) rather than *duty* (what reason demands) is the failure mode. |

---

### Modern and Contemporary (1800 CE–present)

---

#### Existentialism — ~1840–1960 CE (Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Sartre, Camus, Heidegger)

The tradition most directly concerned with T6 — and the one that inverts T6's typical framing: meaning is not out there to be found; *existence precedes essence*. You exist first, then define yourself through choices.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ Sartre: bad faith (*mauvaise foi*) — lying to yourself about your freedom — is the source of inauthenticity and suffering. |
| T2 — Attachment | ⚠️ Existentialism emphasizes *commitment* — which requires something like attachment. The antidote to absurdity is passionate engagement, not detachment. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ⚠️ Split. Sartre: "Hell is other people" — others threaten your freedom. But Camus's absurd hero acts in solidarity with others. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ Heidegger: *Dasein* (being-there) — existence is always in the present, thrown forward toward death. Confronting finitude forces presence. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅✅ The most action-centric modern tradition. You are what you do, not what you intend or believe. "Existence precedes essence" means your choices *constitute* you. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅✅ The defining tenet of Existentialism. Meaning is created through commitment, revolt (Camus), or authentic choice (Sartre). It is never given. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅ Bad faith is the ego protecting itself from the terror of freedom. Authenticity requires dismantling those defenses. |

**Where Existentialism diverges:** On T2, most existentialists are skeptical of detachment — Camus calls for *revolt*, which requires passion. Sartre demands commitment. This is the inverse of Buddhist or Taoist detachment. The existentialist stays fully attached and engages anyway, knowing it's absurd. This is not indifference — it's defiance.

---

#### Nietzsche — ~1880 CE

Nietzsche deserves separate treatment. He's often misread as the opposite of the tenets — glorifying ego, power, and will. But his actual project is more subtle: a critique of *reactive* values (resentment, weakness dressed as virtue) in favor of *active* values (creation, affirmation, self-overcoming).

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ *Ressentiment* (reactive suffering, grudges against the strong) is self-poisoning. The *will to power* properly understood is self-mastery. |
| T2 — Attachment | ⚠️ *Amor fati* (love of fate) — embrace what happens fully, don't resist or wish it otherwise. Overlaps with Stoic attachment teachings. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ❌ Nietzsche is the least interconnection-friendly tradition. The *Übermensch* (overman) is explicitly about individual self-overcoming. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ Eternal recurrence thought experiment: if you had to live this moment infinitely, would you choose it? Forces radical presence and affirmation. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅✅ "God is dead" means no external authority can define values. You must act from your own self-created values — or you're just following the herd. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅✅ Meaning is created through creative, active engagement with life. The *Übermensch* creates values; the herd waits to receive them. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ⚠️ Nietzsche targets the *reactive* ego (resentment, weakness) but celebrates a kind of robust, affirmative self-assertion. More ambiguous here than other traditions. |

---

#### Pragmatism — ~1880–1940 CE (William James, John Dewey, Charles Peirce)

American philosophy. Its core claim: the truth of an idea is determined by its consequences — does it work, does it make a difference in practice? This is the most empirical philosophical tradition and the one most comfortable with uncertainty.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ James: beliefs shape perception and experience — choosing to act *as if* something is true affects your reality. |
| T2 — Attachment | ⚠️ Pragmatism doesn't make metaphysical claims about attachment. Dewey: commit to what works; let go of what doesn't. Functional rather than spiritual. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ Dewey: the individual and the community are inseparable. Democracy is an ethical project of mutual growth. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅ Peirce, James: truth is what works *now*, in this context. Highly present-focused epistemology. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅✅ The defining feature: ideas are instruments for action. A belief that changes nothing is irrelevant. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅✅ Dewey: experience and growth are the only meaning available. Engage with life as a continuous experiment. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ⚠️ Less explicit. Dewey is more concerned with social habits than ego dissolution. |

---

#### Zen Buddhism — Japan, ~1200 CE (from Chinese Chan, ~600 CE)

Zen strips Buddhism down to its bare minimum and makes practice the whole point. No doctrine, no metaphysics, just sitting (*zazen*), *koans* (paradoxical questions), and direct transmission from teacher to student. It has influenced modern psychology, martial arts, and creative practice more than any other Eastern tradition.

| Tenet | Stance |
|-------|--------|
| T1 — Mind as source | ✅ The ordinary, conceptualizing, grasping mind is the problem. *Satori* (sudden awakening) is a break from it. |
| T2 — Attachment | ✅✅ Zen is the most radical application: cut through all attachment to concepts, words, teachers, outcomes, even enlightenment itself. |
| T3 — Interconnection | ✅ No separation between self and world — *mu* (nothingness/emptiness) points at this directly. |
| T4 — Present moment | ✅✅ *Shunryu Suzuki*: "In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, in the expert's mind there are few." Full presence, always. |
| T5 — Action over belief | ✅✅ Zen is anti-doctrine. The raft (teachings) is for crossing the river — once across, you leave it behind. Practice IS the teaching. |
| T6 — Meaning via engagement | ✅ Every action — chopping wood, carrying water, washing bowls — is the meaning. No transcendent purpose needed. |
| T7 — Ego as obstacle | ✅✅ Zen cuts through ego concepts directly with koans that the thinking/ego mind cannot solve. The point is to exhaust the defended self. |

## Part 2 — Tenet-by-Tenet Comparison

### T1 — The Mind Is the Source of Most Suffering

**Universal agreement:** Every tradition locates the *primary* lever of suffering in the mind — not events, not other people, not fate. Stoics, Buddhists, Vedantists, Sufis, Spinoza, and Sartre all agree on this.

**Where they diverge:**
- **How much weight to give external circumstances.** Aristotle is the outlier — he grants that poverty, illness, and bad luck *genuinely* harm flourishing, not just your interpretation of them. Buddhism and Stoicism are more radical: a Buddha or Stoic sage can be tortured and remain unperturbed. Aristotle would say that's asking too much.
- **The mechanism.** Stoics say it's false *judgments*. Buddhists say it's *craving and attachment*. Vedanta says it's *ignorance of the true self*. Sartre says it's *bad faith*. All arrive at the same place through different diagnoses.

---

### T2 — Attachment Is Dangerous

**Universal agreement:** Every tradition warns against attaching your inner peace to something outside your control.

**Where they diverge:**
- **How radical should detachment be?** Buddhism (*anatta*, no-self) and Vedanta go furthest. Cynicism and Sufi *fana* (annihilation) go to the practical extreme. Stoics offer the moderate version: *preferred indifferents* — pursue things, just hold them loosely. Aristotle goes furthest in the other direction: some attachments (deep friendship, civic life) are *constitutive* of flourishing, not obstacles to it.
- **The role of commitment.** Existentialism is the provocative outlier: Camus and Sartre argue you should commit fully and passionately, *knowing* it's impermanent. Not detachment — defiance.

---

### T3 — You Are Not as Separate as You Think

**Universal agreement:** The illusion of radical individual separateness causes distortion and suffering.

**Where they diverge:**
- **Metaphysical vs. practical interconnection.** Vedanta (*Atman = Brahman*) and Zen make a strong metaphysical claim: separation literally doesn't exist. Confucianism and Aristotle make a practical-social claim: you cannot flourish without community. These sound similar but ground themselves very differently.
- **The individual vs. the collective.** Nietzsche is the outlier: his emphasis on self-overcoming and individual creation makes him the least interconnection-friendly of major traditions. Existentialism (Sartre especially) has a similar tension: your freedom is your burden, and "hell is other people."

---

### T4 — The Present Moment Is All You Actually Have

**Universal agreement:** Rumination and anticipatory anxiety extract a price from the only place life occurs — now.

**Where they diverge:**
- **The afterlife and eternal meaning.** Christianity and Islam frame ultimate meaning in *eschatological* terms — it points beyond the present into eternity. Plato's direction of attention is similarly away from the temporal toward the eternal Forms.
- **Flourishing over a lifetime.** Aristotle sees *eudaimonia* as a feature of a complete life — hard to evaluate at any given moment. This doesn't negate presence, but it anchors meaning to a temporal arc rather than to now.
- **The most present-focused traditions** are Buddhism (especially Zen), Taoism, and Stoicism — all of which offer explicit practices for returning attention to the present.

---

### T5 — Virtue and Action Matter More Than Belief

**Strong agreement:** Aristotle, Stoics, Buddhism, Confucianism, Pragmatism, Zen, and Existentialism all treat practice as primary. Knowing is not enough; doing is the point.

**Where they diverge:**
- **Faith and revelation.** Protestant Christianity (*sola fide*) is the clearest disagreement — salvation through faith alone, not works. This is a fundamental inversion of T5.
- **Understanding as prerequisite.** Platonism argues that *knowing the Good* will lead to good action (*akrasia* — acting against knowledge — troubled Plato precisely because it seemed to violate this). This is more belief/understanding-first than practice-first.
- **Wu wei as non-doing.** Taoism complicates this — the ideal is *effortless* action, which means straining, deliberate practice might actually be counterproductive. You practice until you stop practicing, and then it flows.

---

### T6 — Meaning Requires Engagement, Not Discovery

**Strong agreement:** Aristotle, Pragmatism, Existentialism, Zen, and Confucianism all locate meaning in active engagement.

**The clearest split in all of philosophy:**

| Camp | Meaning is... | Examples |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Created / Engaged** | Made through choice and commitment | Existentialism, Pragmatism, Nietzsche, Zen |
| **Given / Discovered** | Found by aligning with something pre-existing | Platonism, Christianity, Islam, Vedanta |
| **Lived / Dissolved** | Not a problem to solve — just life lived fully | Taoism, Zen (also here), Buddhism |

This is the deepest fault line in the philosophy of meaning. The Existentialist says: the universe is indifferent, meaning is your creation, terror and freedom are the same thing. The Platonist/Christian says: meaning exists prior to you in the Good, in God, in the Form of the Beautiful — your job is to find your way toward it. The Taoist says the whole question is a knot you've tied yourself; stop pulling on it.

---

### T7 — Ego Is the Primary Obstacle

**Near-universal agreement:** The reactive, defensive, status-seeking self is identified across virtually every tradition as the central obstacle.

**Different names, same target:**

| Tradition | Name for the ego-obstacle |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Stoicism | The *passions* — irrational reactions from false judgments |
| Buddhism | *Ahamkara* — the I-making faculty; the illusion of self |
| Taoism | Forcing and striving — imposing agenda on the flow |
| Confucianism | Face-saving and self-aggrandizement vs. *ren* |
| Vedanta | The small self (*jiva*) misidentified as the whole |
| Christianity | Pride (*superbia*) — root of all sin |
| Islam/Sufism | *Nafs al-ammara* — the commanding lower self |
| Existentialism | Bad faith — hiding from freedom behind false necessity |
| Nietzsche | *Ressentiment* — reactive resentment masquerading as virtue |
| Zen | The thinking, grasping, conceptualizing mind |

**Where they diverge:** Most traditions call for ego *dissolution* or *subordination*. Nietzsche is the outlier — he targets the *reactive* ego but celebrates a robust, affirmative, creative self. The Confucian *junzi* also has strong self-respect (*yi*, rightness) — the goal is not to erase the self but to discipline it into right relationship with others.

## The Biggest Fault Lines

Three places where traditions genuinely split — not just emphasis, but fundamental disagreement:

**1. Is the external world irrelevant to flourishing?**
Stoics and Buddhists: yes, ultimately. The mind can be free regardless of circumstance.
Aristotle: no. Real poverty, illness, and isolation genuinely harm flourishing — not just your interpretation of them.

**2. Is meaning given or created?**
Plato, Christianity, Islam, Vedanta: meaning exists prior to you. Your job is to align with it.
Existentialism, Pragmatism, Nietzsche: you create it through action and commitment.
Taoism, Zen: wrong question. Stop looking for meaning and live.

**3. Should you engage with the world or withdraw from it?**
Stoics, Aristotle, Confucianism, Existentialism: stay in. Virtue requires engagement with the world.
Epicureans: retreat to a small garden with close friends.
Early Buddhism: the monastic path privileges withdrawal for serious practitioners.
Taoism: neither — flow with what arises.

## The Convergence

Beneath the fault lines, the same small set of moves appears across every tradition, every culture, every century:

> Suffering comes from the inside more than the outside.
> Grasping at what you can't control makes it worse.
> You are more connected to others than you feel.
> The present is where your life is actually happening.
> What you do consistently is who you become.
> Meaning shows up where you show up.
> The defensive, reactive self is the thing in the way.

The metaphysics differ. The cultural framing differs. The practices differ. The convergence on *what the human problem is and how to address it* is remarkable — and, as the core tenets file puts it, is itself a kind of evidence.
