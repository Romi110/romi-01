# Political Realism
*The intellectual tradition that takes states as the primary actors in an anarchic world and national interest as the driver of behavior — the theoretical foundation beneath the entire geopolitics cluster*

---

## What Is It

Political realism (or realism in international relations theory) is the tradition that explains state behavior primarily through the structure of the international system: there is no world government, no authority above sovereign states, and this anarchic condition forces states to prioritize security and power regardless of their domestic politics or stated values.

It is distinct from [philosophy/political-philosophy.md](political-philosophy.md), which asks about legitimacy and justice within political communities. Realism asks about behavior between them. The two traditions use different units of analysis (individuals and states vs. states and international systems), different normative registers (what should happen vs. what does happen), and produce different conclusions.

Realism is not a complete theory of international politics — liberalism, constructivism, and other frameworks capture things realism misses — but it is the framework that explains the most about power competition between states, and ignoring it produces persistent analytical errors.

## Why It Matters

Every significant geopolitical event — Taiwan, Ukraine, the US-China technology competition, the Middle East — is being analyzed through frameworks that are either implicitly or explicitly realist. States act in ways that look inconsistent, hypocritical, or irrational from a moralistic perspective and make immediate sense from a realist one. The US that lectures on democracy while supporting Saudi Arabia; China that talks of peaceful rise while building military infrastructure in the South China Sea; Russia that invokes sovereignty while invading neighboring states — these contradictions dissolve once you understand that states behave according to interest, not declared principle.

This connects directly to the geopolitics cluster: [geopolitics/intelligence-and-power.md](../geopolitics/intelligence-and-power.md), [geopolitics/diplomatic-statecraft.md](../geopolitics/diplomatic-statecraft.md), [geopolitics/economic-statecraft.md](../geopolitics/economic-statecraft.md) all operate on realist assumptions that this file makes explicit.

## Core Concepts

### The Foundation: Thucydides and the Melian Dialogue

The oldest text in the realist tradition is Thucydides' *History of the Peloponnesian War* (~400 BCE). The Melian Dialogue (Book V) is the canonical statement:

Athens demands that the small island of Melos, which has been neutral in the war, submit to Athenian rule and pay tribute. The Melians appeal to justice, honor, and the intervention of Sparta as their ally. The Athenian envoys respond:

> "The strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must."

And: considerations of justice apply between equals. Between unequals, the powerful pursue their interest and the weak accept what they cannot change. Appealing to justice when you lack power is not morality — it's self-deception.

Athens destroyed Melos and enslaved its population. Sparta did not intervene. Thucydides' point was not to endorse Athens but to describe how interstate power actually functions, stripped of the moralizing language that obscures it.

What Thucydides identified as driving the war: fear (the growth of Athenian power threatening Sparta), honor (prestige and status), and interest (economic and strategic). These three motivations appear consistently across realist analysis: states act from security fears, status concerns, and material interests — not from ideals.

### Classical Realism: Morgenthau and National Interest

E.H. Carr (*The Twenty Years' Crisis*, 1939) and Hans Morgenthau (*Politics Among Nations*, 1948) systematized realism into an academic tradition after WWI and WWII demonstrated the failure of liberal internationalism (the League of Nations, the Kellogg-Briand Pact that "outlawed" war) to prevent great power conflict.

Morgenthau's core claims:

**The concept of interest defined in terms of power**: statesmen pursue national interest, and national interest is defined in terms of power — the capacity to influence others and protect yourself. This is not cynicism; it's a structural constraint. A statesman who ignores power considerations in favor of moral principle exposes their country to states that don't.

**The separation of political morality from individual morality**: what is right for an individual (do not lie, do not harm the innocent) cannot be simply applied to states. A state that refuses deception leaves itself vulnerable to states that use it. A state that never uses force abandons its citizens to predation. Political ethics is about consequences for communities, not about the virtue of individual acts.

**Balance of power as the stabilizing mechanism**: in an anarchic system, states balance against threatening powers rather than bandwagon with them (usually). No single state gains permanent hegemony because coalitions form against it. The balance of power is not designed — it emerges from self-interested state behavior.

Morgenthau also identified the constant tension in realist analysis: the aspiration for peace and justice is real, but it must be pursued through power rather than through its abandonment. The statesman who wants a more just world needs to understand power as the instrument, not the obstacle.

### Structural Realism: Waltz

Kenneth Waltz's *Theory of International Politics* (1979) shifted the explanatory focus from human nature (Morgenthau's statesmen are driven by the will to power) to **structure**: the anarchic organization of the international system itself explains state behavior, regardless of who the leaders are or what domestic politics look like.

**The three levels of analysis**: Waltz identified that international outcomes can be explained at the level of (1) individual leaders, (2) state characteristics, or (3) international structure. He argued that structure is the most powerful explanatory level — not because individuals and states don't matter, but because they operate within structural constraints that produce similar behaviors across radically different personalities and regime types.

The anarchic structure forces states toward:
- **Self-help**: you cannot rely on any higher authority to protect you; security is your problem
- **Relative gains vs. absolute gains**: states care not just about how well they're doing but about how well they're doing relative to potential adversaries; a trade that benefits both parties is still bad if it benefits the adversary more
- **Emulation of successful strategies**: states that develop effective military and economic strategies get emulated by others; states that don't either adapt or decline

**Bipolarity vs. multipolarity**: Waltz argued that bipolar systems (two great powers, like the Cold War) are more stable than multipolar ones (three or more, like pre-WWI Europe) because miscalculation is harder and deterrence more credible. This claim is contested but has been influential.

### Offensive Realism: Mearsheimer

John Mearsheimer's *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics* (2001) pushed structural realism further: under anarchy, states are not merely security-seekers — they are power-maximizers. No state can ever be certain about the intentions of other states; the only reliable guarantee of security is to have more power than any possible adversary. Therefore:

- Great powers always seek to expand their power when they can
- Hegemons in one region will oppose the emergence of hegemons in other regions (offshore balancing)
- The "tragedy" is that even states with no aggressive intentions are forced toward aggressive behavior by the structure

Mearsheimer's predictions from this framework:
- China's rise will not be peaceful — it will seek regional hegemony in Asia, and the US will oppose it, producing conflict (argued in 2001; increasingly looks correct)
- The US was wrong to expand NATO toward Russia — this would trigger exactly the security response that structural realism predicts (argued in 2014 and 2022)
- Liberal hegemony (the US project of spreading democracy) is based on a misunderstanding of how states actually work and would produce backlash

His view is controversial, especially the Russia/NATO argument. But the framework produces specific, testable predictions — which is more than most political analysis offers.

### Realism vs. Liberalism

The main alternative tradition in IR theory:

**Liberal internationalism**: states can transcend power competition through institutions (the UN, WTO, international law), economic interdependence (trade creates mutual interest in stability), and democratic peace (democracies don't go to war with each other). The post-WWII order was built on this framework.

**The realist critique**: institutions work when great powers support them; they fail when great powers defy them. Economic interdependence reduces war costs but doesn't eliminate the security dilemma (Russia and Europe were deeply economically intertwined before 2022). Democratic peace is empirically real but limited in scope — democracies fight non-democracies plenty.

**What liberalism gets right that realism misses**: cooperation exists and is sustained; norms constrain behavior; domestic politics and identity matter; institutions create path dependencies. The European Union is not explicable by realism — states gave up meaningful sovereignty for economic and political gains. Realism explains breakdown and conflict better than it explains the sustained cooperation that actually characterizes most international relations.

The honest position: realism explains power competition between great powers well. Liberalism explains the substantial domain of international cooperation and institution-building that realism underweights. Both frameworks are necessary; neither is sufficient.

### Constructivism (What Realism Ignores)

A third tradition, not specifically opposed to realism but filling gaps it leaves:

**Constructivism** argues that the interests states pursue are not given by material structure — they're socially constructed through historical experience, norms, identity, and inter-state interaction. Why does the US treat the UK's nuclear arsenal as benign and North Korea's as threatening? Not because of the material facts (both are nuclear states with delivery capability) but because of the identities and relationships involved.

Constructivism explains: why some rivalries become friendships (France-Germany after WWII), why states sacrifice material interest for norm compliance (democracies accepting WTO rulings against their interest), why identity politics drives state behavior in ways that pure interest analysis misses (Israel's strategic choices, India's non-alignment tradition).

The combination: use realism for great power competition and security dilemmas; use liberalism for institutional design and economic interdependence; use constructivism for the role of identity and norms in shaping what states actually want.

## Key Considerations

- Realism is descriptively powerful but normatively limited. It describes what states do; it doesn't tell you what they should do or how the international order should be designed.
- The realist analysis doesn't require you to endorse it. Understanding that states behave in certain ways doesn't mean those ways are just. Thucydides was describing Athens at Melos, not endorsing it.
- Realism tends to miss domestic politics. The Putin who invaded Ukraine was not responding only to NATO expansion; domestic political dynamics, historical narrative, and personal psychology were essential. Pure structural realism can't explain timing or specific decisions.
- Realist analysis is most useful as a check on idealistic analysis. When a policy argument rests entirely on the good intentions of state actors, ask: what does the structural incentive analysis say?

## Common Mistakes

**Treating realism as cynicism.** Realism doesn't claim states are evil; it claims the structure forces them toward behavior that looks like self-interest regardless of values. This is a structural argument, not a moral one.

**Using realism to excuse any state behavior.** "States will do what states will do" is not analysis; it's capitulation. Realism explains behavior; it doesn't prevent normative evaluation of it.

**Ignoring the liberal critique.** Realism was partially wrong about European integration, wrong about many dimensions of international cooperation, and wrong about the democratic peace. The tradition is useful not complete.

## Resources

- *The Peloponnesian War* — Thucydides (Strassler/Landmark edition). The Melian Dialogue in Book V. Read the first two books and Book V; the rest is campaign-by-campaign detail.
- *Politics Among Nations* — Hans Morgenthau (abridged version available). The systematic classical realism statement. The chapters on national interest and political ethics are the core.
- *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics* — John Mearsheimer. The most rigorous offensive realism statement; readable and specific. His chapters on China are particularly relevant to current events.
- *The Anarchical Society* — Hedley Bull. A third-way position between realism and liberalism: states form an international society with rules and norms even without world government. More subtle than either pole.
