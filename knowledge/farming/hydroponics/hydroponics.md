# Hydroponics
*Growing food without soil — roots in water, nutrients delivered directly, indoors or out, year-round.*

---

## What Is It

Hydroponics is a method of growing plants in a nutrient-rich water solution instead of soil. The roots either float in water, sit in an inert growing medium (rockwool, clay pebbles, coco coir), or hang in air — and all the nutrients the plant needs are dissolved in the water and delivered directly to the roots.

Soil is a nutrient delivery system. Hydroponics replaces that system with a controlled one. Plants don't care where their nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium come from — if the chemistry is right, they grow faster and more efficiently in water than in soil.

The practical implication: you can grow fresh food in a small indoor space, year-round, regardless of season or outdoor conditions. A 2×4 ft grow tent in a spare room can produce lettuce, herbs, spinach, and strawberries continuously. A more advanced setup can produce tomatoes and peppers.

---

## Why It Matters

Three angles connect this to your situation:

**Year-round food production**: NJ has a 6-month outdoor season. Hydroponics adds the other 6 months — indoors, controlled, predictable. You stop being weather-dependent for fresh greens.

**Space efficiency**: hydroponics produces 3–10x more food per square foot than soil growing. Stacked vertical systems multiply this further. For a property where outdoor space is limited, an indoor hydro setup adds real production capacity.

**Speed**: plants in hydroponics grow 30–50% faster than in soil. Lettuce takes 60 days in soil; 25–30 days in a good DWC system. You turn over more crops in less time.

The trade-off is real: hydroponics requires active management. pH drifts, nutrients deplete, equipment fails. Soil is more forgiving. Hydroponics rewards attention and punishes neglect.

## Core Concepts

### How Plants Actually Grow

Plants need: light, CO2, water, and 17 essential nutrients. In soil, microorganisms break down organic matter to release those nutrients in forms plants can absorb. In hydroponics, you dissolve those nutrients directly in water — skipping the biological middleman.

Because nutrient availability is controlled directly, plants spend less energy hunting for food and more energy growing. That's why hydro plants grow faster.

The catch: in soil, biology buffers mistakes. Add too much fertilizer in soil and microbes help absorb and buffer the excess. In hydroponics, there's no buffer — nutrient imbalance hits the plant directly.

### The Six System Types

Hydroponics has six main system designs. They differ in how water and nutrients contact the roots. Each suits different crops and skill levels.

**Kratky (passive — no pump required)**
Plants sit in a net pot above a reservoir of nutrient solution. Roots grow down into the water; an air gap develops above the water line as the plant drinks, providing oxygen to upper roots. Zero electricity beyond optional lighting. Set it and mostly forget it.
*Best for*: lettuce, herbs, spinach. *Not for*: large fruiting plants. See [hydroponics-systems.md](hydroponics-systems.md).

**Deep Water Culture (DWC)**
Plants in net pots suspended over a reservoir. An air pump continuously oxygenates the water. Roots hang directly in nutrient solution. Simple, fast-growing, highly productive.
*Best for*: lettuce, herbs, leafy greens, and with larger systems, tomatoes and peppers. The most popular beginner active system.

**Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)**
A thin film of nutrient solution flows continuously down a slight-angled channel; roots sit on the channel bottom and absorb from the film. Good for high production, common in commercial operations.
*Best for*: lettuce, herbs, strawberries. Requires pump and timer.

**Ebb and Flow (Flood and Drain)**
Grow bed floods with nutrient solution periodically, then drains. Roots in grow medium (clay pebbles, rockwool). More versatile for different plant sizes.
*Best for*: lettuce, herbs, and larger plants with the right medium.

**Drip Systems**
Nutrient solution drips onto the base of each plant through emitters. Familiar concept from soil drip irrigation. Scalable, reliable.
*Best for*: tomatoes, peppers, larger plants. Most commercial cannabis is grown this way.

**Aeroponics**
Roots hang in air; fine mist of nutrient solution sprays them at intervals. Fastest growth of any method. Highest technical complexity and failure risk.
*Best for*: advanced growers; R&D; not a starting point.

### pH: The Most Important Number

pH measures acidity/alkalinity on a scale of 0–14. Neutral is 7. For most hydroponic crops, the target range is **5.5–6.5**.

Why it matters: nutrients become available to plants only within certain pH ranges. Outside 5.5–6.5, specific nutrients lock up even if they're present in the solution — plants show deficiency symptoms despite correct nutrient levels. This is the most common cause of crop failure in hydroponics.

pH drifts constantly as plants absorb nutrients and water evaporates. You check and adjust it regularly — daily in small systems, every 2–3 days in larger ones.

Tools: pH meter ($15–50), pH Up (potassium hydroxide solution) and pH Down (phosphoric acid solution). A $20 digital pH pen is mandatory.

### EC: Measuring Nutrient Concentration

EC (Electrical Conductivity) measures how much dissolved mineral is in the water — your proxy for nutrient concentration. More dissolved nutrients = higher EC. Measured in millisiemens per centimeter (mS/cm) or parts per million (ppm).

Target EC varies by plant and growth stage:
- Seedlings: 0.8–1.2 mS/cm
- Lettuce and leafy greens: 1.2–2.0 mS/cm
- Tomatoes and fruiting plants: 2.0–3.5 mS/cm

Too low: plant starves. Too high: nutrient burn (brown leaf tips, wilting despite wet roots). Check EC with a cheap digital TDS/EC meter ($10–20). See [hydroponics-nutrients.md](hydroponics-nutrients.md).

### Growing Mediums

Plants in hydroponics need physical support for their roots. Common options:

- **Rockwool** (spun basalt fibers) — best for seed germination and cloning; holds moisture and air well; not reusable; rinse and soak at pH 5.5 before use (comes alkaline)
- **Clay pebbles (LECA)** — reusable, excellent drainage and aeration, easy to rinse and sterilize; standard for DWC, flood-and-drain; rinse thoroughly before first use
- **Coco coir** — shredded coconut husk; holds moisture well; often buffered with extra calcium and magnesium; sits between soil growing and hydro in feel
- **Perlite** — light volcanic glass; mixed with coco or used alone in top-feed systems; improves drainage

For Kratky and DWC starting out: clay pebbles in net pots. Easy, reusable, widely available.

### Light for Indoor Systems

Outdoor hydroponics uses sunlight. Indoor systems need grow lights. Light quality and quantity are the rate-limiting factors in indoor growing — everything else can be optimized, but if light is insufficient, nothing works.

The key measurement is PPFD (Photosynthetic Photon Flux Density) — how much usable light hits the canopy per second. Leafy greens need 150–250 µmol/m²/s. Tomatoes and fruiting plants need 400–600+.

**LED grow lights** are the current standard. They run cool, are energy-efficient, and have improved dramatically. For a 2×2 ft tent of lettuce and herbs, a 100–200W LED is adequate. For a 4×4 ft tent with fruiting plants, 400–600W.

See [hydroponics-lighting.md](hydroponics-lighting.md) for the full breakdown.

## Key Considerations

**Hydroponics rewards consistency.** Check pH and EC on a schedule. Change reservoir water every 7–14 days. This is not optional — small systems drift fast.

**Start with leafy greens.** Lettuce, basil, spinach, arugula. They grow fast (25–35 days to harvest), are forgiving of minor pH/EC swings, and give you rapid feedback. Tomatoes and peppers are significantly more complex.

**The Kratky method is the lowest barrier entry.** No pump, no electricity beyond a light, one fill-up per crop cycle (mostly). If you just want to try hydroponics with minimal investment, start here.

**Water quality matters.** Tap water in NJ has chlorine (off-gasses overnight), chloramine (doesn't off-gas), and minerals that affect your EC baseline. If your tap water is above 200 ppm EC, either filter it (reverse osmosis) or account for it in your nutrient mix. Most tap water in NJ is fine for starting out.

**Temperature matters.** Root zone temperature should stay 65–72°F. Water above 75°F promotes root rot; below 60°F slows growth. In summer, this can require a water chiller. In winter, ambient room temperature usually handles it.

## Getting Started

**Simplest possible first system (Kratky lettuce):**
1. Get a 5-gallon opaque bucket, 3-inch net pots, clay pebbles, a basic 2-part nutrient solution, pH meter, pH Up/Down
2. Mix nutrients to 1.2–1.5 mS/cm EC, adjust to pH 6.0
3. Fill bucket to just below net pot level, place seeds in rockwool cube in net pot
4. A single T5 fluorescent or LED panel above provides enough light for lettuce
5. Wait — refill as needed, harvest in 30 days

**Cost to start**: $30–50 for a single-bucket Kratky system. Under $100 with a decent LED.

**First upgrade (DWC system):**
- Replace single bucket with a 4-site DWC system (4 plants); add an air pump and air stones
- $50–100 additional investment; 4x the production

**After first successful crop:**
- Expand to a tent setup (2×4 ft grow tent, 200W LED, 8-site system)
- Total: $200–350; meaningful continuous production

## Common Mistakes

**Ignoring pH** — the single most common failure; check daily in new systems; pH pen is non-negotiable

**Overfeeding** — beginners add too many nutrients; start at the low end of recommended EC and increase if plants show deficiency

**Not changing reservoir water** — old reservoir water accumulates salt buildup and pathogen load; change every 7–14 days regardless

**Using a clear reservoir** — light penetrates clear containers and algae grows; use opaque containers or wrap with reflective tape

**Starting with tomatoes** — they require higher EC, specific calcium and magnesium ratios, pruning knowledge, and trellis support; start with lettuce

**Not stabilizing the system before adding plants** — mix your nutrient solution, check pH and EC, then let it stabilize for a few hours before transplanting
