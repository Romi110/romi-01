# Pest Management
*The goal is not zero pests — it's a balanced system where pests stay below damaging levels.*

---

## What Is It

Pest management in organic growing is the practice of controlling damage from insects, diseases, and other organisms without relying on synthetic pesticides. It operates at the system level — building an environment where pest populations are naturally limited — rather than the treatment level, where you react to each outbreak.

The core principle: a diverse, healthy system resists pest pressure naturally. Monocultures collapse; mixed plantings hold. Stressed plants attract pests; healthy plants resist. Fighting individual pests without addressing system health is fighting symptoms.

---

## Why It Matters

In NJ, pest pressure is real: aphids, Japanese beetles, squash vine borers, tomato hornworms, flea beetles, and a range of fungal diseases are common. Ignoring them costs crops. Over-spraying (even with organic products) kills beneficial insects and destabilizes the system you're trying to build.

The solution is not zero intervention — it's intervention at the right level, with the least-disruptive tools first.

---

## Core Concepts

### The Intervention Ladder

Address pest problems in order from least to most disruptive:

1. **Observation** — identify the pest and damage accurately before doing anything; many "problems" are cosmetic or limited
2. **Physical removal** — hand-pick caterpillars and beetles; brush off aphid colonies; works well at small scale
3. **Barriers and traps** — row cover excludes most flying insects; yellow sticky traps catch aphids and whiteflies; copper tape deters slugs
4. **Habitat for beneficial insects** — plant flowers that attract predators; let some crops bolt
5. **Organic sprays** — neem oil, insecticidal soap, BT (Bacillus thuringiensis for caterpillars); last resort before losses become serious
6. **Acceptance** — some percentage of loss is normal; don't chase perfect plants

Synthetic pesticides are off this ladder entirely in a sustainable system. They kill beneficials, persist in soil, and undermine the biological balance you're building.

### Beneficial Insects

Predatory insects keep pest populations in check. Ladybugs eat aphids. Parasitic wasps lay eggs in hornworm caterpillars. Ground beetles eat slugs and soil pests. Lacewings eat almost everything small and soft.

Attract them by:
- Planting flowers with small florets near vegetables: dill, fennel, cilantro (let them bolt), yarrow, alyssum, marigolds
- Allowing some herbs to flower
- Not using broad-spectrum sprays that kill predator and prey alike
- Building brush or stone habitat for ground-dwelling predators

Once you have a diverse beneficial insect population, many pest outbreaks resolve themselves.

### Companion Planting

Strategic plant combinations that confuse or repel pests, or attract beneficial insects:

**Known to work:**
- Basil with tomatoes — some evidence of whitefly reduction; definitely increases diversity
- Nasturtiums as trap crops — aphids prefer them; sacrifice a few nasturtiums to protect other plants
- Dill and fennel near brassicas — attracts parasitic wasps that attack cabbage worms
- Alliums (onions, garlic) intercropped with carrots — reduces carrot fly pressure

**Myths to skip:**
- "Three sisters" (corn/beans/squash) works for space efficiency but not really for pest control
- Marigolds repelling nematodes is real only in tight spacing with specific marigold species — most companion planting marigolds do little

### Crop Rotation

Never plant the same plant family in the same location two years running. Pest and disease populations build in the soil where host plants grew. Moving the crop breaks that cycle.

**Main plant families to rotate:**
- Solanaceae (tomatoes, peppers, potatoes, eggplant)
- Brassicaceae (kale, broccoli, cabbage, mustard)
- Cucurbitaceae (squash, cucumbers, melons)
- Leguminosae (beans, peas)

A 4-bed rotation (one family per bed per year) is the simplest system.

### Common NJ Pests

**Japanese beetles (July–August)**: shiny copper-and-green beetles that skeletonize leaves. Hand-pick into soapy water in the morning when they're sluggish. Neem oil on foliage before emergence is partially deterrent. Japanese beetle traps attract more beetles than they catch — avoid.

**Squash vine borer**: moth larva that bores into zucchini and squash stems in late June/July; wilting vine, sudden collapse. Row cover over plants until flowering; remove when flowers open for pollination. Inject BT into damaged stems as last resort.

**Tomato hornworm**: large green caterpillar; strips branches. Easily hand-picked once seen. Parasitic wasp egg clusters (white rice-like projections on hornworm's back) — don't kill these; let the wasps emerge.

**Aphids**: clustered on new growth; controlled by ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps. Strong spray of water from a hose knocks them off. Insecticidal soap as a direct contact spray if population is large.

**Fungal diseases** (powdery mildew, early blight, late blight): common in NJ humidity. Prevention > treatment. Space plants for airflow, water at soil level, don't let foliage stay wet. Remove infected leaves immediately. Resistant varieties when available.

## Getting Started

1. **Learn to identify** what's actually in your garden — good and bad; most insects are neutral or beneficial
2. **Plant flowers** in and around beds this season: dill, alyssum, marigolds, nasturtiums
3. **Get row cover** — it physically excludes the most damaging flying insects; use it on brassicas and squash
4. **Practice crop rotation** from your first season — map where each family grows each year

## Common Mistakes

**Spraying at the first sign of damage** — most outbreaks are self-limiting; observe for a week before intervening

**Killing everything** — broad-spectrum sprays (even organic ones like neem oil) kill beneficials alongside pests; spot-treat only

**Ignoring disease as a pest issue** — fungal disease is as damaging as insects; spacing, airflow, and resistant varieties are the first line of defense

**Using Japanese beetle traps** — they attract more beetles from a wider area than you'd otherwise have; net negative
