# Starting From Scratch

*You have never grown food. You have a small space. You want to start. Here's the path from zero to real harvest.*

---

## What Is It

This is the first-timer's roadmap. It assumes you own or control a small plot (even a quarter of a yard), have never farmed, and want to grow enough to see success without being overwhelmed.

It's designed to avoid the two failure modes:
1. Doing nothing because it seems too complex
2. Doing everything and failing because you did too much

---

## The 12-Week Path (Start Early Spring)

### Weeks 1–2: Assess and Plan

**What you do:**
- Look at your space in the morning and afternoon. Where's the sun?
- Note where water pools after rain.
- Walk it daily for a week. This sounds pointless — it's not.
- Get your USDA hardiness zone. (Search "USDA Zone [your city]")
- Find your frost dates. (Your county extension has them)

**What this accomplishes:**
You're learning your specific land. You're not guessing. The sun map and water drainage will inform every decision you make.

### Week 2–3: Soil Test and Site Prep

**What you do:**
- Get a soil test from your county extension ($20–50)
- Pick your growing plot (4×8 ft minimum, 8×16 ft ideal for first year)
- Clear the area (remove grass, weeds, rocks)
- Order/acquire compost (3–4 cubic yards for a 4×8 space)

**Why this matters:**
Soil test tells you what you're actually working with. You're not amending blind. The compost is your foundation. Everything else builds on this.

**Realistic effort:** 3–4 hours of work.

### Week 3–4: Build the Foundation

**What you do:**
- Spread 3–4 inches of compost over your cleared plot
- Mix it lightly into the top 4 inches of existing soil (or just rake it level)
- Water it well
- Let it settle for a week

**What this accomplishes:**
You've started soil building. The microorganisms in compost start working. The organic matter begins to change soil structure.

**Realistic effort:** 2–3 hours

### Week 4: Buy Seeds and Plan Crops

**What you do:**
- Pick 3 cool-season crops: lettuce, spinach, peas (or beans if you're past frost)
- Buy seeds from a reputable supplier (Johnny's, Territorial, or local)
- Read the seed packets: days to maturity, when to plant
- Create a planting calendar for your frost dates

**Example (zone 5, last frost May 15):**
- May 1: Plant lettuce, spinach, peas
- May 20: Plant warm-season crops (tomatoes if you have starts, beans, squash)
- June 15: Succession plant more lettuce
- August 15: Plant fall lettuce and spinach

**Realistic effort:** 2 hours

### Week 5: Plant Cool-Season Crops

**What you do:**
- Soak seeds if the packet says to
- Plant in rows (straight lines, space per packet instructions)
- Water gently
- Mark where you planted so you remember 2 months from now
- That's it

**Realistic effort:** 1–2 hours

**What will probably happen:** Some seeds germinate, some don't. That's expected. If less than half germinate, note that for next year.

### These Weeks: Maintenance (5–15 minutes daily)

**What you must do:**
- Water if it hasn't rained (soil should be moist, not soggy)
- Pull obvious weeds when you see them (don't obsess)
- Watch for seedlings pushing through

**What you should NOT do:**
- Fertilize (compost has enough)
- Use pesticides (wait until you know what the problem is)
- Worry excessively

### Week 8–10: First Harvest

Lettuce, spinach, and peas mature in 60–70 days from planting. You'll eat something you grew.

**What this feels like:**
Disproportionate satisfaction. You grew food. It tasted better than store-bought. You did this.

### Week 10–14: Summer Crops Producing

Tomatoes will be flowering and producing. Beans will be prolific. Squash will be impossible to ignore.

**What will surprise you:**
You'll have more zucchini than you know what to do with. This is not a problem (make bread, grate and freeze). Also: animals will notice your garden. They want lettuce too.

## First Year Mindset

**What success looks like, not perfection:**
- You grew something edible
- You learned what grows in your climate
- You now understand what goes wrong (and why)
- You can point to specific things that worked and specific things that didn't
- You're excited about year two

**What will fail:**
Something will. That's the lesson. You'll lose a crop, get attacked by pests, or have bad weather. You'll learn more from the failure than the successes.

**What to track:**
- What did you plant where? (Simple map/notes)
- When did you plant it?
- When did you harvest?
- Did it work? Why or why not?

A one-page note does this. Detailed spreadsheet is overkill and likely won't get done.

---

## Year Two: Build

Armed with year-one knowledge:
- Soil is better. You'll add 2–3 more inches of compost.
- You know what grows well for you
- You can expand. Add beds. Try new crops.
- You know your mistakes. Fix them.

---

## Year Three: It Actually Works

By year three, soil has changed. Organic matter accumulates. Your gardening eye improves. Yields jump. You're producing something real.

---

## Common First-Year Mistakes

**Trying too much**
5 crops. One planting method. Focus.

**Inconsistent watering**
Water or mulch heavily. Don't wing it.

**Bad soil to start**
That $50 soil test saves $500 of wasted compost.

**Waiting for perfection**
Start now. By the time this season's done, you're infinitely more knowledgeable.

**Not keeping records**
Write it down. Future-you will thank current-you.

## The Absolute First Step

Right now, this week:

1. Check your USDA zone
2. Find your frost dates
3. Spend one hour observing your land
4. Contact your extension office about soil testing

Do these four things. Everything else flows from them.
