# Season Extension
*Adding 6–8 weeks to both ends of your growing season without a greenhouse.*

---

## What Is It

Season extension is the use of simple structures and timing strategies to grow food before your last frost date and after your first fall frost — effectively stretching a 6-month season closer to 8 or 9 months. In zone 7a (NJ), that means pushing past the April 15 last frost date and extending past the October 15 first frost date.

No expensive infrastructure required. The difference between a 6-month and a 9-month season is often just a few pieces of frost fabric and a cold frame built from scrap lumber and old windows.

---

## Why It Matters

More growing time equals more food. Simple math. But the crops suited to shoulder seasons (spinach, kale, arugula, mache, carrots, brassicas) are also some of the most expensive to buy and most nutritionally dense. Cool-season greens in March and November are a real return on minimal investment.

Season extension also lets you start warm-season crops earlier indoors — getting tomatoes and peppers further along before transplanting after last frost.

## Core Concepts

### The Four Tools (in order of cost and complexity)

**1. Row Covers (Floating Row Cover / Remay)**
Spun polypropylene fabric laid directly over plants or low hoops. Available in different weights:
- Lightweight (0.5–0.75 oz/sq yd) — pest barrier, light frost protection to 28°F
- Medium (1.5 oz/sq yd) — extends season by 4–6°F, good for spring and fall
- Heavy (2–3 oz/sq yd) — 8–10°F protection, less light transmission

Cost: $15–30 for a 5×25 ft piece. Reusable for 2–3 seasons. Lowest-barrier season extension available.

**2. Cold Frames**
Box (usually wood) with a transparent lid (old window, polycarbonate, twin-wall panels). Creates a microclimate 10–20°F warmer than outside. Can grow greens through freezes down to 10–15°F in zone 7.

DIY: scrap lumber frame + salvaged windows. Cost: nearly free.
Key detail: vent on warm days — cold frames overheat above 45°F outside temp.

**3. Low Tunnels**
Wire hoops every 3–4 feet over a bed, covered with row cover or clear plastic. Wider than a cold frame, easier to access. Warms air above and around plants. Good for extending tomatoes and peppers in fall.

Cost: $20–40 for wire, row cover already owned.

**4. Hoop House / High Tunnel**
Walk-in structure covered in clear polyethylene. Two full seasons of extension — year-round greens in zone 7. Significant setup (several hundred dollars minimum, days of work). Worth it at scale; overkill for a starter garden.

### What to Grow in Extended Seasons

**Early spring (2–4 weeks before last frost, under cover):**
- Spinach, arugula, mache, claytonia
- Kale and chard starts
- Radishes and turnips
- Overwintered garlic resumes growth without any cover

**Late fall (4–6 weeks after first frost, under cover):**
- All the same cold-tolerant greens
- Root vegetables: carrots sweeten after frost, parsnips same
- Leeks and hardy alliums
- Kale becomes more tender and sweet after frost

**Avoid under frost cover:** tomatoes, peppers, basil, squash, beans. These die at frost regardless of cover weight.

---

### Hardening Off

Seedlings started indoors must transition to outdoor conditions gradually. A week of increasing outdoor exposure (starting 1 hour, increasing daily) prevents transplant shock. Skip this and you lose half your starts.

---

## Getting Started

1. **This season**: buy one roll of medium-weight row cover. Use it over spring crops planted 3 weeks before last frost and over fall crops until hard freeze.
2. **Before next spring**: build one cold frame from scrap lumber and an old window. 2×4 ft minimum. Place on south-facing side of a building or fence for maximum warmth.
3. **Year two expansion**: add low tunnels over your main beds for fall tomato extension and early spring start.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Not venting cold frames** — temperatures spike above 40°F outside and you'll cook your crops. Use a stick to prop the lid, or buy automatic vent openers ($25).

**Using too-light row cover for real frost** — the 0.5 oz fabric barely protects below 28°F; use 1.5 oz or heavier for serious frost events.

**Forgetting about watering** — covered crops receive no rain. Check moisture weekly even in cool weather.

**Waiting until fall to think about this** — season extension is mostly planned in late summer. Order row cover and build cold frames in August.
