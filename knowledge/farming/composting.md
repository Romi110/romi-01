# Composting

*Turning waste into fertility. Closing the loop so that everything that grows goes back into the soil, and you never need to buy fertilizer again.*

---

## What Is It

Composting is the biological process of decomposing organic matter (kitchen scraps, yard waste, plant material) into stable, plant-available nutrients and soil amendment called compost. It's not complicated — it's what nature does with dead things. You're just accelerating it.

In a sustainable farming system, composting is the engine. Everything the garden produces (spent plants, trimmings, failure crops) goes into the compost pile. Everything the kitchen produces (vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells) goes in. Two years later, what comes out is dark, crumbly, and becomes the foundation of next year's growing.

---

## Why It Matters

**Free fertility**
Once you're composting, you're no longer buying fertilizer. You're taking waste and converting it to wealth.

**Circular system**
Food grows → you eat → scraps go to compost → compost builds soil → soil grows food. No external input. The system sustains itself.

**Waste reduction**
The average household throws away 600+ lbs of compostable material per year. That's waste *and* a loss of fertility.

**Soil building**
This is the accelerator for soil improvement. Compost is organic matter concentrated. Add 3–4 inches per year, and in 3 years your soil is unrecognizable.

## Core Concepts

### Carbon vs Nitrogen (Brown vs Green)

Compost needs both:
- **Carbon (browns)**: dry leaves, straw, wood chips, shredded paper, cardboard. These are 80% of the pile.
- **Nitrogen (greens)**: fresh plant scraps, grass clippings, kitchen waste, chicken manure. These are 20%.

The ratio matters. Too much green? Pile smells like ammonia (nitrogen escaping). Too much brown? Pile doesn't break down. Aim for roughly 4:1 or 5:1 brown to green.

### Hot Composting vs Cold Composting

**Hot composting** (3–6 months result):
- Layer browns and greens intentionally
- Turn the pile weekly
- Reaches 130+ degrees F
- Kills most weed seeds and pathogens
- Requires monitoring and effort

**Cold composting** (9–12 months result):
- Pile everything in a corner
- Ignore it
- Let nature work (slowly)
- Easier, requires no work
- Lower temperatures means weed seeds and pathogens can survive

Start with cold composting. When you want faster results, you can learn hot composting.

### What Can and Cannot Go In

**YES:**
- Fruit and vegetable scraps
- Coffee grounds and tea bags
- Eggshells
- Shredded paper, cardboard
- Fallen leaves
- Grass clippings (not if recently treated with herbicide)
- Straw
- Plant trimmings
- Hay
- Wood chips (break down slowly but will eventually)

**NO:**
- Meat or fish (attracts rats)
- Dairy or oils (same reason)
- Pet feces
- Diseased plant material (if you care about disease control)
- Glossy or heavily printed paper
- Herbicide-treated grass/plants

**MAYBE:**
- Human waste (technically compostable, but higher risk of contamination)
- Bones (break down, very slowly)
- Citrus peels (break down, take longer)

## Getting Started: The Simple System

**What you need:**
- A corner of your yard (4×4 ft minimum, more is better)
- A shovel
- Patience

**What you don't need:**
- A fancy bin (a pile works)
- Specific tools
- A thermometer
- A complex ratio calculator

**Setup:**
1. Pick a level spot that drains well (not soggy)
2. Start piling: alternating layers of brown and green
3. Add water if it gets dry (should be damp, not soggy)
4. Keep adding as you have material
5. Ignore it for 9–12 months

**After 9–12 months:**
- Bottom of pile is dark, crumbly, smells like earth
- You have compost
- Spread it on your beds
- Start a new pile

## The Year-Round System

**If you're serious about sustainability:**

Maintain *three piles in rotation*:
1. Current pile — you're adding to this now
2. Working pile — from 6–12 months ago
3. Finished pile — ready to use

This way, you always have finished compost to use while the other piles mature.

For a 4×8 ft growing bed, this requires approximately 3–4 cubic yards of finished compost per year. Three piles of 4×4×4 can generate that over a year-cycle.

---

## Making Accelerated Compost (Hot Method)

If you want finished compost in 3–6 months:

1. Layer 12–18 inches of brown material
2. Layer 3–4 inches of green material
3. Water each layer as you go
4. Repeat to pile height of 4–5 feet
5. Turn the pile completely every 7–10 days (shovel from outside edge to center, then re-stack)
6. When pile stops steaming and reaches room temperature, it's done

**What makes this work:**
The turning adds oxygen. Oxygen activates the bacteria that do the decomposing. That's the only difference between a 12-month pile and a 3-month pile — turning.

This method requires real effort. Do it if you want/can. The cold method works fine if you have time.

## Common Mistakes

**Too much green, no brown**
Smelly pile that doesn't decompose. Must be a solid majority brown.

**Piling meat or dairy**
Rats love it. Smell is terrible. Don't do this.

**Expecting fast results from a pile you ignore**
Without turning, expect 9–12 months minimum. That's fine. But be honest about timeline.

**Watering inconsistently**
Pile should be damp (like a wrung-out sponge). Too dry? Doesn't break down. Too wet? Smells bad. Check occasionally.

**Keeping compost too far away**
The farther from your kitchen, the less likely you'll actually use it. Compost pile should be visible from your back door.

## Integration Into Your Garden

**The cycle:**
1. Grow food
2. Harvest vegetables → kitchen scraps to compost
3. Pull spent plants → garden trimmings to compost
4. Fall leaves → to compost
5. Wait 9–12 months
6. Use finished compost to build beds for next year

By year three, this cycle is self-sustaining. You're producing more fertility than you're using, and your soil never stops improving.
