# Hydroponic Lighting
*Indoor plants need artificial light. How much, what kind, and how to not waste money on the wrong fixture.*

---

## What Is It

Outdoor plants get sunlight — free, full-spectrum, and powerful. Indoor hydroponic systems need a substitute. Grow lights provide the photons plants use for photosynthesis. The right light at the right intensity produces the same growth rates as outdoor sun. The wrong light (too dim, wrong spectrum, too far away) produces slow, stretched, weak plants regardless of how good your nutrients are.

Light is the rate-limiting factor in indoor growing. Everything else can be optimized around it, but if light is insufficient, nothing works.

---

## Why It Matters

Getting lighting wrong is the most common source of disappointment in indoor hydroponics. People buy cheap lights, hang them too high, run them the wrong number of hours, and blame the nutrients or system when the real problem is that the plants are starving for photons.

Understanding lighting means you can spec the right fixture for your space, hang it at the right height, and run it the right number of hours — and know that light is not the variable limiting your growth.

## Core Concepts

### PPFD: The Number That Matters

PPFD (Photosynthetic Photon Flux Density) measures how many photons in the PAR range (400–700nm, usable by plants) hit a square meter of canopy per second. Unit: µmol/m²/s.

This is the actual measurement of light intensity at canopy level — more useful than watts, lumens, or lux (which are not plant-specific).

**Target PPFD by crop**:
- Seedlings and clones: 100–250 µmol/m²/s
- Leafy greens (lettuce, herbs, spinach): 150–300 µmol/m²/s
- Tomatoes, peppers (vegetative): 300–500 µmol/m²/s
- Tomatoes, peppers (flowering/fruiting): 500–800+ µmol/m²/s

**PPFD drops with distance**: double the distance between light and canopy, and PPFD drops to roughly 1/4. This is why hanging height matters more than people expect. Most manufacturers publish PPFD maps at specific hanging heights — use them.

### DLI: Daily Light Integral

DLI (Daily Light Integral) is total light delivered per day: PPFD × photoperiod hours × 3600 (seconds per hour) / 1,000,000. Unit: mol/m²/day.

It's a more complete measure than PPFD alone because it accounts for both intensity and duration.

**Target DLI**:
- Lettuce and herbs: 12–17 mol/m²/day
- Tomatoes and peppers: 20–35 mol/m²/day

You can hit a DLI target either by running higher PPFD for fewer hours or lower PPFD for more hours — within reason. Running lights 20+ hours to compensate for a dim fixture is possible but not efficient.

### Photoperiod

**Day-neutral plants** (grow and flower regardless of light hours): lettuce, spinach, herbs, tomatoes, peppers. Run 16–18 hours for maximum growth. 20 hours is sometimes used for lettuce; gives diminishing returns and stresses some plants.

**Short-day plants** (require 12 hours of darkness to flower): not relevant to most food crops.

**Long-day plants** (require 14+ hours of light to flower): also not relevant to typical food crops.

For a lettuce/herb system: 16 hours on, 8 hours off. Use a timer. Consistency matters more than the exact number.

For tomatoes/peppers: 16–18 hours during vegetative growth. Some growers drop to 12/12 to trigger fruiting (imitating fall), though tomatoes don't technically require it — adequate PPFD and maturity trigger fruiting naturally.

## Light Types

### LED (Current Standard)

Modern LED grow lights are the right answer for most home hydroponic setups. They've improved dramatically since 2018.

**Why LED**: efficient (more light per watt), low heat output, long lifespan (50,000+ hours), full spectrum available, no bulb replacement.

**What to look for in an LED**:
- PPE (Photosynthetic Photon Efficacy) ≥ 2.5 µmol/J — this is efficiency; good LEDs are 2.5–3.0+
- Samsung LM301 diodes or equivalent — the standard for quality quantum board designs
- Published PPFD map at your intended hanging height — if the manufacturer doesn't provide this, assume the worst
- Dimming capability — useful for seedlings and for adjusting intensity as canopy height changes

**Budget guide**:
- 2×2 ft footprint (lettuce and herbs): 100–150W LED; $80–150 for a decent quality board
- 2×4 ft footprint: 200–250W; $150–250
- 4×4 ft footprint (fruiting plants): 400–600W; $250–500

**Brands with documented, consistent performance**: Mars Hydro (TS and FC series), Spider Farmer (SF series), AC Infinity. Avoid no-name "blurple" lights (purple/red/blue LED panels from early 2010s designs) — they're inefficient and often misrepresent wattage.

### T5 Fluorescent

Older standard for seedlings and clones. Good for low-light crops (microgreens, lettuce, herbs) at close range (2–4 inches above canopy).

**Pros**: cheap, available everywhere, runs cool, good for germination and seedling stages.

**Cons**: low intensity compared to LED; insufficient for fruiting plants; bulbs degrade and need replacement; T5 HO (High Output) is the minimum; regular T5 is too dim for most hydro applications.

**Still useful**: starting seeds, Kratky lettuce at close range if you already own them.

### HID: HPS and MH (Legacy)

High-Intensity Discharge lights (High-Pressure Sodium and Metal Halide) were the industry standard for decades. Still used in commercial operations.

**HPS** (High-Pressure Sodium): orange/red spectrum; best for flowering and fruiting; very high output; very high heat; requires ballast and hood.

**MH** (Metal Halide): blue spectrum; best for vegetative growth; often paired with HPS for a full-cycle setup.

**For home setups in 2026**: not recommended. LEDs now match or exceed HID output per watt with far less heat, no bulb replacement, and lower operating cost. The only reason to use HID is if you already own the equipment.

## Heat and Ventilation

All grow lights generate heat. In an enclosed space (grow tent), heat accumulates and can push temperatures above the 75–80°F range where plant growth slows.

**LED in a tent**: most 200–400W LEDs in a 2×4 ft tent require a small inline fan + carbon filter to maintain appropriate temperatures. AC Infinity and similar clip fans handle smaller spaces.

**General rule**: if your canopy temperature is above 80°F, add ventilation. Below 75°F is target.

Root zone temperature matters separately from air temperature. Water temperature in reservoirs should stay 65–72°F. Warm rooms in summer can push reservoir temps up — a small aquarium chiller ($80–200) addresses this if it becomes a problem.

## Hanging Height and Light Distance

Most LED manufacturers publish recommended hanging heights. General guidelines:

| Light Type | Seedlings | Lettuce/Herbs | Fruiting Plants |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| T5 | 2–4 inches | 4–6 inches | Not suitable |
| Budget LED (150W) | 18–24 inches | 16–20 inches | 12–16 inches |
| Quality LED (200–400W) | 24–30 inches | 18–24 inches | 16–20 inches |
| High-power LED (500W+) | 30+ inches | 24–30 inches | 18–24 inches |

Signs the light is too far: stretching (long, thin stems reaching toward light), pale color, slow growth.  
Signs the light is too close: bleaching (white or light patches on top leaves), brown tips, leaf curling.

Adjust height by 1–2 inches at a time and watch plant response over 2–3 days.

## Getting Started

**For a simple Kratky or DWC lettuce system**:
- A 2-bulb T5 HO fixture (4 foot) hung 4–6 inches above canopy is functional and cheap ($30–50)
- A Mars Hydro TS 600 (100W equivalent) or Spider Farmer SF-1000 ($80–120) is a better investment and grows lettuce faster

**For a 2×4 ft tent system**:
- Mars Hydro TS-1000 or Spider Farmer SF-2000 (200–220W actual draw) — covers the footprint, PPFD adequate for lettuce through pepper production at appropriate heights

**Setting up**:
1. Hang light at manufacturer-recommended height
2. Set timer: 16 on / 8 off for leafy greens and herbs
3. Check canopy temperature after 2 hours of operation
4. Adjust height based on plant response in first week

## Common Mistakes

**Trusting wattage claims** — "1000W equivalent" LED panels often draw 100–150W actual; actual draw is what matters; look for actual draw on the spec sheet

**Hanging too high** — a 200W LED at 36 inches delivers 40% of what it delivers at 18 inches; most beginners hang too high and wonder why growth is slow

**Running 24 hours** — plants benefit from a dark period; even day-neutral plants perform better with 16–18 hours, not 24

**Skipping a timer** — manual switching is inconsistent; a $10 mechanical outlet timer is non-negotiable

**Buying blurple lights** — those purple/blue/red LED bars from 5+ years ago; poor spectrum, low efficiency; modern white-spectrum quantum boards outperform them at equivalent wattage
