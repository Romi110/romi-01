# Food Preservation
*Bridging the gap between summer abundance and winter meals — extending the harvest without electricity.*

---

## What Is It

Food preservation is the set of techniques that prevent spoilage and extend the usable life of food beyond its fresh window. It's the second half of self-sustainable farming: growing food matters, but if you can't eat it before it rots, the surplus is wasted. Preservation turns a harvest into a year of food.

The major methods divide by mechanism: removing moisture (dehydrating), removing oxygen (canning/vacuum sealing), lowering temperature (cold storage), acidifying (fermenting/pickling), and saturating with salt or sugar. Each method suits different foods and produces different results.

---

## Why It Matters

A NJ garden produces most of its food in a 60-day window — July and August. Without preservation, you eat more than you can consume and watch the rest go to waste. With preservation, that abundance feeds you through winter. The math changes completely.

For a vegetarian growing food for self-reliance: tomatoes, beans, squash, and greens are all extremely preservable. The combination of canning, fermenting, and cold storage covers the entire harvest.

## Core Concepts

### Canning

Canning seals food in glass jars under vacuum and either water-bath processes or pressure-cooks it to kill microorganisms. Results are shelf-stable for 1–5 years.

**Water bath canning** — for high-acid foods (pH below 4.6): tomatoes with added lemon juice or citric acid, fruit jams and jellies, pickles, salsa. Processing time 10–45 minutes.

**Pressure canning** — for low-acid foods: plain vegetables, beans, soups, stocks. Required because botulism spores survive boiling water (212°F); only pressure achieves the 240°F needed to destroy them. A pressure canner is a specific piece of equipment — not a pressure cooker for cooking.

**Key equipment**: mason jars (widemouth and regular), lids (always new; bands reusable), water bath canner (large pot with rack), pressure canner (separate).

**Start with water bath**: tomatoes are the gateway. A half-dozen tomato plants produces enough to can 20–30 quarts. That's tomatoes all winter.

### Fermentation

Fermentation uses beneficial bacteria (primarily lactobacillus) to produce lactic acid, which preserves food and prevents pathogen growth. No heat required. Often more nutritious than the fresh version. Salt is the primary input.

**Lacto-fermentation** (the most common type): pack vegetables tightly in a jar, add 2% salt brine, submerge under brine, let sit at room temperature 3–7 days. That's sauerkraut, kimchi, fermented pickles, and most vegetable ferments.

**Equipment needed**: mason jars. That's it. Airlocks and fermentation weights are helpful but optional.

**Shelf life**: refrigerated ferments last 6–12 months. Properly made lacto-ferments are extremely safe — the acidic environment prevents pathogens.

**Start with sauerkraut**: 1 head of cabbage, 2% salt by weight, massage until wet, pack tightly. Done. Ready to eat in 3–7 days.

### Dehydrating

Removes moisture (to below 10%) to prevent microbial growth. Results are shelf-stable at room temperature for 1–2 years. Lightweight and space-efficient.

**Uses**: herbs (air-dry on string or screen, no equipment needed), fruit (apple slices, tomato chips, mango), vegetables (kale chips, squash slices), beans (cook then dehydrate for backpacking/storage).

**Equipment**: a dehydrator ($40–150) is far more reliable than an oven because it maintains lower temperatures with better airflow. The minimum useful dehydrator: 500W, stackable trays, adjustable temp.

**Best use in a home garden**: herbs (grow 10x more than you use fresh; dehydrate the excess) and tomatoes (dehydrated tomatoes are 1/8 the volume, concentrated flavor).

### Freezing

Simplest method. No learning required. Flash-freeze in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to bags — prevents a solid block. Most vegetables blanch (briefly boil, then ice bath) before freezing to preserve color, texture, and nutrients.

**Best for**: leafy greens, peas, beans, summer squash, corn, fruit. Also cooked grains and beans.

**Limitation**: requires ongoing electricity. Freezer failure = loss. Not a zero-dependency system — use it alongside non-electric methods.

### Root Cellaring

Cold, humid, dark storage extends the life of root vegetables, winter squash, garlic, onions, and apples for months without any processing.

**Ideal conditions by crop**:
- Potatoes: 38–40°F, 90% humidity, complete darkness
- Winter squash: 50–55°F, 50–70% humidity (warmer than potatoes)
- Carrots and beets: 32–40°F, 90% humidity (pack in damp sand)
- Garlic and onions: 35–50°F, 60% humidity (drier than root vegetables)

**In NJ without a true root cellar**: garage in winter (unheated but not freezing) works for most storage crops. Insulated coolers in an unheated basement work for smaller quantities. A chest freezer turned off in winter at 38–40°F is a cheap root cellar substitute.

## Getting Started

**Year one priority order:**
1. **Fermentation** — starts this week with no equipment; sauerkraut or fermented pickles from garden cucumbers
2. **Dehydrating herbs** — harvest basil, oregano, thyme; hang or lay on screen to dry; takes 1–2 weeks
3. **Canning tomatoes** — when the main harvest hits; water bath canning whole or crushed tomatoes with citric acid
4. **Cold storage** — where to store winter squash, garlic, onions already grown; no equipment needed, just the right conditions

## Common Mistakes

**Using old canning lids** — reusing the flat lids risks failed seals and spoilage; always buy new lids; bands are fine to reuse

**Low-acid water bath canning** — plain canned vegetables without pressure processing can harbor botulism; follow the method strictly

**Not blanching before freezing** — enzymes continue working in frozen unblanched vegetables, causing quality loss; 2–3 minutes in boiling water, then ice bath first

**Skipping headspace** — canned jars need the correct headspace (usually 1/4–1/2 inch) for proper vacuum seal; too much or too little fails the seal

**Storing squash and potatoes together** — squash needs warmth (55°F), potatoes need cold (38°F); stored together, one is compromised
