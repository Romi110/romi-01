# Seed Saving
*True seed independence — growing plants that produce next year's seeds, adapted to your soil and climate.*

---

## What Is It

Seed saving is the practice of harvesting seeds from mature plants, processing and storing them correctly, and replanting them the following season. It is one of the oldest agricultural skills and the final step toward genuine food self-reliance — if you save your own seeds, you never need to buy them again.

There is a critical distinction between seed types:
- **Open-pollinated (OP)**: naturally pollinated by insects, wind, or hand; seeds grow true to the parent plant
- **Heirloom**: a subset of OP varieties with documented history (usually 50+ years); adapted to local conditions over generations
- **Hybrid (F1)**: cross between two different parent lines; vigorous and uniform for one generation, but seeds from hybrids revert to unpredictable parent traits; not reliable for saving

Save seed from open-pollinated or heirloom varieties. Hybrids are not worth saving.

---

## Why It Matters

Commercial seeds cost $3–5 per packet and must be repurchased every year. Open-pollinated varieties from saved seed cost nothing after the first purchase. Over 5 years on 4–6 crops, this is modest but real savings — and more importantly, the practice builds a seed stock adapted to your specific conditions.

Plants grown from seeds saved in your garden over multiple seasons gradually adapt to your soil pH, your water, your local pest pressures, and your climate. Commercial seeds are selected for broad performance across many conditions. Your locally-adapted seeds eventually outperform them in your specific plot.

## Core Concepts

### Easy vs. Hard Crops to Save

Difficulty depends primarily on the plant's pollination method and how easily different varieties cross-contaminate.

**Easy (self-pollinating; no crossing risk; minimal isolation needed)**:
- Tomatoes — flowers pollinate themselves before opening; very little crossing even in dense plantings
- Beans and peas — same mechanism; save from best plants each year
- Lettuce — bolts and produces seeds freely; let a few plants go to seed
- Peppers — mostly self-pollinating; some crossing possible between types but manageable at home scale

**Medium (require isolation between varieties to prevent crossing)**:
- Squash — heavily cross-pollinated by bees; different varieties within the same species (Cucurbita pepo: zucchini and acorn squash; Cucurbita maxima: butternut and hubbard) will cross; need 500+ ft separation or careful hand-pollination
- Corn — wind-pollinated; must plant varieties at least 600 ft apart or time plantings to miss each other's silk windows
- Brassicas (kale, broccoli, cabbage) — bee-pollinated; cross within the species Brassica oleracea; need 300+ ft or isolation

**Hard (biennial — require two seasons; isolation challenges)**:
- Carrots, beets, parsnips — root vegetables that produce seeds in their second year; require overwintering roots and replanting; also cross with wild relatives
- Onions — biennial; complex to manage at scale

**Start with easy crops**: tomatoes and beans produce large seeds easily, require no isolation, and the saved seed is immediately reliable.

### Harvesting Seeds

**Wet process** (tomatoes, cucumbers, squash):
1. Scoop seeds and gel into a jar with water
2. Ferment 2–3 days at room temperature (breaks down gel inhibitor, kills some seed-borne diseases)
3. Good seeds sink; pour off floating seeds and pulp
4. Rinse clean seeds, spread on a ceramic plate to dry
5. Dry completely (2–3 weeks) before storing

**Dry process** (beans, peas, peppers, flowers, most everything else):
1. Leave on plant until fully mature (beans dry on the vine; tomatoes go past "eating ripe")
2. Harvest and spread to finish drying indoors for 2–3 weeks
3. Shell beans/peas; separate pepper seeds from dried fruit; screen or blow chaff from small seeds

### Storage

Seeds are dormant living organisms. Enemies: heat, moisture, light. Friends: cool, dry, dark.

**Recommended**:
- Paper envelopes inside glass jars (paper absorbs residual moisture)
- Add a silica gel packet per jar
- Store in refrigerator or cool basement (35–50°F)
- Label with: crop, variety, date saved

**Seed viability by storage life** (approximate, at ideal conditions):
- Tomatoes, peppers: 4–5 years
- Beans, peas: 3–4 years
- Squash, cucumber: 4–6 years
- Onions: 1–2 years (save fresh each year)
- Carrots: 2–3 years

### Selection Pressure

Seed saving is also selective breeding at a micro scale. Every year, you choose which plants to save seed from. Save from:
- Plants that produced abundantly
- Plants that showed disease resistance
- Plants that performed well in your specific conditions
- Fruits with the characteristics you want (flavor, size, texture)

Over 5–10 seasons, these selections compound. You're developing a locally-adapted variety that performs in your conditions better than anything sold in a catalog.

## Getting Started

**Year one (this season)**:
1. Grow tomatoes and beans from open-pollinated or heirloom varieties (look for "OP" or "heirloom" on seed packets; or buy from Baker Creek, Fedco, or High Mowing)
2. Let your best tomato ripen completely before harvest — almost overripe is right for seed saving
3. Process wet (tomatoes) and dry (beans) as described above
4. Store in labeled envelopes in a cool, dry place
5. Plant saved seeds next spring

That's seed saving. You're now independent for those two crops.

## Common Mistakes

**Saving from hybrids** — the seeds grow unpredictably; look for F1 on the label and avoid for saving

**Saving immature seeds** — seeds must be fully mature; a tomato eaten at peak flavor is not mature enough for seed saving; let it go further

**Poor drying before storage** — residual moisture causes mold during storage; seeds must be fully dry (snap, not bend) before sealing

**Mixing varieties accidentally** — keep clear labels from planting through storage; one mislabeled batch contaminates multiple years

**Saving from only one plant** — genetic bottleneck; save from at least 3–5 plants of each variety to maintain genetic diversity
