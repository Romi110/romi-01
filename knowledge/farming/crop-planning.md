# Crop Planning

*Choosing what to grow, when to plant, and how to get continuous harvests instead of feast-or-famine peaks.*

---

## What Is It

Crop planning is the art and discipline of selecting which plants to grow, choosing varieties suited to your climate, timing plantings so you harvest continuously across the season, and understanding which crops are worth your effort versus which are cheaper to buy.

Good crop planning turns a small space into continuous abundance instead of unpredictable chaos.

---

## Why It Matters

**Small space, maximum output**
A *planned* 4×8 ft bed will produce more than an unplanned quarter-acre. Timing, succession, and variety selection multiply what you get from limited space.

**Reliability**
You know what you'll harvest and when. You can plan meals, preservation, and storage around that. Chaos farming means waste.

**Effort efficiency**
Some crops are worth your time (tomatoes, peppers if your climate supports them, leafy greens). Some are cheaper to buy than grow (most grains, certain root vegetables in bulk). Choose wisely.

---

## Core Concepts

### Growing Zones and Frost Dates

The USDA growing zone is the single most important constraint you have. Zone 3 will never grow what Zone 7 grows without heroic effort. Accept your zone and excel within it.

Your **last spring frost date** and **first fall frost date** define your growing season. Two frost dates change everything:
- Zone 3: ~130-day season (May 20 – Sept 20)
- Zone 5: ~160-day season (May 10 – Oct 20)
- Zone 7: ~200-day season (April 15 – Nov 15)

Every seed packet has "days to maturity." If it says 75 days and your last frost is May 20, the latest you can plant is ~July 25 and still get harvest before first frost (around Oct 15 if you're zone 5). This math is your constraint.

### Cool-Season vs Warm-Season Crops

**Cool-season crops** (spring and fall): lettuce, spinach, peas, broccoli, cabbage, kale. They germinate in cool soil, prefer temperatures below 75°F, and bolt (go to seed) when it gets hot. Plant in early spring and again 6–8 weeks before first fall frost.

**Warm-season crops** (summer): tomatoes, peppers, beans, squash, cucumbers. They won't germinate in cold soil, need warmth to grow, and die at first frost. Plant after last frost when soil is warm (60°F+ minimum).

This is why succession is possible: while your spring lettuce finishes, you plant summer tomatoes. While tomatoes are growing, you start fall lettuce. You're using the full season, not just one crop cycle.

### Succession Planting

Succession planting means planting the same crop every 2–3 weeks for continuous harvest instead of one overwhelming peak.

Example (zone 5):
- Lettuce planted May 1 → harvest June 15
- Lettuce planted May 15 → harvest June 29
- Lettuce planted June 1 → harvest July 15
- Stop (too hot mid-July)
- Lettuce planted August 15 → harvest September 30 (fall crop)

You eat lettuce for 5 months instead of 2 weeks total.

### Variety Selection for Your Climate

All tomatoes are not the same. Some are determininate (fruit all at once), some indeterminate (continuous). Some need 85 days, some need 65 days. Some resist disease, some don't.

Read the *seed description*, not just the name. It will tell you:
- Days to maturity
- Disease resistance
- Growing habits (determinate/indeterminate)
- Heat tolerance (matters if you're in a hot climate)

Choose varieties *suited to your climate*, not varieties you wish were suited to your climate.

### Storage and Processing Crops

Some crops are worth growing because they *store*. These are force multipliers for effort:
- **Winter squash**: grows all summer, harvests in fall, stores 2–3 months with zero effort
- **Root vegetables**: beets, carrots, parsnips — grow, harvest, store in container in cool garage/cellar
- **Beans**: dry beans grown to maturity, stored in jars for the year
- **Alliums**: onions, garlic — both grow, both store for months

If you're serious about resilience/sustainability, storage crops are where the work pays off long-term.

## Getting Started: The Simple First Year

**Choose 3–5 crops you actually eat**
- Leafy greens (lettuce, spinach, kale)
- Tomatoes (1–2 varieties)
- Beans (green or dry)
- Squash (summer or winter)
- Peppers (if your climate supports them)

That's it. Get those right before expanding.

**Plan your plantings**
Create a simple calendar:
- Last frost date → plant cool crops
- After last frost → plant warm crops
- Mid-summer → succession plant fall crops
- Fall → plant garlic if you want spring harvest

**Start with seed packets with high success rates**
Easy wins: lettuce, spinach, beans, zucchini, peas, radishes. These are forgiving and motivating.

**Avoid small quantities of too many things**
You end up with 3 basil plants and 2 eggplants. Instead: 12 basil plants (you'll use them) or 1 variety of eggplant (if your climate supports it).

## Common Mistakes

**Choosing by looks, not by maturity date**
That beautiful heirloom tomato needs 95 days. Your zone has a 90-day season. You won't get ripe fruit. Read the packet first.

**Planting everything at once**
You'll be drowning in zucchini the first week and starving the next. Succession planting is hard at first but transforms the harvest.

**Not accounting for climate**
You don't get to grow watermelons in Minnesota without a greenhouse. Stop fighting reality.

**Too many things, no mastery**
Grow 5 things well. Then expand. This is how learning works.

**Underestimating water needs**
Young plants need consistent water. Inconsistent watering is failure. If you can't water every 2–3 days in dry weather, grow in smaller containers with mulch, not in-ground.

## Resources

**Seed catalogs** (Johnny's, High Mowing, Territorial, regional)
Read them every winter. Pick varieties designed for your zone. Order seeds in January for spring planting.

**Your extension office frost date guide**
Specific to your location. Use it. The dates matter.

**"The Gardener's A-Z Guide to Growing Vegetables" by Lewis and Nancy Bubel**
Has germination temps, days to maturity, spacing, everything. Reference book, not read-cover-to-cover.
