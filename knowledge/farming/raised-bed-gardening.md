# Raised Bed Gardening
*The most practical starting point for serious home food production — controlled soil, better drainage, less work.*

---

## What Is It

A raised bed is a defined growing area elevated above the surrounding ground, with controlled soil mix inside. It can be framed with wood, stone, or metal, or simply mounded without any frame. The key feature is that you fill it with a specific soil mix rather than working with whatever native soil you have.

Raised beds are the default recommendation for home food production because they solve the three most common beginner problems: poor native soil, drainage issues, and lack of defined growing space.

---

## Why It Matters

For a property in NJ, native soil is often heavy clay or sandy fill — both poor for growing. A raised bed lets you bypass years of soil remediation and start growing good food immediately. Combined with annual compost additions, raised beds improve every year without having to overhaul the whole yard.

They also bring the practical advantage of defined space: you know exactly where to mulch, where to water, and where weeds don't belong. This reduces maintenance time significantly.

## Core Concepts

### Dimensions

**Width**: never wider than you can reach from either side without stepping in. 4 feet is standard; 3 feet if you only access from one side. The no-step rule matters — compacted soil loses the structure you built.

**Length**: any length. 8 feet is common because it fits typical lumber dimensions. 12–16 feet is productive but makes corner construction more work.

**Height**: 
- 6 inches — minimum for annuals; soil temp rises faster than in-ground
- 12 inches — standard, good for roots; mostly independent from native soil
- 24 inches — deep beds for root crops and seriously poor native soil; also ADA-accessible

Higher = more soil needed, more build cost, better control.

### Materials

**Untreated pine/fir**: cheap, widely available, lasts 3–6 years before rotting. Fine for a first bed.

**Cedar**: naturally rot-resistant, lasts 10–15 years. More expensive. Worth it if you're building permanent infrastructure.

**Galvanized metal (Corten-style)**: increasingly popular, very durable (20+ years), heats up faster in spring (useful), cools faster too (minor in NJ). More expensive but low-maintenance.

**Cinder blocks / stone**: permanent, no rot, good thermal mass, high initial effort. Better for permanent landscape features.

**Avoid**: pressure-treated lumber (contains preservatives); railroad ties (creosote). Both leach chemicals into soil you're eating from.

### Soil Mix

Never fill a raised bed with topsoil alone — it compacts. The standard mix:

**Mel's Mix** (from "Square Foot Gardening"): 1/3 compost, 1/3 peat moss (or coco coir), 1/3 coarse vermiculite. Stays loose indefinitely, excellent moisture retention, zero compaction.

**Budget version**: 50% high-quality compost + 50% aged topsoil. Compacts more over time, needs annual compost topping.

**Annual maintenance**: top-dress 1–2 inches of compost each season. Do not till. The worms and biology do the mixing.

For a 4×8 ft bed at 12 inches deep: ~32 cubic feet of soil needed (roughly 1.2 cubic yards).

### Spacing and Layout

Raised beds allow closer plant spacing than traditional rows because you never walk between plants. Roots share nutrients and moisture efficiently in built soil.

Spacing guides for common crops (center-to-center):
- Tomatoes: 18–24 inches
- Peppers: 12–18 inches
- Squash/zucchini: 24–36 inches (they sprawl; let them grow outside the bed)
- Kale/chard: 12 inches
- Lettuce: 6 inches
- Carrots/radishes: 3 inches
- Beans: 4–6 inches

Orient beds north-south to minimize shading. Put tall crops (tomatoes, corn) on the north end.

### Watering Systems

The best investment after the bed itself is drip irrigation. A simple drip line or soaker hose through the bed:
- Reduces watering time to near zero
- Delivers water at root zone (not leaves — reduces fungal disease)
- Easily connected to a timer for full automation
- Costs ~$15–30 per bed

Without drip: water by hand with a gentle wand at soil level. Sprinklers are the worst option — they wet foliage and promote disease.

## Getting Started

1. **Choose location**: minimum 6 hours direct sun; ideally 8+; level or near-level ground
2. **Size your first bed**: 4×8 ft (32 sq ft) or 4×12 ft (48 sq ft); one bed is enough for year one
3. **Source materials**: cedar boards (2×6 for 6-inch depth, 2×12 for 12-inch) or skip framing and order a galvanized kit
4. **Source soil**: buy pre-mixed raised bed soil (often labeled "garden mix") or source components and mix; plan for 1.2 cubic yards per 4×8 at 12 inches deep
5. **Set up drip irrigation before planting** — much easier than retrofitting
6. **Plant and add 2 inches of mulch** (straw or wood chips) around seedlings

## Common Mistakes

**Filling with plain topsoil** — it compacts into a hard block by summer; use a proper mix

**Making the bed too wide** — any wider than 4 feet means stepping in and compacting soil

**Forgetting to test pH** — even in built soil, pH can be off; test before assuming it's correct

**Planting immediately after filling** — fresh soil mix needs a week to settle; water it in first

**Under-mulching** — raised beds lose moisture faster than in-ground; 2–3 inches of mulch is not optional
