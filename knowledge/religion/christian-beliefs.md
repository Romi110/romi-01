# Christian Beliefs — What the Evidence Actually Says
*An honest assessment of Christianity's core claims: what's historically supported, what's contested, and what requires faith*

---

## What Is It

Christianity is the world's largest religion (~2.4 billion adherents), built around the life, death, and claimed resurrection of Jesus of Nazareth. Its core claims: that Jesus was God incarnate, born of a virgin, performed miracles, was crucified and rose from the dead, and that belief in him provides salvation.

This file asks a specific question: which of these claims are supported by evidence outside the Bible, which are contested, and which require pure faith? The answer matters because Christianity makes historical claims — not just spiritual ones — and historical claims can be evaluated against evidence.

The short answer: Jesus almost certainly existed and was crucified. The supernatural claims — resurrection, miracles, divinity — have no independent corroboration and are accepted on faith. The Bible itself is a more complex and human document than most believers are taught. This is not wishful thinking across the board, but the parts that are demonstrably true and the parts that require faith are very different from how they're usually presented.

## Why It Matters

Whether or not you're Christian, Western culture, law, philosophy, and ethics are saturated with Christian ideas. Understanding what Christianity actually claims — and what backs those claims up — is intellectual literacy. If you're deciding what to believe, you should know what the honest picture looks like. If you're engaging with people who are Christian, you should understand the structure of the belief system and where the evidence actually sits.

## Core Concepts

### What Historians Actually Agree On About Jesus

The majority of secular historians — including skeptics and atheists — accept that Jesus of Nazareth was a real historical person. This isn't primarily a religious conclusion; it's a historical one based on standard criteria applied to ancient sources.

**What the historical consensus accepts:**
- A Jewish preacher named Jesus existed in first-century Palestine
- He was baptized by John the Baptist
- He gathered followers and had a ministry centered in Galilee
- He was crucified under Pontius Pilate, Roman prefect of Judea (~30–33 CE)
- His followers claimed he had risen from the dead afterward and formed a movement

This is the **historical Jesus** — what we can say with reasonable confidence from secular historical methodology. Beyond this, the picture gets contested.

**What is not historically established:**
- The virgin birth
- Miracles (water into wine, healing, raising the dead)
- The resurrection as a physical event
- His divine nature or identity with God

Claiming Jesus didn't exist at all (**mythicism**) is a fringe position even among atheist scholars. The person existed. Whether he was who his followers claimed he was is a completely separate question.

### Non-Christian Sources — What They Actually Say

This is frequently misrepresented. Here's what the non-Christian sources actually contain:

**Tacitus** (*Annals*, ~116 CE): "Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus." Confirms: a figure called Christus existed, was executed by Pilate. Says nothing about resurrection or miracles. Written ~85 years after the crucifixion.

**Josephus** (*Antiquities*, ~93 CE): Two mentions. The second, in Book 20, is widely accepted: "...James the brother of Jesus who was called Christ." Clear, brief, no miraculous claims. The first, in Book 18 (the **Testimonium Flavianum**), is problematic. In its current form it describes Jesus as a wise man, performer of miracles, the Messiah, and says he appeared alive after the crucifixion. Nearly all scholars believe this passage was partially or wholly altered by later Christian scribes — Josephus was Jewish, not Christian, and would not have written it as it currently stands. Most historians accept a shorter, original version existed (something like: "a wise man... who drew many people to him... condemned by Pilate...") with later additions. The miraculous claims in the Josephus passage are almost certainly not original.

**Pliny the Younger** (*Letters*, ~112 CE): A Roman governor writing to Emperor Trajan about Christians. Confirms they existed, gathered to worship Christ, sang hymns to him "as to a god." Historical evidence of the movement, not of the truth of its claims.

**Summary of what non-Christian sources establish:** Jesus existed. He had followers. He was crucified under Pilate. His followers called him the Messiah and worshipped him after his death. Non-Christian sources provide zero corroboration of miracles, the resurrection, or his divine nature.

### The Bible as a Historical Source

The Bible is used as evidence for its own claims — a circular problem worth addressing directly.

**What the New Testament actually is:** A collection of texts written by multiple human authors, over several decades, with theological purposes. Understanding the documents honestly:

- **Paul's letters** (1 Thessalonians, Galatians, 1–2 Corinthians, Romans, Philippians, Philemon) are the earliest, written ~50–60 CE — roughly 20–30 years after the crucifixion. Paul didn't know Jesus personally and claims his knowledge came through revelation and from Peter and James, whom he met briefly. He is the earliest source for the resurrection claim.
- **The Gospels** — Mark is earliest (~65–70 CE), then Matthew and Luke (~80–90 CE, drawing heavily on Mark), then John (~90–100 CE). All written decades after the events they describe, by authors who were almost certainly not eyewitnesses. The Gospels' authors are anonymous — the names were assigned by later tradition in the 2nd century.
- **The four Gospels contradict each other** on details: the resurrection accounts differ significantly on who went to the tomb, who appeared, what was said, and in what order events occurred. This doesn't prove nothing happened — but it's evidence these are human accounts filtered through tradition, not direct transcription of events.
- **Authorship problems across the New Testament**: of Paul's 13 attributed letters, scholars consider only 7 authentically his. The others (including the Pastoral letters — 1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus) are widely considered pseudonymous — written by later authors in Paul's name, a common practice in antiquity.

**The Old Testament** is a longer and more complex case. The **Documentary Hypothesis** (widely accepted in academic biblical scholarship) proposes the first five books (Torah/Pentateuch) were compiled from at least four distinct source traditions (J, E, D, P) over centuries, edited together by later redactors. Moses almost certainly didn't write Genesis through Deuteronomy. Archaeological evidence has repeatedly failed to corroborate the Exodus narrative — there's no Egyptian record of mass Israelite slavery or departure, and the 40-year desert wandering of ~2 million people leaves no archaeological trace in the Sinai Peninsula despite extensive investigation.

None of this means the Bible has no historical value — it does, for the history of ideas, for ancient Jewish and early Christian communities, and for some verifiable historical details. It means you shouldn't treat it as a neutral historical document about its own claims.

### The Resurrection Claim Specifically

The resurrection is the load-bearing claim of Christianity. Paul himself acknowledges this: "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Corinthians 15:17). If you grant the resurrection, the rest of the theology follows. If you don't, none of it does.

**What we actually know:**
- Jesus was crucified. The disciples scattered.
- Shortly after, his followers claimed he had appeared to them alive.
- This claim spread rapidly and drove the formation of the early church.
- Paul lists resurrection appearances in 1 Corinthians 15:3–8, including to "more than five hundred brothers at one time" — written ~20 years after the event.

**The competing explanations:**
- *The resurrection happened*: requires accepting a supernatural event for which there is no independent evidence — only testimony of committed believers.
- *Hallucination/grief experiences*: group hallucination is unlikely but grief-induced visions are documented. However, the appearances described in the texts are social, interactive, and involve eating — harder to explain as hallucination.
- *The disciples sincerely believed it but were wrong*: the most common secular historian's position. The belief was genuine; the event may have been misinterpreted visions, the wrong tomb, or some other misunderstanding.
- *Deliberate fabrication*: most historians reject this — the disciples suffered and died for this claim, which is strong evidence they genuinely believed it. People generally don't die for claims they know to be false.
- *Legend development*: the resurrection story elaborated over time as the tradition was transmitted. Mark's resurrection account (the earliest) is notably sparse; Luke and John add increasingly detailed appearance narratives.

**The honest answer:** The resurrection is not disprovable from the historical record, but it is also not provable. The evidence is: committed believers reported appearances. That's it. No independent corroboration exists. Believing it is an act of faith, not historical inference.

### Philosophical Arguments for God — Do They Work?

Christianity (like other theisms) is also supported through philosophical argument. The main ones honestly assessed:

**The Cosmological Argument** (Aquinas, Leibniz): Everything that exists has a cause. The universe exists. Therefore it has a cause — God. Problem: Why does the causal chain stop at God? The same question applies to God: what caused God? "God is uncaused" is asserted, not argued. Also assumes the universe requires an external cause rather than existing necessarily or arising from a prior state (quantum fluctuation from a pre-existing quantum vacuum is a live scientific hypothesis).

**The Fine-Tuning Argument**: The fundamental constants of physics (gravitational constant, electromagnetic force, etc.) are precisely tuned to allow life. If they varied slightly, no stars, planets, or life would exist. This apparent fine-tuning requires explanation. Problems: (1) The multiverse hypothesis — if enormous numbers of universes with varying constants exist, we necessarily find ourselves in one that permits life. (2) We don't know whether life requires these specific constants or whether other configurations could also produce complexity. (3) The argument commits the anthropic fallacy — we can only observe the universe from within it; observers necessarily observe conditions compatible with observation.

**The Moral Argument** (C.S. Lewis, William Lane Craig): Objective moral truths exist. Objective moral truths require a grounding — God. Problems: This assumes moral realism (that objective moral facts exist independent of minds), which is itself contested. And even if objective morality exists, it doesn't obviously require a personal God to ground it.

**The Ontological Argument** (Anselm, Descartes, Plantinga): God is the greatest conceivable being. Existence is a property that greatness requires. Therefore God exists in reality, not just conception. Most philosophers consider this argument clever but invalid — existence is not a predicate that adds to a concept.

**Verdict on philosophical arguments:** These are serious philosophical discussions that have occupied rigorous thinkers for centuries. None are considered decisive proofs even by most theist philosophers — they are treated as considerations that raise the probability of theism, not demonstrations. They remain contested. The arguments haven't convinced the majority of professional philosophers, who lean atheist or agnostic (~75% in surveys like PhilPapers).

### The Wishful Thinking Question

This is where honest engagement is required. Psychology and cognitive science have a lot to say about why people believe:

**Terror management theory** (Becker, *The Denial of Death*): The awareness of mortality is the central anxiety of human consciousness. Religion's promise of afterlife is the primary cultural mechanism for managing this terror. This doesn't prove religion is false — but it explains why belief persists regardless of evidence and why it's psychologically difficult to abandon.

**Pascal's Wager logic, deployed badly**: Many people implicitly reason: if Christianity is true and I believe, I gain heaven; if it's false, I lose nothing. But this reasoning applies equally to every religion — you can't simultaneously bet on Christianity, Islam, and every other afterlife claim. The reasoning also underestimates the costs of belief (intellectual integrity, life choices based on false premises).

**Cognitive biases favoring belief**: Patternicity (finding patterns, agents, and intentions in events — "everything happens for a reason"), ingroup-outgroup dynamics (tribal identity formed around shared belief), availability of vivid narratives about God, confirmation bias in prayer outcomes, agency detection (perceiving intentional agents where none may exist). These are documented cognitive tendencies, not moral failures — but they are mechanisms that would produce religious belief whether or not any religion is true.

**What religion genuinely provides**: Community, moral framework, existential meaning, ritual, comfort in grief, purpose — these are real goods. The psychological and social benefits of religious participation are well-documented, regardless of the truth status of the metaphysical claims. This is not the same as the metaphysical claims being true. It does explain why even people with doubts often remain religiously affiliated.

**The competing claims problem**: There are roughly 4,000 religions in the world. Christians are skeptical of every religion's miraculous claims except their own. The epistemological principle that produces atheism toward Islam, Hinduism, and Mormonism is identical to the one that produces skepticism toward Christianity. The born-in-the-right-culture critique: the religion a person holds is overwhelmingly predicted by where and when they were born — which is not how truth tends to work.

### What Christianity Gets Right (That Doesn't Require Miracles)

This matters because conflating "the supernatural claims are unsupported" with "Christianity has nothing to offer" is a mistake.

**The ethical teachings** of Jesus — love your neighbor, care for the poor, forgiveness over vengeance, the value of every person — are serious moral positions with significant independent grounding. The Sermon on the Mount is arguably one of the greatest moral documents in Western history, regardless of who wrote it or whether the author was divine.

**The confrontation with death**: Christianity forces engagement with mortality, meaning, and what constitutes a life well-lived. That confrontation is valuable even if the specific answers Christianity offers are not empirically supported.

**The community and practice**: Regular worship, shared meals, ethical accountability to a community, care for the sick and poor — these are real social goods. Many of these predate Christianity and exist across religious traditions, but Christianity institutionalized them effectively at scale.

**The contemplative tradition**: Christian mysticism (Meister Eckhart, Thomas Merton, the Desert Fathers) contains serious practices of self-examination, silence, and presence that parallel Buddhist and Stoic practices. These aren't contingent on the supernatural claims.

## Key Considerations

**The distinction between the historical Jesus and the theological Christ** is the most important conceptual tool here. The historical Jesus (a real person, crucified, whose followers claimed resurrection) is well-established. The theological Christ (God incarnate, bodily raised, second person of the Trinity) is a claim of faith.

**Most serious Christian theologians are not naive about this.** Academic theology and biblical studies are sophisticated fields that engage seriously with textual criticism, archaeological evidence, and historical scholarship. The fundamentalist claim of biblical inerrancy (every word literally true) is a minority position in academic Christianity. Many Christians hold faith while accepting the documented human authorship, contradictions, and historical limitations of scripture.

**The honest verdict:** The supernatural claims of Christianity are not supported by independent evidence. They are articles of faith — which is what Christianity itself says they are. Faith means believing without proof; that is its definition. The question isn't whether you need faith to believe in the resurrection — you obviously do — but whether you're honest with yourself about that being the basis.

## Getting Started

If you want to engage with this seriously:

1. Read Bart Ehrman — the most accessible rigorous scholar on the historical Jesus and New Testament. He's a former evangelical who became an agnostic through academic biblical scholarship. *Misquoting Jesus* is the entry point.
2. Read a serious Christian apologist in response — N.T. Wright is the most scholarly. *The Resurrection of the Son of God* is the most rigorous historical defense of the resurrection.
3. Read *The Demon-Haunted World* by Carl Sagan — not about Christianity specifically, but about how to think about extraordinary claims generally.
4. Engage with the primary texts yourself. Read the Gospel of Mark straight through in one sitting (it takes an hour). It reads differently from how it's preached.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming "scholars believe Jesus existed" means "scholars believe the miracles happened."** These are completely different questions answered by completely different kinds of evidence.

**Dismissing religion wholesale because the supernatural claims are unsupported.** The cultural, ethical, psychological, and communal dimensions are real and worth understanding separately from the metaphysical truth claims.

**Treating faith as evidence.** The sincerity and intensity of belief is not evidence for what is believed. Many sincere, intelligent people have believed things that were false.

**Assuming the choice is between biblical literalism and atheism.** Most serious Christian thinkers occupy neither position. The space between them is where most interesting theology actually lives.

## Resources

- *Misquoting Jesus* — Bart Ehrman. The most accessible serious treatment of how the New Testament was written, copied, and altered. Former evangelical, now agnostic. Rigorous.
- *Jesus and the Eyewitnesses* — Richard Bauckham. The best scholarly case for the Gospels as based on eyewitness tradition. Counterweight to Ehrman.
- *The Resurrection of the Son of God* — N.T. Wright. 800 pages. The most serious historical defense of the resurrection. Not light reading, but intellectually honest about what the argument requires.
- *The God Delusion* — Richard Dawkins. The most famous atheist critique. Better on biology than philosophy — his treatment of the philosophical arguments is weaker than the biology. Read alongside a serious theist response.
- *Mere Christianity* — C.S. Lewis. The most readable case for Christian belief by a 20th-century intellectual. Argue with it seriously rather than dismiss it.
