# Book Recommendations by Domain
*A living list — one or two books per domain worth owning, not just reading once*

---

## What Is It

A curated list of books by subject area. Not a ranked list or a complete syllabus — each entry is a book that does something specific and does it better than most alternatives. The goal is to have one reliable answer when someone asks "what should I read about X?" and to have that answer be a book Romi has personally prioritized or that is widely acknowledged as the highest-signal option in its domain.

This list is intentionally selective. Three good books per domain beats thirty mediocre ones. A book earns a slot by doing one of three things: teaching something durably (you reference it years later), changing how you think about something, or being the primary source that other good books in the domain cite.

## Why It Matters

Most reading advice is low-signal: "read widely," "read classics," lists of 100 books nobody finishes. This is the opposite — a short list calibrated to Romi's domains of actual interest, where every entry has a specific reason for being there. It compounds: as knowledge files get written, the books that sourced them naturally surface here.

## The List

### Philosophy

**Meditations — Marcus Aurelius**
The journal of a Roman emperor practicing Stoicism. Not written for publication — written to hold himself accountable to his own principles. The most direct Stoic text because it's not instructional; it's someone doing the work in writing. Read the Gregory Hays translation.

**Letters from a Stoic — Seneca**
Where Meditations is private, Seneca is conversational and specific — advice on fear of death, friendship, the use of time, wealth, ambition. More accessible than Meditations and often more practically applicable because Seneca engages with actual situations rather than principles alone.

**A Guide to the Good Life — William Irvine**
The best modern synthesis of Stoic practice. Irvine doesn't just summarize Stoicism; he shows how to apply it specifically: negative visualization, the trichotomy of control, Stoic exercises. Useful as a companion to primary texts in [philosophy/stoicism.md](../philosophy/stoicism.md).

### History

**Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind — Yuval Noah Harari**
The clearest single-volume narrative of human history from prehistory to now. Not a textbook — a thesis-driven argument about what actually changed the human trajectory: shared myths, agricultural revolution, money, empire, science. Read critically (the arguments are provocative, not always airtight) but it's the best starting framework for thinking at civilizational scale.

**The Lessons of History — Will and Ariel Durant**
Hundred pages distilled from an 11-volume history of civilization. Durant identifies the patterns that repeat: the role of biology, economics, religion, and war across all recorded human history. Remarkably dense — each page earns its place. Read slowly.

**The Storm Before the Storm — Mike Duncan**
The generation before Julius Caesar — the collapse of the Roman Republic. The mechanisms by which a functional democratic system deteriorates from the inside: tribalism, norm erosion, political violence becoming normalized. History-as-warning in the best sense. Connects directly to [geopolitics/](../geopolitics/) files.

### Science

**Why We Sleep — Matthew Walker**
The most important book most people haven't acted on. Walker covers sleep architecture, what each sleep stage does, and what chronic sleep deprivation does to cognition, immunity, metabolism, and longevity. The data is alarming enough to actually change behavior. Read after [health/sleep-science.md](../health/sleep-science.md) or instead of it — the file sources Walker extensively.

**The Selfish Gene — Richard Dawkins**
The foundational text for thinking about evolution from the gene's perspective rather than the organism's. Introduces the gene-centered view and the concept of memes (now overused, but the original formulation is precise). Essential for understanding why humans and other organisms behave the way they do — cooperation, competition, altruism, deception.

**Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman**
The research synthesis on dual-process cognition: System 1 (fast, intuitive, pattern-matching) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, analytical). Kahneman summarizes decades of decision-making research. Every bias in the book is worth knowing by name — they show up in financial decisions, medical judgments, and everyday reasoning. Connects to [mind/](../mind/) and [economics/](../economics/) files.

### Mind & Psychology

**Man's Search for Meaning — Viktor Frankl**
Frankl survived Nazi concentration camps and used the experience to develop logotherapy — the idea that meaning, not pleasure or power, is the primary human motivator. The first half (the memoir) is harrowing; the second half (the theory) is the part worth re-reading. Short, dense, changes how you think about suffering and purpose.

**Deep Work — Cal Newport**
The argument that sustained, focused cognitive work is both increasingly rare and increasingly valuable. Newport defines deep work, explains why it's degraded by modern work culture, and gives specific protocols for recovering it. Directly applicable to any knowledge-work environment. Pairs well with [meta/information-power.md](information-power.md).

**Atomic Habits — James Clear**
The clearest practical framework for habit formation available. Identity-based habits, cue/craving/response/reward loops, the 1% improvement compounding argument. Not a philosophy book — an operational one. Useful as a companion to [health/fitness-plan.md](../health/fitness-plan.md) for the habit infrastructure around training.

### Fiction

**Musashi — Eiji Yoshikawa**
Historical fiction about the famous Japanese swordsman Miyamoto Musashi — a masterless samurai who forged himself through decades of combat, discipline, and solitude into one of history's most formidable figures. The longest book on this list but reads fast. Stoic themes in a feudal Japan frame: mastery through suffering, the right action over the expedient one, solitude as a tool not a punishment.

**The Brothers Karamazov — Fyodor Dostoevsky**
Dostoevsky's last and best novel. A murder mystery on the surface; underneath, a sustained examination of faith, doubt, suffering, and moral responsibility. The "Grand Inquisitor" chapter is among the most compressed philosophical arguments in literary history. Difficult but not unreadable — start here if you read one Dostoevsky.

**Siddhartha — Hermann Hesse**
The life of a man seeking enlightenment through every path available: asceticism, pleasure, commerce, and finally surrender to simply being. Slim and fast. The argument isn't Buddhist doctrine — it's that direct experience is the only real teacher. Useful to read alongside [philosophy/zen-buddhism.md](../philosophy/zen-buddhism.md).

### Health & Fitness

**Kettlebell Simple & Sinister — Pavel Tsatsouline**
Two exercises — swing and Turkish get-up — structured into a simple progression that produces genuine athletic development. The philosophy (grease the groove, never go to failure, practice not workout) is as important as the program. Already referenced in [health/strength-training-fundamentals.md](../health/strength-training-fundamentals.md).

**Built to Move — Kelly and Juliet Starrett**
Ten physical tests that predict long-term mobility: can you sit on the floor and stand without using hands? Can you achieve full overhead extension? The book is organized around these tests and the specific practices that restore each. Less programming, more maintenance — the physical hygiene version of fitness.

**The Comfort Crisis — Michael Easter**
The argument that modernity has optimized out all physical and psychological discomfort — and that this is making people weaker and less fulfilled. Easter embeds with subsistence hunters in Alaska and Bhutan monks. The frame is ancestral; the practices are concrete: cold exposure, rucking, fasting, voluntary discomfort. Connects to [health/holistic-health-practices.md](../health/holistic-health-practices.md).

### Economics & Finance

**The Psychology of Money — Morgan Housel**
The most readable book on how people actually think about money as opposed to how models say they should. Not investing mechanics — behavioral finance. Housel argues that behavior matters more than knowledge in personal finance, and gives the specific mental models to improve it. Pairs with [finance/](../finance/) files.

**Poor Charlie's Almanack — Charles Munger (ed. Peter Kaufman)**
The compiled speeches and mental models of Warren Buffett's partner. Dense and unconventional — organized around "mental models" from multiple disciplines applied to investment and life decisions. The latticework of models framework is the most useful thing in it: using ideas from psychology, physics, biology, and history together rather than one discipline in isolation.

**Antifragile — Nassim Taleb**
The argument for systems (investments, careers, bodies, institutions) that don't just survive shocks but improve from them. Taleb's writing is abrasive but the core idea is important: variance is not the enemy if you're positioned correctly. Relevant to geopolitics, personal finance, and physical training simultaneously.

### Technology & Software

**The Phoenix Project — Gene Kim, Kevin Behr, George Spafford**
A novel about IT operations — more specifically, about how DevOps thinking transforms a failing technology organization. The fictional frame makes abstract principles (flow, feedback loops, continual learning) concrete. Required reading in most software organizations. Pairs with anything in [tech/](../tech/).

**A Philosophy of Software Design — John Ousterhout**
Ousterhout's core argument: the fundamental problem in software is complexity, and every design decision should be evaluated by whether it reduces or increases it. More opinionated and less comprehensive than Clean Code, but more specifically useful for design decisions. Works as a framework for code review.

## How to Update This List

This is a living list. When a new knowledge file is written and the source book is worth recommending as a primary resource, add it under the relevant domain. Format: `**Title — Author**` followed by 2–4 sentences explaining specifically what the book does and why it earns a slot.

One slot per book. If a book spans domains, pick the one where it has the most to add — don't list it twice.
