# Knowledge Network Building
*Finding and maintaining high-signal human sources — the people, communities, and publications worth being connected to.*

---

## What Is It

A knowledge network is the set of people, publications, and communities you rely on to stay informed and to encounter ideas you wouldn't find on your own. Books are the foundation — covered in [reading-deeply.md](./reading-deeply.md). This file is about the live layer: real-time sources that update, react, and debate.

The network matters because most important ideas and developments reach you through people before they reach you through formal writing. The person who identifies a trend in their niche 18 months before it becomes a book is more valuable to track than the book itself.

## Why It Matters

Information advantage compounds. Knowing something true before others do — in your domain, in adjacent domains, about your own field's direction — affects every decision: what to learn next, where to specialize, when a trend is real vs. hype, whether to trust a claim. A well-built knowledge network is the input layer that makes everything else in this wiki sharper.

The alternative — passively consuming algorithmic feeds — optimizes for engagement, not insight. What the algorithm shows you is what keeps you on the platform, not what's actually true or important.

## Core Concepts

### The Source Quality Hierarchy

Not all information sources are equal. From highest to lowest signal:

**Primary sources**: original research, primary documents, firsthand accounts. Papers, government reports, earnings transcripts, original data. Hardest to read; highest reliability. Most commentary is downstream of this — if you can get here, you skip all the distortion layers.

**Domain experts with track records**: practitioners with verifiable predictions and demonstrated expertise in a specific area. A virologist writing about respiratory viruses; a structural engineer writing about construction failures; a portfolio manager with a public track record. Expertise is domain-specific — someone who was right about one thing is not automatically an expert in adjacent things.

**High-quality journalism and analysis**: reporters and analysts who cite sources, publish corrections, and have long records. The Financial Times, Bloomberg, Reuters for finance. ProPublica for investigative work. Quality varies enormously within any publication — track writers, not mastheads.

**Thoughtful generalists**: writers who synthesize across domains with intellectual honesty. Best for connecting ideas across fields, not for domain-specific accuracy. Tyler Cowen (economics, culture), Patrick McKenzie (software business), Devon Zuegel (urbanism, technology). These people are nodes connecting many domains — useful for breadth.

**Commentators and pundits**: high confidence, low accountability, optimized for attention. Most TV news, most social media hot takes, most "hot take" newsletters. Information here has low signal density — lots of volume, little update.

The mistake most people make: spending 80% of their information time at the bottom of this hierarchy and wondering why their worldview doesn't sharpen.

### Finding Genuine Experts

Credentials are a starting point, not a conclusion. A PhD in the field is evidence; it's not proof of reliable judgment. What distinguishes real expertise from performance of expertise:

**Track record on specific predictions**: have they made falsifiable claims? Were they right? Did they acknowledge when wrong? A domain expert who never commits to a specific claim is not giving you information — they're managing their reputation.

**Epistemic behaviors**: do they cite their sources? Do they update when evidence changes? Do they acknowledge the limits of their own knowledge? Do they distinguish what they know from what they believe?

**Narrow vs. broad claims**: the best experts are often the most modest about the range of their expertise. A cardiologist who confidently opines on macroeconomics is not acting as a cardiologist — they're acting as a person with an opinion.

**Who quotes them**: are they cited by other people you've already validated? Peer-to-peer credibility is a signal. Who do the genuine experts in a field actually read?

### Building a Curated Feed by Domain

A curated feed is a deliberate selection of sources — not everything from everywhere, but specific voices worth tracking. It should be small enough to read regularly and high enough quality to be worth the time.

Build it by domain, not by medium. For each domain you're actively working on:

**People**: 3–5 writers or practitioners worth following directly. Twitter/X, Substack, personal blogs — wherever they actually publish their thinking. Filter ruthlessly: if someone produces more noise than signal over 90 days, unfollow.

**Publications**: 1–2 publications that cover the domain with consistent rigor. A specific section of a newspaper, a specific journal, a specific newsletter — not the whole publication.

**Communities**: one forum, Discord, or community where practitioners talk. The quality of a community depends entirely on who moderated it and who left when the quality declined. r/slatestarcodex, Hacker News, specific professional Slack groups, academic Discord servers. Communities are for serendipity — you don't know what you'll find; that's the point.

**Primary sources**: a standing practice of reading at least one primary source per month in each active domain. The paper, not the writeup of the paper. The government report, not the summary.

### Cross-Domain Pollination

The most durable information advantage comes not from being more informed within one domain, but from being connected across two or three domains that most people treat as separate.

Examples of cross-domain connection that generates insight:
- Labor economics × AI job market: the task-based model predicts which AI impacts will be gradual vs. sudden
- Soil science × nutrition: the mineral depletion of industrial farming connects to dietary supplementation
- Game theory × geopolitics: understanding why states defect from agreements even when cooperation is optimal

Being the person who connects domains makes you more valuable to people in any one of them. The person who understands both software engineering and regulatory law is more useful than two people who each know one. This is how you become a high-value node rather than just a consumer.

### Reciprocity: Being a Contributor

Knowledge networks are not broadcast media — they're two-way. The people who get the most out of these networks are the ones who contribute: asking good questions, sharing what they've learned, connecting people to each other.

Practically: writing publicly (even briefly), participating in communities with genuine questions and observations, sharing non-obvious things you've encountered. The return is not immediate — but it compounds. People who share good thinking become magnets for more good thinking.

This repo is a version of this: writing down what you learn creates a record that can be shared, which attracts people with overlapping interests and expertise.

### Pruning

A knowledge network that doesn't get pruned becomes an attention sink. Sources decay: a newsletter gets acquired, a writer shifts focus, a community gets captured by a vocal minority. What was high-signal two years ago may be low-signal now.

Prune on a cadence — quarterly is enough. Ask for each source: has this produced anything worth keeping in the last 90 days? If no, cut it. The value of a tight, high-quality feed is not just what's in it — it's what's not in it.

## Key Considerations

**Social media platforms are mostly noise with occasional signal.** The signal exists — certain people use Twitter/X, Mastodon, or LinkedIn to publish genuinely good thinking. The platform algorithm is not your friend in finding it. Curate by following specific people, not by browsing feeds.

**Substack has created a new category of expert-to-reader publishing** that bypasses traditional media gatekeepers. Quality varies more than traditional publications but the ceiling is higher — some of the best domain-specific writing now exists only on Substack. The model rewards consistent, specific expertise: look for writers who have been publishing in one domain for years.

**Be wary of sources that make you feel smart for agreeing with them.** A good source challenges your priors sometimes. If every piece from a source confirms what you already believe, it's not updating your model — it's reinforcing your existing one.

**Track your source quality, not source quantity.** Having 200 newsletters subscribed is not a knowledge network — it's an inbox problem. Ten sources you actually read carefully beats 200 you skim.

## Getting Started

1. **Audit your current information diet.** What did you read last week, and how much of it do you actually remember or use? This tells you where your sources are on the hierarchy.
2. **Identify the 2–3 domains you're actively developing right now.** Build a deliberate feed for each: 3–5 people, 1–2 publications, one community.
3. **Find one primary source per active domain.** For economics: NBER working papers. For AI: arXiv cs.AI, Anthropic's model cards and research blog. For farming: USDA reports and university extension publications. Subscribe to at least one.
4. **Cut half your current subscriptions.** The freed attention is more valuable than the content you cut.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistaking volume for quality.** Reading more isn't the goal. The goal is updating your model of the world accurately. One well-chosen source read carefully beats ten sources skimmed.

**Following people for their personality, not their track record.** Charismatic communicators and genuine experts are different populations with significant overlap — but the overlap isn't 100%. Watch the predictions, not the confidence.

**Only following people who agree with you.** This is epistemically dangerous and practically limiting. You can't spot the flaws in your own model if everyone you follow shares it.

**Treating the network as static.** The best sources change. Prune regularly.

## Resources

- *The Shallows* — Nicholas Carr. What the internet is doing to deep thinking; the case for why curation over consumption matters.
- *How to Think* — Alan Jacobs. Practical epistemology: how to reason in a polarized information environment and how to find people worth thinking with.
- Simon Willison's weblog (simonwillison.net) — a working example of how to track a fast-moving domain (AI tooling) with consistent rigor and intellectual honesty. Worth studying as a model of what good domain coverage looks like.
