# Information Power
*How control over information, narrative, attention, and platforms becomes a source of real power in politics, business, and technology.*

---

## What Is It
Information power is the ability to shape what people know, what they care about, and how they decide. It is not just owning data or technology; it is managing attention, controlling narratives, and making certain ideas the default.

## Why It Matters
Modern power often flows through information systems. Corporations earn monopoly rents by controlling platform attention. States assert influence through propaganda, surveillance, and internet governance. For anyone building systems or trying to make decisions, understanding information power is the clearest way to see why some actors can move markets, policies, or culture with small, targeted inputs.

## Core Concepts

### Attention as a resource
Attention is finite. Information power treats attention as the scarce asset to be captured. Platforms and media compete not only to deliver content, but to keep users engaged. Whoever controls attention gets to shape what problems feel urgent.

### Narrative control
A narrative is a coherent story that explains events and assigns meaning. Controlling the dominant narrative gives actors the power to define enemies, threats, and solutions. This is why political campaigns, brands, and reform movements spend so much energy on framing.

### Gatekeeping and platforms
Platforms are gatekeepers. Search engines, social networks, and news media decide which information reaches an audience. Owning or influencing a gatekeeper is a direct source of power, because it changes the supply of information.

### Data and surveillance
Data amplifies information power. Surveillance gives actors a feedback loop: they can watch behavior, optimize messaging, and target interventions. That creates asymmetries in knowledge that can be exploited for persuasion, control, or market advantage.

### Governance and standards
Information power depends on rules. Standards bodies, trust frameworks, privacy laws, and content moderation policies determine who can use platforms and how. Control over these governance structures is often more powerful than owning the underlying technology.

## Key Considerations
- Information power is often invisible. Policies, algorithms, and narratives can shape outcomes without obvious force.
- Control is partial. Most systems are contested, so power is exerted by shaping incentives rather than outright domination.
- Different layers matter: infrastructure, protocols, platforms, content, and norms all offer distinct levers.
- Information power can backfire. When audiences recognize manipulation, trust collapses and legitimacy evaporates.
- Ethical use is a choice. Information influence can improve coordination and decision-making, but it can also create capture and manipulation.

## Getting Started
1. Map the information ecosystem in one domain: who creates content, who distributes it, who pays for attention, and who governs the rules.
2. Compare attention-driven business models (social platforms) with trust-driven institutions (public media, academia).
3. Study a recent information campaign — political, product launch, or social movement — and identify the levers of narrative, platform, and data.
4. Learn the difference between owning infrastructure and owning culture.

## Common Mistakes
- Equating information power with censorship alone. Influence includes attraction and legitimacy.
- Focusing only on technology. Governance and human habits are equally important.
- Assuming information is neutral. How data is collected, filtered, and presented shapes outcomes before anyone reads it.
- Believing more information equals more power. In many cases, scarcity of attention or trust is the actual constraint.
