# Information Power
*How control over information, narrative, attention, and platforms becomes a source of real power in politics, business, and technology*

---

## What Is It

Information power is the capacity to shape what others know, believe, and pay attention to — and the structural advantage this creates. It is power exercised through epistemic means: not forcing behavior directly, but controlling the information environment that behavior emerges from.

It is distinct from [mind/propaganda-and-mass-persuasion.md](../mind/propaganda-and-mass-persuasion.md), which covers the techniques of mass persuasion. This file is about the structural architecture of information control: who owns the channels, who sets the frames, how information asymmetry creates and maintains power — in politics, markets, organizations, and technology.

The two files form a pair: propaganda is the applied technique; information power is the structural condition those techniques operate within.

## Why It Matters

In an attention economy where information is abundant but attention is scarce, the ability to direct attention — to control which signals reach which audiences — is the central form of power. Governments use it (state media, classification, regulatory capture of platforms). Companies build business models on it (advertising, data asymmetry, recommendation algorithms). Individuals exploit it (epistemic authority through credentials, track records, or audience trust). Understanding the structure makes you both harder to manipulate and better positioned to build genuine authority.

## Core Concepts

### Information Asymmetry as Power

The person who knows what you don't know holds leverage over you. This is the core of information power, and it operates at every scale.

**In markets**: insider trading is the illegal version of information asymmetry turned into financial advantage. Legal versions are everywhere: a real estate agent who knows the neighborhood knows more than the buyer; a car dealer who knows the car's history knows more than the buyer; a company with proprietary data about customer behavior knows more than competitors. Every profitable information asymmetry is a power asymmetry.

**In politics**: the state knows more about citizens than citizens know about the state. Classification systems, executive privilege, and national security exemptions formalize this asymmetry. The surveillance state is not just a civil liberties issue — it's an information asymmetry that translates directly into power: the state can see what citizens are doing; citizens cannot see what the state is doing.

**In organizations**: the person who knows the real budget, the real strategy, the real performance data has power over everyone operating on partial information. Middle management's power often derives not from formal authority but from information position — they know things that reports don't and things that executives don't. This creates leverage in both directions.

**The reversal through transparency**: information asymmetries collapse when the less-informed party gains access. Transparency that embarrasses organizations (whistleblowing, FOIA requests, investigative journalism) is a structural threat to power based on information asymmetry. The consistent prosecution of leakers and suppression of disclosures is the predictable defense of that power, not aberrant behavior.

### Narrative Control: Who Frames the Story

The frame of a story determines what the story means. Facts are insufficient to determine their own interpretation; they acquire meaning within narratives that specify what is relevant, what is cause and effect, and what should be done.

**The power of the frame**: Iraq's WMD program existed (past tense) and did not exist (at the time of invasion) — both statements are factually accurate. Which one is relevant depends on the frame: justification for war vs. intelligence failure. Immigration statistics are rising — is this an invasion crisis or an economic contribution story? The facts are compatible with both frames; the frame determines the policy conclusion.

**Who sets the frame** has structural advantages:
- *The first mover*: who breaks a story often sets the initial frame, which subsequent coverage responds to
- *The authoritative source*: institutions whose statements are treated as presumptively credible (governments, major institutions, credentialed experts) can set frames without arguing for them
- *The gatekeeper*: whoever controls the platform, the front page, the broadcast schedule, the algorithmic feed decides which frames reach which audiences

**Legitimacy as a frame**: regimes that successfully establish their legitimacy can absorb significant policy failures because the frame ("we are the legitimate government doing difficult things") survives individual failures. Regimes without legitimacy narratives cannot survive comparable failures because each failure confirms the counter-frame. This is why invested regime-change operations often target legitimacy narrative before targeting regime resources.

### Attention as a Scarce Resource

Attention is finite; information is not. In an environment of information abundance, controlling attention is more valuable than controlling information.

**The attention economy**: Herbert Simon observed this in 1971: "a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention." The platforms that grew after the internet were attention brokers — they aggregated audience attention and sold it to advertisers. The business model requires maximizing time-on-platform, which produces algorithmic optimization for engagement, which produces systematic amplification of emotionally activating content.

**Outrage as the optimal content type**: content that produces outrage has higher engagement metrics than content that produces understanding, satisfaction, or calm. The platform's revenue model rewards outrage production. This is not conspiracy — it's an emergent property of optimizing engagement metrics. The result: the information environment systematically produces more outrage than accuracy warrants, more division than disagreement requires, more certainty than evidence supports.

**The scarcity shift**: in pre-internet information environments, the constraint was access — most people couldn't reach large audiences, and most information wasn't widely available. The information holder had structural power. Post-internet, access is near-universal; the constraint is attention. The new structural advantage is the ability to capture and direct attention: audience, platform, credential, or distribution network.

**First-mover advantage in attention**: once attention is captured by a frame or narrative, redirecting it requires breaking through an established cognitive structure. It's easier to capture attention first than to correct a story that already has it. This is why communications strategy emphasizes speed — getting your version of a story out before alternative frames establish.

### Epistemic Authority: Who Gets Believed

Power in information environments derives not just from having information but from being believed when you present it.

**Sources of epistemic authority:**
- *Credential*: institutional endorsement (PhD, professional license, government position) that signals expertise
- *Track record*: demonstrable past accuracy that builds trust
- *Institutional weight*: statements from major institutions carry presumptive credibility that individual statements don't
- *Audience*: having an audience that amplifies your statements compounds their reach and authority

**Credential capture**: institutions that confer credentials also control the epistemic authority those credentials convey. Medical licensing boards determine who can authoritatively speak about medicine. Central banks determine whose economic analysis is taken seriously. Academic peer review determines what counts as established knowledge. The authority to confer credentials is itself a form of power.

**The trust asymmetry**: building epistemic authority is slow (requires track record, credentials, and sustained accuracy); destroying it is fast (a single documented deception can permanently damage credibility). This asymmetry means that epistemic authority is genuinely rare and genuinely valuable. Institutions that have maintained it over time have a structural advantage they often undervalue until it's gone.

**The crisis of institutional epistemic authority**: the post-2008 period has seen systematic decline in trust in major epistemic authorities — mainstream media, scientific institutions, government agencies, financial institutions. Each institution had specific failures that damaged their authority; the aggregate effect is an epistemic environment where credibility is harder to establish and easier to attack. This creates opportunity for new authority-builders (Substacks, independent researchers, alternative media) and vulnerability for everyone operating in the information environment.

### Platform Control

The platforms that mediate information flow are structural power. Whoever controls the platform controls the terms on which information reaches audiences.

**The moderation problem**: platforms decide what speech is permitted, what content is amplified, what accounts are suspended. These decisions have political effects regardless of intent. Deplatforming a political actor (Trump from Twitter/Facebook in January 2021) is an exercise of power with political consequences. The companies making these decisions have no democratic accountability and no coherent regulatory framework.

**Algorithmic power**: the algorithm that determines what content users see is making continuous editorial decisions more consequential than any human editor. It decides which information reaches which audiences, in what context, at what emotional moment. This is power with no public visibility, no appeal mechanism, and minimal regulatory oversight.

**Network effects and lock-in**: dominant platforms (Google search, Meta's social graph, Apple App Store) maintain power partly through direct utility and partly through network effects that make alternatives inferior. Switching costs and network effects are structural information power: users remain within the platform's information environment because the alternative infrastructure doesn't exist or isn't compelling.

**The state-platform interface**: governments regulate, request data from, and pressure platforms in ways that shape their behavior. Disclosures have demonstrated systematic coordination between US government agencies and social media platforms on content moderation. China, Russia, and authoritarian governments have state control mechanisms over domestic platforms and exclusion mechanisms against foreign platforms. The interface between state power and platform power is the most consequential and least transparent information power dynamic.

### Building Genuine Information Power

For an individual or organization operating in this environment:

**Proprietary information** — know things others don't. Primary research, direct experience, relationships that produce information unavailable through public channels. The value of knowing the neighborhood, the industry, the technology, the people — before others do — is durable.

**Audience** — the ability to reach and be heard by people who matter. Audience is built slowly through sustained demonstrated value; it compounds. A single person with 10,000 engaged followers in a relevant domain has more information power than a person with access to the same information and no audience.

**Track record** — being demonstrably right over time builds epistemic authority that credentials alone don't. The analyst who called the financial crisis, the journalist who broke the story correctly, the scientist who predicted the finding — these people earn the right to be believed more quickly in the future.

**Framing capability** — the ability to name a phenomenon, define the relevant variables, and propose the evaluative standard. The person who names the problem often determines what counts as a solution. In any domain, developing the language and frameworks that others adopt is structural influence.

**Own your distribution** — email lists, owned publications, and relationship networks are less efficient than rented platforms but more durable. Platform dependency creates fragility; algorithmic changes and policy shifts can destroy information power built on platforms you don't control.

## Key Considerations

- Information power is not primarily about having information; it's about being believed, being heard, and being the source others defer to. Access to information without credibility or distribution is not power.
- The platforms that distribute information are not neutral. They make continuous value-laden decisions about what reaches whom. Treating them as neutral infrastructure is a systematic misreading.
- Epistemic authority is rare and fragile. Build it slowly through accuracy; protect it by admitting uncertainty rather than projecting false confidence.
- The transparency that threatens others' information power also threatens yours. Whatever information asymmetry you maintain is potentially subject to exposure.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing information access with information power.** Everyone has internet access; most people have no meaningful information power. The bottleneck is not information access but the ability to shape what others believe and attend to.

**Underestimating platform dependency.** Building audience on platforms you don't control creates fragility. Deplatforming, algorithmic changes, and policy shifts can destroy information power built on rented platforms.

**Treating credibility as permanent.** Institutional and personal credibility that took decades to build can be destroyed in a news cycle. The investment in accuracy and consistency is never a completed task.

**Mistaking loudness for authority.** Volume and virality are attention; they are not epistemic authority. A viral wrong claim is temporary attention that usually destroys credibility rather than building it.

## Resources

- *The Attention Merchants* — Tim Wu. A history of how media industries monetized human attention, from patent medicine ads to Facebook. The through-line from 19th century to now.
- *Manufacturing Consent* — Herman and Chomsky. The structural analysis of how media ownership and advertising produce systematic filtering without individual bad actors.
- *Surveillance Capitalism* — Shoshana Zuboff. The political economy of data collection and behavioral prediction: how information asymmetry between platforms and users is extracted as commercial and political power.
- *The Master Switch* — Tim Wu. How communication networks move from open to centralized control — the historical pattern that every new information technology follows.
