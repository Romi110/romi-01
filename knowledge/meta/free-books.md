# Free Ebooks and Audiobooks
*Every legitimate source for reading and listening for free — library apps, legal archives, and what's actually worth using*

---

## What Is It

There are several completely legitimate systems for getting ebooks and audiobooks at no cost: your public library card (which unlocks Libby/OverDrive, hoopla, and Kanopy), legal public domain archives (Project Gutenberg, Standard Ebooks, LibriVox), and a few lesser-known sources. Together they cover most of what a serious reader needs — the gaps are recent bestsellers and niche technical books.

This file maps every source worth knowing, what it covers, and how to use it. The goal is to never pay for a book that's available free through a legitimate channel.

## Why It Matters

Books are the highest-ROI input for learning. A serious reader going through 20–30 books a year at $15–25 each is spending $300–750 annually — and that's just the ones you finish. The free alternatives cost nothing except a library card, which is also free. Self-reliance means not paying recurring fees for things you can get free with one-time setup.

## Core Concepts

### Libby (OverDrive) — Your Most Powerful Tool

**What it is**: Libby is an app (iOS/Android) that connects to your public library system and lets you borrow ebooks and audiobooks digitally. It's the same content as Amazon Kindle or Audible — just borrowed instead of purchased.

**How to get it**:
1. Get a library card from your local NJ public library (free, takes 5 minutes in person or online for many systems)
2. Download the Libby app
3. Find your library by searching your library system name
4. Sign in with your library card number

**What's available**: tens of thousands of titles per library system. New releases, bestsellers, classics, nonfiction. The selection depends on what your library has licensed — larger systems (NYPL, JCLC in NJ) have significantly larger catalogs.

**The wait list problem**: popular new books have wait lists — sometimes weeks. The workaround: get cards from multiple library systems. NJ residents can get cards from any NJ public library. Many systems also offer non-resident cards for free or a small annual fee (~$25). NYPL gives free cards to anyone in New York state; some NJ residents also qualify.

**Simultaneous holds**: you can place holds at multiple libraries at once for the same title. Whoever gets it first, you borrow from them. This significantly cuts wait times.

**Libby tip**: "Lucky Day" copies — some libraries have special copies of popular titles with no wait list, borrowed for a shorter period (7 days vs. 21). Watch for these on high-demand titles.

### hoopla — No Wait Lists, Unlimited Borrows

**What it is**: a library-funded streaming service for ebooks, audiobooks, comics, and video. Unlike Libby, there are **no wait lists** — borrow immediately, up to a monthly limit (typically 10 borrows/month per card).

**How to get it**: same library card, different app. Go to hoopladigital.com or the hoopla app, sign in with your library card.

**What's available**: different catalog from Libby — strong on audiobooks, comics/graphic novels, and indie publishers. Weaker on big new releases. For classics, nonfiction, and anything not a current bestseller, hoopla is often faster than waiting on Libby.

**The monthly limit**: 10 borrows sounds like a lot. Each audiobook counts as one borrow regardless of length. Use it strategically — save Libby for popular titles with waits, use hoopla for everything else.

### Project Gutenberg — Public Domain, Everything

**What it is**: projectgutenberg.org. Over 70,000 free ebooks of works whose copyright has expired (published before 1928 in the US, roughly). Everything is free, no account needed, download in any format (epub, Kindle, PDF, plain text).

**What's here**: all of classical literature, philosophy, and history that predates the 20th century. Every Stoic text (Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, Seneca), every work of Plato and Aristotle, Shakespeare, Dickens, Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, Adam Smith, Darwin, Freud, Nietzsche, Sun Tzu, the Bhagavad Gita (multiple translations), the Quran, the Bible, and thousands more.

**For this wiki specifically**: every philosophy file's recommended reading that predates 1928 is on Project Gutenberg. Meditations, the Enchiridion, the Tao Te Ching, the Analects — all free, multiple translations.

### Standard Ebooks — Project Gutenberg, Professionally Formatted

**What it is**: standardebooks.org. Takes public domain books from Project Gutenberg and professionally typesets them — proper typography, cover art, correct formatting for e-readers. Free, open source.

**Why it matters**: Project Gutenberg files are often formatted poorly — inconsistent spacing, no proper chapters, bad hyphenation. Standard Ebooks takes the same content and makes it read as well as a commercial ebook. If a title is available on Standard Ebooks, use that over Project Gutenberg.

**Catalog**: smaller than Gutenberg (curated, not exhaustive) but everything in it is high quality. Covers the most important works — Stoic texts, classical philosophy, major novels.

### LibriVox — Free Public Domain Audiobooks

**What it is**: librivox.org. Volunteer-recorded audiobooks of public domain works. Free, no account required.

**Quality**: variable — these are volunteer recordings. Some readers are excellent; some are difficult to listen to. Check the reader before committing to a long book. Most titles have multiple recordings — browse by book to compare.

**Best use**: Stoic texts and classical philosophy work well here because the content is dense enough that a slightly imperfect reader doesn't ruin it. For novels where narrative voice matters, be more selective. The Marcus Aurelius *Meditations* and Epictetus recordings are solid.

**How to find**: search by title on librivox.org, or find recordings linked from the same books on Project Gutenberg.

### Internet Archive — The Everything Backup

**What it is**: archive.org/details/texts. A digital library with millions of books — scanned from physical copies, donated digitals, and project contributions. Two relevant sections:

**Open Library** (openlibrary.org): a Libby-like borrowing system for scanned physical books. You borrow a digital scan for 1–2 hours at a time (controlled digital lending). Useful for books that aren't on Libby and aren't in the public domain. The system is legally contested — use it for research and preview purposes.

**Fully free texts**: many books on Archive.org are fully free because the rights holder donated them or they're public domain. Filter by "Open Access" to find these.

### Google Play Books, Apple Books — Free Classics

Both stores have large catalogs of free public domain books. Less complete than Project Gutenberg but easier to access if you're already in that ecosystem. Search any classic title and look for the free version — it'll usually be there.

### Spotify — Audiobooks (Limited)

Spotify includes audiobook access (up to 15 hours/month on Premium plans). Catalog is limited and the monthly hour cap is annoying, but if you already pay for Spotify it's worth checking before buying an audiobook elsewhere.

### YouTube — Full Audiobooks

Many full audiobooks exist on YouTube, uploaded by channels that aggregate public domain recordings (and sometimes copyrighted ones, which get taken down periodically). Useful for a one-time listen when you don't want to manage a file. Search "[book title] full audiobook" — filter by duration to find the real thing vs. summaries.

## Key Considerations

**Library card stacking is legal and encouraged.** NJ residents can hold cards from multiple NJ library systems simultaneously. Each card unlocks a separate Libby and hoopla account with its own catalog and borrow limits. Two or three cards meaningfully expands your catalog and cuts wait times.

**Kindle vs. epub**: most library ebooks come as epub files, which work on any e-reader except Kindle (which uses a proprietary format). If you read on Kindle, you can still use Libby — it integrates with the Kindle app and can send books directly to your Kindle device. hoopla works through their app, not Kindle. For the best experience, a non-Kindle e-reader (Kobo, any Android tablet with a reading app) handles epubs natively without friction.

**Audiobook speeds**: listening at 1.5–2x is a learnable skill that roughly doubles your throughput. Most apps support this. Start at 1.25x and increase gradually.

## Getting Started

1. **Get your NJ library card** if you don't have one — walk into any branch, 5 minutes
2. **Download Libby** and connect your card. Browse the audiobook and ebook catalog for anything you've been meaning to read
3. **Download hoopla** with the same card. Search a title you want that has a wait on Libby — it may be immediately available
4. **Bookmark Standard Ebooks** (standardebooks.org). Any philosophy, history, or classic literature you want is likely there, free and well-formatted
5. **Get a second library card** — sign up online for a neighboring NJ county library system that offers online registration

## Common Mistakes

**Not getting multiple library cards.** One card is good. Two or three is the system working as intended. Every NJ public library system is a separate catalog and borrow limit.

**Forgetting hoopla for audiobooks.** Libby gets most of the attention, but hoopla's no-wait-list audiobook catalog is excellent for anything that's not a current bestseller.

**Buying books that are free on Project Gutenberg.** Before purchasing any pre-1928 work, check Gutenberg and Standard Ebooks first. The Stoics, Plato, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy — all free.

**Ignoring the Libby "wait list" workaround.** Popular books have waits, but placing holds across multiple library systems simultaneously makes most wait times manageable.
