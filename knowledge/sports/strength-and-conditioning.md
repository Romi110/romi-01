# Strength and Conditioning
*Sport-agnostic athletic development: building power, speed, lateral quickness, and injury resilience — the physical infrastructure that makes sport skills trainable*

---

## What Is It

Strength and conditioning (S&C) is structured training aimed at developing physical qualities — power, speed, agility, and tissue resilience — that transfer across sports rather than building sport-specific technique. A basketball S&C program and a soccer S&C program look similar at the foundation: both need explosive first steps, lateral mobility, and joints that can absorb repeated impact.

The distinction from general fitness training is orientation. General training targets health and body composition. S&C targets athletic performance. Progressive overload and movement patterns are the foundation of both (see [health/strength-training-fundamentals.md](../health/strength-training-fundamentals.md)) — but S&C adds plyometrics, speed mechanics, agility work, and deliberate prehab that general programs omit.

## Why It Matters

Basketball skill — shooting, defense, ball handling — runs on a physical substrate. A player who lacks explosive first steps will be late to every cut. One who can't move laterally for four quarters degrades on defense. One with fragile ankles or a weak posterior chain spends time recovering instead of practicing. S&C doesn't replace skill work; it raises the ceiling on how much skill is usable under game conditions.

Beyond basketball: the physical qualities S&C develops — power, agility, tissue resilience — are the same ones that determine how well a body ages. A 50-year-old who trained explosively in their 30s and 40s retains jump capacity, change-of-direction ability, and joint integrity that sedentary people lose by default. This is a long game.

## Core Concepts

### Power Development

Power is force applied quickly — it's what separates a strong person from an explosive person. A heavy deadlift builds force capacity; plyometrics teach the nervous system to apply that force fast.

**The stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)** is the mechanism behind all explosive movement. When a muscle is rapidly stretched (landing from a jump, planting to cut), elastic energy is stored in the tendon and muscle. If the next contraction fires immediately, that stored energy is released. Training this cycle is what makes plyometrics transfer to sport; it's distinct from slow strength work.

**Plyometric progression:**
- Entry: box jumps, broad jumps, med ball slams — teach explosive intent, minimal reactive demand
- Intermediate: depth jumps (step off a box and jump immediately on landing), bounds, split-leg jumps — trains SSC with ground contact time as the quality metric
- Advanced: hurdle hops, single-leg reactive work — requires clean mechanics before loading

**Key rule**: ground contact time. Slow, soft landings don't train the SSC. The goal is to minimize the time between landing and takeoff — like a ball bouncing off pavement, not sinking into sand. Start at low height/volume and prioritize stiffness over height.

**Med ball work**: rotational throws, chest passes, overhead slams. Trains power expression through full-body patterns without the landing-force demands of jump training. Good complement to plyometrics and easier to scale.

### Speed and Acceleration

For court sports, straight-line speed matters less than the first 3–5 steps (acceleration). Most basketball actions are changes of direction, not sustained sprints. Acceleration mechanics are learnable:

**Acceleration posture**: shin angle forward (not upright), drive back with the leg rather than lifting the knee, arm drive mirrors leg drive. An upright torso at the start means you're pushing down instead of forward.

**Short sprint work**: 5-, 10-, and 20-yard bursts. Complete rest between reps (45–90 seconds per 10 yards sprinted). Speed training is nervous system work — fatigue degrades mechanics. Never train speed when tired. Run these at the start of a session, before any strength work.

**Resisted sprints** (sled push/pull, resistance band sprints): brief overloading of acceleration mechanics. Resistance forces the forward lean and back-leg drive; removing it produces faster unresisted acceleration. Use 10–20% body weight as resistance.

### Lateral Quickness and Agility

Lateral quickness is the most basketball-specific physical attribute. The mechanics differ from forward/backward movement: low center of gravity, hip abductor strength driving each push, no crossover (which creates a momentary immobility). [sports/skill-development.md](skill-development.md) covers the basketball application specifically.

**Building lateral quickness:**

*Hip abductor strength*: the lateral push in each slide comes from glute med and hip abductors. Lateral band walks, side-lying clamshells, single-leg glute bridges. These are prehab exercises that double as the strength base for lateral speed. Most athletes have weak hip abductors from sitting and from training that is almost entirely forward/backward.

*Reactive agility drills*: cone patterns (5-10-5 shuttle, L-drill) build agility; adding a visual or auditory trigger — reacting to someone's movement rather than pre-planned — builds *reactive* agility, which is the kind that matters in sport. Both matter; reactive is harder to develop and more specific.

*Change-of-direction mechanics*: planting and cutting require deceleration before acceleration. The plant foot should hit heel-to-toe, absorbing force through the hip and glute rather than the knee. A collapsed knee on plant is a missed rep and an injury risk.

**Footwork training** (ladder drills): improves foot speed and coordination, not pure lateral quickness. Ladders are coordination training — useful for warming up the neuromuscular system and building footwork vocabulary. Don't confuse a fast ladder with lateral explosiveness.

### Injury Prevention (Prehab)

Injury prevention isn't caution — it's deliberate loading of structures that commonly fail. The three injury-prone areas for basketball players are ankles, knees, and shoulders.

**Ankle stability**: single-leg balance progressions (eyes open → eyes closed → unstable surface), lateral ankle strengthening. Most ankle sprains are re-sprains — the first one creates proprioceptive deficit. Deliberate single-leg work rebuilds it.

**Knee integrity (ACL/patellar)**: the ACL injury epidemic in cutting sports is largely a hip weakness + landing mechanics problem. Weak hip abductors allow the knee to cave medially on landing and cutting — the highest-risk position. Hip abductor work (see lateral quickness section) directly reduces ACL risk. Landing mechanics: cue soft landings with hips back and knees tracking over toes.

**Posterior chain loading**: most overuse injuries in active people trace back to weak posterior chains creating compensatory patterns upstream. Heavy hinge patterns (see [health/strength-training-fundamentals.md](../health/strength-training-fundamentals.md)) are the primary prehab for lower back, hip, and hamstring injuries.

**Rotator cuff**: overhead athletes (throwers) have specific rotator cuff demands; basketball players less so, but shoulder prehab still matters for anyone doing pressing. Band external rotations, face pulls, and Y/T/W raises take 5 minutes and maintain the balance between pressing and pulling.

**Tissue load management**: most overuse injuries (stress fractures, tendinopathies) are simply too much load added too quickly. The 10% rule — don't increase weekly training volume by more than 10% per week — is a rough but useful guardrail. Acute load spikes kill tissue that would survive a gradual build.

**Mobility as prehab**: restricted hips force the lumbar spine to compensate during hinge and squat movements. Restricted thoracic spine forces shoulder compensation during overhead press. Mobility work is structural — see [health/mobility-and-flexibility.md](../health/mobility-and-flexibility.md).

### Energy System Development

Athletic performance draws on three energy systems depending on duration and intensity:

**Phosphocreatine (ATP-PC)**: fuels 0–10 seconds of maximal effort — the first step, a jump, an explosive cut. Recharged in ~60–90 seconds. Train it with short, maximal efforts followed by full rest (sprints, jump sets).

**Glycolytic (anaerobic)**: fuels 10 seconds to 2 minutes of high-intensity effort — a fast break, a defensive possession, interval conditioning. Trained with work intervals of 20–90 seconds at near-maximal intensity. KB swing intervals and court sprints target this system.

**Aerobic**: fuels sustained effort and — crucially — recovery between high-intensity bouts. Athletes with stronger aerobic bases recharge faster between possessions. Zone 2 cardio (walking, easy bike) builds this base. This is why Romi's 10k step daily goal is performance-supporting, not just health-supporting.

**For basketball**: the game is primarily phosphocreatine and glycolytic, with aerobic capacity determining how much those two systems degrade over 4 quarters. Build all three. The biggest mistake is training only one.

## Key Considerations

**S&C is infrastructure, not the building.** Two days per week of S&C is enough to produce measurable athletic development when combined with sport practice. More is not automatically better — recovery is shared with skill sessions, and overloading S&C eats into the quality of sport practice.

**Sequencing matters.** Speed and plyometric work requires a fresh nervous system — always do it first in a session, before strength work. Doing plyos after a heavy squat session degrades mechanics and defeats the purpose.

**Beginners gain from almost anything.** An untrained athlete will see dramatic improvements in the first 6–12 months from almost any systematic athletic training. Specificity matters more after that base is built.

**Minimum effective dose.** Full S&C programming looks like: 1–2 plyometric sessions, 2 strength sessions, 1 speed/agility session per week. This is a lot on top of sport practice and a full-time job. Start with the highest-ROI elements: plyos (power), hip abductor work (injury prevention), short sprints (acceleration). Add from there.

## Getting Started

1. **Identify your current weakest link.** Injury history? Lateral quickness? First-step explosion? Start the S&C work targeting that first — specificity beats completeness when time is limited.
2. **Add plyometrics to existing training.** 10–15 minutes of box jumps and broad jumps before your current strength sessions, twice a week. This alone produces power gains within 6–8 weeks.
3. **Add hip abductor work.** Lateral band walks and clamshells take 5 minutes. Do them in your warmup. This is the single highest-ROI prehab investment for a basketball player.
4. **Add short sprints once a week.** 6–8 × 10-yard sprints with full rest between. Keep it early in the session, keep it honest.
5. **After 8 weeks, reassess.** Are cuts snappier? Does the first step feel different? Then start adding reactive agility work.

## Common Mistakes

**Doing plyos when tired.** Fatigued jumping teaches fatigued mechanics and doesn't train the SSC. If you can't land cleanly, stop.

**Confusing activity with training.** Playing pickup 5x a week is not S&C. S&C is deliberate, targeted development of specific physical qualities outside of the sport.

**Skipping prehab because nothing hurts.** Prehab works by never getting to the injury. Once the tendon or ligament is compromised, rehab takes months and you lose training time you can't get back.

**Adding everything at once.** S&C on top of basketball skill work on top of a strength program is a recovery math problem. Integrate — replace one existing session per week with S&C rather than stacking on top.

**Training speed when fatigued.** Speed work requires neural freshness. A 5x10 sprint after a hard KB session isn't speed training — it's fatigue expression.

## Resources

- *Athletic Body in Balance* — Gary Gray / NSCA. Sport-agnostic movement assessment and corrective framework. Specifically useful for identifying movement deficiencies before they become injuries.
- *Triphasic Training* — Cal Dietz. Advanced plyometric and power development programming. Dense but contains the most rigorous framework for SSC training available in print.
- *Speed, Agility and Quickness for Soccer* — Alan Pearson. Ignore the sport-specific title — the drills and progressions are transferable to any cutting sport and are clearly organized by physical quality.
- NSCA (nsca.com) — National Strength and Conditioning Association. The research-backed professional body for S&C. Position statements on plyometrics, speed, and youth training are freely available.
