# Basketball
*A 5-on-5 team sport built on scoring, defending, and reading the game in real time*

---

## What Is It

Basketball is played between two teams of five on a rectangular court with a hoop at each end. The goal is to score more points than the opponent by putting the ball through their basket. Games are structured around **possessions** — each team alternates between offense (trying to score) and defense (trying to stop the other team).

The game runs on a shot clock (24 seconds in the NBA, 30 in college) — the offense must attempt a shot before time expires or surrender the ball. This forces continuous decision-making and makes tempo a strategic weapon.

## Why It Matters

Basketball is the most accessible team sport — all you need is a ball and a hoop. It develops lateral quickness, explosiveness, coordination, and spatial awareness simultaneously. As a sport to watch, it's uniquely readable once you understand spacing and flow. As a sport to play, even casual pickup games reward basketball IQ over raw athleticism.

## Core Concepts

### Positions

Five positions, each with a distinct role:

- **Point guard (1)** — primary ball-handler and playmaker. Runs the offense, initiates sets, controls tempo.
- **Shooting guard (2)** — scorer and secondary ball-handler. Often the team's best perimeter shooter.
- **Small forward (3)** — versatile wing. Can score inside and out, often guards the opponent's best player.
- **Power forward (4)** — physical player near the basket. Rebounds, defends the post, can shoot from mid-range.
- **Center (5)** — tallest player, anchors the paint. Rim protection on defense, interior scoring on offense.

Modern basketball has blurred these — a player who can shoot, handle, and defend multiple positions is called **positionless**. Most elite teams now play position-free lineups.

### Offense

Offense is about **creating and converting open shots**. A shot is open when the defender can't contest it cleanly. Everything — screens, cuts, ball movement, dribble penetration — is in service of that.

Key offensive principles:
- **Spacing** — spread defenders across the court so driving lanes open. Crowded spacing helps the defense.
- **Ball movement** — passing moves defenders. Still ball = static defense.
- **Read and react** — good offense responds to what the defense gives, not a rigid script.

Covered in depth in [offensive-fundamentals.md](offensive-fundamentals.md).

### Defense

Defense is about **preventing open shots** and forcing the offense into difficult decisions. The best defenses don't just guard the ball — they protect the basket.

Key defensive principles:
- **Positioning** — stay between your man and the basket. One step out of position creates a layup.
- **Communication** — switches, screens, and help rotations require constant talk.
- **Effort** — defense is the most controllable part of the game. You don't need talent to play hard.

Covered in depth in [defensive-fundamentals.md](defensive-fundamentals.md).

### Scoring

Points are scored three ways:
- **2-point field goal** — any shot made inside the three-point arc
- **3-point field goal** — shot made beyond the arc (~23 feet in the NBA)
- **Free throw (1 point)** — uncontested shot from the foul line, awarded after certain fouls

**Efficiency** matters more than volume. A player who shoots 50% on 2-pointers scores the same points-per-shot as one who shoots 33% on 3-pointers. Modern basketball optimizes for high-value shots: layups, dunks, and open threes — and avoids mid-range jumpers (low value, high difficulty).

### Game Flow

A regulation NBA game is 4 × 12-minute quarters. College is 2 × 20-minute halves. Pickup games are typically played to 11 or 21 by ones (each basket = 1 point, 3-pointers count as 2).

**Turnovers** (losing the ball without a shot) are the biggest momentum killers — they give the opponent a free possession. **Rebounding** on both ends is equally critical: offensive rebounds extend possessions, defensive rebounds end the opponent's.

## Key Considerations

**Pickup vs. organized play**: Pickup basketball has its own rules and culture — calling fouls, taking turns, respect for the court. Learn the social norms before assuming structured-game rules apply.

**Athleticism is less important than IQ early on**: A player who understands spacing, knows when to pass, and plays hard defense is more valuable than an athletic player with no feel for the game. Skill development comes from reps, not just physical gifts.

**Watching builds vocabulary**: Watching NBA or college basketball with intent — tracking how defenses rotate, where cutters go, how spacing shifts — builds understanding that speeds up your own development.

## Getting Started

1. **Watch 5 full games** with intent — pick one team each game and track only their defensive rotations. Builds pattern recognition before you play.
2. **Find a hoop and work on shooting form** — even 20 minutes alone at a hoop ingrains the muscle memory. See [skill-development.md](skill-development.md) for form basics.
3. **Play pickup** — no better teacher than live reps. Expect to be bad. Look for open teammates before looking to score.
4. **Learn one thing per session** — don't try to improve everything. Pick one focus (finishing at the rim, staying in front of your man) and execute it intentionally.

## Common Mistakes

- **Ball-watching** — staring at the ball instead of your man on defense. You lose track of cutters and give up easy baskets.
- **Dribbling without purpose** — dribbling to dribble, not to create. Every dribble should have a destination.
- **Ignoring defense** — most beginners focus on scoring. Teams win with defense. It also gets you on the court in pickup games.
- **Forcing shots** — taking a contested shot because you "feel ready" instead of reading whether it's actually open.
- **Not talking** — silence on defense means missed switches and open shooters.
