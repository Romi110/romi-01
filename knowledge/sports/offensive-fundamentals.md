# Offensive Fundamentals
*How basketball offense works — spacing, movement, shot creation, and decision-making*

---

## What Is It

Offensive fundamentals are the principles and skills that allow a team or individual to create and convert high-quality shots. The overview of offense is in [basketball.md](basketball.md). This file goes deeper: how spacing works, what makes a good shot, and how to read defenses to create opportunities.

## Why It Matters

Offense isn't just scoring — it's decision-making under time pressure. The same mental skills apply: read the situation, act on what's there, don't force what isn't. Poor offensive players react to their own impulse (I want to shoot). Good offensive players react to the defense (the defense gave me this, I'll take it).

## Core Concepts

### Spacing

Spacing is the foundation of all offense. When offensive players are spread across the court, defenders can't simultaneously guard their man and help on drives. When players crowd the paint, one defender can cover two people.

**The basic principle**: if you're not the ball-handler and you're not cutting, stand at a spot that stretches the defense — corner, wing, or top of the arc. Don't drift toward the ball. Drifting collapses spacing and kills driving lanes.

**Corner three**: the most efficient spot on the floor. Closest three-point shot, and standing in the corner pulls a defender far from the paint. A player who can shoot from the corner forces the defense to make a hard choice every time the ball enters the paint.

### Ball Movement and the Extra Pass

Moving the ball forces defenders to move. A pass shifts the entire defensive structure — rotations happen, someone is briefly out of position. The **extra pass** is the pass made after a good pass: the ball goes to the open man, who immediately finds the *more* open man. Teams that make the extra pass consistently get open threes and layups. Teams that stop at the first pass get contested shots.

**Why players don't make the extra pass**: ego (I want my shot), lack of vision (didn't see the open man), slowness (the window closed by the time they processed it). All three improve with deliberate attention.

### Shot Selection

Not all open shots are equal. A framework:

- **Layups and dunks** — highest percentage shots. Always the right choice if available.
- **Open corner three** — efficient. Take it if you're a competent shooter.
- **Open above-the-break three** — slightly less efficient than the corner but still correct if open.
- **Mid-range jumper** — low value, moderate difficulty. Avoid unless it's a specific weapon you've built.
- **Contested shot of any kind** — low value. The defender being close drops your percentage by 15–20 points. Pass out and reset.

**The rule**: if the defense gives you a high-value shot, take it. If it doesn't, move the ball until it does.

### Dribble Penetration

Driving into the paint attacks the defense at its core. When the ball-handler beats their defender, two things happen: a help defender must leave their man, and the offense is now facing a rotation. Both create open shots — either a floater/layup for the driver, or a kick-out pass to the player whose defender helped.

**Reading the drive**:
- If help comes late → finish at the rim
- If help comes early → kick out to the open shooter
- If no help comes → the defender sagged off, punish it with a floater

**Floater**: a high-arcing shot over a shot-blocker's reach, released before reaching the rim. Essential for guards who penetrate against taller defenders.

### Screens

A screen (pick) is when an offensive player uses their body to block a defender, freeing a teammate. Two main actions come from screens:

**Pick and roll**: screener sets a screen on the ball-handler's defender, then rolls (cuts) toward the basket after the ball-handler uses the screen. Creates two problems for the defense simultaneously — guard the ball-handler coming off the screen AND the screener rolling to the rim.

**Catch-and-shoot off a screen**: the ball-handler uses a screen to get open for a shot. The screener stands still ("pick and pop") as a secondary option. Used heavily in off-ball movement.

**Using a screen well**: the offensive player must wait for the screen to be set, run their defender into it (not around it), and read what the defense does. If the defender goes under the screen → pull up for a shot. If they go over → keep driving.

### Reading the Defense

Offense is a conversation with the defense. Every action has a counter:

| Defense does this | Offense does this |
|---|---|
| Guard sags off (gives space) | Shoot the open shot |
| Guard cheats toward ball | Back-cut behind them to the basket |
| Help defense collapses | Kick to open shooter on the perimeter |
| Defender overplays passing lane | Drive the other direction |
| Double team on the post | Pass to the open man the double creates |

The goal isn't to execute a play — it's to read what the defense gives and take it.

## Key Considerations

**Offense starts with positioning before you touch the ball.** Where you stand before you receive a pass determines what's available when you catch it. Getting to the right spot, at the right time, is half the job.

**Rhythm vs. freelancing**: good teams have structures (plays, sets) that put players in position. But reads happen within the structure. Plays create advantages — basketball IQ converts them.

**Turnovers are the worst offensive outcome.** A missed shot gives the defense a rebound — they still have to earn the possession. A turnover gives it to them free. Protect the ball more than you protect your stats.

## Common Mistakes

- **Standing and watching** — if you don't have the ball, you're not done. Move, cut, or space. Defenders who stand get ignored.
- **Over-dribbling** — dribbling until a shot opportunity dies. Pass and cut, or drive with intent.
- **Taking the first look** — catching a pass and immediately shooting or driving without reading. Take one beat to see the floor.
- **Ignoring the screener** — on a pick-and-roll, beginners use the screen and forget the rolling big is often the best option.
- **Hero ball** — forcing a tough shot when the ball movement would create an easy one for someone else.
