# Defensive Fundamentals
*How basketball defense works — positioning, man coverage, rotations, and protecting the basket*

---

## What Is It

Defensive fundamentals are the principles that prevent the offense from getting open shots. The overview is in [basketball.md](basketball.md). This file covers the specifics: stance, man-to-man coverage, help rotations, and the difference between guarding the ball and protecting the basket.

## Why It Matters

Defense is the part of basketball most within your control. Offense requires your shot to go in — defense requires only effort and positioning. A player who commits to defense gets played regardless of offensive ability. In pickup, showing you can guard someone earns respect faster than scoring does.

There's a Stoic logic here: defense is the discipline side of basketball. You don't control whether your shots fall. You control whether you're in the right spot, talking, and working.

## Core Concepts

### Defensive Stance

Everything starts with stance. A good defensive stance:
- Feet shoulder-width apart, slightly wider
- Knees bent, weight on the balls of your feet (not heels)
- Hips low — if your hips are high, you'll be slow laterally
- Hands active — one low (mirror the ball), one up (contest passes)
- Eyes on the defender's chest/hips, not the ball — ball fakes work on eye-watchers, hip fakes don't

**Sliding, not crossing**: when moving laterally, slide your feet — don't cross them. Crossing feet means a moment where you can't change direction. Sliding keeps you ready.

### On-Ball Defense

On-ball defense is guarding the player with the ball. The goal is to **influence where they go**, not just react.

**Positioning**:
- Stay between your man and the basket at all times
- Give enough space that they can't blow past you in one step, but not so much that they get an open shot
- Against a strong driver: give a half-step more space to keep your feet under you
- Against a shooter: close out fast, hand up, don't leave your feet until they do

**Closeout**: when running to contest a shooter who just caught the ball, sprint to close the distance, then chop your steps in the final few feet to brake. A full-speed closeout runs past the shooter or flies past them when they pump fake.

**Riding the drive**: when the ball-handler beats you off the dribble, don't reach — sprint to recover the angle. Reaching fouls. Getting your body in front of them again forces a tougher shot.

### Help Defense and Rotations

Man-to-man defense is never purely one-on-one. Every defender on the court has a responsibility off the ball.

**The help line**: when your man doesn't have the ball, position yourself on an imaginary line between your man and the ball. You can see both, and you're in position to help if the ball-handler drives.

**Help and recover**: when a teammate gets beaten, you step into the lane to stop the ball (help), then recover back to your man if the ball-handler kicks it out. This is a rotation — and it must happen at the right time or it creates a worse opening somewhere else.

**The rotation chain**: when one defender helps, the rest of the defense must rotate toward the side the help came from. The weak-side corner shooter becomes the most dangerous player after a help rotation — someone must pick them up.

**Communicating rotations**: "Ball!" (your man has the ball), "Help!" (calling for help), "Switch!" (trading assignments on a screen), "Hedge!" (step up to slow the ball-handler on a pick-and-roll). Silence means missed rotations.

### Man-to-Man vs. Zone

**Man-to-man**: each defender is responsible for one offensive player. Clean accountability — you know exactly who you're guarding. Forces players to be individually capable.

**Zone defense**: defenders guard areas of the court, not specific players. Common zones:
- **2-3 zone**: two defenders at the top, three along the baseline. Strong in the paint, weak at the three-point line and in the gaps.
- **3-2 zone**: three at the top, two on the baseline. Opposite strengths/weaknesses.
- **1-3-1**: one up top, three in the middle, one at the baseline. Aggressive and trapping-oriented.

**Zone weaknesses**: the seams between zones. Players on offense pass quickly to move the zone, then attack the gaps before defenders can shift.

**When to use zone**: to disrupt a team's offensive rhythm, protect defenders in foul trouble, or exploit a team that can't shoot from outside. Zone is not a substitute for individual defensive effort — good ball movement beats zone every time.

### Defending the Pick-and-Roll

The pick-and-roll is the most common play in basketball. Two defensive responses:

**Hedge and recover**: the screener's defender steps out aggressively to stop the ball-handler while the ball-handler's original defender fights over the screen to recover. Protects against the drive; leaves the rolling big momentarily open.

**Switch**: both defenders trade assignments — the screener's defender takes the ball-handler, and the ball-handler's defender takes the screener. Clean and simple, but creates mismatches if one defender is much smaller or larger than their new assignment.

**Going under**: the ball-handler's defender goes under the screen (between the screener and the basket). Only valid if the ball-handler is a poor shooter — it gives them an open pull-up.

### Rebounding

Defense ends with a rebound. A missed shot is wasted if the offense gets the ball back.

**Box out**: when a shot goes up, turn and place your body in front of your man — not to watch the shot, but to keep them from the ball. One step toward them, pivot, and seal. This is effort, not athleticism.

**Defensive rebounding rate** is one of the most predictive stats in basketball — teams that rebound well after misses don't give up second-chance points, which are among the easiest points in the game.

## Key Considerations

**The best defense is the one you execute correctly.** Man-to-man done poorly is worse than zone done well. Know what you're running and know your assignment at all times.

**Fatigue is a defensive problem first.** When tired, your stance gets high, your feet slow, and you start reaching. Conditioning is a defensive asset.

**Foul trouble changes everything.** When a defender has 2–3 fouls early, they play tentatively — they can't challenge drives. Limiting fouls while playing hard defense is a skill: contest shots without leaving your feet on fakes, don't reach on drives.

## Common Mistakes

- **Ball-watching** — taking your eyes off your man to watch the ball. Cutters score when defenders ball-watch.
- **Reaching** — slapping at the ball instead of staying in front. Reaches foul, they don't stop drives.
- **Not boxing out** — running toward the rim to grab the rebound instead of sealing your man first. The person closest to the ball doesn't always win — the person with inside position does.
- **Relaxing off the ball** — assuming you don't matter because the ball is on the other side. The skip pass finds the open man in the corner in one second.
- **Not talking** — missed screens, missed switches, open shooters. All caused by silence.
