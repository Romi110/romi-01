# Programming a Training Block for Fat Loss
*How to structure an 8-week KB/mace block specifically for body recomposition — training design, progression rules, nutrition integration, and how to track what's actually working*

---

## What Is It

A training block is a planned multi-week period with a defined goal, specific sessions, built-in progression, and a scheduled deload. This file is the applied layer on [health/strength-training-fundamentals.md](strength-training-fundamentals.md) — not the principles, but the actual program design for Romi's specific goal: **body recomposition**, the combination of losing fat and building or maintaining muscle simultaneously.

This is different from a simple "cut" (caloric deficit + any training) or a "bulk" (surplus + heavy lifting). Recomposition is the middle path: near-maintenance or small deficit calories, high protein, strength training to preserve/build muscle, conditioning to increase energy expenditure. Slower than a hard cut but avoids muscle loss and rebound.

## Why It Matters

Training without a plan produces random results. But more specifically: **fat loss without deliberate structure often loses muscle along with fat**, producing a lighter but softer body with worse long-term metabolism. The structure in this file is specifically designed to prevent that — to create a caloric deficit primarily through activity while preserving the training stimulus needed to maintain and build muscle.

## The Fat Loss Equation First

Before programming, be clear on what actually drives fat loss:

**Fat loss = sustained caloric deficit over time.** No training program creates fat loss if calories eaten exceed calories burned. Training matters for three reasons:
1. It increases energy expenditure (burns calories during and after sessions)
2. It preserves and builds muscle (which maintains resting metabolic rate and produces the "recomposition" in body recomposition)
3. It improves insulin sensitivity (which affects how the body partitions calories — toward muscle vs. fat storage)

**The numbers**:
- 1 lb of fat ≈ 3,500 calories
- A 500 cal/day deficit = ~1 lb/week fat loss (theoretical maximum without muscle loss)
- A 250 cal/day deficit = ~0.5 lb/week — slower, but higher percentage of loss is fat rather than muscle
- For recomposition (not hard cut): target 200–350 cal/day deficit on average, higher protein to protect muscle

**NEAT is the underrated lever.** Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis — all movement that isn't formal exercise. Walking, fidgeting, standing vs. sitting. Romi's 10,000 steps/day goal burns roughly 400–600 extra calories per day compared to a sedentary baseline. This is the single most sustainable fat-loss tool available, because it doesn't trigger the appetite compensation that intense training does. Protect the walks.

**Training contributes 300–600 calories per session** depending on intensity and duration. HIIT burns less during the session than slow steady cardio (shorter duration) but more in total if it leads to higher overall activity. Strength training burns less during the session than cardio but creates the metabolic environment that preserves muscle during a deficit.

## The 8-Week Block Structure

### Goal: Body recomposition with a fat-loss lean

**Primary**: reduce body fat while maintaining or building lean muscle  
**Secondary**: increase work capacity (conditioning improves week over week)  
**Tertiary**: improve movement quality (KB hinge depth, mace arc, overhead stability)

### Weekly Template

| Day | Session | Focus | Duration |
|-----|---------|-------|----------|
| Mon | KB Strength — Push/Squat | Vertical press, goblet squat, swings | 35–45 min |
| Tue | Walk | Zone 2, 10k steps | ~90 min total |
| Wed | KB Conditioning + HIIT | Swing intervals, AMRAP, metabolic circuit | 25–35 min |
| Thu | Walk | Zone 2, 10k steps | ~90 min total |
| Fri | KB Strength — Hinge/Pull + Mace | Deadlift, rows, mace 360s | 35–45 min |
| Sat | Long walk or outdoor activity | 60+ min | Active recovery |
| Sun | Mobility / light stretch | Priority joints | 15–20 min |

**Three types of sessions in the week**:
- **2 strength sessions** (Mon + Fri): preserve and build muscle. These are the non-negotiables. Missing a strength session hurts recomposition more than missing a conditioning session.
- **1 conditioning/HIIT session** (Wed): increases weekly energy expenditure, builds work capacity, trains anaerobic system. Keep this honest — true HIIT means going hard.
- **Daily walking** (Tue, Thu, Sat + any other day): the primary fat-loss tool. Non-negotiable at 10k steps minimum.

### The Two Strength Sessions

**Monday — Push/Squat/Swing**

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| KB Press (single arm) | 4 × 8 each | Rest 90s between sides |
| Goblet Squat | 4 × 10 | Pause 1s at bottom, drive through heels |
| KB Swing | 4 × 15 | Explosive hip drive, reset each rep |
| Push-up | 3 × max | Full range, lock out at top |
| Plank | 3 × 40s | Squeeze glutes and abs simultaneously |

**Friday — Hinge/Pull/Mace**

| Exercise | Sets × Reps | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| KB Romanian Deadlift | 4 × 10 | Slow eccentric (3 count down), hip hinge |
| KB Row (single arm) | 4 × 10 each | Chest-supported or braced, full ROM |
| KB Swing | 4 × 15 | Same as Monday — skill practice across both sessions |
| Mace 360 | 3 × 10 each direction | Thoracic rotation, shoulder packed |
| Farmer Carry | 3 × 40 steps | Heavy, upright posture, no lean |

### Wednesday — Conditioning Session

Pick one of these rotating formats. Rotate through them across the 8 weeks so the body doesn't adapt to a single stimulus.

**Option A — Swing Intervals (baseline)**  
30s KB swings / 30s rest × 12 rounds (12 minutes total)  
Use a weight you can swing explosively for the full 30s. When you can complete all 12 rounds without breaking form, increase weight or go to 40s on / 20s off.

**Option B — EMOM (Every Minute on the Minute)**  
Minutes 1–5: 15 KB swings  
Minutes 6–10: 8 KB goblet squats  
Minutes 11–15: 6 KB rows each arm  
Rest for whatever time remains in each minute. If you finish 10+ seconds early, increase the rep count. If you're not finishing in time, reduce reps.

**Option C — AMRAP (As Many Rounds As Possible)**  
Set a timer for 15–20 minutes. Complete as many rounds as possible of:  
- 10 KB swings  
- 8 goblet squats  
- 6 KB press (each arm)  
- 30s plank  
Record rounds completed. Add 1–2 reps to any exercise when you improve by 2+ rounds.

**Option D — Mace Conditioning Circuit**  
4 rounds, minimal rest between exercises, 90s between rounds:  
- Mace 360: 10 each direction  
- KB swing: 15  
- Mace spear + pull: 8  
- Push-up: max reps  

### Progression Rules (Week by Week)

Progressive overload for fat loss works the same way as for hypertrophy — you must keep adding challenge. For a fat-loss phase, adding reps before weight is often smarter (more total work = more caloric expenditure without the fatigue of heavier loads).

**Rule 1 — Rep progression first**: if you can complete all prescribed reps with 2+ reps left in the tank on the last set, add 2 reps to each set next session.

**Rule 2 — Load progression**: once you hit the top of the rep range prescribed (e.g., 4×12 when programmed at 4×10), increase weight by the smallest increment available (usually 2–4kg for KB). Reset to 4×8 with the new weight.

**Rule 3 — Conditioning progression**: for HIIT sessions, track rounds or intervals completed. Add intensity (more reps, shorter rest, heavier KB) when you consistently finish without form breakdown.

**What NOT to do for fat loss**: don't dramatically reduce weights "to do more reps for fat loss." The muscle-preserving signal comes from challenging loads. Light weights with high reps do not preserve muscle as effectively as moderate-heavy weights with moderate reps.

### Weekly Progression by Week

| Weeks | Strength Focus | Conditioning | Notes |
|-------|---------------|--------------|-------|
| 1–2 | Establish baseline, perfect form, note starting weights | Option A — learn the format | Don't push to failure; assess movement quality |
| 3–4 | Add 1–2 reps per set vs. weeks 1–2 | Option B or C, add reps vs. week 1 | First progression point |
| 5–6 | Load increase where reps hit ceiling, or volume increase (+1 set) | Option C or D, longer duration | Peak volume weeks |
| 7 | Deload — 50% volume, same weights | Light EMOM, shorter AMRAP | Recovery week |
| 8 | Test week — repeat week 1 sessions and compare performance | Option A at heavier weight | Measure improvement |

## Nutrition Integration

Training without nutrition alignment won't produce fat loss. These are the minimum parameters — [health/nutrition.md](nutrition.md) and [health/meal-prep.md](meal-prep.md) have the detail.

**Caloric target for recomposition**:
- Estimate TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure): bodyweight in lbs × 14–16 (moderately active). For 170 lbs with daily walks + 3x training: ~2,400–2,700 cal/day maintenance.
- Recomposition deficit: eat 200–400 calories below maintenance on average. Not rigidly every day — slightly higher on training days (±100–200), slightly lower on rest days. This small deficit with high protein is the recomposition formula.

**Protein is the non-negotiable**: 0.8–1g per lb of bodyweight, every day, including rest days. Muscle protein synthesis requires dietary protein; it doesn't pause because you didn't train. For vegetarian sources: paneer, dal, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, eggs, tofu, quinoa, whey — [health/nutrition.md](nutrition.md) covers portions.

**Carbs and training**: carbohydrate is muscle's preferred fuel for high-intensity work (swings, HIIT). Eating adequate carbs is not counterproductive to fat loss on a moderate deficit — cutting carbs too aggressively reduces training performance, which reduces the quality of the stimulus, which reduces muscle preservation. Don't carb-cut to the point where Wednesday's conditioning session feels depleted.

**Meal timing for training days**: eat a mixed meal (protein + carbs) 1.5–2 hours before strength sessions. After training, prioritize protein within 1–2 hours (post-workout window is real but not as narrow as old fitness culture suggested — within a few hours is fine). See [health/fasting-and-eating-windows.md](fasting-and-eating-windows.md) if you're using an eating window.

## Tracking Progress

Fat loss progress is noisy week to week. Body weight fluctuates 2–4 lbs daily based on water, sodium, and glycogen. You need multiple signals to read actual progress.

**Body weight**: weigh daily first thing in the morning, average the 7-day readings. Single day weights are noise; the weekly average trend is signal. Expect the average to move 0.25–0.75 lbs/week on a recomposition deficit.

**Measurements**: waist (at navel), hips, and if you want — chest, upper arm, thigh. Measure every 2 weeks. These change more slowly than the scale but more accurately reflect fat loss vs. scale weight fluctuations.

**Progress photos**: same lighting, same time of day, same angles, every 2–3 weeks. The camera sees body composition changes more clearly than the scale. Especially useful for catching muscle gain that offsets fat loss on the scale.

**Training performance**: the most underused metric. If your swings are heavier, your AMRAP rounds are higher, and your conditioning sessions don't destroy you the way they did in week 1 — that's concrete evidence of adaptation. Performance improving while scale weight drops is the ideal recomposition signal.

**What not to track obsessively**: caloric burn from fitness apps and smartwatches. They're wrong by 20–30% consistently. Use them for trend data (this week vs. last week), not absolute numbers.

## The Deload (Week 7)

A deload is not optional — it's part of the program. During weeks 5–6 (peak volume), connective tissue, joints, and the nervous system accumulate fatigue that doesn't show up as soreness but does show up as flat performance, elevated resting heart rate, and poor sleep quality.

**Deload parameters**:
- Keep all exercises the same (important — this isn't a rest week, it's a recovery week)
- Cut sets by 50% (4 sets → 2 sets)
- Keep weight the same or slightly reduce
- Skip the HIIT session; replace with a walk or easy mobility
- Sleep an extra hour if possible
- Eat at or slightly above maintenance during the deload week (this aids full recovery without setting back fat loss significantly)

**What a deload does**: glycogen replenishes, connective tissue recovers, the nervous system resets. After a proper deload, week 8 usually produces the best performance of the block — lifts feel easier, conditioning sessions feel sharper.

## Key Considerations

**The scale will stall for weeks at a time.** This is not failure — it's how body recomposition works. Muscle is being built while fat is being lost; these can offset each other on the scale for 2–4 weeks before the fat loss shows. The measurements and training performance tell the real story during stalls.

**Two strength sessions is the minimum to preserve muscle on a deficit.** One is not enough. Three is ideal but requires the recovery to support it. With daily walking and HIIT, two sessions is the right balance for a full-time job schedule.

**Cardio does not eat muscle at moderate levels.** The concern about cardio "burning muscle" applies to very high volume endurance training (marathon training, daily 2-hour sessions). Daily walking and 1 HIIT session per week do not produce measurable muscle loss if protein is adequate.

**The fat-loss plateau is usually a diet problem, not a training problem.** When fat loss stalls beyond 3–4 weeks despite the training staying hard, the issue is almost always caloric intake creep — portions growing, tracking loosening. Add the training; tighten the tracking.

**After the 8-week block, reassess.** Run one block, measure the results, then decide: run a second similar block (if fat loss goal isn't met), shift to a maintenance/strength block (if the goal is met and you want to consolidate), or increase volume for a harder push (if recovery is strong).

## Common Mistakes

**Cutting calories too aggressively from week 1.** A 700+ calorie/day deficit will produce faster scale weight loss but significant muscle loss alongside fat. Recomposition requires patience — the deficit must be moderate.

**Making the strength sessions "cardio" on a fat-loss block.** Short rest periods, light weights, chasing sweat. This reduces the training stimulus for muscle preservation. Keep the strength sessions strength sessions — adequate weight, adequate rest, adequate effort.

**Not tracking food on the first block.** You don't have to track forever, but tracking for the first 4 weeks calibrates portion intuition accurately. Most people eat 20–40% more than they estimate. One accurate 4-week tracking period is worth months of guessing.

**Skipping the deload.** The most common mistake. Week 7 feels like a waste when you're building momentum. It isn't — it's what makes week 8 possible.

**Measuring success only by scale weight.** If the scale hasn't moved in 2 weeks but your AMRAP round count is up and your waist measurement is down, that is successful recomposition. Read all the signals.
