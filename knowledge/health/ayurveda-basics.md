# Ayurveda Basics
*India's 3,000-year system for maintaining health through daily rhythm, food, and constitution — practical without the mysticism.*

---

## What Is It
Ayurveda is a system of medicine and daily living that originated in India around 1500–2000 BCE. The name means "knowledge of life" (*ayur* = life, *veda* = knowledge). Its central premise: health is not a fixed state you either have or don't — it's a dynamic balance you maintain through daily choices about food, sleep, movement, and routine.

Unlike Western medicine, which intervenes when something goes wrong, Ayurveda is a prevention and maintenance system. Its instructions are for the well person trying to stay well, not the sick person trying to recover.

You don't have to accept the metaphysical framework to use it. The practices stand on their own.

## Why It Matters
Ayurveda codified practical health principles — food timing, digestive health, daily rhythm, seasonal adaptation — thousands of years before modern chronobiology and nutrition science. Much of it holds up. For Romi: this is the most actionable traditional health system, particularly for daily routine design, digestion, and seasonal eating.

## Core Concepts

### The Three Doshas — Your Constitutional Type
Ayurveda organizes all physiology around three forces (*doshas*): Vata, Pitta, and Kapha. Every person has a natural balance of all three (*prakriti*) and a current state (*vikriti*). Health is staying close to your prakriti.

| Dosha | Elements | Qualities | In balance | Out of balance |
|-------|---------|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Vata | Air + Space | Light, dry, mobile, cold | Creative, energetic, quick | Anxious, scattered, poor sleep, constipation |
| Pitta | Fire + Water | Hot, sharp, intense | Sharp mind, driven, good digestion | Irritable, inflamed, heartburn, impatience |
| Kapha | Earth + Water | Heavy, slow, cool, stable | Calm, strong, steady | Sluggish, congested, weight gain, depression |

**Most people are dual-dosha** (e.g., Vata-Pitta). You don't need a practitioner to get value here — just notice what qualities feel dominant for you and which imbalances you tend toward.

**Practical use:** Rather than trying to determine your exact prakriti, use the dosha qualities directionally. If you're running hot, irritable, and inflamed — cool it down (food, environment, pace). If you're scattered, anxious, and constipated — ground and warm things up.

### Dinacharya — The Daily Routine
This is Ayurveda's most practical and portable concept: a structured daily routine that maintains biological rhythm. The consistency of the routine *is* the practice — it's not about what each individual element does, it's about the aggregate effect of living in rhythm.

**Core dinacharya practices (in order):**

| Time | Practice | Why |
|------|---------|-----|
| Wake before sunrise (~6am) | Rise consistent with daylight | Aligns circadian rhythm, Vata time (5–7am) is best for mental clarity |
| Tongue scraping | Remove bacterial coating from overnight | Removes ama (waste) before it gets reabsorbed; improves digestion and taste |
| Oil pulling (optional) | Swish sesame or coconut oil for 5–10 min | Oral health; draws out bacteria |
| Warm water | Drink 1–2 glasses, room temp or warm | Stimulates digestion and bowel movement |
| Abhyanga (self-massage) | Warm oil massage before shower | Nervous system regulation, circulation, skin health, grounding |
| Movement / yoga / training | Before breakfast if possible | Kapha time (6–10am) — body is ready for physical output |
| Breakfast | Light — not the biggest meal | Digestive fire (agni) not yet at peak |
| Midday meal | Largest meal of the day | Agni peaks at midday; digest the most here |
| Evening meal | Small, easy to digest, by 6–7pm | Agni slows after sunset; heavy food at night burdens digestion |
| Wind down | No screens, low light, by 9–10pm | Supports sleep onset and melatonin production |
| Sleep | Before 11pm ideally | Pitta time (10pm–2am) is for cellular repair |

You don't implement all of this at once. Pick the two or three practices that would change your day most if they were consistent. Start there.

### Agni — Digestive Fire
Agni is the central concept in Ayurvedic nutrition. It refers to the metabolic and digestive capacity of the body — how well you break down food, absorb nutrients, and eliminate waste.

Strong agni = clear mind, stable energy, healthy weight, good immunity.
Weak agni = fatigue after eating, bloating, brain fog, accumulation of *ama* (metabolic waste).

**How to strengthen agni:**
- Eat your largest meal at midday — agni naturally peaks with the sun.
- Don't eat until genuinely hungry and the previous meal is digested (~4–6 hours for a full meal).
- Eat warm, cooked food — especially in winter. Raw food is harder to digest.
- Avoid ice water during meals — dampens agni.
- Small amount of ginger, cumin, or black pepper before eating stimulates digestive enzymes.
- Don't overeat — agni can't process more than it has capacity for.

### Seasonal Eating (Ritucharya)
Ayurveda adjusts food and routine with the season. The logic: your constitution and environment interact, and what supports health in July is different from what supports health in January.

**NJ-relevant seasonal guidance:**

| Season | Qualities | Eat more | Eat less |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| Summer | Hot, dry | Cooling foods (cucumber, coconut, mint, sweet fruit, dairy) | Spicy, oily, heavy foods |
| Fall/early winter | Cold, dry, windy | Warming, oily, grounding foods (root vegetables, ghee, sesame, soups) | Raw foods, cold foods, light salads |
| Late winter/spring | Cold, wet, heavy | Light, warm, spicy foods; bitter greens; ginger, turmeric | Heavy dairy, sweets, excess grains |

**The practical takeaway:** Eat warm, dense, oily food in cold weather. Eat light, cooling food in hot weather. This is what your body naturally wants — Ayurveda just makes it explicit.

### Ama — Metabolic Waste
Ama is the Ayurvedic concept for undigested food and metabolic byproducts that accumulate when agni is weak. You can roughly map it to modern concepts like chronic inflammation, gut dysbiosis, and lymphatic congestion.

Signs of ama accumulation: morning tongue coating, brain fog, fatigue after meals, body heaviness, weakened immunity.

**Reducing ama:** Strong agni prevents its formation. Fasting, ginger tea, light seasonal eating, and movement burn through existing accumulation.

### Herbs and Supplements
Ayurveda has an extensive pharmacopeia. The most well-studied and practical for daily use:

| Herb | Primary use | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Ashwagandha | Adaptogen — reduces cortisol, supports energy and recovery | Evidence-backed; 300–600mg KSM-66 extract is the studied form |
| Triphala | Digestive health, gentle laxative, antioxidant | Three-fruit blend; use at night to support bowel regularity |
| Turmeric + black pepper | Anti-inflammatory | Black pepper increases curcumin absorption ~2000%; use together |
| Ginger | Digestive stimulant, anti-nausea, anti-inflammatory | Fresh or dry; add to food or tea |
| Brahmi (Bacopa) | Cognitive function, memory | Takes 4–6 weeks to show effect; well-studied |

Start with food-based use (ginger, turmeric in cooking) before adding supplements. The herbs amplify an existing foundation — they don't replace it.

## Key Considerations

**Ayurveda is highly individual.** The same food or practice can be right for one person and wrong for another depending on constitution and season. Use it directionally — move toward balance, don't follow prescriptions rigidly.

**Daily routine beats intensity.** Ayurveda doesn't value dramatic interventions. It values *dinacharya* — the steady, daily practice that prevents accumulation. Same principle as discipline.

**Digestion is the foundation.** Almost every chronic issue in Ayurveda traces back to impaired agni. Before adding supplements or complex practices, optimize digestion: meal timing, food temperature, portion size, food combinations.

## Abhyanga — The One Practice Worth Starting With
Self-massage with warm oil before showering. 5–10 minutes.

**Why:** The skin is a sensory organ wired to the nervous system. Slow, deliberate touch activates the parasympathetic nervous system — the same response as meditation. Daily abhyanga reduces cortisol, improves circulation, grounds Vata, and has the cumulative effect of starting every day with something calming and intentional.

**How:** Warm sesame oil (cooling in summer: coconut). Apply from scalp to feet, moving toward the heart. Use long strokes on limbs, circular on joints. Let sit 5 minutes if possible, then shower.

Practically — it's 5 minutes of intentional contact with your own body before the day starts. Most people who try it for 30 days continue indefinitely.

## Getting Started

1. **Tongue scraping** — costs nothing, takes 30 seconds, done before brushing teeth. Best first practice.
2. **Warm water on waking** — before coffee, before food. 2 minutes.
3. **Midday as the main meal** — shift your largest eating window to lunch if it isn't already.
4. **Last meal before 7pm** — specifically observe how your sleep and morning energy change.
5. After 30 days on those: try abhyanga for one week.

## Common Mistakes

- **Starting with doshas as a personality quiz.** Dosha theory is useful for directional guidance, not self-categorization.
- **Buying the herbs before doing the practices.** Ashwagandha won't fix a 6am phone habit and midnight eating. Routine comes first.
- **All-or-nothing dinacharya.** Doing 2 practices consistently beats starting all 12 and sustaining none.
- **Ignoring season.** Following Ayurvedic summer protocols in winter is actively counterproductive.
- **Substituting Ayurveda for medical care.** This system is for maintenance and prevention. It is not treatment for diagnosed conditions.
