# Fasting and Eating Windows
*Time-restricted eating and periodic fasting — how limiting when you eat improves digestion, metabolic health, and energy.*

---

## What Is It
Fasting is the deliberate restriction of eating to a defined window of time, or the periodic abstention from food entirely. Time-restricted eating (TRE) compresses all eating into an 8–10 hour window each day. Longer fasts — 24 to 72+ hours — are periodic and serve different purposes.

The core principle isn't which foods you eat — it's when. Your digestive system, hormones, and metabolism are governed by circadian rhythms. Eating in alignment with those rhythms improves how your body processes food. Eating outside them — late at night, erratically, or continuously — disrupts every downstream system.

## Why It Matters
Most people eat across 14–16 hours of the day without thinking about it. The research on narrowing that window is consistent: better insulin sensitivity, improved sleep quality, cleaner digestion, and often easier body composition management — without calorie counting. For Romi: this is one of the highest-leverage changes with the lowest effort cost. It doesn't require new food, new prep, or new skill — just a boundary on when eating happens.

## Core Concepts

### Why Timing Matters — The Circadian Connection
Every organ involved in digestion and metabolism — the stomach, liver, pancreas, gut — has its own circadian clock. These clocks coordinate digestive enzyme secretion, insulin sensitivity, and nutrient processing. They expect food during daylight hours and expect rest at night.

**Insulin sensitivity is highest in the morning and drops through the day.** The same meal eaten at 8am produces a lower blood glucose spike than the same meal eaten at 8pm. Eating late at night means processing food with reduced metabolic machinery — more is stored as fat, digestion is slower, and sleep quality drops.

**The migrating motor complex (MMC)** is the intestinal cleaning cycle. It sweeps the small intestine between meals, moving waste through and preventing bacterial overgrowth. It requires a ~4–5 hour fasted window to activate. Constant eating (grazing, late snacks, eating within 1–2 hours of sleep) suppresses it. Chronic MMC suppression is associated with bloating, sluggishness, and poor gut health.

### Time-Restricted Eating (TRE)
The most practical fasting protocol for daily use. All eating happens within a set window each day.

| Window | Protocol | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 12:12 | 12 hours eating, 12 hours fasting | Minimum to see any benefit; easy to achieve |
| 10:14 | 10 hours eating, 14 hours fasting | Good starting point; e.g., 8am–6pm |
| 8:16 | 8 hours eating, 16 hours fasting | Most studied protocol; e.g., 10am–6pm |
| 6:18 | 6 hours eating, 18 hours fasting | More aggressive; not necessary for most |

**The window should be anchored early, not late.** An 8am–4pm window produces measurably better outcomes than a noon–8pm window, even at the same total hours. This is because insulin sensitivity is highest in the first half of the day. If you can only shift one thing, close the eating window earlier in the evening.

**Coffee and non-caloric beverages during the fasted window:** Black coffee and plain tea don't meaningfully break a fast. They suppress appetite and have negligible metabolic effects. Anything with calories — cream, milk, sugar — breaks the fast.

### What Fasting Does
**During the fasted window:**
- Insulin levels drop → fat burning can occur (insulin presence suppresses lipolysis)
- MMC activates → digestive tract cleans
- Autophagy begins (cellular cleanup process) — significant after 16–18+ hours
- Growth hormone pulses increase — relevant for body composition and tissue repair

**After 16–18 hours:**
- Autophagy is more active — damaged proteins and cells are recycled
- Blood glucose is very stable
- Mental clarity often improves (brain runs well on ketones from fat metabolism)

**After 24+ hours:**
- Deep autophagy
- Gut rest — significant relief for people with digestive issues
- Immune system reset (some evidence for stem cell activity after 72-hour fasts)

### Intermittent Fasting (IF) vs. Periodic Fasting
**TRE / IF (daily):** The daily practice of eating within a window. Sustainable as a lifestyle. Benefits: insulin sensitivity, digestion, sleep quality, weight management.

**Periodic extended fasting (24–72+ hours):** Done 1–4 times per year. Benefits: deep autophagy, gut rest, metabolic reset, immune function. Not a daily or weekly practice — it's a periodic intensive.

For most people: daily TRE in an 8–10 hour window is sufficient and sustainable. Extended fasts are optional and additive, not required.

### Eating Window Placement — Practical Guide

**Ideal:** First meal 1–2 hours after waking (not immediately), last meal 2–3 hours before bed.

Example for someone waking at 6:30am and sleeping at 10:30pm:
- 8am: first meal (breakfast or skip entirely)
- 6pm: last meal
- 10:30pm: sleep
- This is a 10-hour window with a 14-hour fast.

Narrower windows mean earlier last meals — the challenge for most people is moving dinner earlier, not delaying breakfast.

**The practical constraint:** for most people with standard work schedules, a 10-hour window (e.g., 8am–6pm) is sustainable. An 8-hour window (e.g., 9am–5pm or 10am–6pm) is achievable with some adjustment. Don't target a window you can't hold.

### Fasting and Training
Common concern: will fasting cause muscle loss?

**Short answer:** not with adequate protein and reasonable window lengths. The muscle-protective protocols:
- Hit protein targets within the eating window — 128–160g doesn't get easier if you compress the window, so plan protein distribution carefully.
- Training within the eating window (or just before it opens with protein immediately after) is fine.
- Fasted training (training before breaking the fast) is effective for fat oxidation but not superior for muscle building.

The research on 16:8 specifically shows no muscle loss compared to matched caloric intake without fasting, with adequate protein.

### Refeeding — Breaking the Fast Well
How you break a fast matters more as the fast length increases.

**After overnight (12–16 hours):** No special protocol needed. Eat normally.

**After 24+ hours:**
- Start small. The digestive system has been at rest; a large meal will cause discomfort.
- First food: something easily digested — bone broth, fruit, cooked vegetables, a small portion of lentils.
- Wait 30–60 minutes, then eat more normally.
- Avoid high-fat, high-sugar meals immediately after extended fasts — blood glucose and insulin are particularly responsive.

## Key Considerations

**This interacts with sleep.** The most immediate benefit most people notice is improved sleep when they stop eating 2–3 hours before bed. If nothing else, move the last meal earlier.

**Appetite adjusts within 1–2 weeks.** Hunger is largely a conditioned response to when you normally eat. Within 1–2 weeks of a consistent eating window, hunger outside the window diminishes significantly.

**The window can flex occasionally.** TRE is not an all-or-nothing commitment. Occasional meals outside the window (social dinners, travel) don't undo the practice.

**Fasting is not a license to eat poorly.** The metabolic benefits of TRE don't override poor food quality or caloric excess within the window. The two are complementary, not substitutes.

## Ayurvedic Alignment
Ayurvedic meal timing is functionally time-restricted eating. Largest meal at midday, small dinner, nothing after sunset. This is a 10–12 hour eating window, anchored early. The Ayurvedic reasoning (agni peaks at midday) and the modern mechanism (circadian insulin sensitivity) describe the same phenomenon differently. See [ayurveda-basics.md](ayurveda-basics.md).

## Getting Started

1. **Identify your current eating window.** Track when you have your first and last calorie (including drinks with calories) for 3 days. Most people discover a 14–16 hour window.
2. **Close the window by 1–2 hours in the evening.** This single change — moving the last meal earlier — produces the most immediate benefit (better sleep, better morning energy).
3. **Hold that for 2 weeks before narrowing further.** Let appetite adjust.
4. **Target a 10-hour window** as your stable practice. Narrower if it fits naturally.
5. Optional: try one 24-hour fast once you've been doing TRE consistently for a month.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Starting with a 16-hour fast immediately.** The adjustment period is real — start with 12 hours and work down.
- **Eating a big meal right before the window closes.** Defeats the sleep benefit. The last meal should be smaller, not larger.
- **Treating the window as permission to eat whatever.** TRE is an eating pattern, not a diet. Food quality still matters.
- **Counting coffee with milk as fasted.** If there are calories, the fast is broken.
- **Not accounting for protein.** Compressing eating to 8 hours makes hitting 140g+ protein harder. Plan the distribution — don't discover the gap at 7pm.
- **Extended fasting too frequently.** 24-hour fasts more than once a week can impair training, cause muscle loss, and become unsustainable. Once or twice a month maximum, and only after establishing daily TRE.
