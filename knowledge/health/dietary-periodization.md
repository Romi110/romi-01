# Dietary Periodization
*Alternate day fasting and carb cycling — tools for deliberate nutritional variation beyond a fixed daily deficit*

---

## What Is It

Dietary periodization is the planned variation of caloric intake or macronutrient distribution across days, rather than eating the same thing every day. Instead of a uniform daily deficit, you alternate between higher-intake and lower-intake days in a structured pattern.

Two protocols dominate this approach:

**Alternate Day Fasting (ADF)**: alternating between very low calorie days (under 500 cal) and normal eating days. You're not fasting continuously — you're creating a large weekly deficit through concentrated restriction every other day.

**Carb Cycling**: keeping protein and fat relatively stable while systematically varying carbohydrate intake based on training load. High-carb days align with heavy training; low-carb days align with rest or light activity.

Both use the same core idea: work with the body's anabolic and catabolic cycles rather than applying uniform downward pressure every day.

## Why It Matters

These are not beginner protocols. If energy balance, protein targets, and basic training aren't already working consistently, periodization won't help — it'll just add complexity to an already shaky foundation.

For someone with the basics dialed in: carb cycling can improve training performance and body composition simultaneously, and ADF is a legitimate alternative for people who find daily mild restriction harder to sustain than concentrated restriction on specific days. Both have decent research behind them.

---

## Core Concepts

### The Periodization Principle

The body responds differently to caloric surplus and caloric deficit. Surplus: anabolic — muscle is built, glycogen is replenished, hormones (testosterone, IGF-1, leptin) rise. Deficit: catabolic — fat is mobilized, but so is some muscle protein; hormones fall; metabolic rate adapts.

Continuous dieting applies constant catabolic pressure. Periodization introduces planned surpluses (or reduced restriction) to keep the anabolic environment alive while still achieving a weekly deficit. The weekly caloric total is what drives fat loss; how those calories are distributed across days determines how the body handles training and muscle retention.

The mechanism behind refeeds and diet breaks (covered in [fat-loss-science.md](fat-loss-science.md)) is the same principle — ADF and carb cycling apply it more deliberately.

---

### Alternate Day Fasting (ADF)

**True ADF**: fast days at ~0 calories, feed days at ~2,000+ calories, alternating. Creates a large weekly deficit (roughly 50% of TDEE) while allowing full eating every other day. Krista Varady at the University of Illinois has done the most rigorous research on this protocol — her trials show fat loss comparable to continuous caloric restriction over 8–24 weeks, with potentially better LDL particle improvement.

**Modified ADF (5:2 protocol)**: the more practical version. Five days of normal eating, two non-consecutive fast days at 500–600 calories. Popularized by Michael Mosley. Easier to sustain than true ADF because fast days are non-consecutive (no back-to-back restriction) and are very low calorie rather than zero.

**Who ADF suits**: people who find daily mild restriction (250-500 cal/day) psychologically harder to maintain than full restriction on specific days. Some people handle "today I eat nothing, tomorrow I eat freely" better than "I eat a little less every day forever." If that describes you, ADF is worth trying. If you find fasting days miserable even after 2-3 weeks of adaptation, it's not your protocol.

**ADF and hunger**: fast days are genuinely difficult for the first 1-2 weeks. Hunger on fast days typically diminishes significantly by week 3 — the same adaptation curve as TRE. Black coffee and sparkling water are the tools. Fast days go faster when busy; planning them on high-workload days helps.

**The caloric math**: on a 5:2 protocol, 2 days × 500 cal instead of 2 days × 2,000 cal = 3,000 cal weekly deficit. At 3,500 cal per pound of fat, that's ~0.85 lbs/week in theory — in the normal sustainable range. On feed days, eat at maintenance or slight surplus. The 5:2 deficit comes entirely from the fast days.

### Carb Cycling

Carb cycling varies carbohydrate intake based on training demand while keeping protein constant and letting fat fill remaining calories.

**Why carbs specifically?** Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for high-intensity resistance training and conditioning. They replenish muscle glycogen (the stored form of glucose your muscles run on during hard efforts). Eating more carbs on heavy training days means better performance, better recovery, and a more anabolic hormonal environment. On rest days when glycogen demand is low, reducing carbs and letting fat oxidation do more of the work keeps the weekly caloric deficit intact without sacrificing training quality.

**Basic structure:**

| Day Type | Carbs | Protein | Fat |
|----------|-------|---------|-----|
| High-carb (heavy training) | 200–300g | 1g/lb bodyweight | low–moderate |
| Low-carb (rest or light activity) | 50–100g | 1g/lb bodyweight | higher |
| Optional medium-carb | 100–150g | 1g/lb bodyweight | moderate |

Protein stays constant — it's the anchor. Fat fills caloric gaps. Carbs vary.

At 160 lbs: protein ~160g (640 cal), high-carb day might be 250g carbs (1,000 cal) + 60g fat (540 cal) = ~2,180 cal total. Low-carb day: 160g protein + 75g carbs (300 cal) + 90g fat (810 cal) = ~1,750 cal total. Weekly deficit comes from the low-carb days.

**Training day carb distribution**: prioritize carbs around training. Pre-workout (1-2 hours before): fast-digesting carbs prime glycogen and blood glucose. Post-workout: rapid carb + protein (4:1 ratio) replenishes glycogen and signals muscle repair. The rest of the day's carbs matter less.

**How many high-carb vs. low-carb days**: depends on training frequency. 3-day lifting schedule: 3 high-carb, 4 low-carb days. 4-day schedule: 4 high, 3 low. More training days = more high-carb days. Adjust the ratios if fat loss stalls (fewer high-carb days) or training quality drops (add more).

---

### ADF + Training: The Compatibility Question

Combining ADF with serious strength training requires planning. Training on a 500-calorie day is possible but not ideal for heavy compound work — glycogen is depleted, strength is lower, and recovery is impaired.

**The best approach**: schedule training on feed days. On the 5:2 protocol, this means keeping your training days as non-fast days. Most people train 3-5 days/week, so this is achievable — just plan the calendar before committing to fast days.

If you must train on a fast day: lighter work — skill practice, mobility, lower-intensity cardio. Don't attempt a max squat or a heavy conditioning session at 500 calories.

**Carb cycling integrates better with training** than ADF does, because high-carb days are specifically scheduled around training. This is why carb cycling tends to be the preferred protocol for people who take training seriously — ADF doesn't let you choose which days have fuel; carb cycling does.

## Key Considerations

**These are intermediate tools.** The research on ADF and carb cycling shows results comparable to — not dramatically better than — continuous moderate restriction with appropriate protein. The advantage is adherence and training quality, not magical fat loss mechanisms. If you wouldn't adhere to the periodization structure, a simpler approach will outperform it.

**Carb cycling requires macro tracking.** You can't carb cycle by feel. The whole protocol is built on knowing how many grams of carbs you're eating. If tracking macros isn't already part of your practice, build that skill first.

**ADF social friction**: every other day is a social constraint. Dinners, events, and spontaneous meals on fast days either break the fast or require planning around. The 5:2 version is easier to manage — two specific days can be chosen for low social demand (typically weekdays).

**Neither eliminates metabolic adaptation.** Both create a weekly caloric deficit; the body's hormonal response to that deficit (leptin drop, ghrelin rise, metabolic rate reduction) still occurs over time. Carb cycling's high-carb days help maintain leptin and training performance better than a flat deficit, but the adaptation still builds over 8-12 weeks. Diet breaks (2 weeks at maintenance) are still warranted every 2-3 months regardless of which protocol you use.

**Vegetarian protein on low-carb days**: if most protein comes from legumes (lentils, chickpeas, tofu), you're also getting carbs with it. A "low-carb day" at 75g carbs from vegetables and lean protein sources is fine — the carb reduction doesn't need to be extreme. Eggs, Greek yogurt, paneer, and protein powder are the lowest-carb protein sources in a vegetarian setup.

## Getting Started

**To try carb cycling:**
1. Establish your maintenance calories and current macro breakdown for 1-2 weeks first.
2. Set protein at 1g/lb bodyweight on all days — this doesn't change.
3. Assign your training days as high-carb days, rest days as low-carb.
4. High-carb target: 200-250g carbs (adjust down if fat loss stalls). Low-carb: 50-75g.
5. Let fat fill remaining calories. Don't force high fat on low-carb days — eat to satiety within the caloric budget.
6. Track for 3-4 weeks, then assess body composition trend and training quality.

**To try the 5:2 protocol:**
1. Pick two non-consecutive weekdays (Tuesday and Thursday work for most schedules).
2. On fast days: 500 calories total, distributed however you tolerate best (some people prefer one meal, others split two small meals). High protein, low carb, high volume (vegetables).
3. On feed days: eat at maintenance. Don't "make up" for fast days.
4. Schedule training on feed days.
5. Give it 3 weeks minimum before evaluating — fast day hunger is worst in weeks 1-2.

## Common Mistakes

**Using high-carb days as cheat days.** A high-carb day is not an untracked day. The carbs go up; total calories stay roughly at maintenance. 300g of carbs is not a license to eat 5,000 calories.

**Low-carb days becoming low-everything days.** On low-carb days, calories need to come from somewhere — protein and fat. Dropping carbs without increasing fat leads to very low total calories, which is its own problem.

**Training on ADF fast days.** Can be done, but don't attempt hard work. Move the heavy sessions to feed days.

**Starting carb cycling before tracking is solid.** Cycling around numbers you don't know doesn't work. Macro tracking is a prerequisite.

**Expecting carb cycling to work without a weekly deficit.** Carb cycling improves how you feel and train during a deficit — it doesn't create fat loss on its own. The weekly caloric total still needs to be below maintenance.

## Resources

- *The Every-Other-Day Diet* — Krista Varady. The ADF researcher. Readable translation of her UIC research into a practical protocol. The scientific foundation here is sound.
- *Flexible Dieting* — Layne Norton's work on macro tracking and nutrient timing — most accessible through his podcast (NLIF) and YouTube rather than a single book. Best source on the evidence behind carb timing.
- Cronometer or MacroFactor (app) — essential for tracking macros accurately during carb cycling; MacroFactor's adaptive TDEE model is the best available for ongoing adjustments.
