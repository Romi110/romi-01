# Healthy Hair Growth
*The biology, nutrition, and practices that actually move the needle — and what doesn't*

---

## What Is It

Hair growth is a biological process driven by follicle health, hormonal environment, nutritional status, and scalp circulation. The hair you see is dead protein (keratin). The work happens in the follicle — a living structure embedded in the scalp that cycles through growth, transition, and rest phases continuously.

Understanding the cycle and what supports or disrupts it gives you a framework for every decision: what to eat, what to put on your scalp, what habits accelerate loss. Most hair care marketing targets the visible dead strand. The actual leverage is inside the follicle.

## Why It Matters

Hair quality is a downstream indicator of health: protein intake, micronutrient status, stress load, and hormonal balance all show up in hair before they show up elsewhere. Hair that's thinning, growing slowly, or breaking easily is often a signal to fix something upstream — not to buy a new product.

For Romi: the nutritional and Ayurvedic practices that support hair growth overlap heavily with overall health. This isn't a vanity topic — it's an application of the same systems. The Ayurvedic dimension is particularly deep and specific; that's covered in [health/ayurveda-hair.md](ayurveda-hair.md).

## Core Concepts

### The Hair Growth Cycle

Each follicle operates independently on a cycle:

| Phase | Name | Duration | What's Happening |
|-------|------|----------|-----------------|
| Growth | Anagen | 2–7 years | Active hair production; 85–90% of hairs at any time |
| Transition | Catagen | 2–3 weeks | Growth stops; follicle shrinks |
| Rest | Telogen | 3–4 months | Old hair held in follicle; new hair begins forming underneath |
| Shedding | Exogen | Days–weeks | Old hair shed; cycle restarts |

**Why this matters practically:**
- Shedding 50–100 hairs/day is normal — these are telogen hairs completing their cycle
- Nutritional deficiencies, stress events, or hormonal shifts push more follicles into telogen simultaneously — causing telogen effluvium (diffuse shedding), which appears 2–4 months after the trigger, not immediately
- Recovery from a deficiency or stressor takes a full cycle to show in new growth: 3–6 months minimum before visible improvement

### Nutritional Foundations

Hair follicles are among the most metabolically active structures in the body. They're sensitive to nutritional deficit. The most common dietary causes of poor growth or increased shedding:

**Protein:**
The single most important macronutrient for hair. Keratin is protein — insufficient protein slows growth and increases shedding. Target: 0.8–1g/lb bodyweight for active individuals. On a vegetarian diet, specific attention to complete protein sources is required: dal + rice, paneer, eggs, Greek yogurt, tofu.

**Iron:**
Ferritin (stored iron) below ~70 ng/mL is associated with hair loss even when hemoglobin is normal. Vegetarians are at higher risk. Pairing iron-rich foods (lentils, spinach, tofu, pumpkin seeds) with vitamin C increases absorption. Coffee and tea taken with meals block iron absorption.

**Zinc:**
Supports follicle cell division and protein synthesis. Deficiency causes diffuse shedding. Found in pumpkin seeds, hemp seeds, chickpeas, cashews. Note: excess zinc supplementation can also cause hair loss — food sources first.

**Biotin (B7):**
Most biotin marketing is overblown — true deficiency is rare. Biotin supplementation helps only if you're actually deficient. It does not meaningfully improve hair growth above baseline in people with adequate biotin. Eggs, nuts, sweet potato, and leafy greens cover the requirement.

**Omega-3 fatty acids:**
Support scalp health and reduce inflammation. Sources: flaxseed, chia, walnuts (ALA form). If relying on plant sources, conversion to EPA/DHA is limited — algae-based omega-3 supplements cover this without fish.

**Vitamin D:**
Deficiency is associated with hair loss (particularly alopecia areata). Most people in the northern hemisphere are insufficient in winter. 2,000–4,000 IU/day supplementation is reasonable without testing; optimize with a blood test.

**Micronutrient summary table:**

| Nutrient | Role | Best vegetarian sources | Deficiency sign |
|----------|------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Protein | Keratin building block | Dal, paneer, Greek yogurt, tofu, eggs | Shedding, slow growth |
| Iron (ferritin) | Oxygen to follicles | Lentils, spinach + vitamin C | Diffuse shedding |
| Zinc | Follicle cell division | Pumpkin seeds, hemp, chickpeas | Shedding, brittle hair |
| Vitamin D | Follicle cycling | Sun, supplements | Thinning, slow regrowth |
| Omega-3 | Scalp inflammation | Flaxseed, walnuts, algae oil | Dry scalp, brittle |
| B12 | Cell metabolism | Eggs, dairy, supplement if vegan | Diffuse thinning |

### Hormonal and Lifestyle Factors

**DHT (dihydrotestosterone):**
The primary driver of androgenetic hair loss (pattern baldness). DHT is converted from testosterone by the enzyme 5-alpha reductase. DHT binds to follicle receptors, progressively miniaturizing follicles until they no longer produce visible hair. Genetic sensitivity to DHT determines whether this is a factor — not DHT levels alone.

Natural approaches that reduce 5-alpha reductase activity: saw palmetto (evidence is moderate), pumpkin seed oil (small studies, promising), green tea (EGCG). These are maintenance-tier interventions, not treatments for significant loss.

**Cortisol:**
Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which disrupts the growth cycle by pushing follicles into telogen prematurely. This is the mechanism behind stress-related shedding. The 2–4 month delay means shedding that starts today reflects a stressor from months ago. Managing stress is not optional for hair health.

**Thyroid function:**
Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) causes diffuse hair loss that doesn't respond to nutritional or topical interventions. If shedding is significant and doesn't respond to nutritional optimization, thyroid function (TSH, T3, T4) is worth checking.

**Sleep:**
Growth hormone (GH) peaks during deep sleep and plays a role in follicle activity. Poor sleep quality reduces GH output. Sleep is covered in [health/sleep-science.md](sleep-science.md).

### Scalp Health and Circulation

Follicle health depends on blood supply delivering nutrients and removing waste. The scalp is well-vascularized, but circulation can be improved.

**Scalp massage:**
The most evidence-backed topical intervention. A 4-minute daily scalp massage with fingertips (not nails) over 24 weeks increased hair thickness in one controlled study. The mechanism: mechanical stimulation of follicles and improved blood flow. No oil required — dry massage works — but oil massage (as in Ayurvedic practice) adds additional benefit.

Technique: fingertips on scalp, small circular motions applying gentle pressure, moving across the full scalp over 4 minutes. Can be done in the shower or before bed.

**The scalp microbiome:**
A balanced scalp microbiome supports follicle health. Over-washing (stripping natural oils), harsh sulfate shampoos, and silicone buildup disrupt this balance. Frequency: 2–3 times per week for most hair types is sufficient; daily washing removes protective sebum and can cause dryness and irritation.

The scalp dimension is covered in detail in [health/scalp-care.md](scalp-care.md).

### What the Evidence Doesn't Support

**Biotin megadosing:** No evidence for growth improvement in people without deficiency. Does interfere with thyroid and cardiac lab tests at high doses.

**Most "hair growth shampoos":** Shampoo is a rinse-off product. Ingredients in contact with the scalp for 30 seconds don't meaningfully penetrate follicles. Exceptions: ketoconazole shampoo (reduces scalp DHT locally; some evidence) and caffeine shampoo (limited but real effect on DHT blocking).

**Cutting hair to make it grow faster:** Hair growth rate is determined by follicle activity, not length. Cutting removes split ends and reduces breakage, which preserves length — it doesn't affect the growth rate.

**Inversion method (hanging head upside down):** No reliable evidence. Increased blood flow to scalp from inversion is transient and returns to baseline immediately.

## Key Considerations

**The timeline problem:** Hair responds slowly. A dietary change or new practice takes 3–6 months to show up as visibly improved hair. This makes it hard to attribute cause and effect. Pick evidence-backed practices and maintain them for 6 months before evaluating.

**Fix upstream first.** If ferritin is low, no topical product will compensate. Blood work (CBC, ferritin, vitamin D, thyroid) is the most actionable diagnostic tool for unexplained hair loss. Fix the deficit; the hair follows.

**The Ayurvedic system is specific and deep.** Amla, bhringraj, and neem have documented mechanisms for hair support. This isn't just tradition — the phytochemicals are real. See [health/ayurveda-hair.md](ayurveda-hair.md) for the full practice.

**Vegetarian diet is both an advantage and a risk.** High in zinc-competing phytates, low in heme iron (which absorbs better than non-heme), and potentially low in B12 and omega-3 EPA/DHA. Knowing the gaps and closing them is the work.

## Getting Started

1. Audit nutrition first: is protein intake at 0.8–1g/lb consistently? If not, that's the first fix.
2. Get ferritin and vitamin D checked if you haven't recently — the two most common deficiencies behind hair thinning.
3. Start 4-minute daily scalp massage. Low cost, some evidence, cumulative.
4. Add a weekly oil massage using amla or bhringraj oil (see [health/ayurveda-hair.md](ayurveda-hair.md)).
5. Reduce washing frequency to 2–3x/week if currently washing daily.
6. Give any changes 6 months before evaluating.

## Common Mistakes

**Chasing topical fixes while ignoring nutrition.** Products touch dead hair. Follicles respond to nutrition and circulation.

**Expecting fast results.** Hair grows ~0.5 inches/month. A protocol started today shows visible improvement in 3–6 months.

**Taking biotin megadoses without deficiency.** Waste at best, interference with lab values at worst.

**Washing too frequently with harsh shampoos.** Strips sebum, disrupts microbiome, causes dryness and scalp irritation that works against follicle health.

**Attributing shedding to the wrong cause.** Shedding events have a 2–4 month lag. The stress or deficiency that caused it happened months ago — don't blame what you changed recently.
