# Scalp Care
*The microbiome, sebum balance, and topical practices that support follicle health*

---

## What Is It

The scalp is skin — with the same microbiome, sebaceous glands, and barrier function as skin elsewhere, but with higher follicle density and unique sebum production characteristics. Scalp health is the environmental condition in which follicles operate. A healthy scalp doesn't guarantee strong hair growth, but a disrupted scalp guarantees compromised growth.

This file covers the practical maintenance layer: washing frequency, product choices, ingredients that help or harm, and the topical interventions with actual evidence behind them. The nutritional and systemic drivers of hair growth are in [health/hair-growth.md](hair-growth.md). The Ayurvedic oil practices are in [health/ayurveda-hair.md](ayurveda-hair.md).

## Why It Matters

Most hair care is marketed at the strand — shampoos, conditioners, serums. But the strand is dead. The scalp is where every meaningful intervention happens. Getting the scalp environment right costs very little and produces measurable results over months. Getting it wrong (over-washing, harsh products, buildup, uncontrolled dandruff) actively degrades follicle conditions.

## Core Concepts

### The Scalp Microbiome

The scalp hosts a diverse community of microorganisms, primarily fungi (Malassezia species) and bacteria. This community is normal and necessary — it's not something to eliminate.

**Malassezia** is the dominant scalp fungus. It feeds on sebum lipids and is harmless at normal levels. Overgrowth of certain Malassezia species (particularly M. globosa and M. restricta) causes dandruff and seborrheic dermatitis — not because the fungus is foreign, but because its metabolic byproducts (oleic acid) irritate susceptible scalps and trigger inflammation.

**Disruptions that destabilize the microbiome:**
- Over-washing: strips sebum, forces overproduction, creates feast-famine cycles for microbes
- Harsh sulfate shampoos: removes too much protective lipid layer
- Silicone-heavy products: build up over time, clog follicle openings
- Antibacterial products: indiscriminate; kills beneficial bacteria alongside harmful ones

**Signs of a disrupted scalp:** flaking (dandruff), itching, excessive oiliness within 24 hours of washing, chronic scalp tightness or dryness.

---

### Sebum — The Scalp's Native Conditioner

Sebaceous glands at the base of each follicle produce sebum — the scalp's own oil. Sebum lubricates the shaft, provides antimicrobial protection, and signals the microbiome. Stripping it entirely (as harsh shampoos do) triggers compensatory overproduction.

**The sebum balance goal:** enough to protect without excess. This is a calibration process — not an ingredient on a label.

**Over-washing creates a rebound cycle:**
1. Wash with stripping shampoo → sebum removed
2. Sebaceous glands upregulate to compensate → excess oil by day 2
3. Perceived need to wash daily to manage oiliness
4. Cycle repeats and entrains

Breaking the cycle: extend washing intervals by one day at a time. The scalp regulates sebum production to match demand — reduced washing frequency trains the glands to produce less. The adjustment period takes 2–4 weeks of discomfort before normalization.

**Washing frequency targets by hair type:**
- Fine or straight hair: 2–3x/week (distributes sebum easily, shows oiliness faster)
- Thick or coily hair: 1–2x/week (sebum travels the shaft more slowly, dryness risk)
- Daily washing: rarely necessary; reserved for heavy sweating or specific scalp conditions

### Shampoo — What to Look For and Avoid

Shampoo's job is to remove dirt, excess sebum, and product buildup without stripping the scalp barrier or disrupting the microbiome.

**Ingredients to avoid:**

| Ingredient | Issue |
|-----------|-------|
| Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) | Highly stripping; disrupts scalp barrier |
| Sodium laureth sulfate (SLES) | Milder than SLS but still stripping for sensitive scalps |
| Dimethicone and other silicones | Builds up, requires sulfate shampoo to remove — a cycle |
| Parabens (preservatives) | Endocrine disruptors; not necessary in well-formulated products |
| Fragrance (parfum) | Common irritant and allergen, especially on already-sensitive scalps |

**Ingredients that help:**

| Ingredient | Mechanism | Evidence |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Ketoconazole (1–2%) | Antifungal; reduces Malassezia overgrowth and scalp DHT locally | Moderate — meaningful for dandruff and androgenetic loss |
| Zinc pyrithione | Antifungal/antibacterial; controls dandruff | Strong for dandruff control |
| Salicylic acid | Exfoliates dead skin and sebum buildup; clears follicle openings | Good for buildup and mild seborrheic dermatitis |
| Caffeine | Penetrates follicle shaft; blocks DHT locally; stimulates follicle activity | Small but real effect in studies |
| Piroctone olamine | Milder antifungal alternative to ketoconazole | Moderate, better tolerated for daily use |

**Practical guidance:** for most people without specific scalp issues, a sulfate-free, silicone-free shampoo is sufficient. Add ketoconazole shampoo (Nizoral or generic) 1–2x/week if dandruff is present or as a DHT-management tool.

### Scalp Massage

The most evidence-backed scalp intervention. A controlled study (Koyama et al., 2016) showed standardized 4-minute daily scalp massage over 24 weeks significantly increased hair strand thickness versus controls.

**Mechanism:** mechanical stimulation stretches follicle cells, potentially activating stretch-sensitive channels involved in growth signaling. Improved local circulation delivers more oxygen and nutrients to the follicle bed.

**Technique:**
- Use fingertips (not nails) distributed across the scalp
- Apply firm but comfortable pressure
- Small circular motions, 1–2 cm diameter
- Move systematically across the scalp — frontal hairline, temporal sides, crown, occiput — over the 4-minute duration
- Can be done dry or with oil (oil massage done separately or combined)

**Dry vs oil massage:** dry massage is effective and can be done anytime. Oil massage (using amla, bhringraj, or coconut oil) adds the benefit of the oil but requires washing out. See [health/ayurveda-hair.md](ayurveda-hair.md) for oil massage protocol.

### Dandruff and Seborrheic Dermatitis

Dandruff is mild scaling from Malassezia metabolites causing surface skin cell turnover to accelerate. Seborrheic dermatitis (seb derm) is the more severe form — yellowish or white flakes, itching, redness, sometimes extending to eyebrows, nasolabial folds, or ears.

**Triggers:** stress (cortisol increases sebum production), high-fat diet, infrequent washing (allowing Malassezia to proliferate), hormonal changes.

**Treatment ladder:**
1. Zinc pyrithione shampoo (Head & Shoulders) 2–3x/week — first line
2. Ketoconazole 1% shampoo (Nizoral OTC) — more potent antifungal
3. Selenium sulfide or salicylic acid — for persistent buildup
4. Ketoconazole 2% prescription — for severe or resistant seb derm
5. Dermatologist: if not responding to OTC treatment after 6–8 weeks

Rotation: alternating between zinc pyrithione and ketoconazole shampoos prevents resistance and maintains effectiveness.

### DHT and Topical Approaches

DHT-blocking approaches at the scalp level are a secondary lever — useful for maintenance, not treatment of established pattern loss.

**Pumpkin seed oil:** applied topically or taken orally (400mg/day in one small study), showed reduced hair loss vs placebo. Mechanism: beta-sitosterol and other phytosterols inhibit 5-alpha reductase locally.

**Saw palmetto (topical):** similar mechanism to pumpkin seed oil. Available in some shampoos and serums. Evidence is moderate.

**Rosemary oil:** a 2015 study compared 2% minoxidil to rosemary oil after 6 months — comparable hair count improvement with less scalp itching in the rosemary group. Mechanism unclear but plausible (improved circulation, possible 5-alpha reductase inhibition). Dilute to 2–3% in carrier oil (jojoba or coconut) — do not apply undiluted.

**These are not replacements for medical treatment** (finasteride, minoxidil) if pattern loss is present and significant. They are appropriate for general maintenance.

---

### Ingredients That Don't Deliver What's Promised

**Biotin in shampoo:** biotin is water-soluble and doesn't penetrate the scalp barrier from a rinse-off product. No mechanism for effect.

**Collagen in shampoo:** molecular weight too large to penetrate skin. Adds slip/texture to the product, not to your follicles.

**Most "growth serums":** the ingredient list is typically peptides and plant extracts with minimal evidence at scalp-applicable concentrations. Not harmful; rarely effective.

## Key Considerations

**The scalp is skin — treat it like skin.** Avoid stripping it, support its barrier, don't over-treat it. Most scalp problems are caused by too much product use, not too little.

**Calibrate washing frequency first.** Before adding any products, get washing frequency right. Most people with oily scalps are over-washing and triggering rebound sebum — reducing frequency fixes the problem without any product change.

**Address dandruff immediately.** Chronic Malassezia inflammation around the follicle opening degrades follicle health over time. Dandruff is not just cosmetic.

**Patch test new products.** The scalp is reactive. New shampoos and oils should be tested on a small area first, especially if you have a history of scalp sensitivity.

## Getting Started

1. Audit current washing frequency — if daily, extend to every other day for 2–3 weeks, then to 2–3x/week.
2. Switch to a sulfate-free, silicone-free shampoo.
3. Add 4-minute daily scalp massage — can be dry, during a work break or before bed.
4. If dandruff is present: add zinc pyrithione or ketoconazole shampoo 1–2x/week alongside your regular shampoo.
5. Optionally add rosemary oil diluted in jojoba (2–3%) applied to scalp 2x/week, left on for 30 minutes before washing.

## Common Mistakes

**Washing too often and wondering why the scalp is oily.** The oiliness is caused by the washing. Extend intervals.

**Using sulfate shampoo to clear silicone buildup from conditioner.** Avoid the silicone; the sulfate shampoo isn't the answer.

**Scratching the scalp with nails during washing.** Nails cause micro-abrasion, disrupting the barrier and potentially introducing bacteria. Fingertip pads only.

**Applying undiluted essential oils.** Rosemary, peppermint, and tea tree oil are irritating or caustic at full strength. Always dilute in a carrier oil.

**Over-applying oils and not washing them out.** Heavy oil buildup clogs follicle openings and creates ideal conditions for Malassezia overgrowth.
