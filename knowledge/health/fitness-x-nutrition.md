# Fitness × Nutrition
*How training and eating interact — recovery, timing, and body recomposition mechanics*

---

## What Is It

Training and nutrition are not separate systems — they're inputs to the same adaptation process. Training is the stimulus; nutrition is the raw material and the recovery environment. Get one without the other right and you cap your results.

This file covers the interaction layer: nutrient timing, how caloric context shapes adaptation, how training type changes what you need to eat, and the mechanics of body recomposition. The fundamentals of each domain are in [nutrition.md](nutrition.md) and [strength-training-fundamentals.md](strength-training-fundamentals.md) — read those first.

## Why It Matters

Romi's goal is body recomposition: build muscle, reduce fat, sustain both. That goal lives exactly at the intersection of training and nutrition — it cannot be achieved through either alone. Understanding how they interact is what separates someone who trains hard and eats reasonably from someone who actually moves the needle on body composition.

## Core Concepts

### Protein Synthesis and the Training Window

Resistance training triggers muscle protein synthesis (MPS) — the process of building new muscle tissue. MPS is elevated for 24–48 hours post-session. During this window, dietary protein is used more efficiently for muscle repair and growth.

**The practical implication:** distribute protein intake across the day (3–4 meals) rather than backloading it all at dinner. Each meal should have 30–45g protein to maximally stimulate MPS. A single large protein meal doesn't compensate for low-protein meals earlier in the day — MPS operates on a per-meal basis.

**Pre-workout protein:** eating protein 1–2 hours before training is modestly beneficial — it raises amino acid availability during the session. Not mandatory but useful if you're training fasted.

**Post-workout protein:** the "anabolic window" (30-min urgency) is largely overstated for people eating adequate protein throughout the day. Within 2 hours is fine. Urgency matters more if you trained fasted.

### Caloric Context and Adaptation

Total calories determine the direction of your body composition. Training type affects the equation but doesn't override it.

**In a caloric surplus:**
- Muscle protein synthesis is upregulated — surplus provides energy for building
- Fat gain accompanies muscle gain unless surplus is small (250–300 cal above maintenance)
- Best environment for pure strength and size gains

**At maintenance:**
- Body recomposition is most achievable here — muscle gain and fat loss occur simultaneously, but slowly
- Requires high protein intake (1g/lb bodyweight) to support muscle building without excess calories for fat gain
- The right place to be if you're not in a hurry and want sustainable progress

**In a caloric deficit:**
- Fat loss is the outcome; the deficit is what drives it
- Muscle retention requires: (1) high protein, (2) continued resistance training, (3) deficit that isn't too aggressive
- A 300–500 cal/day deficit is the practical range — aggressive enough to lose fat, moderate enough to retain muscle and support training quality
- Body recomposition is still possible in a moderate deficit, especially for beginners or those returning after a break

**For Romi:** maintenance or a mild deficit (~300 cal) is the target zone. Recomposition at maintenance is slow but avoids the trade-offs of aggressive cutting.

### How Training Type Changes Nutritional Needs

Different training types draw on different energy systems and create different recovery demands.

**Strength training (heavy, low rep):**
- Low caloric cost per session (~150–250 cal)
- High CNS demand — recovery requires protein and adequate total calories
- Carbohydrate need is moderate — this is phosphocreatine and glycolytic work, not endurance
- Protein is the critical input

**Hypertrophy training (moderate load, higher volume):**
- Moderate caloric cost (~200–350 cal)
- Creates significant muscle damage — protein synthesis is highly elevated post-session
- Carbohydrates matter more here — glycolytic demand is higher, and carbs support intra-workout energy and recovery
- Pre- and post-session carbs are most useful for this training type

**HIIT:**
- High caloric cost relative to duration (~200–400 cal for 20–30 min)
- Glycolytic — depletes muscle glycogen significantly
- Post-HIIT: carbs are useful for glycogen replenishment, particularly if another session follows within 24 hours
- HIIT elevates cortisol — high HIIT frequency without adequate carbs and calories amplifies cortisol's muscle-breakdown effect

**Zone 2 / walking:**
- Runs primarily on fat for fuel at low intensity
- Low protein demand
- No specific pre/post nutrition urgency unless sessions are long (1+ hour)
- Carbs are not required pre-walk for most people

### Nutrient Timing — Practical Version

The research supports a simple version: get protein at every meal, eat carbs around training, don't go more than 4–5 hours without protein during the day. The elaborate intra-workout nutrition protocol is for competitive athletes, not recreational lifters.

**Practical daily structure for Romi's 3x/week training:**

On training days:
- Pre-workout (1–2 hr before): protein + moderate carbs. Dal and roti, Greek yogurt and oats, eggs and toast. Avoid high fat pre-workout — it slows digestion.
- Post-workout (within 2 hr): protein + carbs. Whey shake with a piece of fruit, dal-rice, paneer and roti. This is the highest-priority meal of the day.
- Rest of day: hit protein targets, don't overthink timing.

On rest days:
- Same protein targets, slightly lower total calories if in a deficit (less energy expenditure)
- No meaningful change to meal timing

### Body Recomposition Mechanics

Recomposition — simultaneous muscle gain and fat loss — is possible under specific conditions:

1. **Caloric maintenance or mild deficit** — aggressive deficit impairs MPS too much; large surplus adds fat
2. **High protein** — 0.8–1g/lb bodyweight minimum; protein provides building material and is calorically compensated by the fact it's the least likely macro to be stored as fat
3. **Resistance training stimulus** — without the training signal, there's nothing to build toward; the body preferentially uses excess protein for energy or converts it
4. **Sufficient calories from carbs and fat** — protein gets spared for muscle building when carbs and fat are adequate to fuel activity

**Who gets the best recomposition results:**
- Beginners (first 1–2 years of training) — rapid adaptation regardless of caloric precision
- People returning after a layoff — muscle memory dramatically accelerates regain
- Individuals with higher body fat percentages — fat stores can fuel deficit without impairment

**Realistic rate:** natural recomposition at maintenance averages 0.5–1 lb of fat loss and 0.5–1 lb of muscle gain per month. Slow. The trade-off is that you don't need to bulk then cut cyclically.

### Sleep as a Nutritional Variable

Sleep isn't just recovery — it's when the body does the building. Growth hormone secretion peaks during slow-wave sleep (first half of the night). Protein consumed earlier in the day is used most actively during this window.

**Practical:** a protein-containing snack before bed (cottage cheese, Greek yogurt, casein if supplementing) modestly improves overnight MPS. The effect is real but small. Bigger lever is just getting 7–9 hours of sleep — compromised sleep cuts MPS by ~30%.

Covered in more depth in [sleep-science.md](sleep-science.md).

## Key Considerations

**Protein is the one macro that unambiguously matters for training goals.** Carb and fat ratios are flexible; protein is not. Hit the target.

**Carb timing matters more than carb amount.** Placing carbs around training sessions (pre and post) does more than the same carbs distributed randomly throughout the day.

**Deficit size has a ceiling.** Below ~1,500 cal/day, most people lose muscle regardless of protein intake. The body downregulates MPS when total energy is too low.

**Cortisol is the enemy of recomposition.** Excessive HIIT, large caloric deficits, and poor sleep all elevate cortisol — which promotes muscle breakdown and fat storage (especially visceral). Managing stress and sleep is as important as macros.

**Track for a week, then adjust.** You don't need to track long-term. Track once to calibrate — understand where your calories actually come from and where protein falls short. Adjust habits, then stop tracking.

## Getting Started

1. Know your maintenance calories (~15 × bodyweight in lbs). Decide: maintenance, mild deficit (-300), or mild surplus (+250).
2. Set protein at 0.8–1g/lb bodyweight. Build meals around hitting it.
3. On training days, put a solid protein + carb meal within 2 hours post-session.
4. Track for one week to see where you actually land on calories and protein. Adjust one thing.
5. Sleep 7–9 hours. Non-negotiable — it's the cheapest performance drug available.

## Common Mistakes

**Training hard, eating poorly, wondering why nothing changes.** Training creates the need; nutrition is what pays it.

**Protein only at dinner.** MPS requires amino acid availability throughout the day. Backloading protein into one meal doesn't work the same way.

**Eating at maintenance and expecting fast fat loss.** Recomposition is slow. If the goal is meaningful fat loss on a timeline, a moderate deficit is required.

**Eating at a large deficit and wondering why strength is stalling.** You can't build in a large energy hole. Moderate deficit or accept that strength gains will be minimal during a cut phase.

**Treating rest days as cheat days.** Recovery requires protein regardless of training. Rest day protein should be the same as training day protein.
