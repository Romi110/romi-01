# Fiction Story Building
*How literary and commercial fiction work — character vs plot, scope, realism, voice, and the conventions that define the form.*

---

## What Is It

Fiction is the broadest category in storytelling — everything from Dostoevsky to Stephen King falls under it. Within fiction, the key divisions are between **literary fiction** (prioritizes language, interiority, ambiguity, theme) and **commercial fiction** (prioritizes plot, pace, genre satisfaction, accessibility). Most published novels sit somewhere between the two extremes.

This file covers what makes fiction work as a form: how character and plot interact, how scope and voice are chosen, and how realism functions. See [story-structure.md](story-structure.md) for the underlying structural frameworks that apply across all fiction.

---

## Why It Matters

If you want to write novels or short stories, you need to understand the specific expectations of the form you're writing in. Applying a screenplay framework to a literary novel will produce something mechanical. Applying literary fiction instincts to a thriller will produce something that doesn't sell. Knowing the conventions — so you can use or subvert them deliberately — is the baseline.

## Core Concepts

### Character-Driven vs Plot-Driven

The most fundamental distinction in fiction.

**Character-driven**: The external plot exists to force internal transformation. What happens matters less than what it *means* to the character. The central question is often about identity, relationship, or moral reckoning. Literary fiction defaults here. *Normal People*, *The Remains of the Day*, *A Little Life*.

**Plot-driven**: Characters exist to make the plot happen. Transformation is optional or secondary. The central question is external — will they solve it, survive it, win? Thrillers, mysteries, and most genre commercial fiction default here. *Gone Girl*, *The Da Vinci Code*, *The Martian*.

The false dichotomy: the best commercial fiction has character depth; the best literary fiction has plot momentum. What differs is emphasis and what the reader is promised by page one.

**Test**: If you removed your protagonist and replaced them with a competent but generic character, what breaks? In character-driven fiction, the whole story collapses. In plot-driven fiction, only the details shift.

---

### Domestic vs Epic Scope

**Domestic scope**: The story is small in geography and cast, large in emotional terrain. A family, a relationship, a single year. All pressure is internal and interpersonal. Most literary fiction, many short stories, domestic thrillers.

**Epic scope**: The story spans years, continents, generations, or civilizations. External stakes are enormous — war, survival, revolution. Emotional depth can still be present, but it's carried by plot action more than interiority.

The mistake is trying to write at epic scope with domestic interiority — the pacing collapses. Conversely, treating a domestic story as if the fate of nations depends on it usually reads as self-important.

**Practical consequence for structure**: Domestic fiction lives in scenes — long scenes, multiple beats, emotional aftermath. Epic fiction lives in sequence — scenes serve plot movement more than emotional dwelling.

---

### Realism and Verisimilitude

Fiction doesn't have to be realistic, but it has to be *internally consistent*. Every story sets up a contract with the reader about what kind of world it is, and it must honor that contract.

**Social realism**: Characters behave according to recognizable social, economic, and psychological pressures. The most grounded form. A character's class, race, and era shape what they can do. Ignoring these produces characters who feel like puppets.

**Psychological realism**: Characters have coherent inner lives — they want things, fear things, contradict themselves, and change for reasons. A character who does what the plot needs without internal logic is a plot convenience, not a person.

**Magical realism** (Márquez, Allende): Fantastical elements treated matter-of-factly inside an otherwise socially realistic world. The magic is metaphor made literal. Not a genre — a technique.

**Naturalism vs stylized prose**: Naturalism aims for invisible prose — language serves story. Stylized prose (Nabokov, Woolf, McCarthy) draws attention to language as a thing in itself. Neither is superior; both require skill and consistency.

### Voice

Voice is the personality of the narration — how the story talks to the reader. It's one of the hardest things to teach and the easiest to recognize.

**First-person voice**: The narrator is a character. Voice is their psychology made explicit. The trap: the narrator must be interesting company for 300 pages. The reward: unparalleled intimacy and unreliability as a tool.

**Third-limited voice**: The narration is colored by the POV character's sensibility without being their literal words. The narrator can observe what the character wouldn't articulate. Most flexible for commercial fiction.

**Omniscient narrative voice**: The narrator is its own entity — sometimes ironic, sometimes godlike, sometimes warm. Dickens, Austen, Tolstoy. Harder to execute in contemporary fiction because readers are trained for deep POV. When it works, it allows social panorama.

**The danger of voicelessness**: A story told in flat, neutral prose with no personality usually isn't humble — it's just underdeveloped. Voice is the first thing agents and editors notice.

### The Unreliable Narrator

One of fiction's most powerful structural tools. The narrator tells the story, but the reader comes to understand that their account is wrong, incomplete, or self-serving.

Types:
- **Naive unreliable**: The narrator doesn't understand what they're describing (children, innocents). *To Kill a Mockingbird* (Scout is too young to understand half of what she witnesses).
- **Lying unreliable**: The narrator is consciously deceiving the reader. Twist often at the end.
- **Deluded unreliable**: The narrator genuinely believes their account but is wrong. *The Remains of the Day* (Stevens can't see his own emotional repression even as he describes it in perfect detail).

The technique requires two simultaneous narratives: what the narrator says happened, and what actually happened. The reader must be given enough to reconstruct the truth.

### Short Fiction vs the Novel

Short stories and novels are different forms, not just different lengths.

**Short story** principles:
- One central effect or revelation (Poe's unity of effect)
- No subplots — every element serves the central thing
- Often begins mid-scene, close to the story's critical moment
- Endings tend toward revelation or resonance rather than full resolution
- Compression is the craft — what's left out is as important as what's in

**Novel** principles:
- Multiple threads — subplot and main plot mirror and counterpoint each other
- Space for development: the reader needs time to know the characters before the climax means anything
- Pacing variation is necessary — you cannot sustain maximum tension for 300 pages
- The midpoint must genuinely shift the story's direction or it reads as saggy middle

**Novella** (20,000–40,000 words): Rare commercially but a natural fit for single-arc stories that need more space than a short story but aren't carrying subplot weight. *Of Mice and Men*, *The Old Man and the Sea*, *Animal Farm*.

### Subgenres and Their Conventions

Commercial fiction has genre contracts — reader expectations that must be met or deliberately subverted.

| Genre | Core Promise | Must Deliver |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Literary fiction | Emotional truth, beautiful language | Resonance, complexity |
| Thriller | Sustained tension, external danger | Resolution of danger, escalation |
| Mystery | A puzzle to solve | Fair-play clues, satisfying solution |
| Romance | Love story | HEA (happily ever after) or HFN (happy for now) |
| Horror | Fear, dread, transgression | Genuine threat, earned scare |
| Upmarket fiction | Commercial readability + literary depth | Both: plot momentum AND emotional weight |

Breaking genre contracts is a creative choice, not a freedom. Readers who picked up a romance and got a tragedy feel betrayed. Signal subversions early.

## Key Considerations

**The premise test**: State your premise in one sentence: a specific character type in a specific situation forced to make a choice about a specific thing. If you can't do this, the story doesn't have a clear engine yet.

**Earning the ending**: The final scene must be the inevitable consequence of who this character is and everything that happened. If you could swap in a different ending without changing the story, the ending wasn't earned.

**The saggy middle**: Most novels fail in Act 2. Causes: the protagonist is too passive (things happen to them instead of them making choices), stakes plateau, or subplots don't connect to the central dramatic question. Fix by raising the cost of failure at the midpoint and making the protagonist's choices actively worsen their situation.

**Research and specificity**: Specific, accurate detail creates the illusion of reality. You don't need to know everything about nursing homes to set a story there — you need to know five or six exact, sensory, correct details. Wrong details break immersion; vague details create no immersion.

## Getting Started

1. **Decide character-driven or plot-driven** — this determines your structure, pacing, and what the reader is promised on page one.
2. **Write your premise sentence** — protagonist + situation + forced choice + stakes.
3. **Choose scope and POV** — they're linked. Domestic + deep third-limited. Epic + omniscient or rotating POVs.
4. **Write the opening scene without setup** — drop into life already in motion. The reader doesn't need the character's full backstory before they care.
5. **Let the first draft be wrong** — most structural fixes happen in revision. The first draft is for finding out what the story actually is.

## Common Mistakes

**Over-writing backstory**: Most backstory can be cut or distributed in small doses through action. The character's history should emerge from how they behave, not from explanation.

**Underdeveloped antagonists**: Antagonists who are simply evil are lazy. They need coherent wants and internal logic. The best antagonists believe they're right.

**Waiting for permission to start the real story**: Chapters 1-3 that are just setup before "the story begins." Cut them. Start where it starts.

**Passive protagonist**: The protagonist reacts but never acts. Events happen to them. Fix: give them something they want from scene one, and show them actively pursuing it.

**Resolving subplots too cleanly**: Real life doesn't wrap up every thread. Subplots that resolve perfectly undermine the story's emotional honesty. Leave some things ambiguous or unresolved.

## Resources

- *The Art of Fiction* by John Gardner — the most demanding craft book; literary fiction focus; argues that fiction must create a continuous vivid dream
- *Bird by Bird* by Anne Lamott — process over theory; the permission to write badly first; particularly useful for writers who freeze
- *The Emotional Craft of Fiction* by Donald Maass — how to generate emotional response in the reader; practical exercises, genre-neutral
- *Writing Fiction* by Janet Burroway — standard university textbook; covers all the craft elements systematically; worth reading cover to cover once
