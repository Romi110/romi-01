# Magic System Design
*Sanderson's three Laws, the hard/soft spectrum, and how to design a magic system that serves the story*

---

## What Is It

Magic system design is the craft of building a coherent, functional supernatural framework for a fantasy world. The goal is not creativity for its own sake — the goal is a system whose rules, limits, and costs generate story rather than resolve it.

[fantasy-story-building.md](fantasy-story-building.md) covers the hard/soft distinction and Sanderson's First Law at overview level. This file goes deeper: all three Laws, the design process from cost outward, internal vs. external systems, and how magic integrates with the world's political and social structure. If you're actively designing a magic system, start here.

## Core Concepts

### Sanderson's Three Laws

The most useful framework for magic system design, developed from Brandon Sanderson's experience writing and teaching fantasy.

**First Law**: *Your ability to use magic to solve problems is proportional to how well the reader understands that magic before the climax.*

If your protagonist defeats the villain using magic the reader has never seen before, it's a deus ex machina regardless of how elaborate the magic is. The reader must understand the system well enough to see the win coming as *possible* before it happens — otherwise it feels like cheating. This doesn't mean you telegraph the solution; it means you lay the groundwork. The reader should think "I didn't see that coming, but of course — the rules allow it."

**Second Law**: *Limitations are more interesting than powers.*

This is the most counterintuitive law. Writers spend effort designing what their magic *can do* when the interesting territory is what it *can't do*, what it *costs*, and where it *fails*. Gandalf's soft magic is compelling because its limits are unclear — you don't know what he can and can't do, which creates sustained tension. Hard magic (Mistborn's allomancy) is compelling because its explicit limits create the puzzle: the protagonist must solve problems within the system's constraints.

Limitations are also where drama lives. A healer who can heal any wound eliminates stakes. A healer who can heal any wound but ages one year for every hour of healing makes every healing a tragedy-in-waiting.

**Third Law**: *Before adding something new, expand what you have first.*

Writers reach for new magic elements when their system feels stuck. Usually the problem is that they haven't explored the implications of what they already have. New elements add complexity without depth; exploring existing elements adds both. Before adding a new power, ask: what happens when the existing power interacts with this situation in a way I haven't explored?

### The Hard/Soft Spectrum

Magic doesn't divide neatly into hard and soft — it's a spectrum.

**Fully hard magic**: Complete rule sets, known costs, explicit limits. The reader can predict (in broad terms) what's possible. *Mistborn* (allomancy: swallow specific metals, burn them to gain specific powers, run out of metal and you lose the power); *Avatar: The Last Airbender* (bending elements, each with explicit style and limits, no shortcuts between them).

**Mostly soft magic**: Rules implied but never stated. Powers feel limitless but the narrative never actually uses them that way. Wonder is the primary effect. *Lord of the Rings* (Gandalf's power is vast and undefined; Tolkien almost never has him solve a plot problem with direct magic force). *Princess Mononoke*.

**Mixed (most published fantasy)**: Hard magic for the protagonist's system (reader can follow the logic), soft magic for the world's deeper mysteries (Aes Sedai can do things most characters don't understand). This lets you have earned climaxes for your protagonist while preserving awe at the world's scale.

The rule of thumb: if you want your magic to solve plot problems at the climax, it needs to be hard enough for the reader to follow the solution. If you want magic to create atmosphere and wonder, keep it soft.

### Designing from Cost Outward

The most common mistake: designing what the magic *does* first. Design what it *costs* first.

**Step 1 — The cost.** What does using magic take? Physical toll (exhaustion, pain, blood, shortened lifespan), material input (specific ingredients, metal, words, blood), social cost (marked as different, feared, excluded), moral cost (corrupts you, requires sacrifice of something you value). The cost is where the drama is.

**Step 2 — The limit.** What can't this magic do? Hard limits (can't create matter, can't bring back the dead, can't change what already happened) and soft limits (can't do X without crossing a moral threshold the protagonist hasn't reached yet). Limits determine the puzzles your plot can set for your protagonist.

**Step 3 — What it can do.** With cost and limits established, define the powers. The powers will be less exciting than you imagined, but they'll be more useful because you know exactly how they interact with your story's dramatic situations.

**Step 4 — Distribution.** Who has this magic, and why? Is it inherited (creates a magic aristocracy)? Learned (creates an institution — guild, school, temple — that controls access)? Random (creates fear, suspicion, unpredictability in the social fabric)? Distribution is the bridge between magic and politics.

**Step 5 — Society implications.** *If this magic exists, then what else must be true?* (See below.)

### Internal vs. External Systems

**Internal magic**: power flows from within the practitioner. The practitioner is transformed by its use. Examples: blood magic (cost is the self), will-based magic (strength of character determines power), emotion-fueled magic (rage, love, grief as fuel). Internal systems connect magic to character arc — the practitioner must become something different to access more power, which is inherently dramatic.

**External magic**: the practitioner shapes forces or materials outside themselves. The power doesn't come from them — they're a technician or conduit. Examples: elemental manipulation, runic/sigil systems, ritual magic requiring specific materials. External systems feel more like craft or engineering — they can be learned, systematized, even industrialized. They connect naturally to economy, trade (who controls the materials?), and institutions.

Most interesting systems have both: external mechanics that require internal sacrifice or capacity.

### The Society Implication Test

The most common worldbuilding failure with magic: the magic exists, but the world looks exactly like it would without it. If magic can do something significant, the world should reflect that it's been doing it for 1,000 years.

Ask these questions for every significant power in your system:

- **Medicine**: if magic can heal, what does that do to the medical profession, life expectancy, disability, warfare (injured soldiers return to the field)?
- **Military**: if magic can kill at range, how does that change armor, castle design, battle formation? If only a few can do it, do armies try to recruit or protect them?
- **Economy**: if magic can transmute materials, create objects, enhance crops, or transport goods — what happens to the industries it replaces?
- **Politics**: who controls access to magic? If it's inheritable, does a magic aristocracy emerge? If learnable, what institution controls training and does it become a power center?
- **Religion**: how does religion explain magic? Is it divine gift, dangerous heresy, neutral craft? The religion's attitude shapes who uses magic publicly and who hides it.

You don't need to answer every question in depth — but you should know the answers, because the world's surface-level culture will reflect them.

### Thematic Alignment

The best magic systems aren't just mechanical — they embody the story's theme.

*Mistborn's* allomancy (power is determined by noble birth, with an underclass who can't access it) is literally the theme: power is hoarded by the few, and the system exists to be overthrown. *The Wheel of Time's* One Power (only women can safely channel; male channelers go mad and break the world) encodes the story's gender politics directly into the physics.

Ask: what does your magic system say about the world? What is the implicit argument it makes about power, identity, sacrifice, or change? When magic's rules reinforce theme, the system does double work — it creates plot mechanics *and* meaning.

## Getting Started

1. **Name your theme first** — what is your story actually about? Power corrupts / sacrifice defines identity / institutions preserve the wrong things? Let the theme suggest the magic's shape.
2. **Design the cost** — what does it take out of the user? Make it personal, specific, and proportional to the power it gives.
3. **Define three hard limits** — three things this magic definitively cannot do. This is where you close the plot holes before they open.
4. **Determine distribution** — who has access and why? Trace the political consequence of that distribution.
5. **Run the society implication test** — for each significant power, ask: if this has been true for 500 years, what does the world look like?

## Common Mistakes

**Powers without costs.** Magic that solves problems without taking anything from the protagonist is wish fulfillment, not story. The cost creates tension and the cost is what makes using magic a meaningful choice.

**Ignoring the society implications.** The world looks like a rough medieval default despite magic that should have transformed medicine, warfare, and economics centuries ago. The inconsistency breaks immersion for attentive readers.

**Adding powers when the story stalls.** New powers don't fix narrative problems — they mask them. When the story stalls, the problem is almost always stakes, character motivation, or structure. A new magic element is a distraction.

**Making limits arbitrary.** Limits that exist only to create plot convenience feel like cheating — "this magic works on everything except the villain because reasons." Limits should emerge from the system's internal logic, established early, and held consistently.

## Resources

- Brandon Sanderson's "Laws of Magic" essays (brandonsanderson.com) — the original source; three short essays, essential reading before designing any system
- *The Stormlight Archive* by Brandon Sanderson — the most elaborate demonstration of hard magic in modern fantasy; study how Stormlight (fuel), Surgebinding (powers), and Shardplate (cost consequence) interact
- *The Broken Earth* trilogy by N.K. Jemisin — soft magic that's also hard: the orogeny system is precise in some ways, mysterious in others, and saturated with thematic weight about power, exploitation, and identity
