# Sci-Fi Story Building
*How science fiction works as a form — the novum, extrapolation logic, hard vs. soft SF, and the two kinds of sci-fi story*

---

## What Is It

Science fiction is fiction whose premise requires a significant departure from current reality — a technological, scientific, or social change that determines the story's events. The departure has to be *coherent*: grounded in some logic (scientific, speculative, or social) that makes the world internally consistent.

The departure separates SF from fantasy: fantasy's impossible elements are accepted as given; SF's impossible elements are *explained* by some extrapolation of real-world science or sociology, even when that extrapolation is loose. "Because magic" is fantasy. "Because physics allows it, given these assumptions" is SF.

This file covers SF-specific craft. See [story-structure.md](story-structure.md) for the underlying structural frameworks and [fiction-story-building.md](fiction-story-building.md) for character, voice, and scope decisions that apply across all fiction.

## Core Concepts

### The Novum

Darko Suvin's term, from his theory of SF: the *novum* is the one central cognitive innovation — the single thing that makes this world different from ours and drives the story.

Good SF usually has one novum, developed fully. *The Left Hand of Darkness* (Le Guin): the novum is a society with no fixed gender, and the entire novel explores what that means. *The Martian* (Weir): the novum is a person stranded on Mars with existing technology — the novel explores the science of surviving that. *Never Let Me Go* (Ishiguro): the novum is that clones are raised as organ donors — the novel is about what that does to identity and complicity.

**The test**: can you state your novum in one sentence? "In this world, X is true, and that changes everything about Y." If you can't, you may have multiple competing novums — which usually produces a cluttered, unfocused book. Great SF picks one big idea and commits.

**Novum vs. premise**: the novum is the worldbuilding element; the premise is the story built around it. The novum for *The Hunger Games* is a totalitarian future where children are forced to kill each other as political spectacle. The premise is one girl's decision to fight back. Don't confuse the innovation with the story.

### Hard vs. Soft Sci-Fi

A spectrum, not a binary.

**Hard SF**: The science is as accurate as the author can make it, and the story's events are constrained by scientific plausibility. The pleasures are intellectual — watching problems solved within real constraints. Kim Stanley Robinson (*The Mars Trilogy*), Greg Egan (*Diaspora*, *Quarantine*), Andy Weir (*The Martian*). Errors in the science produce reader alienation in this subgenre — the contract promises accuracy.

**Soft SF / social SF**: The science is a backdrop for exploring human and social questions. The science doesn't need to be accurate; it needs to be internally consistent enough that the social exploration works. Ursula K. Le Guin (*The Left Hand of Darkness*, *The Dispossessed*), Octavia Butler (*Kindred*, *Parable of the Sower*), Ted Chiang (*Stories of Your Life and Others*). The questions are: what does this technology do to human relationships, institutions, identity?

**Space opera**: SF with epic scope, galactic settings, and adventure-forward plots. The science is often hand-waved. The genre conventions are closer to epic fantasy than to hard SF. *Dune* (Herbert), *A Fire Upon the Deep* (Vinge), *The Culture* series (Banks).

For a first SF story: decide which end of the spectrum you're writing toward. They require different research, different world-building priorities, and signal different reader expectations from page one.

### The Two Kinds of SF Story

**The engineering story**: a problem is presented, and the plot is the process of solving it through ingenuity, science, and technical knowledge. The protagonist's competence is the point. *The Martian* is the purest example — every chapter is a new survival problem solved through chemistry, physics, and agriculture. The pleasure is watching smart people solve hard problems.

The engineering story has a weak character arc by design — Watney doesn't transform; he survives. That's fine if the reader bought in for the engineering. The contract must be set early.

**The sociological story**: a technological or social change is introduced, and the plot explores its human consequences — on relationships, institutions, identity, and power. The protagonist typically represents a point of view through which the reader sees what the change means. *Never Let Me Go*, *The Handmaid's Tale*, *Station Eleven*. Character arc is central.

The best SF mixes both, but most stories have a dominant mode. Know yours — because the questions you need to answer in planning differ significantly between them.

### Extrapolation Logic

The discipline that separates good SF from bad SF: if X is true in your world, what else follows?

**First-order effects**: the direct consequence of the novum. If humans can live to 200, the first-order effect is people staying healthy longer.

**Second-order effects**: what the first-order effects cause. Life expectancy to 200 means: the workforce doesn't turn over, young people can't advance, gerontocracy is stable, social mobility collapses, the meaning of "career" changes completely, marriage is a century-long commitment, generations no longer replace each other at 30-year intervals.

**Third-order effects**: what the second-order effects cause. Gerontocracy + blocked social mobility produces: revolutionary pressure, black markets in youth or longevity suppression, radical religious movements around "natural death," a culture obsessed with age that looks nothing like anything in present history.

The extrapolation is where SF does its real work. A world that has your novum but otherwise looks like the present is the main failure mode — "the present in disguise with chrome." If your future humans use smartphones and drive cars and have the same political institutions as 2024, you haven't extrapolated.

The extrapolation test: *For each major aspect of your world (economy, politics, religion, family structure, daily life), ask: how is this different because of the novum? If the answer is "it isn't," that aspect needs rethinking.*

### Exposition in SF

SF has a harder exposition problem than fantasy. Fantasy can use the "secondary world" convention — the reader accepts that they're in a different world and needs to learn its rules. SF is set in a version of reality, which means the world needs to feel familiar enough to be recognizable while being different enough to be SF. Straddling that requires more precision.

**Strategies:**

**In medias res with context from action**: throw the reader into a scene that demonstrates the world's rules through character action. The exposition happens because the character *needs* the information in the scene, or because the reader infers it from what characters do and don't react to.

**The outsider**: a character new to an environment who needs things explained. The tourist, the new arrival, the person returned after a long absence. This is slightly clichéd but effective because it's realistic — people do explain things to newcomers.

**Distributed exposition**: break the world-building into fragments, spread across many scenes. The reader assembles the picture piece by piece. This requires you to know exactly which pieces are essential and in what order they need to arrive.

**The info-dump problem**: SF has a tradition of longer exposition than most genres can survive because readers self-select for tolerance of world-building. This is a trap — you can get away with more info-dump in SF than in literary fiction, but you should still minimize it. Every page of explanation is a page without story.

### SF Subgenres and Their Conventions

| Subgenre | Core Novum | Reader Contract |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Hard SF | Rigorous science extrapolation | Scientific accuracy; engineering problem-solving |
| Cyberpunk | Near-future surveillance, corporate power, hacker counter-culture | Dystopian atmosphere, moral complexity, tech as oppressor |
| Space opera | FTL travel, galactic civilization | Epic scope, adventure, the cosmos as canvas |
| Climate fiction (cli-fi) | Climate collapse and its consequences | Urgency, near-future realism, survival or political response |
| Military SF | Near-future warfare, soldiers, strategy | Competence, unit cohesion, moral cost of violence |
| First contact | Encounter with alien intelligence | The alien as genuinely alien; humanity seen from outside |
| Post-apocalyptic | Collapse of civilization | Survival, social rebuilding, what we lose and keep |
| Solarpunk | Optimistic ecological/social futures | Community, sustainability, possibility |

Genre contracts matter: a reader who picks up hard SF for rigorous science and finds space opera hand-waving feels cheated. Signal your subgenre in the first chapter.

## Key Considerations

**The sense of wonder is a structural goal.** SF has its own version of the sublime — the moment when the scale or strangeness of the universe hits the reader emotionally. *The stars, Holden realized, had always been fighting this war.* Plan for at least one scene that earns genuine wonder. It doesn't require pyrotechnics; it requires the reader to feel the vertigo of the SF premise fully.

**Technology has politics.** Who benefits from this technology and who is excluded? Whose problems does it solve? Who controls access? Technology in fiction that doesn't change power relationships hasn't been fully thought through. The most resonant SF extrapolates technology's consequences for the disadvantaged first.

**Character arc doesn't disappear.** Even engineering stories need a protagonist with something to learn or become. Watney in *The Martian* doesn't transform, but we care because his competence and personality are vivid and his survival matters. If you're writing sociological SF, the character arc is the engine. If you're writing hard SF, character can be lighter — but it still can't be absent.

## Common Mistakes

**The present in disguise.** A future where everything is the same except people have spaceships. Technology changes culture, politics, economics, and identity. If none of those have changed, the world isn't built — it's skinned.

**The protagonist-explains-the-world.** The character narrates the world's history and rules to the reader via interior monologue or dialogue with convenient strangers. Real people don't think about their world's history in textbook form. Find mechanisms that deliver exposition naturally.

**One novum, poorly extrapolated.** You've picked a great big idea but followed it only one layer deep. Push to the second and third-order effects — that's where the story actually lives.

**Hard SF that forgets character.** Solving technical problems is interesting for a limited time without emotional stakes. The best hard SF uses technical problems to reveal character under pressure. What does a person *do* when every option is bad? That's still a character question even when it's expressed in chemistry.

## Resources

- *The Left Hand of Darkness* by Ursula K. Le Guin — the best example of sociological SF extrapolated fully: one novum (no fixed gender), an entire civilization built from its consequences, a plot that earns its emotion through the worldbuilding
- *Stories of Your Life and Others* by Ted Chiang — short fiction; every story is a model of how to take one speculative idea and pursue it to its most human conclusion
- *Ender's Game* by Orson Scott Card — engineering + character; shows how to blend technical problem-solving with emotional stakes; the most studied military SF novel for structural lessons
- Darko Suvin's *Metamorphoses of Science Fiction* — the academic origin of "novum" theory; useful if you want the literary-critical vocabulary for what SF does
