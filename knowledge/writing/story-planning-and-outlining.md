# Story Planning and Outlining
*The layer between idea and manuscript: how to plan a novel without killing the story*

---

## What Is It

Story planning is everything that happens between "I have an idea" and "I'm writing chapter one." It covers the spectrum from a single-sentence concept to a detailed chapter-by-chapter map — and the question of how much planning you need before starting.

The structural frameworks for *what* a story needs (three-act structure, character arc, dramatic question) live in [story-structure.md](story-structure.md). This file covers the *process* of how to get from idea to plan: the tools, the methods, the tradeoffs, and what to figure out before you start versus what to discover as you write.

---

## Core Concepts

### The Planning Spectrum

Every writer lands somewhere on this axis:

**Discovery writing** (writing into the dark): You start with a character or a situation and follow it. You don't know where the story ends until you get there. Benefits: organic character development, surprises that feel earned because they surprised you too. Costs: dead ends, structural problems that only appear at 60,000 words, sometimes writing 30,000 words to find out the story starts at page 200. Stephen King and George R.R. Martin are the most cited examples.

**Full plotting**: You outline the entire book before writing a word. Beat by beat, scene by scene. Benefits: you know the story works before investing in prose; structural problems get solved cheaply in outline form. Costs: can produce mechanical prose, kills spontaneity, characters sometimes refuse to follow the outline. Brandon Sanderson is the most cited example.

**The hybrid (most useful position)**: Know your major beats — the inciting incident, the midpoint shift, the dark night, the climax, and the ending — before you start. Discover the scenes between those beats as you write. This gives you a through-line you won't lose while preserving freedom in the moment.

The right position depends on how you think. If you write yourself into walls and abandon projects, you need more structure. If your outlines produce prose that feels like following instructions, you need less.

### The Three-Act Skeleton as Planning Tool

Before anything else, answer these five questions. They're the load-bearing elements — everything else can be discovered.

1. **What is the inciting incident?** The event that disrupts the protagonist's ordinary world and starts the story's clock.
2. **What is the protagonist's lock-in moment?** The point at which they commit to the conflict and can't retreat. End of Act 1.
3. **What is the midpoint?** The event that shifts the story's direction — a false victory or false defeat that raises stakes or changes the goal.
4. **What is the dark night?** The lowest point. The protagonist has failed, lost something essential, and doesn't know how to win.
5. **What is the ending?** How does the central dramatic question resolve? What does the protagonist gain or lose or become?

With these five answers, you have a skeleton. The entire planning process is filling in between the bones.

### Beat Sheets

A beat sheet is a one-page (or one-document) list of the story's major structural moments — the beats — without scene-level detail. It gives you a map without telling you every step.

**What makes a good beat sheet:**
- Each beat is a concrete event, not a theme: "Elena discovers the treaty was forged" not "tension escalates"
- The emotional consequence of each beat is named: what does the protagonist feel/decide/lose?
- The beats connect causally: this happens *because* that happened, not just *after* it

**What a beat sheet shouldn't be:** a chapter outline with every scene listed. That's too much specificity too early. Keep the beat sheet at 15–25 beats for a novel.

The Save the Cat 15-beat structure (from [story-structure.md](story-structure.md)) is a useful template for the beat sheet, though it's more natural for commercial genre fiction than for literary work.

### The Snowflake Method

Developed by Randy Ingermanson. The idea: start from the smallest unit and expand outward, like a mathematical fractal.

**The five steps:**

1. **One sentence**: Your premise in one sentence. A specific protagonist in a specific situation forced to make a specific choice with specific stakes. No abstractions.
2. **One paragraph**: Three disasters and an ending. Act 1 disaster (end of setup), Act 2 midpoint disaster, Act 2 final disaster (dark night), and how it resolves. Four sentences.
3. **One page**: Expand each sentence of the paragraph into a paragraph. Now you have four paragraphs covering the major movements.
4. **Character synopses**: For each major character — their story from their own POV. What do they want? What are they hiding? How does the story end for them?
5. **Chapter summaries**: Expand the one-page outline into one paragraph per chapter.

The method is more systematic than most writers need, but the first two steps (one sentence, one paragraph) are universally useful. If you can't do them, your story doesn't have a clear engine yet.

### Scene Cards

Index cards (physical or digital) with one scene per card. Each card contains:
- **Who**: the POV character
- **Goal**: what do they want in this scene?
- **Conflict**: what opposes them?
- **Outcome**: what happens (success / failure / complication)?
- **Function**: what does this scene do for the story (advance plot / reveal character / change the situation)?

Scene cards let you move scenes around without rewriting prose. Discovering that the scene you wrote at chapter 4 actually belongs at chapter 2 is a 30-second rearrangement of cards versus a major revision. Do this planning before writing.

**Practical use**: create scene cards for the beats you've already locked in, then fill in the gaps one-by-one as you plan. By the time you've got 25-30 scene cards that causally connect, you're ready to draft.

### Character Bibles

A character bible is a private document that captures everything about a major character that may or may not appear in the story. It's an iceberg document — what you know shapes how you write them, even when it never surfaces.

**Minimum viable character bible:**
- **Want (external)**: what do they pursue in the plot?
- **Need (internal)**: what do they actually need to be whole? Usually different from what they want.
- **Lie**: what false belief shapes their behavior?
- **Ghost**: the wound or event in their past that produced the lie
- **Voice**: two or three sentences describing how this character talks and thinks — the specific texture of their interiority

The lie and the ghost are the most important elements for planning purposes. The character's arc is the story of the lie being exposed and either embraced or rejected. If you know those two things, you know the shape of their arc before you write a word.

---

### Tracking POV and Timeline

Multi-POV or non-linear stories need structural tracking tools that single-POV linear stories don't.

**POV tracking**: create a simple table — chapter number, POV character, scene location, timeline date, what this chapter accomplishes. Scan it to see: is one character disappearing for 8 chapters? Are two POVs covering the same timeline events redundantly? Is the pacing even?

**Timeline tracking**: for non-linear stories (flashbacks, multiple timelines), a timeline document prevents paradoxes and keeps you honest about when characters know what. Two columns: story time (when events happen) and narrative time (when the reader sees them). If those diverge, that's intentional — mark it.

**The chapter map**: a single page with all chapters listed, POV labeled, timeline location noted, and one-line summary. The aerial view. This is where you spot that your protagonist disappears for 60 pages or that the midpoint isn't actually in the middle.

## Key Considerations

**The outline is not the story.** Characters may refuse to do what the outline needs. This is information. When a character would never plausibly do what the outline requires, the outline is wrong — either the character is wrong for the story, or the story needs a different turn. Don't force it.

**Some problems are only visible in the draft.** Outlining can solve structural problems cheaply. It cannot solve prose-level problems: voice, scene pacing, dialogue rhythm, emotional texture. Don't use outline complexity as a substitute for drafting.

**Planning and revision are the same skill.** The same thinking that goes into planning before the draft applies when diagnosing problems in the draft. A writer who can plan well can also revise well — they're both about understanding story structure from the outside.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Outlining every scene before writing.** Usually counterproductive — too much specificity at the wrong stage kills the discovery process that makes prose feel alive. Outline the beats; discover the scenes.

**Starting without a dramatic question.** If you can't state what the reader is reading toward, you don't have a story engine yet. You have a premise or a character. Those are inputs, not stories.

**Planning to avoid writing.** Expanding the character bible when you should be drafting. Building lore documents when you should be drafting. The plan is a tool for the story, not the thing itself. At some point you have to stop planning and write into the uncertainty.

**Abandoning the outline at the first divergence.** When the story wants to go somewhere unexpected, evaluate: is this better? If yes, update the outline to match and keep the divergence. Don't abandon the plan entirely because one scene changed — update it.
