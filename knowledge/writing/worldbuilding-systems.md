# Worldbuilding Systems
*How to build a secondary world from geography outward — the systematic process for internal consistency*

---

## What Is It

Worldbuilding is the discipline of constructing a fictional setting that behaves as if it were real. The key word is *behaves* — a world that looks detailed on a map but falls apart when you ask "what do they eat?" or "why is there a city here?" isn't built, it's decorated.

The systems approach starts with geography and works outward through ecology, economy, culture, religion, and politics — each layer emerging from the one below it. This file covers that process. See [fantasy-story-building.md](fantasy-story-building.md) for how to integrate a built world into a fantasy narrative, and [magic-system-design.md](magic-system-design.md) for how magic fits into (and disrupts) the system.

## Core Concepts

### Geography as the Root

Everything downstream of geography. Before designing cultures, design terrain.

**Mountains**: create rain shadows (wet windward side, dry leeward). They channel trade through passes, which become strategically critical and often controlled by whoever wants to tax or block movement. Mountain peoples tend to be insular, independent, and skilled at specific crafts that compensate for agricultural limitation.

**Rivers**: the arteries of civilization. Pre-industrial trade moves by water because roads are expensive and slow. Cities form at river confluences, river mouths, and the lowest fordable points (which are also the cheapest place to build a bridge, which is also where armies cross). Rivers define political borders — and cause wars when they shift course.

**Coastlines and harbors**: natural harbors produce maritime culture, trade networks, and naval power. A people with no harbor learns to be self-sufficient; a people with three good harbors builds an empire through trade. The Mediterranean made Greece; the Atlantic made Britain.

**Arable land**: where food grows determines where people live at scale. Civilizations cluster around river deltas, plains, and temperate zones. Deserts are not uninhabited — they're crossroads for nomadic people who control trade routes others can't.

The practical tool: before placing a city, ask why anyone would build here. Safe harbor, river crossing, defensible position, fertile valley, access to a specific resource. Cities without reasons feel like stage sets.

### Economy and Resources

What a society has shapes what it needs, what it trades for, and what it will fight over.

**Resource map first**: where are the metals, the forests, the fish, the fertile land, the stone, the herbs, the dyes? Whoever controls a critical resource has power. Iron ore near the surface created the Iron Age — the civilization sitting on it had a structural military advantage.

**Trade routes**: resources that are unevenly distributed create trade. Trade routes create wealth for whoever controls them (taxation, tolls, intermediary trade). Control of trade routes is why empires expand — they're often not after land but after the roads.

**Specialization and dependency**: a city in a dense forest specializes in lumber and furniture; a city on volcanic soil specializes in wine or olives; a desert people specializes in camels, trade facilitation, and water management. Dependency creates alliances and vulnerabilities. A nation that imports its grain is one bad harvest from political collapse.

**Currency and exchange**: how does trade work? Barter systems collapse above a certain complexity. What acts as currency — metal, grain receipts, credit issued by a temple? Who controls the currency supply controls the economy.

### Culture from Material Conditions

What people eat, how they build, what they wear, and what they value all emerge from the environment they live in.

**Diet and agriculture**: rice cultures (East Asia) develop around communal irrigation — coordinated labor creates cultural emphasis on collective harmony. Wheat cultures can be farmed by individual families — emphasis on individual land ownership. Pastoral nomadic cultures value mobility, raiding, and honor codes around hospitality and reciprocity.

**Architecture**: what materials are locally abundant? Stone buildings in stone-rich regions; timber in forested regions; mudbrick in the Nile valley. The lifespan of building material shapes the culture's relationship to permanence. A culture whose buildings last 3,000 years thinks differently about history than one whose buildings rot in 50.

**Clothing and craft**: what fibers are available (wool, linen, silk, cotton)? Who controls dye sources (purple was power in Rome because Tyrian purple required thousands of sea snails per ounce). Craft traditions reflect material availability and trade access.

**Values**: cultures that survive by herding livestock through contested territory develop honor cultures with swift, public responses to disrespect — reputation is practical survival. Cultures with stable surplus develop institutional trust systems, contracts, and rule of law because they can afford the slower machinery of justice.

### Religion and Mythology

Religion emerges from three sources: existential fear, social coordination, and explanation of the unexplained. Build religion from those functions and it feels real.

**What does this culture fear?** Drought? Winter? Sea storms? Plague? The things that can kill a people at scale become the domains of their most powerful gods. Agricultural civilizations worship fertility and rain gods; seafaring civilizations worship storm and sea gods; pastoral nomads worship sky gods.

**What does it coordinate?** Religion enforces social norms through supernatural sanction (break the taboo, god punishes you) and calendar coordination (everyone plants/harvests on the same day because the priest says so). Both functions are pragmatically useful — religion spreads where it provides real coordination value.

**What does it explain?** Creation myths explain why the world is the way it is — and usually mirror that culture's values and power structure. A warrior culture's creation myth often involves gods at war. An agricultural culture's myth often involves a dying and rising god (the cycle of seasons).

**Pantheons vs monotheism**: polytheism handles an uncertain, diverse world — different gods for different domains, negotiated with differently. Monotheism tends to emerge with empire (one god mirrors one king) or with a people under existential threat (a single, focused covenant creates group cohesion).

### Power and Politics

Who controls the food, the metal, the soldiers, and the money controls the political structure.

**How does leadership transfer?** Hereditary monarchy concentrates power in bloodlines and produces succession crises. Elected leadership produces coalition politics and influence-trading. Theocracy concentrates power in whoever controls religious authority. Military rule produces short-lived stability followed by coup cycles.

**What holds the coalition together?** Every political system is a coalition — king + nobles + church + military. The coalition holds while all parties believe they gain more from cooperation than defection. When one party grows strong enough that defection becomes viable, the system destabilizes.

**What does the bureaucracy look like?** Empires require record-keeping, tax collection, and law enforcement at scale. Who are the civil servants? How are they recruited and controlled? The Tang dynasty's examination system, the Ottoman devshirme, the Roman equestrian class — the mechanism for producing bureaucrats shapes what the empire can and cannot do.

**Local vs. central power**: the tension between local lords (who have land, soldiers, and loyalty) and central authority (who has legitimacy, law, and currency) is the engine of most political history. Fantasy politics is most realistic when this tension is alive.

### The Consistency Test

Once you've built the layers, test each element against every other.

The core question: *if X is true, what else must be true?*

- If magic can heal any wound → there are no permanent disabilities, the surgery profession doesn't exist, armies fight differently
- If a city is on a river crossing → it has a bridge, a toll, bridge guards, people who build and repair bridges, inns for travelers, merchants
- If this culture worships a harvest god → they have harvest festivals, taboos around food waste, priests who control agricultural timing, and political power concentrated in whoever controls the temple

The inconsistency test catches what feels wrong before readers do. Run it on your world's most powerful elements: the magic, the geography, the dominant religion, the political structure. Each should leave marks on everything else.

## Key Considerations

**Build only what your story touches.** A world map with six continents you'll never visit is set dressing. Build the geography your characters move through, the economy they participate in, the religion that shapes their assumptions. Depth in the places that matter; sketches elsewhere.

**The iceberg ratio.** Tolkien had etymology, genealogies, and cosmology that never appeared on the page — but it gave him confidence and his world its weight. You need to know more than you show. How much more is a judgment call; the rule is that you shouldn't be inventing things in the moment of writing that contradict what you've already built.

**Magic disrupts every layer.** If your world has magic, it doesn't slot into an otherwise normal medieval world. It changes the economy, the military, the religion, the politics. See [magic-system-design.md](magic-system-design.md) for the society-implication checklist.

## Common Mistakes

**The generic European medieval default.** A world with no internally generated culture, just a rough approximation of 1200 AD England because that's what fantasy looks like. Geography produces culture; culture is specific. Give your world a material foundation and the culture will be distinctive without effort.

**Cities in bad locations.** Cities exist where people can survive and trade. A city in the middle of a barren plain with no water, no trade route, and no resources is a writer's convenience, not a world. Ask why anyone built here.

**Ignoring the second and third order.** Magic exists → so? Technology shifts → so? The first-order effect is obvious; what matters is what follows from that. A culture that has had flying couriers for 400 years has different political boundaries, different trade networks, and different military doctrine than one that doesn't.

**Cultures with no internal conflict.** Real cultures are coalitions of groups with different interests — classes, guilds, regional factions, religious orders, ethnic minorities. A culture that is uniformly one thing is a cartoon. Layered internal conflict makes a world feel inhabited.

## Getting Started

1. **Sketch your geography first** — continent shape, mountain ranges, major rivers, climate zones. Everything else follows from this.
2. **Identify your world's critical resource** — what one thing does power depend on? Metal, magic, water, fertile land, a trade route? This is your world's fundamental political driver.
3. **Build one civilization fully** — the one your story is set in. Geography → economy → culture → religion → power structure. Leave others as sketches.
4. **Run the consistency test on your magic** — if magic exists in your world, ask what it changes about economy, military, medicine, religion, and politics.
5. **Build backward from your story's conflict** — what political or economic condition makes your story's central conflict possible? That's what needs to be built in detail.

## Resources

- *The Art of World Building* by Randy Ellefson — the most systematic treatment; comprehensive checklists for every layer from geography to language
- *Guns, Germs, and Steel* by Jared Diamond — a genuine argument for how geography determines civilization; the most useful non-fiction source for understanding why cultures are the way they are
- Brandon Sanderson's worldbuilding lectures (YouTube, BYU creative writing course) — practical, focused on what a working fantasy novelist actually builds
