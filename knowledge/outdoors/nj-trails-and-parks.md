# NJ Trails and Parks
*The best outdoor destinations in New Jersey — Pinelands, shore, mountains, and water trails — organized by region and what they're actually good for*

---

## What Is It

New Jersey has more preserved open space per square mile than most states. The Pinelands alone is 1.1 million acres. The Delaware Water Gap is one of the best hiking regions in the mid-Atlantic. Cape May is a world-class birding and nature destination. Island Beach State Park is a pristine barrier island that looks nothing like the developed shore. Most NJ residents don't use any of it seriously.

This file covers the parks and trails worth knowing — where they are, what they're good for, and what to actually do there. Organized by region, starting from Atlantic County and radiating out.

---

## Why It Matters

Romi is 20 minutes from one of the largest wilderness areas on the East Coast. The Pinelands is in the backyard — and most people who live in Atlantic County have never canoed the Mullica River or hiked the Batona Trail. Building an outdoor practice here is a self-reliance and physical practice available immediately, at low cost, with rare access to genuine wilderness that most of the eastern US doesn't have.

## Core Concepts

### South NJ — Home Territory

**Wharton State Forest**
The largest single tract of land in the NJ state forest system — 122,880 acres in the heart of the Pinelands, 20–30 minutes from Galloway. Most people have never been past the parking lot.

*What it's actually good for:*
- **Batona Trail (53 miles):** The Pinelands' signature hiking trail, running from Ongs Hat in Burlington County through Wharton to Bass River State Forest. Pink-blazed, flat by hiking standards, but long sections of pure Pinelands forest — pitch pine, scrub oak, cedar swamps, open sand. Day sections: the Batsto to Apple Pie Hill section (5 miles out-and-back to the highest point in the Pinelands — 205 feet — with a fire tower, views over the entire forest).
- **Batsto Village:** Historic bog iron and glass-making village from the 1700s–1800s. The Batsto River runs through it. Free to walk the grounds; small fee for the mansion tour. Not a tourist trap — actually interesting. The general store, the workers' village, and the charcoal iron furnace are intact. Good for a 2-hour visit.
- **Water trails (the main draw):** The Mullica, Batsto, Wading, and Oswego Rivers wind through Wharton and are some of the best flatwater paddling in the Northeast. The water is naturally tea-colored (tannic acid from cedar roots) — not polluted, actually cleaner than most streams. Batsto River trip: put in at Quaker Bridge, paddle to Batsto Lake, 9 miles, minimal portages. Mullica River: longer routes possible. Rentals available through Adams Canoe Rentals (Atsion) and Bel Haven Canoe Rental (Green Bank).
- **Atsion Recreation Area:** Swimming lake (lifeguarded in season), picnic area, campground. Good base camp for Wharton exploration.
- **Camping:** Wharton has backcountry sites along the water trails — paddle-in only, first-come. Also car-camping at Atsion and Godfrey Bridge. Backcountry permit required, obtainable from the park office.

*Season:* Year-round. Spring and fall are ideal for paddling (cooler, fewer bugs). Summer is hot and buggy but the swimming holes compensate. Winter hiking is surprisingly good — quiet, no ticks.

*Note on ticks:* The Pinelands has deer ticks (Lyme disease vector). Spray DEET, check after every outing, long socks into boots in tick season (April–October). This is the cost of the wilderness.

**Island Beach State Park**
A 10-mile barrier island south of Seaside Heights — undeveloped, accessible only by foot or bike beyond the parking areas. One of the last pristine barrier island ecosystems on the mid-Atlantic coast.

*What it's good for:*
- **Surf fishing:** One of the best surf fishing spots in NJ. Striped bass runs in fall (October–November) are legendary. Bluefish in summer. Fluke in the bay side. A NJ saltwater fishing license is required (resident: $22/year from nj.gov). The state record for many species has been broken here.
- **Swimming:** Several lifeguarded beaches in the northern recreation area. No boardwalk, no concessions beyond basics. The beach itself is what you come for.
- **Birding:** The southern natural area has birding trails through maritime shrubland. Osprey nest here; migratory shorebirds in spring and fall.
- **Kayaking:** Bay side (Barnegat Bay) is calm and excellent for kayaking. Launch from the park's bay access.

*Getting there:* Garden State Parkway to Exit 82, then Route 37 east to the park entrance. 75 minutes from Galloway. Day-use fee: $6–$20 depending on season (fee from Memorial Day–Labor Day).

---

**Edwin B. Forsythe National Wildlife Refuge**
A 47,000-acre National Wildlife Refuge straddling Atlantic and Ocean counties, headquarters in Oceanville (5 minutes from Galloway). One of the most accessible birding refuges on the East Coast.

*What it's good for:*
- **Birding (world-class):** Forsythe sits on the Atlantic Flyway — the primary migration route for birds traveling the East Coast. Peak birding: fall migration (September–November) for shorebirds, ducks, and raptors; spring (April–May) for wading birds and songbirds. The 8-mile Wildlife Drive (self-guided car/walk loop) overlooks salt marsh impoundments — thousands of ducks and geese in fall is a real spectacle.
- **Wildlife observation:** Snow geese, Canada geese, great blue herons, ospreys, bald eagles (resident), diamondback terrapins (spring), red fox.
- **Photography:** The open marsh views are outstanding for wildlife photography. Golden hour light on the impoundments is excellent.
- **Walking:** The Songbird Trail (0.5 mile) and Akers Woods Trail (1 mile) are short but pass through diverse habitats.

*Cost:* $4/vehicle (fee station on Wildlife Drive). No fee for walking trails.

---

**Cape May**
The southern tip of NJ — a Victorian resort town, National Historic Landmark, and the best birding location in the eastern US. 75 minutes from Galloway.

*What it's actually good for:*
- **Birding:** Cape May Peninsula is a geographic funnel — birds migrating south hit the Delaware Bay and stage at the tip before crossing. In September and October, millions of birds concentrate here. The Cape May Bird Observatory runs guided walks; hawkwatch at Cape May Point State Park is free and extraordinary in fall.
- **Cape May Point State Park:** Lighthouse, beach, hawk watch platform, birding trails through dunes and freshwater ponds. Free entry. The lighthouse ($12 to climb) has views of both the Atlantic and Delaware Bay.
- **The Beach:** Cape May's beaches are calmer and less crowded than the central shore. Beach tag required in season ($10/day).
- **Sunset Beach:** Small beach on the Delaware Bay side where the sun sets over the water — unusual for NJ's Atlantic-facing coastline. Cape May diamonds (quartz pebbles) can be found on the beach. Free, no tags.
- **Whale and dolphin watching:** Cape May Whale Watcher runs trips April–November. Humpbacks and fin whales are reliably seen in season.
- **Wine:** Turdo Vineyards (Cape May) and Cape May Winery — both worth the stop for a tasting if you're already down there.

---

**Brigantine (Back Bays / AC area)**
The bay behind Atlantic City and Brigantine Island is overlooked. Excellent kayaking — calm water, osprey nests, views of the AC skyline from the water. Put in at Gardners Basin (AC) or Brigantine City parks.

### Central NJ

**Allaire State Park (Monmouth County)**
Historic village (Allaire Village, bog iron industry like Batsto), trail system through second-growth coastal plain forest. Quieter than Wharton. 90 minutes from Galloway.

**Cheesequake State Park (Middlesex/Monmouth)**
Rare — sits at the transition zone between northern deciduous forest and southern Pinelands coastal plain forest. Unusual mix of plant communities. Good hiking, freshwater lake swimming.

**Round Valley Reservoir (Hunterdon County)**
NJ's deepest lake — 180 feet. Cold, clear water. Trout fishing is excellent. Backcountry camping accessible only by boat or foot (3 miles). A rare experience in NJ.

---

### North NJ and Day Trips

**Delaware Water Gap National Recreation Area**
45,000 acres straddling NJ and Pennsylvania along the Delaware River. The closest significant mountain hiking to Atlantic County — 2 hours from Galloway.

*What it's good for:*
- **Hiking:** Mount Tammany (1,527 feet) — the signature hike. Red dot trail from the parking lot is steep, about 3.5 miles round trip, views of the Delaware River Gap. Challenging by NJ standards. Also Dunnfield Creek Trail (waterfall, rhododendron forest), Appalachian Trail sections.
- **Swimming:** Dingmans Falls (PA side), Buttermilk Falls (NJ side), and the Delaware River beaches. The river swims are cold, clear, and dramatically beautiful.
- **Canoeing/tubing:** Delaware River tubing is a summer ritual — Kittatinny Canoes and Wild and Scenic River Tours offer rentals. Multi-day canoe camping trips are possible with backcountry permits.
- **Fall foliage:** Peak usually mid-October. One of the best foliage destinations within 2 hours of Atlantic County.

*Getting there:* Route 78 west to Exit 1 (NJ) or I-80 west to Exit 1. 2 hours from Galloway.

---

**Harriman State Park (Rockland County, NY — 2.5 hours)**
Technically New York, but worth knowing. 47,000 acres, 200+ miles of trails, 30 lakes. Genuinely challenging hiking — rocky, technical by NJ standards, real elevation gain. The Appalachian Trail crosses through. Popular but large enough to find solitude on weekdays.

**High Point State Park (Sussex County)**
NJ's highest point (1,803 feet). Monument at the summit. Views into NY, PA, and NJ on clear days. Appalachian Trail access. Good in summer and fall.

**Skylands Region (Warren/Sussex/Hunterdon)**
NJ's rural northwest — covered bridges, apple orchards, farm wineries, small towns. Worth a day trip in fall (October apple/cider season). Hackettstown, Hope, Blairstown area.

## Key Considerations

**The Pinelands requires gear adjustment.** Sandy trails mean boots or trail runners with good grip. Water trails require a dry bag for keys, phone, wallet — the water is cold and dark.

**Beach tags are required May–September** at most NJ shore towns. They're sold daily or seasonally at borough offices or beach entrances. Cape May, Island Beach State Park, and state-owned beaches typically don't have local tags — just the state fee or no fee.

**A NJ fishing license is required** for freshwater fishing (any age 16+) and saltwater fishing (16+). Both available at nj.gov/dep/fgw for $22–$32/year resident. Worth getting if you'll fish more than once.

**Tick protocols are non-negotiable.** Lyme disease is endemic in NJ — particularly in the Pinelands, Delaware Water Gap, and any wooded area. DEET spray before entering brush, full-body check after every outing, remove any attached tick within 24 hours to significantly reduce transmission risk.

**High Point, Delaware Water Gap, and Harriman are 2–2.5 hours from Galloway.** Leave early — trailhead parking fills by 9 AM on weekends at popular spots.

## Getting Started

1. **This week:** Forsythe Wildlife Refuge — 5 minutes away, free, requires no planning or gear. Walk the Wildlife Drive.
2. **Next month:** Batsto Village + a section of the Batona Trail — 25 minutes from Galloway. Bring water and bug spray.
3. **Spring:** Rent a canoe from Adams Canoe Rentals and paddle the Batsto River — one of the better half-day experiences available from home.
4. **Fall:** Delaware Water Gap for Mount Tammany during foliage (mid-October). Book early; parking fills.
5. **Get a fishing license** at nj.gov if surf fishing or freshwater fishing interests you.

## Common Mistakes

**Going to the shore and skipping Island Beach.** The developed beach towns are fine but Island Beach is a different experience entirely — worth driving past Seaside Heights to reach it.

**Canoeing the Pinelands in summer without bug prep.** Dusk and dawn on the rivers in June–August are intense. Long sleeves, DEET, start early.

**Underestimating Mount Tammany.** It's not technical mountaineering, but it's a real hike with real exposure and significant elevation gain. Not a stroll. Wear proper footwear.

**Never going to Forsythe.** It's 5 minutes from Galloway. The fall migration spectacle of 50,000 snow geese lifting off the impoundments is one of the better wildlife experiences in the region and most people who live adjacent to it have never seen it.
