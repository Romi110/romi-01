# Legal Literacy
*How to read a contract, recognize what matters, and know when to hire a lawyer*

---

## What Is It

Legal literacy is the ability to read and interpret legal documents at a functional level — contracts, agreements, notices, and legal correspondence — without needing a lawyer for every transaction. It's not becoming a lawyer. It's being able to tell what a document actually commits you to, which clauses are high-risk, and when the situation warrants professional help.

Most legal documents you encounter in life are standard agreements written in dense language that most people sign without reading. The people who write them count on that.

## Why It Matters

At 28, with homeownership, a business, and growing assets: you will sign significant legal documents regularly. Leases, contractor agreements, LLC operating agreements, vendor contracts, employment agreements, IP assignments. The default is to sign without understanding. The cost of that default ranges from minor inconveniences to significant financial and legal exposure.

The goal isn't to negotiate every clause — it's to know which ones are worth pushing back on, which are standard and fine, and when to stop and get an attorney.

## Core Concepts

### How Contracts Work

A contract is an enforceable agreement. For it to be valid: offer, acceptance, consideration (something of value exchanged on both sides), and capacity (both parties are legally able to contract — adults, not incapacitated). Verbal contracts can be binding, but proving them is difficult. Put anything that matters in writing.

**Reading order that actually matters:**
1. Parties — who is actually signing and in what capacity (personally vs. as an LLC)
2. What's being exchanged — deliverables, payment, timeline
3. Limitation of liability clause
4. Indemnification clause
5. Termination and cancellation terms
6. Dispute resolution (arbitration vs. court)
7. Everything else

The rest of the document usually elaborates on these seven. If you understand those sections, you understand the contract.

### Key Clauses to Know

**Limitation of Liability**
Caps the maximum damages one party can owe the other. Usually written as "in no event shall either party be liable for amounts exceeding..." followed by a number or formula. Standard in B2B contracts. Two things to check: (1) whether it's mutual or one-sided, and (2) whether it excludes certain categories (like intentional misconduct or IP infringement — those carve-outs are appropriate).

**Indemnification**
One party agrees to defend and hold harmless the other against certain claims. This is one of the highest-risk clauses for a small business or individual. Watch for: (1) overly broad scope ("any claim arising from your work"), (2) one-sided indemnification (only you indemnify them, not the reverse), and (3) indemnification without a limitation of liability — meaning you're on the hook for unlimited third-party claims.

Standard, reasonable indemnification: each party indemnifies the other for their own gross negligence, willful misconduct, or IP infringement. Unreasonable: you indemnify them for anything, even events outside your control.

**Arbitration Clause**
Requires disputes to be resolved through private arbitration rather than court. Arbitration is faster and cheaper for small disputes but removes your right to a jury trial, limits discovery, and often caps damages (or makes them harder to collect). Many consumer and employment contracts use mandatory arbitration. For significant commercial relationships, this clause deserves attention — arbitration can be fine or problematic depending on the stakes and who pays for it.

**IP Assignment**
In service contracts and employment agreements, this clause determines who owns work product. The default in a work-for-hire arrangement (employment or contractor creating something for a client) is that the client/employer owns the IP — but only if the contract says so clearly. Ambiguity here is expensive.

For a contractor: make sure the clause specifies that you retain ownership of pre-existing work and tools, and that assignment only covers the specific deliverables under the agreement. A clause assigning "all IP created while providing services" can inadvertently grab side projects if poorly written.

For an employee: NJ restricts overly broad IP assignment — NJ law allows employers to claim inventions created on company time or with company resources, but not inventions created on your own time with your own resources unrelated to the employer's business. Know what you signed.

**Non-Compete**
An agreement not to work for competitors for a period after employment or a contract ends. NJ law limits their enforceability — courts look at scope (geography, time, activity), whether the restriction is reasonable, and whether you received adequate consideration. As of recent NJ court decisions, overly broad non-competes are frequently not enforced. Still: read them, understand what you're agreeing to even if you believe it's unenforceable, and push back on scope that's clearly excessive.

**Termination and Cancellation**
How either party exits the agreement, under what conditions, with how much notice, and what happens to obligations on exit (payment for work done, IP ownership, confidentiality survival). "At-will termination with 30 days notice" is standard and reasonable. Watch for: long notice periods that trap you, automatic renewal clauses without easy opt-out, and terms that require payment even after you cancel.

**Governing Law and Venue**
Which state's law applies and where disputes are heard. For significant contracts with parties in other states, governing law matters — some states are more favorable for certain types of disputes. If you're an NJ-based business dealing with a California company, try to keep governing law in NJ (or at least not in a state you'd have to litigate in).

### When to Hire a Lawyer

**Don't need a lawyer:**
- Standard residential lease (read it; negotiate the obvious; sign)
- Small vendor or service agreements under $5,000 where liability is limited
- Credit card agreements, phone contracts — standard, non-negotiable
- Simple NDAs from large companies (they're standard boilerplate; if you sign a lot, learn to read them yourself)

**Get a lawyer:**
- Any real estate transaction (purchase, commercial lease) — this is the highest value use of legal spend you'll make
- Business formation and LLC operating agreements — the document that governs ownership, equity splits, and control
- Any agreement where you're taking on significant indemnification or uncapped liability
- Employment agreements with significant non-compete or IP assignment terms at a new senior role
- Any dispute that's been threatened in writing or involves more than ~$3,000-5,000
- Contracts you can't walk away from if they go wrong

The test: could a dispute under this contract damage your financial life in a meaningful way? If yes, $300-500 for a lawyer's review is cheap insurance.

### Small Claims Court

NJ Small Claims Court handles disputes up to $3,000 (or $5,000 for certain consumer claims). No lawyers required. File a complaint, serve the defendant, appear in court. Used most often for: security deposit disputes, contractor non-performance, damage to personal property, unpaid debts.

The practical steps:
1. File online or in person at your local Superior Court (Special Civil Part — Small Claims Division)
2. Pay filing fee (~$30-50)
3. Serve the defendant (the court can help with this)
4. Attend the hearing — bring documentation (contracts, photos, receipts, text/email records)
5. If you win, you still have to collect — a judgment doesn't mean automatic payment

Before filing: send a certified letter demanding payment with a deadline. Many small disputes resolve with a formal demand letter because the other party doesn't want court appearances either.

### Basic Legal Concepts Worth Knowing

**Statute of limitations**: the deadline to file a legal claim. In NJ, most contract claims: 6 years. Personal injury: 2 years. Medical malpractice: 2 years. Miss the deadline and you lose the right to sue, regardless of merit.

**Burden of proof**: in civil cases (contracts, torts), the standard is "preponderance of the evidence" — more likely than not. Much lower than criminal's "beyond a reasonable doubt." Documentation (emails, contracts, receipts) wins civil cases.

**Personal liability vs. entity liability**: an LLC is a legal entity separate from you. If the LLC is properly maintained (see [finance/asset-protection.md](../finance/asset-protection.md)), debts and judgments against the LLC don't automatically reach your personal assets. This is the core protection and why business structure matters.

**Tort vs. contract**: breach of contract is failing to do what you agreed to do. A tort is a civil wrong independent of any contract — negligence, trespassing, defamation. Most everyday legal disputes are either one or the other; the distinction affects what you're claiming and what damages are available.

## Getting Started

1. **Read the next contract you sign** — all of it, in this order: parties, what's exchanged, limitation of liability, indemnification, termination. Most contracts take 15 minutes to read.
2. **Learn NDA basics** — NDAs are the most commonly signed contracts for anyone in tech. Understand mutual vs. one-sided, what's excluded (public knowledge, independently developed info), and the typical term.
3. **Know your NJ rights as a tenant and employee** — you've probably signed both a lease and an employment agreement. NJ has specific tenant and worker protections that can't be waived by contract.
4. **Identify your next lawyer use case** — if you're buying property or forming a business entity this year, find an attorney before you need them, not after. A 30-minute consultation to establish the relationship is $0-150 and worth it.

## Common Mistakes

**Signing without reading** — the most common and costliest. "I figured it was standard" is not a legal defense. If the terms are truly standard, it takes 15 minutes to confirm that.

**Treating verbal agreements as binding without documentation** — they can be, but proving them is expensive. Immediately after any important verbal agreement, send a confirming email: "Following up on our conversation — we agreed to..." This creates a record.

**Confusing a lawyer's opinion with legal certainty** — lawyers advise on probability and risk, not guarantees. "This clause is likely unenforceable" is not the same as "this clause will not be enforced against you."

**Waiting until there's a problem** — lawyers are far more valuable structuring agreements upfront than litigating bad ones afterward. A $500 contract review prevents a $15,000 dispute.

**Personal guarantees without understanding them** — a personal guarantee on a business lease or loan means your personal assets are on the hook if the business defaults. This is different from normal LLC protection and frequently signed without awareness of the implications.
