# Woodworking Basics
*Hand tools, joinery, and finishing — the foundational making skill for home repair and building things that last*

---

## What Is It

Woodworking is the craft of shaping and joining wood into functional objects — furniture, structures, storage, repairs. At its foundational level it requires almost no power tools: a saw, a chisel, a plane, a mallet, and a square. These hand tools have been the same for centuries and the techniques with them are learnable in a garage or shed.

This file covers the entry point: understanding wood as a material, the essential hand tools and how to use them, the main joinery methods, and how to finish a project properly.

---

## Why It Matters

The ability to build and repair things from wood is one of the most direct expressions of self-reliance. A broken fence rail, a sagging cabinet, a shelf that doesn't exist — a person who can work wood fixes these things rather than waiting or paying. More broadly, woodworking develops spatial reasoning, patience, precision, and the satisfaction of making something real with your hands.

There's also a Stoic angle here: working with a material that has grain, quirks, and natural variation — and learning to work with those rather than against them — builds a kind of practical humility that carries over.

## Core Concepts

### Understanding Wood as a Material

**Grain direction**: Wood has a grain — the direction wood fibers run. Cutting with the grain (along it) is smooth. Cutting against the grain (across it) is harder and tears. Planing against the grain tears the surface; with the grain produces a smooth cut. This is the first principle of working with wood rather than against it.

**Hardwood vs. softwood**: Botanical categories, not hardness ratings. Hardwood comes from deciduous trees (oak, maple, walnut, cherry). Softwood comes from conifers (pine, fir, cedar). Softwood is cheaper and easier to work but dents and scratches more easily. For furniture: hardwood. For construction and learning: pine or fir.

**Wood movement**: Wood expands and contracts with humidity — across the grain, not along it. A board 12 inches wide might move 1/8–1/4 inch seasonally in NJ's humidity swings. Joinery that doesn't account for movement will eventually crack the wood or fail. This is why you don't glue large panels across the grain without allowance for movement.

**Dimensional lumber vs. hardwood**: Dimensional lumber (2×4, 1×6, etc.) from a home center is the starting material for structural and utility work. The stated dimensions are not the actual dimensions (a 2×4 is 1.5"×3.5") — know this before measuring. Hardwood lumber from a wood supplier comes in random widths and thicknesses in rough-sawn form, or S4S (surfaced four sides) for ready-to-work material.

**Common wood species for beginners**:
- *Pine* (softwood): Cheap, available everywhere. Good for learning. Dents easily.
- *Poplar* (hardwood): Affordable, easy to work, takes paint well. Good first hardwood project material.
- *White oak*: The prestige material for furniture. Strong, attractive grain, very durable. More expensive but worth it for things you want to last.

### Essential Hand Tools

You can do meaningful woodworking with seven tools:

**1. Measuring tape and marking tools**: A 25-foot tape, a combination square (measures 90° and 45°, doubles as a depth gauge and level), and a marking knife. A marking knife makes more precise lines than a pencil — the line is a scored groove that guides the saw.

**2. Hand saw**: A standard crosscut saw (cuts across the grain) and a back saw (for fine, precise cuts). The Japanese-style pull saws (Suizan, Gyokucho) cut on the pull stroke and are significantly easier to use and control than Western push-saw designs. A panel saw for rough work, a ryoba (two-edged Japanese saw) for fine work, covers most needs.

**3. Chisels**: A set of bench chisels (¼", ½", ¾", 1") for cutting mortises, trimming joints, and paring wood. Chisels must be sharp — a dull chisel requires force, force causes loss of control, loss of control causes mistakes and injuries. A sharp chisel cuts with hand pressure alone.

**4. Mallet**: For driving chisels. A wooden or rubber mallet — not a metal hammer, which damages chisel handles.

**5. Block plane**: A small hand plane for smoothing end grain, fitting joints, and trimming small amounts of material. Sharp plane on a workpiece: transforms rough surfaces. Dull plane: tears and judders. Sharpening is part of the skill.

**6. Clamps**: The other hand when gluing. F-clamps and spring clamps. You always need more clamps than you think. Clamps hold glue-ups while the adhesive cures and can serve as a third hand during assembly.

**7. Workbench or solid work surface**: You can't work without something to work on. A workbench with a vise is ideal; a sheet of plywood on sawhorses gets you started.

**On power tools**: A drill/driver and a circular saw or miter saw are the useful entry-level power tools that extend what you can do efficiently. A random-orbit sander replaces hand sanding for large surfaces. These are additions, not replacements, for the hand tool foundation.

### Sharpening — The Non-Negotiable Skill

Sharp tools are safe tools. Dull tools require force; force causes slipping; slipping causes cuts. Sharpness is also the difference between work that goes smoothly and work that fights you at every step.

**Sharpening equipment**:
- *Waterstone sharpening stones*: 1000 grit (primary sharpening) and 4000–6000 grit (finishing). Basic sharpening kit.
- *Honing guide*: Holds the chisel or plane blade at a consistent angle while sharpening. Removes the need to freehand the angle. Essential for beginners.
- *Leather strop with compound*: Final polish, removes the wire edge. Optional but produces a sharper final edge.

**The sharpening process**:
1. Flatten the back of the chisel on the 1000-grit stone (only need to do this once on a new tool)
2. Set the bevel angle in the honing guide (typically 25° primary bevel for bench chisels)
3. Work the bevel on 1000-grit until a burr forms on the flat back
4. Move to 4000-grit, work bevel until burr transfers to the bevel side again
6000-grit if you have it
5. Strop both sides to remove the wire edge
6. Test: the blade should shave arm hair and catch on your thumbnail rather than sliding over it

**How often to sharpen**: Whenever the tool feels like it's not cutting cleanly. Experienced woodworkers stop and sharpen frequently — they spend less total time fighting dull tools and more time working efficiently.

### Joinery

Joinery is how two pieces of wood are connected. The choice of joint determines strength, appearance, and how movement is handled.

**Butt joint**: The simplest — one piece ends against the face of another. Held with glue, screws, or nails. Weakest joint; acceptable for basic construction where strength comes from the assembly rather than the joint itself.

**Pocket hole joint**: A screw driven at an angle through one board into another using a jig (Kreg Pocket-Hole Jig, ~$35). Fast, strong for face frames and cabinet construction, invisible from the front. The beginner's shortcut for cabinet and furniture work.

**Dado joint**: A slot cut across the grain to receive another board's end (like a shelf in a bookcase). Provides mechanical support in addition to glue. Strong for shelf applications. Cut with a router or a dado stack on a table saw.

**Rabbet joint**: An L-shaped notch cut along an edge. Used for cabinet backs, drawer bottoms, and door frames. Cut with a router or rabbet plane.

**Mortise and tenon**: The strongest joint in traditional woodworking. A rectangular projection (tenon) on one piece inserts into a matching hole (mortise) in another. Used for chair legs, table aprons, and any joint under significant stress. Cut by hand (chisel for mortise, saw for tenon) or with a drill press and router.

**Dovetail joint**: The traditional joint for drawer construction — interlocking wedge-shaped pins and tails that resist pulling apart. Strong and beautiful. Cut by hand (the hand-cut dovetail is the classic test of woodworking skill) or with a router and dovetail jig.

**Dowel joint**: Round wooden cylinders inserted into aligned holes drilled in both pieces. Aligns boards and adds glue surface area. Less mechanically strong than mortise-and-tenon but much faster.

**Wood glue is strong**: PVA wood glue (yellow glue — Titebond II or III) makes a joint stronger than the wood itself if properly prepared. Proper preparation means: mating surfaces flat and in good contact, clamping pressure sufficient to bring surfaces together, adequate cure time (30 minutes clamping, 24 hours full cure). The glue joint doesn't fail — when an improperly glued joint fails, it's usually because the surfaces weren't flat or clamping pressure was inadequate.

### Finishing

**Why finish wood**: Bare wood absorbs moisture, swells, and stains. A finish seals the surface, controls moisture exchange, and provides appearance and protection.

**The main finish types**:
- *Oil (Danish oil, tung oil, linseed oil)*: Penetrating finish — soaks into the wood rather than sitting on top. Enhances natural color and grain. Provides limited protection but easy to repair. Apply with a rag, wipe off excess, let cure. Good for furniture that will be lightly used and refinished periodically.
- *Wax*: Applied over oil or bare wood. Low protection, beautiful sheen, requires periodic reapplication. Traditional furniture wax (Renaissance Wax, paste wax).
- *Polyurethane*: Film finish — builds up on the surface. High protection, durable, resists water and abrasion. Thick and plastic-looking in multiple coats. Wipe-on poly (thinned polyurethane applied with a rag) builds more slowly but looks less plastic. Good for tabletops and floors.
- *Shellac*: Fast-drying, natural, revivable with alcohol. Good sealer and primer under other finishes. Not water-resistant.
- *Lacquer*: Fast-drying film finish, very smooth. Requires spraying for best results. Professional cabinetmakers' standard.
- *Hard wax oil (Rubio Monocoat)*: One-coat penetrating finish that provides good protection. Popular for flooring and furniture.

**Surface preparation determines finish quality**: Finish reveals imperfections it doesn't hide them. Sand progressively: 80 → 120 → 150 → 180 → 220 grit. Each grit removes the scratches from the previous. Always sand with the grain; cross-grain scratches will show under finish.

**Raise the grain before final sanding**: After sanding to 180, wet the surface lightly with water, let dry completely, sand lightly with 220. This removes the raised grain fibers that would otherwise raise under the first coat of finish.

**Between coats**: Sand lightly with 320-grit or fine steel wool (0000) between finish coats to knock down dust nibs and improve adhesion. Wipe clean before applying the next coat.

## Key Considerations

**Measure twice, cut once is real**: Wood removed cannot be replaced. Marks must be precise, measurements verified. Cutting a piece 1/16" short ruins it; cutting it 1/16" long means you can trim.

**Start simple**: A small box, a shelf bracket, a picture frame. These cover the core skills — measuring, cutting to length, forming a joint, finishing — without the complexity of full furniture. The skills compound.

**Safety fundamentals**: Clamp the workpiece rather than holding it. Cut away from your body. Sharp tools are safer than dull ones. When using power tools: safety glasses always, hearing protection for prolonged use, no loose clothing near rotating blades.

**Wood is not a uniform material**: Knots, figure, and grain variation make every board different. Embrace this — learning to read a board and plan the cut with its character (rather than against it) is one of the things that separates experienced woodworkers from beginners.

## Getting Started

**First project: a simple shelf**
1. Two pieces of 1×10 pine (from any home center), a few feet each
2. Lay out a dadoed joint or use pocket holes for the corner joint
3. Cut to length with a hand saw and finish with a block plane
4. Sand to 220, apply two coats of wipe-on poly
5. Hang with French cleats (a simple cleat system that holds tremendous weight)

**Tool acquisition order**: Combination square → marking knife → Japanese hand saw → set of bench chisels → honing guide + stones → block plane → clamps (buy more than you think you need) → drill/driver.

## Common Mistakes

**Skipping sharpening**: No skill makes up for a dull edge. Sharp is the prerequisite, not a nice-to-have.

**Measuring with a tape when a rule or story stick would be more accurate**: For precise joinery, transfer measurements directly from the part being fitted — the "measure" is the actual part. Tape measures introduce cumulative error.

**Applying too much glue**: Squeeze-out (glue appearing at the joint line under clamping pressure) means adequate coverage. Excess squeeze-out wastes glue and must be cleaned up before it cures — dried glue under a finish creates a white, sealed spot that resists staining.

**Rushing the finish**: Applying a second coat before the first has fully cured traps solvents and causes a cloudy, soft finish. Follow the manufacturer's stated cure times, not estimated drying by touch.
