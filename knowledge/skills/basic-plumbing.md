# Basic Plumbing
*Shutoffs, fixing leaks, replacing fixtures, and what to never DIY*

---

## What Is It

Basic plumbing knowledge covers how residential water systems work, how to find and use shutoffs, how to fix common leaks, replace faucets and toilets, and clear drains — and where the hard stop is before you need a licensed plumber.

Plumbing has a clear DIY/professional divide. Supply line work (water coming in under pressure) is unforgiving — a mistake can cause serious water damage before you can respond. Drain work is more forgiving — failures are messy but not destructive. Knowing this divide determines your risk tolerance.

---

## Why It Matters

A leaking faucet, a running toilet, a clogged drain — these are recurring small failures in any home. Calling a plumber for each one at $150–250/hr adds up fast. More importantly, knowing where the shutoffs are and how to use them can mean the difference between a contained incident and thousands of dollars of water damage.

## Core Concepts

### How Residential Plumbing Works

Two systems: **supply** (pressurized water in) and **drain-waste-vent** (gravity drain out).

**Supply system**: Cold water enters from the street main (municipal) or well through the main shutoff. It splits — cold water goes directly to fixtures; hot water routes through the water heater first. Supply lines are always under pressure (~40–80 PSI). This is the unforgiving part — any open fitting sprays continuously until shutoff.

**Drain-waste-vent (DWV)**: Waste exits fixtures by gravity through large-diameter pipes sloped toward the main sewer line or septic tank. Vent pipes extend up through the roof — they equalize pressure and prevent siphoning of the water in P-traps. No pressure in this system; failures are slow leaks or blockages, not sprays.

**P-trap**: The curved pipe under every sink and fixture. It holds a small amount of water that blocks sewer gases from entering the home. When a drain smells like sewer, the P-trap has dried out (common in rarely-used fixtures) — run water to refill it.

### Know Every Shutoff in Your Home

**Main shutoff**: Usually where the water line enters the house — in the basement, crawl space, or utility room. Ball valve (quarter-turn lever) or gate valve (round handle, turn many times). Ball valves are more reliable. Know where this is. In an emergency, this is the first thing you go for.

**Meter shutoff**: Located at the water meter, usually near the street in a ground box. Requires a meter key (pentagon socket wrench, ~$15) or a crescent wrench. Used if the main house shutoff fails or isn't accessible.

**Fixture shutoffs**: Under sinks and behind toilets — small angle-stop valves. Turn clockwise to close. These let you isolate one fixture without shutting off the whole house. Test them periodically — they seize if never used, and a seized shutoff in an emergency is a serious problem. If they're stiff, a gentle turn (not forcing) with a wrench usually breaks them loose. If they're corroded shut, replace them while there's no emergency.

**Water heater shutoff**: A valve on the cold water supply line going into the heater. Used when servicing the water heater or if it leaks.

### What You Can DIY

**Replace a faucet**: Entirely within DIY range. Close the fixture shutoff valves, disconnect the supply lines, disconnect the drain linkage, remove the old faucet, install the new one in reverse. The hardest part is working in the confined space under the sink. A basin wrench ($20) makes this much easier.

**Fix a running toilet**: Almost always caused by one of three things:
- *Flapper*: The rubber seal at the bottom of the tank. If the toilet runs continuously, the flapper is worn. $5 part, 10-minute fix — turn off the supply valve, flush to empty tank, unhook old flapper, snap on new one.
- *Fill valve*: Controls water refilling the tank. If water is running into the overflow tube, the fill valve isn't shutting off. Replace the fill valve (~$15, 30 minutes).
- *Float*: Tells the fill valve when the tank is full. Sometimes needs adjustment (bend the arm or adjust the clip on ballcock-style floats).

**Fix a leaking faucet**: Dripping faucets are almost always a worn washer or O-ring (compression faucets) or cartridge (cartridge faucets). Close fixture shutoffs, disassemble the handle, replace the worn part. The challenge is identifying the faucet manufacturer and finding the right cartridge — keep the old part and take it to the hardware store.

**Replace a toilet**: Moderately difficult but doable. Shut off supply, flush and sponge out the tank, disconnect supply line, remove mounting bolts, lift the toilet (heavy — it's porcelain). Replace the wax ring. Set new toilet, compress the wax ring, bolt down, reconnect supply. Test for leaks.

**Unclog a drain**: Plunger first (cup plunger for sinks/tubs, flange plunger for toilets). If that fails, try a hand snake (drain auger) — feed it into the drain until you hit the clog, rotate to break it up. Chemical drain cleaners (Drano) work for grease/soap clogs in sink traps but can damage pipes with repeated use and don't work on solid obstructions.

**Replace supply lines**: The flexible braided hoses connecting shutoff valves to faucets and toilets. These should be replaced every 10 years — they fail silently and can cause significant water damage. Replacing them is simple: close shutoff, unscrew old line, thread on new one hand-tight plus a quarter turn with a wrench. Don't overtighten.

### What to Never DIY

**Any work on the main line or meter**: Requires shutting off street service and potentially involves the utility.

**Moving or adding supply lines inside walls**: Requires opening walls, potentially soldering copper or working with PEX — get a permit, get a plumber.

**Water heater installation or replacement**: 240V electrical, gas lines (if applicable), pressure relief valves, proper venting — all require licensed contractors in NJ.

**Gas line work**: Any gas lines (water heater, stove, dryer) are never DIY in NJ. Full stop. Licensed plumbers only.

**Sewer main work**: If you have a main line backup, a plumber with a camera and a snake handles this. Attempting to clear a main blockage without the right equipment causes more damage.

**Anything behind walls**: Opening walls creates permit requirements. A leak behind a wall needs a plumber because finding and accessing it properly is not amateur work.

### Materials — Know What You Have

**Copper**: Older standard. Soldered joints. Durable, but soldering requires skill and a torch.

**CPVC** (plastic, cream/tan color): Used for hot and cold supply. Glued joints. More DIY-accessible than copper.

**PEX** (flexible plastic tubing, red/blue): The modern standard for new work. Crimped or push-fit connections. Extremely DIY-accessible, very durable. If you're replacing supply lines inside a wall (with a permit), PEX is the right choice.

**ABS/PVC** (black/white rigid plastic): Drain pipes. Glued fittings. ABS is older, PVC is current standard. These are the drain lines you see under sinks and in basements.

**Galvanized steel**: Found in homes built before ~1970. Corrodes from the inside, restricting flow and eventually failing. If you have galvanized supply lines, plan for eventual replacement. Do not connect copper directly to galvanized (galvanic corrosion) — use a dielectric union.

## Key Considerations

**Water damage is expensive and fast**: A supply line failure at 60 PSI can dump hundreds of gallons before you respond. This is why knowing your shutoffs cold is more important than any repair skill.

**The smell of sewer gas is not trivial**: Hydrogen sulfide (the rotten egg smell) is toxic in concentration. If you smell sewer gas consistently, there's a dry P-trap or a venting problem — find it. A cracked drain line inside a wall is a licensed plumber job.

**NJ permit requirements**: Like electrical, any modification beyond maintenance (adding new supply lines, moving fixtures, replacing a water heater) requires a permit. Routine replacement (faucets, toilets, supply lines in kind) is maintenance and doesn't require a permit.

**Hard water in NJ**: Much of South Jersey has moderately hard water. Hard water deposits (calcium carbonate) clog aerators, showerheads, and eventually fixture valves. Clean aerators annually by unscrewing them and soaking in white vinegar overnight.

## Getting Started

1. **Find every shutoff in your home today**: Main, water heater, and every fixture shutoff. Test each one — they should turn smoothly. Note any that are seized.
2. **Replace the supply lines** under your kitchen and bathroom sinks if they're original or over 10 years old. This is the highest-return preventive maintenance in plumbing.
3. **Fix a running toilet**: Most homes have one eventually. Buy a flapper kit ($5) for your toilet model before you need it.

## Common Mistakes

**Not turning off the fixture shutoff** before loosening supply lines. Supply lines under pressure spray immediately when loosened.

**Over-tightening fittings**: Hand-tight plus a quarter turn with a wrench is enough for most threaded fittings. Over-tightening cracks the fitting — especially on plastic supply lines.

**Ignoring slow leaks**: A slow drip under a sink is actively damaging the cabinet floor and potentially the subfloor. Fix it immediately.

**Pouring grease down the drain**: It congeals in the drain trap and creates stubborn blockages. Dispose of cooking grease in a container in the trash.
