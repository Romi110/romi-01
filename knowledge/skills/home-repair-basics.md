# Home Repair Basics
*The 20 things every homeowner should be able to do — what to DIY, what to hire out, and how to not make it worse*

---

## What Is It

Home repair literacy is the ability to diagnose and fix the most common failures in a home's systems — plumbing, electrical, HVAC, structure, and exterior — without calling a professional for everything. It's a self-reliance skill with a direct financial return: a plumber for a simple fix costs $150–300; doing it yourself costs $10 in materials and 45 minutes.

This is an overview file. It covers the full territory at orientation depth and proposes dedicated sub-topic files for the areas where depth matters. The goal here is: know what the systems are, know what you can reasonably DIY, and know the line you shouldn't cross without training.

## Why It Matters

A home is likely the largest asset Romi will ever own. Every deferred repair compounds — a small leak becomes rot becomes structural damage. And the contractors you call for small things are the same ones you rely on for big things; being able to assess and fix small problems yourself gives you better judgment about when the big ones actually need professional help.

Self-reliance is not about doing everything yourself. It's about understanding systems well enough to make informed decisions, do the straightforward things yourself, and know when to hire — and who to trust.

## Core Concepts

### The 20 Things Every Homeowner Should Know

Organized by system:

#### Plumbing (5 essentials)
1. **Know your shutoffs.** Main water shutoff location, individual fixture shutoffs (under sinks, behind toilets). When a pipe bursts or a toilet overflows, you have ~60 seconds before this matters. Know it before you need it.
2. **Fix a running toilet.** Almost always the flapper (a $5 rubber seal) or the fill valve. A running toilet wastes 200+ gallons/day. Diagnose with food coloring in the tank; if it appears in the bowl without flushing, the flapper is leaking.
3. **Clear a clogged drain.** Plunger first (always). Then a drain snake (manual or electric). Avoid chemical drain cleaners — they damage pipes over time and rarely fully clear blockages.
4. **Replace a faucet or showerhead.** Turn off the supply, disconnect the supply lines, swap the fixture. Basic mechanical assembly, no special skill required.
5. **Fix a minor leak at fittings.** Teflon tape on threaded connections, pipe joint compound for harder seals. Know what kind of pipe you have (copper, PVC, PEX) — each repairs differently.

**Don't DIY**: main line blockages (requires a powered snake or hydro-jetting), pipe replacement behind walls, water heater replacement (gas line involved), septic system work.

#### Electrical (4 essentials)
6. **Understand your panel.** Breaker locations, labeling, how to reset a tripped breaker vs. replace a blown fuse (if older panel). Every breaker should be labeled — if it isn't, label it.
7. **Replace an outlet or switch.** Turn off the breaker, verify it's off with a voltage tester (always — never trust the breaker alone). Swap like-for-like. This is well within DIY territory.
8. **Install a GFCI outlet.** Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter outlets are required near water. Replacing a standard outlet with a GFCI, or troubleshooting a tripped GFCI, is basic.
9. **Replace a light fixture or ceiling fan.** Turn off the breaker, cap the wires, swap the fixture. Ceiling fans require knowing whether the existing box is fan-rated (it must be — standard junction boxes aren't rated for the weight and vibration).

**Don't DIY**: adding new circuits (requires permits, licensed electrician in most municipalities), panel work or anything involving the main service entrance, knob-and-tube or aluminum wiring (requires specialist), anything you're not confident about — electricity is the one system where mistakes are immediately dangerous.

#### HVAC (3 essentials)
10. **Change air filters.** Every 1–3 months depending on filter type and household. A clogged filter reduces efficiency, strains the system, and degrades air quality. This is pure maintenance, not repair.
11. **Clean condenser coils (AC).** The outdoor unit accumulates debris. Annual cleaning with a coil cleaner and gentle hose spray improves efficiency and extends compressor life.
12. **Bleed radiators (if you have steam or hot water heat).** Air gets trapped in radiators over time, reducing heat distribution. Bleeding is simple: a radiator key, a towel, 60 seconds per radiator. Do it at the start of heating season.

**Don't DIY**: refrigerant handling (requires EPA certification), furnace heat exchanger inspection (cracked heat exchangers leak carbon monoxide — get it professionally checked annually), ductwork modifications.

#### Structure and Interior (4 essentials)
13. **Patch drywall.** Small holes (nail, anchor, doorknob): mesh patch and joint compound. Larger holes (fist-sized): cut a clean square, install backing, tape and mud. Painting over requires primer or the repair shows through.
14. **Caulk around tubs, showers, and windows.** Old or cracked caulk is how water infiltrates behind walls. Remove old caulk completely (a caulk remover tool), clean the surface, apply new caulk, tool it smooth with a wet finger.
15. **Fix a sticking door.** Usually caused by seasonal wood expansion or a shifting house. Identify where it sticks (look for paint rub marks), sand or plane that edge. If the door is dropping, tighten the hinge screws or install longer screws into the stud.
16. **Re-grout tile.** Grout deteriorates, cracks, and harbors mold. Remove old grout with an oscillating tool or grout saw, clean the joints, apply new grout, wipe excess, seal when cured.

#### Exterior (4 essentials)
17. **Inspect and clean gutters.** Clogged gutters send water against the fascia, behind siding, and toward the foundation. Clean twice yearly (spring and fall). While up there, check for sagging gutters and missing gutter spikes.
18. **Seal gaps and cracks in the building envelope.** Gaps around pipes, wires, and vents entering the house are how air and pests get in. Expanding foam for larger gaps, caulk for smaller ones, steel wool stuffed into larger openings before foam (rodents chew through foam alone).
19. **Basic exterior paint maintenance.** Catching peeling or cracking paint early prevents wood rot. Scrape loose paint, sand, prime bare wood, paint. Ignoring it means replacing siding instead of repainting.
20. **Know your foundation.** Walk the perimeter periodically. Horizontal cracks in block foundations are structural (call a professional). Vertical and diagonal cracks in poured concrete are usually settling and typically manageable. Know the difference.

### Tools to Own

A complete home repair toolkit is ~$300–500 and lasts decades. Minimum viable set:

**Hand tools**: hammer, flathead and Phillips screwdrivers (multiple sizes), adjustable wrench, channel-lock pliers, needle-nose pliers, utility knife, tape measure, level, stud finder, pry bar.

**Power tools**: cordless drill/driver (most-used tool in the house), jigsaw (cuts curves and holes), circular saw (cuts lumber), oscillating multi-tool (the most versatile tool — cuts, sands, scrapes, grinds).

**Plumbing**: plunger, drain snake (manual 25' minimum), pipe wrench, Teflon tape.

**Electrical**: non-contact voltage tester (safety critical — never work without one), wire stripper, needle-nose pliers.

**Finishing**: putty knife, 4" and 10" drywall knives, paint brushes (2" and 3" angled sash), rollers and frames, caulk gun.

**Safety**: safety glasses, work gloves, dust mask/respirator (N95 minimum for drywall work), hearing protection for power tools.

### The Line: When to Call a Professional

DIY line is shaped by three factors: safety risk, permit requirement, and skill ceiling.

**Always hire**:
- Structural work (load-bearing walls, foundation repairs, roof replacement)
- Gas line work (any modification — leaks kill)
- Main electrical panel work or new circuit installation
- Asbestos or lead paint abatement (NJ homes pre-1978 may have both; disturbing either requires certified abatement)
- Septic system pumping and inspection
- Any work requiring a permit where you can't legally pull the permit as a homeowner

**Hire if you're not confident**: electrical work beyond simple outlet/switch replacement, anything involving cutting into walls without knowing what's inside, plumbing that requires soldering or significant pipe work.

**DIY freely**: everything in the 20 above, painting, caulking, weatherstripping, minor tile work, cabinet hardware, fixture replacement, furniture assembly, basic landscaping.

### How to Learn a Specific Repair

1. Diagnose before buying anything. Understand what failed before you replace it.
2. YouTube the specific repair. Search "[brand] [model] [problem]" for appliance repairs. Search "[repair type] DIY" for home repairs. This Old House, Fix It Home Improvement, and Matt Risinger channels are consistently accurate.
3. Buy materials for the repair, not a general supply run.
4. Do it once slowly, correctly. Don't rush.
5. Keep the old part until you've verified the repair holds.

## Key Considerations

**Permits exist for a reason.** Unpermitted work can void homeowner's insurance, create liability when you sell, and is occasionally genuinely dangerous. In NJ, most significant structural, electrical, and plumbing work requires a permit. When in doubt, call your municipality's building department — the call is free.

**Water is the enemy.** The majority of serious home damage comes from water intrusion — either from outside (gutters, flashing, grading) or inside (supply leaks, drain failures). Prioritize keeping water out and moving water away from the foundation.

**Don't ignore small things.** A $5 repair today is a $500 repair in two years. Most major home failures are preceded by minor symptoms — discoloration, soft spots, dripping, sticking, smells. Notice them early.

**NJ-specific**: NJ has strict contractor licensing requirements. For hired work, verify the contractor holds a NJ Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) registration. Unregistered contractors can't be held to NJ's consumer protection laws.

## Getting Started

1. **Learn your house's systems.** Walk through and find: main water shutoff, individual shutoffs, electrical panel (label every breaker), gas shutoff, HVAC filter location, attic access, crawl space or basement condition.
2. **Assemble a basic toolkit.** Start with: cordless drill, set of screwdrivers, adjustable wrench, pliers, tape measure, level, and a non-contact voltage tester.
3. **Fix one small thing this week.** A running toilet, a leaky faucet, a clogged drain. Small wins build competence and confidence faster than reading about repairs.

## Common Mistakes

**Not turning off the power.** Verify it's off with a voltage tester before touching any wire. This is not optional.

**Not turning off the water.** A simple repair becomes a flood because someone forgot the shutoff. Turn it off, verify it's off, then work.

**Using the wrong materials.** PVC cement on CPVC pipe, the wrong caulk type for the application (interior vs. exterior, paintable vs. non-paintable), the wrong drywall screw gauge. Materials matter.

**Rushing through finishing work.** Drywall mud and paint are 90% prep and patience. Trying to speed through drying times produces visible, permanent imperfections.

**Assuming one YouTube video makes you qualified.** Watch two or three. Read the comments for failure modes. Understand the repair fully before starting.
