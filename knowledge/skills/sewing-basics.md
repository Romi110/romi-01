# Sewing Basics
*Hand stitching, seam repair, button reattachment, and hemming — closing the loop on repair-over-replace*

---

## What Is It

Basic sewing is the ability to maintain and repair clothing and fabric items by hand — without a sewing machine. It covers the stitches and techniques used to fix seams that have come apart, reattach buttons, patch small holes, reinforce stress points, and adjust hems. These skills take an afternoon to learn and are genuinely useful for the rest of your life.

Machine sewing (garment construction from scratch) is a separate, deeper skill. This file covers the hand-sewing layer that closes the gap between "I'll throw it away" and "I can fix this."

---

## Why It Matters

Clothing fails in predictable ways: buttons fall off, seams split at stress points, small holes appear in fabric, hems come undone. Each failure is either a $0 repair or a replacement purchase. The ability to do the repair keeps quality items in service longer and reduces the reflexive-replace habit that costs money and generates waste.

For someone building self-reliance skills, the ability to repair rather than replace is a direct expression of that value — and sewing is one of the clearest examples of a skill that saves real money over time with minimal investment to acquire.

## Core Concepts

### Tools You Need

Minimal kit — all of it fits in a small tin:

- **Needles**: A variety pack of hand-sewing needles. Sizes 1–5 for general fabric; smaller numbers are coarser needles. Sharps (general purpose), betweens (quilting, fine fabric), darning needles (large eye for yarn/thick thread), tapestry needles (blunt tip for needlepoint/weaving) — a variety pack covers all these.
- **Thread**: Polyester all-purpose thread (Coats & Clark, Gutermann) is stronger and more durable than cotton for repairs. Stock basic colors — black, white, grey, navy, khaki, brown. For buttons and stress points: upholstery thread (heavier) or Gutermann heavy-duty thread.
- **Scissors or thread snips**: Dedicated sewing scissors. Don't use them for anything else — cutting paper dulls the edge.
- **Seam ripper**: Small hook-blade tool for removing stitches. Invaluable for taking out old stitching before re-sewing.
- **Thimble**: Protects the middle finger when pushing a needle through heavy fabric. Leather or metal. Optional on light fabric, necessary on denim or canvas.
- **Pins**: Straight pins to hold fabric in place while sewing.
- **Beeswax or thread conditioner**: Running thread through beeswax reduces tangling and strengthens the thread. Worth keeping in the kit.

### Threading the Needle and the Knot

**Threading**: Cut a 18–24 inch length of thread (longer tangles; shorter runs out mid-seam). Pull the thread through the needle eye. Pull one end longer — you'll work with the doubled thread, or with a single long end depending on the technique.

**The knot**: The basic starting knot is a simple overhand knot at the end of the thread. A cleaner approach for sturdy knots:
1. Loop the thread around your index finger
2. Roll the loop off the finger with your thumb while pulling the thread tail
3. This creates a knot at the end

**To prevent tangling**: Pull thread through beeswax, or condition it between your fingers. Work with thread no longer than 18–24 inches from needle to knot.

### The Essential Stitches

**Running stitch**: The simplest stitch. Weave the needle in and out of the fabric at regular intervals — in, out, in, out — creating a dashed line. Good for gathering fabric and basting (temporary stitching). Not the strongest for permanent repairs.

**Backstitch**: The strongest hand stitch — approximates the look and strength of a machine stitch. Go forward one stitch length, then bring the needle back to the end of the previous stitch, then forward again past the first stitch by one length. The stitches appear continuous on the front; they overlap on the back. Use this for seam repairs and any place you need real strength.

**Whipstitch (overcast stitch)**: Loop thread around the edge of the fabric with each stitch. Used for joining two fabric edges together, finishing raw edges to prevent fraying, and closing gaps. Faster than backstitch but less strong.

**Slip stitch (blind stitch)**: Used to close openings invisibly — the stitching is almost undetectable on the outside. Pick up a tiny thread from the folded edge of one side, then pick up a tiny thread from the opposite side, alternating. Pull snug but not tight. Used for closing hems invisibly and closing pillow covers.

**Blanket stitch**: An edging stitch that creates a decorative border while preventing fraying. Each stitch loops over the fabric edge. Common for finishing fleece and felt edges.

### Repairs — The Common Scenarios

**Button reattachment**:
1. Thread a needle with doubled thread, knot the end
2. If there's still thread from the old button, remove it cleanly with a seam ripper
3. Mark the button position
4. Bring the needle up through the fabric from the back, through one buttonhole, then back down through the adjacent buttonhole, into the fabric
5. Repeat 6–8 times through each pair of holes (for a 4-hole button, sew in an X pattern or parallel rows)
6. On the last pass, come up through the fabric but not through the button. Wrap the thread 5–6 times around the thread shank (the column of thread between button and fabric). This creates a thread shank that gives the button room to pass through the buttonhole.
7. Secure with a knot on the back of the fabric

**Button types**: Flat buttons have 2 or 4 holes; sew-through pattern as above. Shank buttons have a loop on the back — pass the needle through the loop instead of holes. Decorative/suit buttons on outerwear may need a small flat backing button sewn to the underside of the fabric for reinforcement (keeps the button from tearing through).

**Seam repair**:
1. Identify where the seam has opened and how far
2. Trim any loose threads
3. Pin the seam closed, aligning the original stitch line
4. Use backstitch along the original seam line, extending slightly beyond the opening at each end to reinforce the existing stitching
5. Knot off securely on the back side

**Hemming**:
1. Determine the desired hem length. Fold the fabric up to that length and press with an iron to crease.
2. Fold the raw edge up again 1/4–1/2 inch and press — this encases the raw edge so it doesn't fray.
3. Pin the hem in place
4. Use slip stitch (invisible from outside) or whipstitch (faster, slightly visible) to secure
5. The stitches should catch just a thread or two of the outer fabric — not enough to show on the right side

**Small hole or tear repair**:
- *Darning*: For woven fabric holes. Stitch horizontal running stitches across the hole, then vertical running stitches over and under the horizontals, creating a woven patch. Used for socks, sweaters, and woven fabric holes.
- *Patch*: Cut a piece of matching or contrasting fabric slightly larger than the hole. Pin or baste in place on the back. Whipstitch or backstitch around the perimeter. For a decorative patch on the outside, turn edges under and slip stitch.
- *Iron-on patch*: For quick repairs — cut to size, position, press with a hot iron for 30–60 seconds. Less durable than sewn patches but acceptable for interior patches and casual clothing.

**Reinforcing seams and stress points**: Add extra stitching at the base of a pocket (where weight causes stress), the end of a zipper seam, and the crotch seam of pants — these are common failure points. A bar tack (dense parallel stitches perpendicular to the seam) reinforces stress points against tearing.

### Fabric Types and Thread Choice

Different fabrics require different approaches:

**Woven fabrics (cotton, linen, denim, canvas)**: Easy to hand sew. Thread through layers cleanly. Standard sharp needle, general-purpose thread.

**Knit fabrics (jersey, stretch materials)**: Require a stretch stitch or will pop. Use a backstitch with some give, or use a hand stretch stitch (loop stitch). Use ball-point needles (rounded tip doesn't pierce the knit loops).

**Wool and heavy fabrics**: Upholstery thread or doubled regular thread. A thimble is necessary. The needle goes through slowly — patience.

**Delicate fabrics (silk, chiffon)**: Use a very fine needle (size 10–12) and fine thread. Test stitches on a scrap first — silk shows needle holes.

**Thread color matching**: Take the garment to the store or pull a thread from an inside seam to match. An exact match is often impossible — choose slightly darker rather than lighter. The stitches will be less visible.

## Key Considerations

**Tension matters**: Stitches should lie flat without puckering the fabric. If the fabric puckers, the thread is too tight. Consistent, even tension through each stitch comes with practice.

**Iron before and after**: Pressing seams with an iron before sewing makes them easier to work with. Pressing after sewing makes stitches lay flat and look professional.

**Seam allowance**: The distance between the stitch line and the raw edge. Standard is 5/8 inch for garment seams. When repairing, match the original seam line — don't crowd the edge.

**Knot securely at both ends**: Thread that slips out undoes all your work. Use two or three knots at the start and end, and take a small stitch through the knot before cutting.

## Getting Started

1. **Assemble the kit**: A basic hand-sewing kit (tin with needles, thread assortment, seam ripper, small scissors, thimble) costs $20–30. Most craft stores carry complete kits. Keep it where you'll use it — near your closet or in a drawer.
2. **Reattach a button**: Find something in your wardrobe with a missing or loose button. Reattach it. This one repair covers the core technique and takes 10 minutes.
3. **Repair a seam**: Find something with a split seam (very common in jeans at the crotch or back pockets). Use backstitch to close it.
4. **Practice the stitches on scrap fabric**: Run through the five basic stitches on a piece of old fabric before working on clothing you care about.

## Common Mistakes

**Thread too long**: Over 24 inches tangles constantly. Use shorter lengths and re-thread.

**Knots that slip through the fabric**: If the fabric weave is loose, the knot passes through rather than anchoring. Make a larger knot, or take a small backstitch at the start before proceeding.

**Stitches too loose**: Stitches that aren't snug enough allow the repair to shift and re-open. Pull each stitch snug (not tight) as you go.

**Not pressing/ironing first**: A wrinkled seam is hard to sew straight. A quick iron press before sewing makes a significant difference.

**Mismatched thread color**: Take the garment to the store. Don't guess — slightly wrong color reads immediately on light or flat-weave fabrics.
