# First Aid Basics
*Bleeding, burns, choking, CPR, and the first response to medical emergencies*

---

## What Is It

First aid is the immediate care given to an injured or suddenly ill person before professional medical help arrives. It does not replace medical care — it bridges the gap between the incident and EMS arriving, and for certain emergencies (choking, cardiac arrest), it is the only thing that works fast enough to matter.

This file covers the scenarios most likely to occur and most likely to benefit from a capable bystander: severe bleeding, burns, choking, unconsciousness, and cardiac arrest. It's structured to be remembered under stress, not just read once.

---

## Why It Matters

The window for effective first aid is narrow. Cardiac arrest: survival drops 10% per minute without CPR. Arterial bleeding: unconsciousness can occur in minutes. Choking: 4–6 minutes to brain damage from hypoxia. Being capable in these windows is a concrete skill that costs almost nothing to acquire and can preserve a life.

Self-reliance includes being capable in a medical emergency — not as a substitute for the medical system, but as the first node in the response chain.

## Core Concepts

### The First Steps — Any Emergency

Before touching anyone:

1. **Ensure scene safety**: Don't create a second victim. A car accident scene, electrical emergency, or chemical exposure can injure rescuers. If the scene isn't safe, call 911 and stay clear until it is.
2. **Call 911 (or direct someone specific to call)**: "You, in the red shirt — call 911 now." Vague calls to a crowd often result in everyone assuming someone else called. Be specific.
3. **Check for responsiveness**: Tap shoulders firmly, call loudly: "Are you okay?" If no response, proceed.

### Severe Bleeding — Control First

Uncontrolled severe bleeding is the most common preventable cause of traumatic death. The goal is direct pressure that doesn't stop until professional help arrives.

**Arterial vs. venous bleeding**:
- Arterial: Bright red, spurting in pulses. Extremely fast blood loss. Immediately life-threatening.
- Venous: Dark red, steady flow. Still dangerous but slower.

**Control:**
1. **Apply direct pressure immediately** — press firmly with whatever is at hand (cloth, shirt, your hand). Do not remove the pressure to check. If blood soaks through, add more material on top.
2. **Maintain pressure for minimum 10 minutes continuously** — most bleeding stops with sustained pressure. Clock it.
3. **Tourniquets for limb bleeding that won't stop**: Apply 2–3 inches above the wound (not over a joint). Tighten until bleeding stops and note the time. A tourniquet causes significant tissue damage over time — it's a last resort for life-threatening limb bleeding, not a first-line choice for minor wounds. A proper CAT tourniquet (Combat Application Tourniquet, ~$30) should be in your first aid kit. A belt or shoelace improvised tourniquet is far less effective.
4. **Wound packing for deep wounds** (groin, axilla, neck — areas where a tourniquet can't be applied): Pack gauze (hemostatic gauze like QuikClot if available) directly into the wound cavity with firm pressure. This is uncomfortable but necessary for deep arterial wounds.
5. **Do not remove a deeply embedded object** — it may be plugging the wound. Stabilize it and let EMS handle removal.

**Signs of significant blood loss**: Pale, cold, clammy skin; rapid weak pulse; confusion; thirst; unconsciousness. These are signs of hemorrhagic shock — the body is shunting blood to vital organs. Lay the person flat (unless head/spine injury suspected), keep them warm, and continue pressure.

### Burns

**Classification**:
- *First degree*: Redness, no blisters, superficial (sunburn). Home treatment.
- *Second degree*: Redness, blisters, painful. Partial thickness. Home treatment for small burns; medical care for large (>3 inches), or on face/hands/genitals/over joints.
- *Third degree*: White, brown, or black skin; dry, leathery; no pain (nerves destroyed). Always requires emergency care.

**First aid for burns**:
1. Remove from the heat source. Remove clothing around the burn (don't remove if stuck).
2. Cool with cool (not cold, not ice) running water for at least 10–20 minutes. Ice causes additional tissue damage.
3. Do not apply butter, toothpaste, or any home remedies — they trap heat and cause infection.
4. Cover loosely with a non-stick sterile dressing or clean cloth after cooling.
5. Seek medical care for anything beyond a minor first-degree burn.

**Chemical burns**: Brush off dry chemicals before water application. Flush with large volumes of water for 20+ minutes. Call Poison Control (1-800-222-1222) for specific chemical guidance.

**Electrical burns**: Often have entrance and exit wounds with internal damage not visible externally. Always require emergency evaluation.

### Choking

**Mild choking** (can cough, breathe, or speak): Encourage coughing. Do not interfere — coughing is more effective than any intervention.

**Severe choking** (cannot speak, breathe, or cough effectively; may be clutching throat):

**Conscious adult/child over 1 year:**
1. **5 back blows**: Stand to the side and slightly behind. Support the chest with one hand. With the heel of your hand, deliver 5 firm blows between the shoulder blades.
2. **5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver)**: Stand behind the person. Make a fist with one hand, thumb side against the abdomen between the navel and the base of the sternum. Grab your fist with the other hand. Give 5 firm, upward thrusts.
3. Alternate 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until the object is expelled or the person becomes unconscious.

**Unconscious choking**: Begin CPR. Chest compressions may dislodge the object. Before rescue breaths, look in the mouth — only remove a visible object. Do not perform blind finger sweeps.

**Infant (under 1 year) choking**:
- 5 back blows: Face down on your forearm, supporting the head. Deliver 5 back blows with the heel of your hand.
- 5 chest thrusts: Turn face up on your forearm. Two fingers on the center of the chest, just below the nipple line. Give 5 chest thrusts.
- Alternate until the object is expelled.

**Choking alone**: Perform abdominal thrusts on yourself, or drive your abdomen over a hard edge (chair back, countertop).

### CPR — Cardiac Arrest

Cardiac arrest: the heart stops pumping. Without CPR, brain death begins in 4–6 minutes. With CPR, you're maintaining minimal circulation until a defibrillator arrives. CPR alone rarely restarts a fibrillating heart — it buys time for the AED.

**Check for responsiveness and breathing**: Tap, call out. Look for breathing (normal breaths, not the occasional gasping "agonal breathing" which can occur in cardiac arrest). If unresponsive and not breathing normally: call 911, begin CPR.

**Adult CPR (Hands-Only, which is what bystanders should do):**
1. **Position**: Kneel beside the person. Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest (lower half of the sternum). Place your other hand on top. Fingers interlaced, fingers off the chest.
2. **Compressions**: Arms straight, shoulders directly over your hands. Compress at least 2 inches deep at 100–120 compressions per minute. Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions. Don't stop.
3. **Rate**: The Bee Gees' "Stayin' Alive" is approximately 104 BPM — an actual useful memory aid.
4. **If trained in CPR**: Give 30 compressions, then 2 rescue breaths (tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, seal mouth, give 1 breath over 1 second, watch for chest rise). Repeat. Untrained bystanders: hands-only CPR is nearly as effective for adult cardiac arrest.
5. **AED**: As soon as an AED (Automated External Defibrillator) is available, use it. Turn it on, attach the pads as shown in the diagram, follow the voice prompts. The AED will analyze the rhythm and instruct you to shock or continue CPR. AEDs are in most public buildings in NJ — know where the nearest one is.
6. **Continue until**: EMS arrives and takes over, the person shows obvious signs of life (breathing, movement), an AED advises no shock and person is revived, or you're physically unable to continue.

**Infant CPR**: Two fingers on the breastbone, 1.5 inch depth, 30 compressions followed by 2 gentle puffs (just enough to see chest rise).

### Recovery Position

For an unconscious but breathing person: roll onto their side (the "recovery position") to prevent airway obstruction from the tongue or vomit. Support the head. Do not do this if spinal injury is suspected.

### Shock

Shock is inadequate tissue perfusion — the body can't deliver enough oxygenated blood to organs. Causes include severe bleeding, allergic reaction (anaphylaxis), heart attack, severe burns.

**Signs**: Pale, cold, clammy skin; rapid/weak pulse; rapid breathing; confusion; nausea; thirst.

**First aid**: Lay flat, elevate legs 8–12 inches if no spinal injury suspected (increases central blood volume), keep warm with a blanket, do NOT give anything by mouth, reassure calmly, call 911.

**Anaphylactic shock**: Severe allergic reaction. Airway swelling, hives, drop in blood pressure. If the person has an epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) — help them administer it or administer it yourself (outer thigh, even through clothing). Call 911 immediately. Effects of epinephrine last 10–20 minutes; without further treatment, the reaction can return.

## Key Considerations

**Get trained**: Reading this is not a substitute for a hands-on CPR/AED/first-aid course. The American Red Cross and American Heart Association both offer 4–6 hour courses. Do one. Skills performed under stress need to be practiced, not recalled from text.

**First aid kit — what actually matters**:
- Nitrile gloves (multiple pairs) — your first protection
- Israeli bandage or pressure bandage — for wound packing and pressure
- CAT tourniquet (genuine — not a knockoff) — for limb arterial bleeding
- Hemostatic gauze (QuikClot or Celox) — for wound packing
- Sterile gauze pads
- Medical tape
- Non-stick burn dressing
- Trauma shears (cut clothing without risking the wound)
- Mylar emergency blanket

**Know your AED locations**: Schools, gyms, offices, airports, many NJ public buildings are required to have AEDs. Knowing where the nearest one is in the places you frequent is a one-time effort with a high potential payoff.

## Getting Started

1. **Take a CPR/AED course** — Red Cross or AHA, 4 hours, ~$60–80. Do this year.
2. **Assemble a real first aid kit** — not a drugstore kit (mostly bandages with little trauma capability). Get the items listed above.
3. **Practice the Heimlich on yourself** to understand the mechanics. Practice the chest compression position on a couch cushion.
4. **Know your AED** — if you work in a building, find where the AED is. If your gym has one, take note of its location.

## Common Mistakes

**Removing pressure to check the wound**: The biggest mistake in bleeding control. Keep pressure on. Clock 10 minutes without releasing.

**Using ice on a burn**: Causes vasoconstriction and additional tissue damage. Cool water only.

**Doing abdominal thrusts on a mildly choking person**: Mild choking (can cough or speak) should be encouraged to cough. Intervention can push the object deeper.

**Shallow CPR compressions**: 2 inches minimum in adults. Most untrained bystanders compress too shallowly. Lean into it — the sternum needs to depress fully.

**Not calling 911 first or directing someone specific to call**: Vague "someone call 911" fails. Point at a specific person and give a specific instruction.
