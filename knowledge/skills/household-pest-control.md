# Household Pest Control
*Keep pests out of the house through exclusion first, sanitation second, and treatment only where necessary*

---

## What Is It

Household pest control is the practice of preventing and eliminating unwanted insects and rodents from your living space. The dominant approach sold by the pest control industry — quarterly spray contracts — treats the symptom. The effective approach treats the cause: entry points, food sources, moisture, and harborage.

This file covers what actually works for the pests most likely to appear in an NJ home, with emphasis on Pinelands-area pressure (carpenter ants, stink bugs, spotted lanternfly, ticks). The goal is to handle common problems yourself and know when to call a professional.

## Why It Matters

Homeownership puts pest management on you. A typical pest control contract runs $800-1,500/year and mostly provides peace of mind — the same results are achievable with one afternoon of exclusion work and targeted treatment when needed. Understanding what you're dealing with and why it's happening is the difference between solving a problem and paying someone to spray repeatedly without fixing it.

## Core Concepts

### The Three Lines of Defense

**1. Exclusion — the only permanent fix**
Pests don't appear in sealed homes. Every ant trail, every mouse, every stink bug entered through a gap. Finding and sealing those gaps is more durable than any treatment.

Common entry points:
- Gaps around utility penetrations (pipes, wires, HVAC lines entering the wall) — fill with expanding foam or steel wool (mice chew through foam alone)
- Door sweeps and weatherstripping — gaps under exterior doors are primary ant and mouse highways
- Foundation cracks — inspect the base of the foundation where it meets the sill plate
- Weep holes in brick — necessary for drainage but can be screened with fine mesh
- Garage door seals — rubber bottom seals degrade; replace when gaps appear
- Roof soffit and fascia gaps — entry for squirrels, wasps, and some insects

**2. Sanitation — remove the incentive**
Pests come for food, water, or shelter. Eliminate those and the pressure drops.
- Store pantry items (grains, nuts, dried fruit) in airtight containers — cardboard boxes are not pest barriers
- Empty kitchen compost daily or keep it sealed
- Fix dripping faucets and under-sink leaks — moisture attracts silverfish, cockroaches, and odorous house ants
- Keep firewood stacked away from the house (20+ feet) — it's carpenter ant and mouse habitat
- Clear leaf litter and debris from the foundation perimeter — it creates harborage and moisture

**3. Treatment — targeted, not broadcast**
When exclusion and sanitation aren't enough, treatment is the third lever. The hierarchy: bait > dust > spray. Spraying is the least targeted option and often counterproductive — it kills surface-contact insects while leaving the colony intact and may scatter it.

### Ants

Ants are the most common household complaint. Treatment depends entirely on which ant you have.

**Odorous house ants** (small, dark brown, smell like rotten coconut when crushed): the most common indoor ant in NJ. They nest both inside (wall voids, under floors) and outside, and follow moisture. The mistake is spraying the trail — you kill scouts but not the colony, and the colony detects the disruption and splits into satellite colonies.

What works: slow-acting bait. Terro (borax-based) liquid bait stations placed directly on the trail. The workers carry the bait back to the colony, which collapses over 1-2 weeks. Don't spray anything near the bait — it will repel the ants from taking it. Seal the entry point once the trail dies out.

**Carpenter ants** (large, black, sometimes winged): don't eat wood — they excavate it to nest in it. Finding carpenter ants indoors, especially near windows or in bathrooms, signals moisture-damaged wood somewhere in the structure. They come from outside (often from a rotting stump or woodpile) or from an indoor nest in softened wood.

What works: find the moisture source. A carpenter ant problem that persists after treatment almost always has rotting wood or a leak behind it. Bait is less effective here than with odorous house ants. Perimeter spray (bifenthrin) around the foundation in spring disrupts their foraging. If you find frass (sawdust-like debris) inside walls, that's an active indoor colony — professional treatment is warranted.

**Pavement ants** (small, with parallel ridges on head/thorax, found near concrete): nest under slabs and pavement, enter through cracks. Same bait approach as odorous house ants. Less likely to be a persistent problem once sealed out.

### Mice and Rodents

A mouse can compress its body through a gap the diameter of a pencil (about 6mm). Sealing gaps is mandatory — traps without exclusion are a losing battle.

**Snap traps** (not glue boards, which are inhumane and ineffective at scale) set along walls perpendicular to the wall — mice run along baseboards, not in the open. Bait with peanut butter or chocolate. Check daily. A mouse dropping in a single area with no activity elsewhere usually means one entry point.

**Exclusion materials**: steel wool packed into gaps, then covered with expanding foam or caulk. Mice won't chew through steel wool. Hardware cloth (1/4 inch) over larger openings (weep holes, vents, foundation gaps).

In the Pinelands area, field mice (white-footed mouse) are common and are the primary carrier of Lyme disease-infected ticks. Keeping them out of the house reduces tick exposure indoors.

### Stink bugs (Brown Marmorated)

A NJ fixture from September through November: stink bugs looking to overwinter in warm spaces. They enter through window screens, door gaps, and soffit penetrations. Once inside they're dormant until spring — they don't breed indoors, don't damage anything, and don't bite.

Management: exclusion in late summer (August) before they start aggregating. Seal any gap in the envelope. If they're already inside, a vacuum with a bag (stink bugs release their chemical when crushed) is effective. Avoid squashing. A jar of soapy water under a light source overnight catches large numbers.

---

### Pantry Moths (Indian Meal Moths)

Small moths with coppery wing tips; larvae spin webbing in dry goods. Usually introduced in a purchased bag of grain, flour, nuts, or dried fruit — not a sign of a dirty kitchen.

Find the source: inspect everything in your pantry, especially older items at the back. Discard anything with webbing, larvae, or clumping. Transfer all dry goods to sealed glass or hard-sided plastic containers. Pheromone traps (sticky cards) catch adult males and break the mating cycle over 2-3 weeks. No spray needed.

---

### Silverfish

Flat, fast, silver insects found in humid areas (basements, under sinks, bathrooms). Feed on starch — book bindings, wallpaper paste, cardboard. Indicator of too much moisture.

Fix the moisture first. Dehumidifier in the basement, fix any drips. Diatomaceous earth (food-grade) applied in cracks, behind baseboards, and in cabinet voids kills silverfish through desiccation — safe around pets and food once settled.

---

### Spotted Lanternfly (NJ-specific)

An invasive planthopper, not a household interior pest, but a significant NJ outdoor concern. Feeds on tree of heaven (invasive itself) and will also attack fruit trees, hops, grapes, and hardwoods. Bright red hindwings visible in flight. Egg masses look like dried mud smears on tree bark.

Your role: scrape and destroy egg masses (November–June) on any hard surface; kill adults on sight; report infestations to NJ Department of Agriculture if in a newly affected county. Do not transport firewood from infested areas. A real concern for any fruit tree or hop growing operation.

---

### Ticks (Deer Tick / Black-Legged Tick)

Not a household interior pest but a perimeter pest with serious health implications in NJ. Deer ticks are the primary Lyme disease vector in Atlantic County.

Yard management:
- Keep grass mowed and leaf litter cleared — ticks don't survive in dry, sunny areas
- Create a 3-foot wood chip or gravel barrier between lawn and wooded areas; ticks rarely cross dry barriers
- Keep woodpiles away from the house; mice carry ticks into the yard
- Deer exclusion fencing reduces tick pressure significantly — deer are the primary adult tick host

Personal protection when in tick habitat: check within 2 hours of exposure (ticks usually take 24-36 hours to transmit Lyme after attachment). The nymph tick is the size of a poppy seed — check carefully.

## Key Considerations

**Pinelands aquifer sensitivity**: the NJ Pinelands sits over the Kirkwood-Cohansey aquifer — extremely permeable sandy soil means pesticides applied to the ground can reach groundwater quickly. Minimize outdoor pesticide use near the foundation; use targeted baits and physical barriers whenever possible.

**Spray last**: broadcast spraying the perimeter kills beneficial insects (ground beetles that eat pests) alongside the targets, requires repeated application to maintain a barrier, and doesn't address the source. Reserve it for active carpenter ant perimeters and targeted spot treatment.

**When to call a professional**: active carpenter ant colony inside walls with visible frass; termites (mud tubes along foundation, hollow-sounding wood, discarded wings near windows); large rodent infestation with multiple access points; bed bugs (entirely different protocol).

## Getting Started

1. **Do a perimeter walk.** Look at the base of the foundation, all utility penetrations, door and window frames, and roof overhangs. Photograph gaps — seal them with steel wool + foam or exterior caulk within a week.
2. **Inspect the pantry.** Transfer all dry goods to sealed containers. This eliminates pantry moths and reduces the food incentive for ants.
3. **Check moisture.** Under every sink, the basement, and around any window that sweats. One dripping pipe is an odorous house ant colony waiting to happen.
4. **Buy these before you need them**: Terro ant bait stations, snap traps (Victor brand), diatomaceous earth, expanding foam + steel wool. Having them on hand means you treat the day you see the problem, not after a hardware store run.

## Common Mistakes

**Spraying the ant trail** — kills scouts, triggers colony splitting, makes the problem worse. Bait the trail; don't spray it.

**Trapping mice without sealing entry** — you'll catch mice indefinitely. Seal first, then trap the ones already inside.

**Assuming one treatment solves it** — pest control is ongoing maintenance, not a one-time event. Exclusion holds until something opens a new gap (settling, aging caulk, construction). Check annually.

**Reacting to every spider** — most spiders in an NJ home are beneficial, eating actual pest insects. The only medically significant spiders in NJ are black widows (uncommon, found in outdoor structures like sheds and woodpiles) and brown recluses (rare). Leave house spiders alone.

**Calling a pest control company before identifying the pest** — a professional who doesn't know what they're treating will spray broadly and return quarterly. Know what you have before you call; it gives you the ability to evaluate whether the proposed treatment makes sense.
