# Emergency Preparedness
*72-hour kit, water storage, power outages, and NJ-specific scenarios*

---

## What Is It

Emergency preparedness is having the supplies, plans, and knowledge to sustain yourself and your household through disruptions that temporarily cut off normal services — power outages, water supply failures, evacuation orders, and severe weather events. The goal is self-sufficiency for 72 hours minimum, ideally 2 weeks, without depending on infrastructure that may be unavailable.

This is not prepper culture or catastrophizing — it's the same logic as having car insurance. You're not planning for the apocalypse; you're planning for a hurricane, a winter ice storm, a regional grid failure, or an evacuation.

---

## Why It Matters

FEMA explicitly recommends that Americans be self-sufficient for at least 72 hours after a major disaster — because that's roughly how long it takes for emergency services to be systematically deployed after a large event. In South Jersey/Atlantic County specifically: hurricanes, nor'easters, coastal flooding, and ice storms are not theoretical risks. They happen on a predictable cycle.

Preparing during normal conditions is nearly free. Preparing during an emergency (buying bottled water the day a storm is forecast) is expensive, stressful, and often impossible.

## Core Concepts

### Water — The Highest Priority

The human body can survive roughly 3 days without water, 3 weeks without food. Water comes first.

**Minimum storage**: 1 gallon per person per day, 72-hour minimum = 3 gallons per person. Practical target: 2 gallons per person per day (drinking, cooking, basic hygiene) × 14 days = 28 gallons per person in the household.

**Storage options**:
- *Commercial water jugs (WaterBrick, 3.5-gallon): Stackable, BPA-free, designed for long-term storage. The most practical for most households.
- *55-gallon water barrel*: Efficient long-term storage, but heavy when full (460 lbs) and requires a siphon pump to access. Good for the garage or basement.
- *WaterBOB*: A bathtub liner that holds 100 gallons from the tap when a storm is imminent. Fills in 20 minutes. One-time use but cheap (~$30) and stores flat.
- *Regular commercial bottled water*: Works. The 5-gallon jugs are more practical than individual bottles.

**Purification if stored water runs out**:
- *Boiling*: Bring to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 feet). Most reliable method for biological contaminants.
- *Chemical (unscented household bleach, 6–8.25% sodium hypochlorite)*: 8 drops per gallon of clear water, 16 drops if cloudy. Wait 30 minutes before drinking. Effective against bacteria and viruses; not against Giardia or Cryptosporidium.
- *Filtration (Sawyer Squeeze, LifeStraw, Katadyn Pocket)*: Removes protozoa, bacteria, and most particulates. The Sawyer Squeeze (~$35) filters 100,000 gallons and is the best value emergency water filter.
- *SteriPen (UV)*: Treats biological contaminants in clear water in 90 seconds. Requires batteries.

**Note on tap water in an emergency**: After a major event, municipal water may be pressurized but unsafe (boil advisory). Continue boiling until authorities confirm safety.

### Food — 72 Hours to 2 Weeks

**Priorities for emergency food**:
- Requires no refrigeration
- Minimal preparation (no water needed, or minimal)
- Familiar enough to actually be eaten under stress
- Adequate calories and nutrition

**Practical approach** — the "rotate and eat" method: Keep 2 weeks of food your household actually eats, stored in accessible quantity, and rotate it normally. This avoids the "bucket of freeze-dried survivalist food" that sits for 25 years unopened. Actual shelf-stable food you know how to prepare.

**Good categories**:
- Canned goods (beans, lentils, tomatoes, vegetables, chickpeas) — 2–5 year shelf life
- Dried grains (rice, oats, lentils, pasta) — years of shelf life in sealed containers
- Peanut butter, nuts, seeds — calorie-dense, no preparation
- Jerky, crackers, dried fruit — snack foods with decent shelf life
- Shelf-stable milk (UHT cartons)
- Multivitamins — fill nutritional gaps in a restricted diet

**Cooking without power**: Camp stove with stored propane (Coleman stove + 1-lb canisters, or a 20-lb tank with a camp stove adapter). Matches/lighter. Cast iron cookware works on any heat source. Never use a gas camp stove indoors — carbon monoxide risk. Outdoors or in a garage with ventilation only.

**Special needs**: Track any dietary restrictions, medications that require food, or infant formula in the household and plan specifically for them.

### Power Outages — NJ Context

NJ's grid is vulnerable. Atlantic County specifically experiences outages from hurricanes, nor'easters, and summer thunderstorm lines. The 2012 Superstorm Sandy left parts of NJ without power for weeks. Planning for 3–7 days without grid power is realistic.

**Lighting**:
- Headlamps (not flashlights — hands-free matters): One per person. Rechargeable (USB) or AA battery. Keep charged.
- Lanterns for ambient light: LED lanterns on battery or rechargeable.
- Candles: Functional but a fire hazard. Use in stable holders only.

**Communication**:
- Battery-powered or hand-crank NOAA weather radio: Receives emergency alerts without cell service. Essential. The Midland ER310 or Eton FRX3 are reliable options (~$40–60).
- Portable battery bank (20,000–30,000 mAh): Can charge phones several times. Keep it charged.
- Car charger: Your car's 12V outlet charges phones as long as the car has gas. Don't run the engine in the garage — carbon monoxide.

**Temperature**:
- Heat in winter: A wood stove or pellet stove provides heat without electricity. A propane heater (Mr. Heater Buddy, indoor-safe) with ventilation works for a room. Layer sleeping — sleeping bags rated below expected temperatures.
- Cooling in summer: NJ summer heat during a power outage is genuinely dangerous for elderly, infants, and people with certain medical conditions. Cooling centers open during heat emergencies — know where the nearest one is in Atlantic County.

**Generators**:
- *Portable gas generator*: Most common. Run outdoors only — carbon monoxide kills people every year who run generators in garages or near windows. Powers a refrigerator, a few lights, and phone charging. A 3,500–5,000 watt generator covers most household essentials. Requires stored gasoline (add fuel stabilizer — untreated gas degrades in 30 days; stabilized gas lasts 12 months).
- *Standby generator (whole-home)*: Natural gas or propane, automatically activates on power loss. Professional installation, expensive ($5,000–15,000), and requires permits in NJ. Worth it for households with medical equipment or frequent outages.
- *Portable power station (EcoFlow, Jackery)*: Battery-powered, silent, indoor-safe. Limited capacity (1,000–3,600 Wh). Can't run high-draw appliances (central AC, electric range, well pump) but handles lights, phone charging, CPAP, and small fans comfortably. The EcoFlow Delta 2 Max (~$1,200) is the most capable practical option.

**Refrigerator management during an outage**: A full refrigerator holds temperature for 4 hours; a full freezer for 48 hours if unopened. Keep a cooler and ice available. Prioritize insulin, medications requiring refrigeration. When in doubt about meat/dairy safety after more than 4 hours at room temperature: throw it out.

### NJ-Specific Scenarios

**Hurricanes and tropical storms**: Atlantic County is in the coastal flooding zone. Major hurricanes produce storm surge (water pushed onshore by wind) that can inundate low-lying areas. Know your evacuation zone (check the Atlantic County OEM website for zone maps). Hurricane season: June through November, peak August–October.

**Pre-storm actions**:
1. Fill the car with gas before the storm (pumps stop working in power outages, and gas sells out quickly)
2. Fill the WaterBOB or fill bathtubs with water as additional supply
3. Charge all devices and portable batteries
4. Have cash — ATMs and card readers don't work without power
5. Board or hurricane-tape windows if a major storm is forecast

**Nor'easters**: Can produce 12–24 inches of snow in South Jersey with sustained power outages. Ice storms are more dangerous than snow — ice on trees causes widespread utility line damage and very long outages. Plan for 3–5 days without power after a major ice storm.

**Evacuation**:
- Know your evacuation route (two routes — bridges and roads can close)
- Know where you'd go (family, friend, hotel outside the area)
- Pack a "go bag" in advance — documents, medications, phone charger, 3 days of supplies, change of clothes
- Know Atlantic County's evacuation shelters if needed

**Carbon monoxide**: CO poisoning from generators, grills, and heating equipment kills people during NJ weather events every year. Never run gas-powered equipment in enclosed spaces. Install CO detectors on every floor.

**Flooding**: If your area floods, do not drive through flooded roads. Six inches of moving water can knock a person down; one foot can sweep a car. If water enters your home, turn off electricity at the main breaker (if safe to reach) before water reaches outlets.

### Documents and Financial Preparedness

Physical copies of essential documents in a waterproof bag:
- Identification (passport, driver's license)
- Insurance policies (home, auto, health)
- Bank account information
- Medical records and prescriptions
- Property documents (deed, mortgage)
- Photos of valuables (for insurance claims)

Cash: $200–500 in small bills. When power is out, card payments don't work. Cash is the universal fallback.

### Communication Plan

Before a disaster, when phones work:
1. Designate an out-of-area contact that all family members check in with (local lines often overload; out-of-area calls sometimes get through more reliably)
2. Agree on a meeting place if family members are separated and can't communicate
3. Know where the nearest emergency shelter is

## Key Considerations

**The 72-hour kit is just the start**: FEMA says 72 hours; Sandy showed 2+ weeks. Build to 2 weeks for water and food.

**Rotate your supplies**: Water has a shelf life (replace every 6–12 months for stored tap water; commercial bottled water has a date). Check expiration dates on food annually.

**The car is a resource**: Full gas tank, car charger, ability to drive to a hotel — the car is part of your emergency toolkit. Keep the tank above half during storm season.

**Don't wait for the emergency to prepare**: The Costco run the day before a hurricane produces overflowing parking lots, empty shelves, and panic. Everything here can be assembled over 2–3 months with minimal individual investment.

## Getting Started

**Week 1**: Water. Buy or fill 3 gallons per person in the household. Minimum.

**Week 2**: Food. Identify 1 week of shelf-stable food the household will actually eat. Buy it. Track expiration dates.

**Week 3**: Power and light. Headlamps for everyone. A battery bank charged and ready. A NOAA weather radio.

**Month 2**: Communications plan, document copies, cash reserve.

**Month 3**: Generator or power station decision. First aid kit upgrade. Assess what's missing based on your specific household.

## Common Mistakes

**Preparing only for "big" disasters**: A 3-day power outage happens multiple times per decade in NJ for most households. That's who you're actually preparing for.

**Forgetting medications**: Any medication that requires refrigeration or has a 30-day supply rhythm needs to be part of the plan. Running out of a critical medication during an extended outage is a medical emergency.

**Single point of failure on water**: One method of water storage that fails is a real problem. Layer: stored water + purification tablets + filter.

**Not testing the gear**: A generator you haven't started in 18 months may not start when you need it. A portable power station at 20% charge isn't useful. Test equipment annually.
