# Oil and Fluids
*Engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, transmission fluid — what they do, how to check them, and when to change them*

---

## What Is It

This file covers the fluid systems in a car — what each fluid does, how to check the level and condition, what to use, and the intervals that matter. Fluids are the most accessible maintenance layer: no special tools for checking, relatively low cost, and high consequence for neglect.

---

## Why It Matters

Every major fluid in a car serves an active protective function. Degraded or low fluid means that protection is reduced — and the damage accumulates invisibly until it isn't. Oil is the clearest example: a $50 oil change prevented; an $8,000 engine replacement incurred.

---

## Core Concepts

### Engine Oil

**What it does**: Lubricates all moving parts in the engine, reduces friction and heat, suspends and transports contaminants to the filter, and provides a protective film between metal surfaces.

**Oil viscosity**: Described as XW-XX (e.g., 5W-30). The first number is cold-flow rating (W = winter); the second is high-temperature viscosity. Lower first number = better cold start lubrication. Use exactly the viscosity specified in your owner's manual — not "close enough."

**Conventional vs. synthetic**: Synthetic oil is more stable at temperature extremes, degrades slower, and flows better in cold. Most modern cars specify synthetic. If the manual says synthetic, use it — conventional in a synthetic-specified engine can't maintain the required oil film at operating temperature.

**How to check**:
1. Engine off, parked on level ground, wait 5 minutes after shutting off
2. Pull the dipstick (usually a brightly colored handle, near the front of the engine)
3. Wipe clean with a rag, reinsert fully, pull again
4. Read the level — should be between MIN and MAX marks. Closer to MAX is correct.
5. Check color: new oil is amber. Dark brown is normal use. Black means overdue. Milky/frothy = coolant contamination (head gasket problem — do not drive).

**When to change**: Follow the owner's manual interval, not the sticker the shop puts on your windshield. Most modern synthetic oil is rated for 5,000–10,000 miles. Some manufacturers specify 10,000–15,000 miles. The 3,000-mile rule is outdated marketing.

**How to do an oil change (DIY)**:
1. Warm the engine for 2–3 minutes (warm oil drains better than cold)
2. Locate the drain plug under the engine (owner's manual or lookup the location)
3. Position an oil drain pan underneath
4. Remove the drain plug with the correct socket wrench — use a rag to keep from burning your hand
5. Let drain completely (5–10 minutes)
6. Replace drain plug with a new washer (reusing old washers risks stripping)
7. Move the drain pan under the oil filter
8. Remove the old oil filter with an oil filter wrench — it will drip oil
9. Lightly coat the new filter's rubber gasket with fresh oil
10. Install new filter hand-tight
11. Add the correct amount of new oil through the oil cap on top of the engine (volume is in the manual — typically 4–6 quarts)
12. Run engine for 30 seconds, check for leaks at drain plug and filter
13. Recheck dipstick level

**Dispose of used oil**: Do not pour it out. Auto parts stores (AutoZone, O'Reilly) accept used oil for free.

### Coolant (Antifreeze)

**What it does**: Absorbs heat from the engine and carries it to the radiator to dissipate. Also prevents freezing in winter and boiling in summer (it raises the boiling point and lowers the freezing point vs. plain water). Contains corrosion inhibitors that protect the cooling system metals.

**Types**: Green (conventional, older), orange/pink/red (OAT — organic acid technology, longer life), yellow/blue (HOAT — hybrid). They are not compatible — mixing causes the corrosion inhibitors to neutralize and can form a gel. Use the correct type for your car (owner's manual). Premixed formulas (already 50/50 with distilled water) are the simplest to use.

**How to check**:
- The coolant reservoir is a translucent plastic tank near the radiator. There's a MIN/MAX mark visible without opening it — check the level cold.
- Do NOT open the radiator cap when the engine is hot. Pressurized coolant at 200°F+ will spray and cause burns. If you must check the radiator itself, wait until the engine is completely cool.
- Color should be consistent — not rusty, brown, or particulate-filled.

**Coolant test strips**: $5 at any auto parts store. Dip in the reservoir and compare the color to the chart — tells you if the freeze protection is adequate and if the pH is still in range (low pH = acidic = corroding your engine from inside).

**When to flush**: Every 30,000–50,000 miles or 5 years, whichever comes first. Even low-mileage cars degrade coolant with time.

### Brake Fluid

**What it does**: Hydraulic fluid that transmits force from your brake pedal to the calipers at the wheels. Must withstand very high temperatures (brake calipers get extremely hot). Hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture from the air over time.

**Why moisture absorption matters**: As brake fluid absorbs water, its boiling point drops. Under heavy braking (long downhill, repeated stops), degraded fluid can boil, creating vapor bubbles in the brake lines. Vapor is compressible; fluid isn't. Result: spongy brakes or complete brake fade. This is a safety-critical failure.

**How to check**: The brake fluid reservoir is a small translucent plastic container on top of the master cylinder (near the firewall, driver's side). Check level against MIN/MAX. Color should be light amber — dark brown means it's absorbing moisture and needs changing.

**When to change**: Every 2 years regardless of mileage. This is the one interval that's primarily time-based, not mileage-based, because moisture absorption is continuous. Many manufacturers list longer intervals, but this is a safety system — 2 years is conservative and correct.

**DOT ratings**: DOT 3, DOT 4, DOT 5.1 (glycol-based, mixable), DOT 5 (silicone-based, NOT mixable). Most cars use DOT 3 or DOT 4. DOT 4 has a higher boiling point. Check the cap on your brake fluid reservoir or owner's manual.

**Brake fluid change is NOT a straightforward DIY**: Requires bleeding the brakes (purging old fluid from all four calipers), which requires a pressure bleeder or a helper and specific technique. Incorrect bleeding leaves air in the system. Leave this to a shop or learn brake bleeding properly before attempting.

### Transmission Fluid

**What it does**: Lubricates the transmission, acts as hydraulic fluid for automatic transmissions, cools the transmission, and protects clutch packs.

**How to check (automatic)**: Many modern automatics have a sealed transmission with no dipstick — requires dealer equipment to check. If yours has a dipstick (usually a red-handled dipstick toward the rear of the engine bay), check it with the engine warm and running in Park. Level should be in the "Hot" range.

**Manual transmission**: A drain plug and fill plug on the transmission case. Checking requires raising the car and using a fluid pump. More involved than automatic.

**When to change**: Manufacturer guidance varies widely — some claim "lifetime fluid," meaning they don't specify a change interval. Many independent mechanics disagree. A reasonable approach: every 30,000–60,000 miles for most driving, especially if you drive in stop-and-go or tow. Fluid should be pink/red (automatic) or yellow/amber (manual) — dark brown or burnt-smelling fluid is overdue.

**Transmission fluid change is a moderately advanced DIY**: Dropping the transmission pan (automatic) or using the drain and fill plugs (many modern cars) is accessible. Full fluid and filter replacement on an automatic is cleaner than most people expect but requires care.

### Power Steering Fluid

Most modern cars have electric power steering with no fluid. If your car has hydraulic power steering, there's a reservoir (usually labeled with a steering wheel icon). Check monthly. Use the type specified — don't substitute ATF or other fluids unless the manual explicitly permits it.

### Windshield Washer Fluid

Not a mechanical protection fluid, but worth noting: use actual washer fluid, not water. NJ winters require a fluid rated for below-freezing temps. Water in the washer reservoir will freeze and crack the reservoir or damage the pump.

## Key Considerations

**Check oil and coolant monthly**: 5 minutes. These are the two highest-consequence fluids to neglect. Make it automatic — first of each month.

**Know your intervals before a shop tells you**: When a shop says "your transmission fluid is due," you should already know whether it is. Check your manual, track your mileage.

**Oil leaks don't heal themselves**: If you're consistently low on oil between changes (more than half a quart), there's a leak or the engine is burning oil. Find the source — don't just keep adding oil.

## Getting Started

1. **Check your oil and coolant today**: Find the dipstick, wipe and check. Find the coolant reservoir, check the level cold. This takes 3 minutes.
2. **Record your current mileage and the dates of your last oil change and other services**: If you don't know when the last ones were, assume they're overdue and start from now.
3. **Buy the correct oil, filter, and drain pan**: For your car's specs. Then do the next oil change yourself.

## Common Mistakes

**Using the wrong oil viscosity**: 5W-30 in an engine that specifies 0W-20 creates inadequate cold-start protection — the biggest wear period in an engine's life is cold starts.

**Topping off with the wrong coolant type**: Mixing coolant types neutralizes the corrosion inhibitors and can form a gel. If you don't know what type is in there, a full flush with distilled water and refill with the correct type is the clean solution.

**Checking oil on a slope**: Level ground only. A car on a slope will give a false reading on the dipstick.

**Not replacing the drain plug washer**: The crush washer on the oil drain plug is designed for one use. Reusing it risks a slow leak or a suddenly stripped drain plug.
