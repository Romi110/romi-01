# Drywall and Patching
*Patching holes, taping seams, mudding, sanding, and painting — wall repair from nail holes to major damage*

---

## What Is It

Drywall (also called sheetrock or wallboard) is the standard wall and ceiling material in American construction since the 1950s. It's gypsum plaster pressed between two paper layers. It's cheap, fast to install, easy to paint — and easy to damage. Knowing how to repair it is one of the most useful home skills because damage to drywall is constant and repair companies charge disproportionately for it.

This file covers everything from small nail holes to 6-inch repairs, plus taping new drywall and painting the repair invisibly.

---

## Why It Matters

Every home has drywall damage over time — nail holes, door handle dents, accidental kicks, plumbing access, and renovations all leave holes. A handyman charges $150–300 to patch a hole. Learning to do it yourself correctly produces results indistinguishable from new — and the skills transfer to any wall in any home.

## Core Concepts

### Types of Damage and the Right Fix

**Nail/screw holes and small dents (under ½ inch)**:
Apply spackle (pre-mixed compound in a tub) with a flexible putty knife. Press it in, scrape flush, let dry completely (15–30 min for lightweight spackle). Sand lightly. Prime. Paint. Done in under an hour.

**Small holes (½ inch to 3 inches)**:
Use the California patch or a mesh patch kit:
- *Mesh patch*: Peel-and-stick fiberglass mesh that bridges the hole. Apply joint compound (mud) over the mesh in thin coats. Feather the edges wide. Three coats minimum, sanding between each coat.
- *California patch*: Cut a square piece of drywall larger than the hole. Score and snap off the paper backing on two sides, leaving a paper flap. Apply mud to the wall, press the patch in, the paper flap embeds in the mud. Coat over it like any joint.

**Medium holes (3 to 6 inches)**:
The backing board method: Cut the hole to a clean rectangle with a drywall saw. Cut a piece of wood (1×3 or 1×4) longer than the hole's height. Insert it through the hole and screw it to the existing drywall on both sides (it spans the hole, giving you a surface to screw into). Cut a drywall patch to fit the rectangle. Screw the patch to the wood backing. Tape and mud the seams.

**Large holes (over 6 inches)**:
Cut back to studs on each side of the damage (studs are 16 inches on center in most construction). This gives you a solid nailing/screwing surface on each edge. Cut a drywall patch to fit between studs. Add horizontal backing at top and bottom if needed. Tape and mud.

### The Taping and Mudding Process

This is where most amateur patches fail — they don't apply enough coats, don't feather wide enough, and skip sanding properly.

**Joint compound (mud)**: The material applied over tape and for filling. Comes pre-mixed in buckets (all-purpose mud) or as a powder you mix. Pre-mixed is the right choice for repairs.
- *All-purpose compound*: Dries by evaporation. Can be recoated when dry. Used for everything.
- *Setting compound (Durabond)*: Dries by chemical reaction (hard set). Much harder when dry — harder to sand. Use for first coat on large repairs when you need structural strength.
- *Topping compound*: Fine, smooth finish coat. Good for the final coat on large repairs.

**Paper tape vs. mesh tape**: Paper tape is stronger for seams and corners; it requires embedding in a wet mud coat first. Mesh tape is self-adhesive and easier for patches — stick it on, then mud over it. Mesh is more prone to cracking on long seams but is fine for patch work.

**The coat sequence**:
1. *Tape coat*: Bed the tape in wet mud. For paper tape: spread a thin layer of mud, press tape into it, smooth with light pressure to remove bubbles. For mesh: tape is already on, apply mud over it.
2. *Filler coat*: When tape coat is completely dry, apply second coat of mud. Wider than the first — the key is feathering the edges. The coat should extend 3–4 inches beyond the tape on each side.
3. *Finish coat*: Third thin coat, wider still (6–8 inches from center for a patch). Sand smooth between this coat and painting.

**Feathering**: The technique that makes patches invisible. Feathering means thinning the mud at the edges to zero — no abrupt edge. Hold the putty knife at a low angle, press the far edge down, and draw outward to thin the mud at the perimeter. The transition should be imperceptible when painted.

**Drying time**: Mud must be completely dry before the next coat. Still-wet mud is darker in color. Dry mud is uniformly light. Impatience here causes cracks and bumps.

### Sanding

Proper sanding is what separates invisible repairs from visible ones.

**Sandpaper grits**:
- 80-grit: Aggressive. For knocking down high spots on filler coats.
- 120-grit: Standard for between-coat sanding.
- 150–220-grit: Final sanding before primer. Should feel smooth to the touch.

**Sanding tools**:
- *Sanding block*: A rubber or foam block that holds sandpaper flat. Essential — never sand drywall with just your hand, which creates uneven pressure and hollows.
- *Pole sander*: Flat pad on an extension pole. Makes ceiling and large-area sanding manageable.
- *Sanding sponge*: Dual-grit. Good for gentle final passes and corner work.

**Dust control**: Drywall dust is pervasive and fine. It gets everywhere. Use a dust mask (N95 minimum), seal doorways with plastic sheeting if working in a room, and plan for cleanup. Wet sanding with a damp sponge (rather than dry sandpaper) significantly reduces dust but requires more skill — the sponge can pull and distort the mud if done wrong.

**Check your work with raking light**: Hold a work light at a low angle to the wall. Every imperfection appears in relief. Fix anything you see before priming.

### Priming — The Step People Skip

Prime the repaired area before painting. Always. Unprimed mud absorbs paint differently than the surrounding painted wall — the repair will show through even perfectly matched paint as a "flash" (different sheen).

**PVA drywall primer**: The right primer for fresh drywall mud. Seals the porous surface. One coat is sufficient.

**Spot primer**: For small repairs where you don't want to prime an entire wall, use a spray primer spot-applied just to the repair and feathered out a few inches.

After priming, check again with raking light. Prime raises imperfections you can sand and re-prime if needed.

### Painting the Repair Invisibly

**Color matching**: Take a photo of the paint can label (manufacturer, color name, and code) if you have it. If not, many paint stores can match existing wall paint by scanning a chip or small sample. Cut a small square from behind an outlet cover plate — same color, protected from light and cleaning.

**Sheen matters**: Flat/matte, eggshell, satin, semi-gloss — the sheen affects how light reflects and whether a repair is visible. Match the existing finish. A flat wall painted with eggshell on the repair will show the patch even with perfect color match.

**Rolling vs. brushing**: For large repairs, roll the entire wall from corner to corner with the original paint to avoid lap lines. Rolling just the patch creates a visible "island" of fresh paint. If you have to spot-paint, feather aggressively and use a light touch — stippling with a brush can help blend the texture.

**Texture matching**: If your walls have texture (orange peel, knockdown, or skip-trowel), you must replicate it before painting. Smooth mud over textured walls looks wrong after painting.
- *Orange peel*: Spray texture in a can. Apply lightly, let dry, sand minimally before priming.
- *Knockdown*: Apply mud through a hopper sprayer or with a sponge, let set slightly, then "knock down" with a wide knife at a low angle.
- *Skip-trowel*: Applied with a rounded trowel in random overlapping strokes. Practice on cardboard first.

## Key Considerations

**Every coat must be dry**: The most common mistake is recoating wet mud. It looks dry on the surface but can still be wet underneath. A full day between coats is conservative; overnight in most NJ conditions. In high humidity (summer), it takes longer.

**Wide feathering hides repairs**: Beginner patches are too small. The mud should extend 3–4× the size of the actual damage. A 3-inch hole needs a 12–15-inch mud zone total.

**Don't over-sand**: Sanding through the paper face of the surrounding drywall creates fuzzy areas that need to be primed and coated separately. Sand gently near the edges of a patch.

---

## Getting Started

1. **Practice on a scrap piece of drywall** before touching walls: tape a joint, run three mud coats, sand, and see what it looks like. The feedback loop is fast.
2. **Fix nail holes first**: Every home has them. Buy a tub of lightweight spackle and fix every nail hole and small ding in one room. Prime and touch up. This is the confidence-builder.
3. **Buy a drywall patch kit**: Most hardware stores carry kits for 4–6 inch holes. They include mesh, compound, and instructions. Do one real repair before you need to do a critical one.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Not priming before painting**: The most common reason a repair shows through.

**Too few coats**: Two coats of mud don't hide tape. Three is the minimum, four is better for large repairs.

**Sanding too soon**: Wet mud sands rough and tears. It also looks smooth until it dries and shrinks. Wait.

**Not matching texture**: A perfectly mudded, sanded, and painted repair on a textured wall stands out immediately because the smooth patch reflects light differently than the surrounding texture.
