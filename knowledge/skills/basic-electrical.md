# Basic Electrical
*Circuits, breakers, outlets, and what you can safely do yourself in NJ*

---

## What Is It

Basic home electrical knowledge covers how residential electrical systems are wired, how to safely work on outlets and switches, how to read your breaker panel, and what the limits of safe DIY work are. This isn't about becoming an electrician — it's about being capable with 80% of the common tasks and knowing when to stop.

NJ requires permits for most electrical work beyond simple fixture/outlet replacement, and inspections are enforced more strictly here than in many states. This file covers both the technical knowledge and the NJ-specific legal context.

---

## Why It Matters

Electrical ignorance is expensive (paying an electrician $200/hr for a $20 job) and occasionally dangerous (touching the wrong wire). The ability to replace an outlet, install a dimmer, add a GFCI, trace a tripped breaker, and diagnose a dead circuit is genuinely useful and within reach of anyone willing to learn the rules.

## Core Concepts

### How Residential Electrical Works

**The panel (breaker box)**: Receives power from the utility (typically 200A service for modern homes). Distributes it through individual breakers to circuits throughout the house. Each breaker protects one circuit — when current exceeds the breaker rating, it trips.

**Circuits**: A circuit runs from the panel, through the hot wire, to the devices, back through the neutral wire. Most household circuits are 15A (for lights, standard outlets) or 20A (for kitchens, bathrooms, laundry). The breaker amperage matches the wire gauge:
- 15A circuit: 14 AWG wire (14 gauge)
- 20A circuit: 12 AWG wire (12 gauge)
- Never use 14 AWG wire on a 20A breaker — it's a fire hazard

**The three wires**:
- *Hot* (black or red): Carries current from the panel. The dangerous wire.
- *Neutral* (white): Returns current to the panel. Still carries current — not "safe."
- *Ground* (green or bare copper): Safety wire. Provides a fault path — if the hot contacts the device chassis, current flows to ground and trips the breaker instead of flowing through you.

**Voltage**: Standard outlets are 120V. Large appliances (dryers, ranges, EV chargers, central AC) use 240V — two hot wires plus neutral and ground. Do not work on 240V circuits without clear training and understanding.

**GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter)**: Required by code in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, outdoors, and anywhere near water. Detects small current imbalances (indicating current leaking through a person) and trips in milliseconds. A GFCI outlet protects all downstream outlets on the same circuit. You can replace a standard outlet with a GFCI outlet to protect a circuit without running new wire.

**AFCI (Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter)**: Required by modern code in bedrooms and living areas. Detects arcing (damaged wires sparking). Built into the breaker itself, not the outlet. Existing homes aren't required to retrofit, but new construction and significant renovations require them.

### Safe DIY Electrical Work

**Rule 1 — Always turn off the breaker and verify it's off**: Use a non-contact voltage tester (essential tool, ~$15) to confirm the circuit is dead before touching any wire. Don't trust your memory of which breaker controls which outlet — verify with the tester.

**What NJ homeowners can legally do themselves** (without a permit for routine maintenance):
- Replace outlets (same type, same location)
- Replace switches (same type)
- Replace light fixtures (same box, same rating)
- Replace a GFCI outlet
- Reset a tripped breaker

**What requires a permit in NJ**:
- Adding new circuits or outlets
- Installing new breakers
- Any work in the panel
- Running new wire through walls
- Installing EV chargers (240V)
- Adding outdoor outlets

The NJ permit requirement isn't just bureaucratic — it means a licensed electrician inspects the work. For anything beyond maintenance, this is the right answer.

**The process for replacing an outlet**:
1. Identify the circuit at the breaker panel. Label if it isn't already.
2. Flip the breaker. Verify dead with voltage tester at the outlet.
3. Remove the cover plate. Unscrew the outlet from the box.
4. Photograph the wire connections before disconnecting anything.
5. Note which wires connect where: hot (black) to brass screws, neutral (white) to silver screws, ground (bare/green) to green screw.
6. Replace with the new outlet, reconnect same-to-same.
7. Push back into box, secure, replace cover plate.
8. Restore power, test with outlet tester (~$10).

### Reading and Troubleshooting the Panel

**Labeling**: Every breaker should be labeled with what it controls. If yours isn't (common in older homes), use a circuit tracer or methodically plug a lamp into each outlet and trip breakers until you identify them. Label immediately — this knowledge is time-sensitive and you'll forget.

**Tripped breaker**: A tripped breaker sits between ON and OFF. Switch fully to OFF, then back to ON. If it immediately trips again, there's a short or overload on the circuit — don't keep resetting, find the cause.

**Common causes of tripped breakers**:
- *Overload*: Too many devices on one circuit. Calculate load (watts / 120 = amps) and stay under 80% of breaker rating.
- *Short circuit*: Hot and neutral touched each other somewhere. Find the damaged device or wiring.
- *Ground fault*: Current leaking to ground — a GFCI would have tripped first if installed.

**Double-pole breakers** (two breakers linked together): Control 240V circuits. Don't work on these.

**Main breaker**: Shuts off all power to the house except the wires coming in from the utility. Even with the main off, the wires entering from the street remain live. Those are always dangerous and owned by the utility.

### Tools You Actually Need

- Non-contact voltage tester (~$15): Non-negotiable safety tool
- Outlet tester (~$10): Verifies correct wiring after installation
- Flathead and Phillips screwdrivers
- Wire strippers
- Needle-nose pliers
- Electrical tape
- Wire nuts (various sizes)

Optional but useful: multimeter (measures voltage, continuity, and resistance — more precise than non-contact tester).

## Key Considerations

**Never work in a wet environment**: Water and electricity kill. If anything near water is involved, the circuit must be off and verified dead.

**Old wiring in NJ homes**: Many older NJ homes (pre-1970s) have aluminum wiring instead of copper. Aluminum requires special outlets and connectors (rated CO/ALR) — standard copper connectors on aluminum wiring is a fire hazard. If your home has aluminum wiring, have it evaluated by a licensed electrician before doing any DIY electrical work.

**Knob-and-tube wiring**: Found in some NJ homes built before 1940. No ground wire. Do not add ground outlets to ungrounded circuits — it creates a false sense of safety. Knob-and-tube should be evaluated by an electrician.

**The 80% rule**: Don't load a circuit above 80% of its rated capacity (12A on a 15A circuit, 16A on a 20A circuit) for continuous loads. Appliances running continuously (space heaters, EV chargers) must be sized with this margin.

## Getting Started

1. **Map your panel**: Label every breaker if it isn't already. Know what's on what circuit.
2. **Buy a non-contact voltage tester**: Before anything else.
3. **Replace one outlet**: Choose a simple standard outlet in a low-stakes location. Practice the process. Check with the outlet tester when done.
4. **Add GFCI outlets** anywhere near water that doesn't have them — this is legitimate maintenance and significantly improves safety.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Working on a live circuit**: The most dangerous mistake. Always verify with a tester. Breaker labels are wrong more often than you'd think.

**Connecting wires wrong**: Hot to neutral is a short. Neutral to ground causes hazardous floating neutral conditions. Photo before disconnecting.

**Overloading a circuit**: Adding an outlet to an already-loaded circuit can create chronic tripping or overheating. Count the load before adding devices.

**Using the wrong wire gauge**: 14 AWG on a 20A circuit is a fire risk. Match wire to breaker.
