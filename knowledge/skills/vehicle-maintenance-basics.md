# Vehicle Maintenance Basics
*What every driver must know, what you can DIY, and where to stop*

---

## What Is It

Vehicle maintenance knowledge covers the systems that keep a car running, the routine tasks any owner should perform or understand, and the diagnostics that tell you when something is wrong before it becomes expensive. This is not about becoming a mechanic — it's about not being helpless and not being taken advantage of.

Modern cars are genuinely complex and much of the powertrain is off-limits without diagnostic equipment. But the maintenance layer — fluids, tires, lights, filters, batteries — is accessible to anyone willing to learn it.

---

## Why It Matters

Cars are expensive assets. Neglecting routine maintenance accelerates wear, causes breakdowns, and turns small problems into large ones. On the other side: knowing how to do basic maintenance saves real money (a shop oil change costs 2–4× the DIY cost) and means you're never completely stranded by something simple.

Equally important: knowing what questions to ask at a shop means you can't be easily upsold on work you don't need.

## Core Concepts

### The Major Systems You Need to Understand

**Lubrication (oil)**: Engine oil lubricates moving parts and prevents metal-on-metal wear. It degrades with heat and use. Oil changes are the single most important maintenance task — neglecting them shortens engine life significantly. See [oil-and-fluids.md](oil-and-fluids.md) for specifics.

**Cooling**: Engine coolant (antifreeze) absorbs heat from the engine and dissipates it through the radiator. Coolant degrades and becomes acidic over time, corroding the cooling system from inside. Check coolant level and condition regularly. Flush per the manufacturer's schedule (typically every 30,000–50,000 miles or every 5 years).

**Tires and brakes**: The two systems that determine whether the car stops and stays on the road. See [tires-and-brakes.md](tires-and-brakes.md) for specifics.

**Battery and charging**: The 12V battery starts the car and powers electronics when the engine is off. The alternator charges the battery while the engine runs. Batteries typically last 3–5 years. Signs of a failing battery: slow cranking, electronics acting strange, battery warning light. Test annually at any auto parts store (free).

**Belts**: The serpentine belt drives the alternator, power steering pump, AC compressor, and sometimes the water pump. It's a single belt on most modern cars. Inspect for cracking, fraying, or glazing. Replace per manufacturer schedule (typically every 60,000–100,000 miles). A broken serpentine belt leaves you immediately stranded. Timing belts/chains are a separate system — the timing belt (if your car has one vs. a chain) must be replaced on schedule because failure destroys the engine.

**Filters**: Air filter (engine air intake), cabin air filter (HVAC), fuel filter (on older cars — most modern cars have long-life or in-tank filters). Air filter replacement is simple DIY; check annually and replace when dirty.

### The Maintenance Schedule — Your Owner's Manual Is the Authority

Every car has a manufacturer maintenance schedule in the owner's manual. It supersedes generic advice. "Change oil every 3,000 miles" is outdated for most modern cars — many use synthetic oil rated for 5,000–10,000 miles or more. The manufacturer's schedule, based on your actual driving conditions, is correct.

**Two schedules exist** in most manuals: normal and severe. Severe applies if you frequently drive short trips under 10 miles (oil doesn't fully warm up, moisture accumulates), tow or haul, drive in extreme temperatures, or drive in dusty conditions. NJ commuters doing short highway stints often fall into the normal category; short-trip city driving trends toward severe.

**Key intervals** (typical — verify in your manual):
- Oil change: 5,000–10,000 miles (synthetic), 3,000–5,000 (conventional)
- Tire rotation: Every 5,000–7,500 miles
- Air filter: Every 15,000–30,000 miles
- Cabin air filter: Every 15,000–25,000 miles
- Brake fluid: Every 2 years (absorbs moisture over time regardless of mileage)
- Coolant flush: Every 30,000–50,000 miles or 5 years
- Spark plugs (iridium/platinum): Every 60,000–100,000 miles
- Serpentine belt: Every 60,000–100,000 miles
- Transmission fluid: Every 30,000–60,000 miles (varies widely; some claim "lifetime" fluid but many mechanics recommend changing anyway)

### What You Can DIY

**Oil change**: If you have space and basic tools. Requires oil drain pan, oil filter wrench, new filter, and the correct oil. Shop cost: $60–100 for a full synthetic change. DIY cost: $35–50 in materials. Time: 30 minutes once you know your car.

**Cabin air filter**: Usually behind the glove box. Remove glove box, slide out old filter, slide in new one. 10 minutes, $15–25 part.

**Engine air filter**: Under the hood in a plastic box connected to the intake. Typically no tools required. 5 minutes, $15–25 part.

**Battery replacement**: Disconnect negative terminal first, then positive. Remove hold-down bracket. Lift out old battery, set in new one, reconnect positive first, then negative. 15–20 minutes. Bring the old battery to the store — most have a core charge refund.

**Wiper blades**: Most wiper arms have a simple hook connection. Lift the arm, press the release tab, slide out old blade, snap in new one. 5 minutes.

**Lights**: Bulb replacement for most exterior lights is accessible from the engine bay side. Some headlights require removing the headlight assembly (increasingly common on modern cars and may be DIY-hostile). Check your specific car's procedure before attempting.

**Tire pressure check and inflation**: Every owner should do this. See [tires-and-brakes.md](tires-and-brakes.md).

### Understanding Warning Lights

**Check Engine Light (CEL)**: The most misunderstood light. It means the ECU (engine control unit) has stored a fault code — it does not mean the engine is about to fail. To know what it means, you need the code. An OBD-II scanner ($25–100) plugs into the port under the dashboard (below the steering column) and reads the codes. Many auto parts stores will also read them for free. Common causes include a loose gas cap (tighten it — the light may clear itself in a few drive cycles), oxygen sensor failures, and catalytic converter issues. A steadily on CEL that doesn't affect performance can often wait; a flashing CEL indicates a misfire and should be addressed quickly (it can damage the catalytic converter).

**Temperature gauge**: Should sit in the middle range when warmed up. If it climbs toward red, pull over immediately and turn off the engine. Continuing to drive an overheating engine causes catastrophic damage. Let it cool before opening the coolant reservoir.

**Oil pressure light**: Red oil can on the dashboard. If this illuminates while driving, stop immediately. Low oil pressure means inadequate lubrication — continued driving damages or destroys the engine. Check oil level (wait for engine to cool slightly, use the dipstick). If the oil level is fine and the light is still on, the oil pressure sensor or pump may be failing — do not drive.

**Battery light**: Alternator or charging system issue. The car is running on battery alone. You have perhaps 20–30 minutes of runtime. Get to a shop or safe location immediately.

### What to Never DIY

**Anything requiring specialized tools or knowledge beyond routine maintenance**: Transmission internal work, timing belt/chain replacement (on most cars), fuel system repairs, brake hydraulic system beyond brake pad replacement, suspension geometry.

**Airbag system**: Any airbag component is dangerous to handle. They can deploy without warning.

**Brake hydraulic system**: Opening brake lines incorrectly can introduce air into the system, making brakes fail. Caliper replacement and brake line work is for someone who knows brake bleeding.

**Any warranty-affecting work** if you're still under warranty: Document everything and use authorized service centers.

## Key Considerations

**OBD-II scanner is the most valuable diagnostic tool**: A $30 scanner from Amazon or AutoZone prevents shops from fabricating problems (CEL on = expensive fishing expedition vs. CEL on = code P0420 = O2 sensor issue). Know what the code says before authorizing work.

**Shop relationships matter**: Find one honest mechanic and stick with them. The upsell culture at chain shops (Jiffy Lube, Midas) is real — they're incentivized to sell services. An independent mechanic with good reviews who knows your car is worth the relationship.

**Preventive maintenance is cheaper than repairs**: $80 in oil changes per year vs. $8,000 for an engine that failed from oil neglect. The math is extreme. Follow the schedule.

**NJ inspection requirements**: NJ requires biennial vehicle inspections. Emissions and safety checks. A car that fails inspection can't be legally driven. Keeping up with maintenance means fewer surprises at inspection.

## Getting Started

1. **Find your owner's manual**: If you don't have it, search for "[make] [model] [year] owner's manual PDF" — most are available free online. Read the maintenance schedule section.
2. **Buy an OBD-II scanner**: Keep it in the car. Whenever the CEL comes on, read the code yourself before taking it to anyone.
3. **Check your tire pressure and oil level today**: These are the two most important quick checks. Instructions in the sub-topic files.
4. **Replace your cabin air filter**: High probability it's overdue. Takes 10 minutes.

## Common Mistakes

**Using the wrong oil**: Always use the viscosity specified in the owner's manual (usually printed on the oil cap too). Using 5W-30 when the car calls for 0W-20 affects cold-start lubrication.

**Ignoring the coolant**: Engine oil gets attention; coolant often doesn't. Degraded coolant corrodes the cooling system and causes expensive head gasket and radiator failures.

**Deferring recommended maintenance "until something goes wrong"**: Timing belt failure means engine replacement. Neglected coolant means head gasket. These are not wear-and-fail parts — they're time-bombs with known schedules.

**Trusting chain lube shops to identify what your car needs**: Their job is to sell services. Cross-reference recommendations against your owner's manual.
