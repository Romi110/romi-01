# Land Navigation
*Map and compass, triangulation, terrain reading, and pace counting — offline self-reliance when GPS fails*

---

## What Is It

Land navigation is the ability to determine your location and plan a route using a topographic map and compass, without GPS or electronic assistance. It combines map reading (understanding a 2D representation of 3D terrain), compass work (orienting yourself to magnetic north), and terrain association (matching what you see on the ground to what's shown on the map).

It's a skill from military fieldcraft and wilderness survival that has direct civilian applications: hiking off-trail, navigating after power/phone failure, or simply being someone who can't be lost.

---

## Why It Matters

GPS fails in canyon terrain, under heavy canopy, in emergencies when the phone battery is dead, and in the scenarios where you most need navigation. Paper maps and a compass have no battery, no signal requirement, and no failure mode besides user error.

More fundamentally: understanding terrain and being able to read a topographic map changes how you see landscape. It converts hiking from "following a trail" to genuinely understanding where you are in a physical place.

## Core Concepts

### Topographic Maps — Reading the Terrain

A topographic map (topo map) represents three-dimensional terrain in two dimensions using contour lines, symbols, and colors.

**Contour lines**: Lines connecting points of equal elevation. The closer together the lines, the steeper the terrain. Wide spacing = gentle slope; tight spacing = steep slope; touching lines = cliff.

**Contour interval**: The elevation difference between adjacent contour lines. Typically 20, 40, or 80 feet depending on the map scale. Printed in the map legend.

**Index contours**: Every fifth contour line is darker and labeled with its elevation. Use these to track your altitude.

**Reading slope from contours**:
- V-shape pointing uphill (upstream on a drainage): Indicates a ridge
- V-shape pointing downhill: Indicates a drainage (valley, creek, ravine)
- Closed circles (rings): Hilltop if numbers increase toward center; depression if marked with hatch marks on the inner side
- Saddle (col): Two high points with a low area between them — the contour lines form an hourglass shape

**Map scale**: The ratio of map distance to real distance. 1:24,000 (USGS standard 7.5-minute quad) means 1 inch on the map = 24,000 inches (2,000 feet) on the ground. 1:50,000 covers more area but less detail.

**Colors and symbols**:
- Green: Vegetation (forest, brush)
- Blue: Water (streams, lakes, swamps)
- Brown: Contour lines (elevation)
- Black: Man-made features (buildings, roads, trails)
- White: Open areas (meadows, cleared land)
- Red: Major roads, boundaries, survey information

**Declination**: The difference between true north (the geographic North Pole) and magnetic north (where a compass points). In NJ, magnetic declination is approximately -13° (magnetic north is about 13° west of true north). Topo maps show the declination in the margin. You must account for this when using a map with a compass — otherwise your bearings will be off by 13°.

### The Compass — Types and Parts

**Base plate compass** (Brunton, Silva, Suunto): The standard for map-and-compass navigation. Consists of:
- *Baseplate*: Transparent rectangular plate with a direction-of-travel arrow
- *Rotating bezel (housing)*: Graduated in degrees 0–360; contains the magnetic needle and orientation lines/arrow
- *Magnetic needle*: Red end points to magnetic north (always)
- *Orienting lines*: Lines inside the bezel that align with the map's north-south grid lines
- *Orienting arrow*: An arrow inside the bezel that aligns under the north end of the needle when you take a bearing

**Lensatic compass (military style)**: More durable, used by the military, but harder to use for map work. The baseplate compass is more practical for civilian map-and-compass navigation.

### Taking a Bearing and Following It

**Magnetic bearing**: The direction from your current location to your destination, measured in degrees clockwise from magnetic north (0° = north, 90° = east, 180° = south, 270° = west).

**How to take a bearing to a visible target**:
1. Point the direction-of-travel arrow at the target
2. Rotate the bezel until the orienting arrow aligns under the north end of the magnetic needle ("put red in the shed")
3. Read the bearing at the index line (where the bezel meets the direction-of-travel arrow)

**How to follow a bearing**:
1. Set the bearing by rotating the bezel to the desired degree
2. Hold the compass flat
3. Rotate your body until the magnetic needle sits over the orienting arrow ("red in the shed")
4. The direction-of-travel arrow now points to your bearing
5. Pick an intermediate landmark on that line (tree, rock, peak) and walk to it
6. Repeat — don't stare at the compass while walking

**Accounting for declination in NJ** (-13°):
- Converting a map bearing to a compass bearing (what to set on your compass): Add 13° (East declination = subtract; West = add — in NJ, we add)
- Many baseplate compasses allow declination adjustment — set it once and forget it
- USGS topo maps show a declination diagram in the margin

### Map Orientation — Aligning Map to Ground

Before using a map for navigation, orient it so north on the map aligns with north on the ground.

**With a compass**:
1. Lay the map flat
2. Place the compass on the map with the edge along a north-south gridline
3. Rotate the map (not the compass) until the magnetic needle aligns with north, accounting for declination
4. The map now mirrors the terrain around you

**By terrain association**: Find a distinctive feature visible from your location — a mountain, road, lake. Find it on the map. Rotate the map until the feature on the map aligns with the feature on the ground. The map is now oriented.

### Triangulation — Finding Your Position

Triangulation (more accurately called resection) determines your position when you're not sure where you are.

**How to triangulate**:
1. Identify two or three distinctive landmarks visible from your position that are also on the map (peaks, road intersections, distinctive terrain features)
2. Take a compass bearing to the first landmark. Plot that bearing line on the map from the landmark (your position is somewhere on that line going back toward you).
3. Take a bearing to the second landmark. Plot that line on the map.
4. Your position is at or near where the two lines intersect.
5. A third landmark provides a check — the three lines should form a small triangle (the "triangle of error"). Your position is within that triangle.

**Converting compass bearing to map line**: The bearing to landmark A is 45° (magnetic). To plot your back bearing (the line back toward your position): add or subtract 180° (the reciprocal bearing is 45° + 180° = 225°). Draw a line from landmark A on the map at that angle — your position is somewhere on that line.

**Terrain association is faster and usually sufficient**: Rather than precise triangulation, an experienced navigator continuously matches terrain features to the map — running ridges, drainages, saddles, elevation changes — and maintains a continuous picture of their location. Triangulation is the tool when terrain association has failed.

### Pace Counting — Measuring Distance on the Ground

When traveling cross-country without clear landmarks to gauge progress, pace counting measures distance traveled.

**Your pace**: Count every time your left foot hits the ground. One "pace" = two steps. Most adults have a pace count of 65–75 paces per 100 meters on flat ground. Uphill increases count (shorter steps), downhill may decrease.

**Calibrating your pace**:
1. Find a measured 100-meter course (a running track: 100m straight, or use GPS/Google Maps to measure a flat trail segment)
2. Walk it naturally and count your paces (every left-foot strike)
3. Record your count. This is your individual 100-meter pace.

**Ranger beads**: A set of beads on a cord used to track paces without mental counting. String of 9 beads for 900 meters, separate string of 4 beads for 4 kilometers. Move one bead after every 100 meters (your calibrated pace count). When all 9 are moved, move one on the kilometer string and reset the 9.

**Practical use**: On a known bearing to a destination estimated at 600 meters, count 6 × your 100-meter pace count. When you hit your count, you should be near the destination. If you're in a drainage or on terrain that doesn't match, you've drifted.

### Route Planning

**Before leaving**:
1. Study the map: trace your planned route, note major terrain features along the way, identify bail-out routes, note water sources
2. Identify handrails: linear features (ridgeline, creek, road) that you can navigate along without constant compass work
3. Identify catching features: something beyond your destination that would stop your travel and tell you you've gone too far — a road, a river, a major ridgeline
4. Identify attack points: obvious terrain features near your destination that you navigate to first, then use a precise bearing and pace count for the final approach

**In NJ / Pinelands**: The flat, forested terrain of South Jersey provides few elevation landmarks. Navigation in the Pinelands relies heavily on trails, unpaved sand roads, and drainage features. Detailed USGS 7.5-minute topo maps and a compass are the right tools. Cedar streams (tea-colored due to tannins) and sand roads are your primary handrails.

## Key Considerations

**Magnetic declination must be accounted for**: In NJ at -13°, ignoring declination causes 13° of error on every bearing — over 300 meters of lateral error per kilometer traveled. Not a small mistake.

**GPS as a supplement, not a replacement**: Carry GPS (your phone with an offline map app like Gaia GPS or CalTopo, downloaded before you leave cell service). Use it to verify, not to navigate. If you rely solely on GPS and it fails, you're lost. If you use map and compass primarily and GPS as backup, you're navigated.

**Navigation is a perishable skill**: It must be practiced to stay sharp. Day hikes with a topo map (even on trail) are the practice environment. Navigate by map even when you know the trail — maintain the skill.

## Getting Started

1. **Get a baseplate compass** (Suunto A-10 or Silva Ranger 2.0, ~$20–40) and the USGS 7.5-minute topo map for your area (available at mytopo.com or nationalmap.gov — free download and print, or order print copies).
2. **Orient the map at home**: Set it on a table, use your compass to align it to north. Find your house on the map. Find one visible landmark from your window and locate it on the map. This is the first skill.
3. **Take a bearing on a local hike**: Before following the trail, take a compass bearing toward the trail's destination. Note it. Compare to what the map says the bearing should be. These won't be identical — the practice is in the doing.
4. **Calibrate your pace count** on a measured course.

## Common Mistakes

**Not accounting for declination**: In NJ, this is a 13° error on every bearing. It compounds over distance.

**Looking at the compass constantly while walking**: Take the bearing, pick an intermediate landmark on that line, walk to it, retake the bearing. Staring at the compass while walking causes drift and gets you lost.

**Reading the south end of the needle as north**: The red end points north. It sounds obvious but is a common field error under stress. "Red in the shed" — the red needle aligns with the orienting arrow.

**Navigating only to your destination without a bail-out plan**: Know the catching features — what would you hit if you've gone too far? This prevents minor navigation errors from becoming serious.
