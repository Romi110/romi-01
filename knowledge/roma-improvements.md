## 2. Tenet Balance Is Uneven

Tenets 1 and 2 were written first and it shows — they have slightly more texture and lived-in quality. Tenets 3–7 are well-written but feel more like excellent teaching and less like something pulled from personal experience.

**Specific observations:**
- **Tenet 3 (Interconnection)**: The "What It Looks Like in Real Life" examples are good but generic. Could use one example that is distinctly yours — something from your actual life, not a universal scenario.
- **Tenet 4 (Present moment)**: Solid, but the examples overlap with Tenet 1's territory (both use the "mind wandering" pattern). Differentiate.
- **Tenet 5 (Action)**: The strongest of the later tenets. The paragraph about consuming information instead of doing the thing is excellent.
- **Tenet 6 (Meaning)**: The Camus paragraph is the best philosophical writing in Roma. The examples could be stronger — "someone becomes a parent" feels borrowed, not experienced.
- **Tenet 7 (Ego)**: The meeting example and the feedback example overlap (both are about being wrong at work). Cut one or replace with a non-work example.

**Suggestion**: Add one personal example per tenet. Not autobiography — just enough to make it clear these aren't observations from a textbook but from a life.

---

## 3. Stories Need Tenet Connections Surfaced

Each story has a tenet tag at the bottom (e.g., *Tenets: 1, 4, 7*), but the reader has to figure out the mapping themselves. This is deliberate — you said "not parables with lessons attached" — and it mostly works. But a few stories are ambiguous enough that the connection is unclear.

**Options (pick one):**
- **Leave as-is.** The ambiguity is the point. The reader does the work.
- **Add a single italicized line after each story** (you already do this for some). Make it consistent — every story gets one line. Not a lesson. A seed.
- **Remove the tenet tags entirely.** If the stories should stand alone, the tags undercut that. They turn the stories into illustrations rather than experiences.

The current state (some have italicized reflections, all have tenet tags) is the weakest of the three options. Pick a lane.

## 5. Quotes Sourcing Is Inconsistent

Most quotes have author, source, and date. A few are loose:

- The "Between stimulus and response" quote attributed to Viktor Frankl is widely disputed — it doesn't appear in *Man's Search for Meaning* and may be a paraphrase or misattribution. Worth verifying or noting the ambiguity.

**Suggestion**: Do one pass through all quotes. Verify sources. Use a consistent format: *"Quote."* — Author, *Source*, Date. You're close already.
