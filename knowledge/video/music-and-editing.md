# Music and Editing
*Cutting on the beat, choosing music that fits what you shot, royalty-free sources, and Content ID*

---

## What Is It

Music does more work in a video than most creators realize. It sets emotional register, drives pacing, creates momentum, and determines whether a sequence feels energetic or contemplative. The edit and the music are not independent — a cut that's wrong rhythmically breaks the feeling of a track; a track that's wrong for the footage makes good cuts feel off.

This file covers the craft side: how to find the beat and cut to it, how to choose music that matches your footage, and how to navigate the royalty-free landscape (sources, licensing, and what Content ID means for your videos). The mechanical mixing workflow — ducking, levels — is in [video-editing-basics.md](video-editing-basics.md); the J-cut and L-cut definitions are there too.

## Core Concepts

### Why Music Defines the Edit

Music gives the viewer permission to feel something before they understand why. A piece of footage with no emotional valence — a wide shot of a field — becomes melancholy, uplifting, or tense purely through the choice of music. The editor's job is to make the footage and the music feel like they were made for each other.

The emotional consequence of this: you can't always know what music to use until you've built a rough assembly of your footage. Experienced editors often watch the rough cut several times with different tracks to find what opens the footage up versus what fights it. The music selection is an edit decision, not a pre-edit administrative task.

---

### Reading the Beat — Cutting on the Music

Cutting on the beat means placing your edit points at rhythmically significant moments in the track — usually on a downbeat, an accent, or a transition in the music. When the cut lands on the beat, the viewer feels it as fluid. When the cut lands off the beat, it reads as accidental.

**How to find the beat in your NLE**:

In DaVinci Resolve and Premiere, drag your music track onto the timeline and use the waveform display to navigate. The peaks in the waveform correspond to beats and accents. Zoom in to the audio track to see the waveform clearly.

**Beat markers**: Most NLEs let you place markers. Listen to the track and tap a key (M in Resolve and Premiere) on each downbeat. You'll see a row of markers along the timeline — these become your cut targets. Build the sequence by placing clips so cut points align with the markers.

**Finding sync points**: A sync point is a specific moment in the music — a snare hit, a bass drop, a chord change, a silence — that creates a natural "landing" for a visual cut. The impact of an object hitting a surface, a door opening, a person stopping walking — these sync naturally to percussive moments in the music. Training your ear to hear sync points is what separates an editor who cuts to music from one who cuts with music.

### J-Cuts and L-Cuts as Rhythm Tools

The J-cut and L-cut (defined in [video-editing-basics.md](video-editing-basics.md)) become more powerful in music-driven editing.

**Music as the J-cut**: Bring the next music section in before the visual cut. This smooths the transition between sections and lets the music lead the edit rather than follow it. A common technique: the music changes from quiet to energetic two beats before the visual sequence transitions from slow to fast. The music sets the expectation; the visuals fulfill it.

**Letting music breathe**: Not every cut needs to land on a beat. A well-placed cut between beats, in a moment of quiet, can create weight and space. The contrast of a well-timed beat cut and an intentional off-beat cut makes both feel more deliberate.

### Choosing Music That Fits What You Shot

The mistake: picking music you like and cutting footage to it. The discipline: finding music that serves what the footage already is.

**Match tempo to pacing**:
- Fast-cut montage sequences need tracks with fast tempo (120+ BPM) and strong accents
- Slow, contemplative footage — landscape, process, still moments — needs space in the music: slower BPM, longer phrases, breathing room
- Talking-head sections need music that doesn't compete with voice — instrumental, mid-tempo, no prominent melody that the ear follows instead of the words

**Match emotional register**:
- Warmth vs. cool: major keys read warm, uplifting, open; minor keys read melancholy, tense, internal
- Energy vs. stillness: acoustic instrumentation tends to feel intimate; electronic and percussion-heavy music tends to feel kinetic
- Nostalgia: strings and piano read nostalgic; synthesizers read contemporary or futuristic

**The "write first, search second" approach**: Watch your rough cut, identify the emotion you want each section to produce, then search your music library using those adjectives. Most royalty-free platforms have mood and genre filters built in. "Uplifting instrumental" and "melancholy acoustic" will get you much closer than browsing randomly.

**When music fights the footage**: If a track feels off but you can't identify why, try: a different tempo (same mood, faster or slower), a different key (same tempo, major vs. minor), or silence. Sometimes footage that feels like it needs music actually works better without it.

### Royalty-Free Music Sources

"Royalty-free" means you pay once (subscription or per-track license) and can use the music without paying additional royalties per view or per use. It does not mean "free" and does not mean "no restrictions."

**Epidemic Sound** ($15/month personal, $49/month commercial): The largest and most creator-focused catalog. Their licensing is clear: subscribe, download, use in any video on any platform. No Content ID claims on videos while subscribed. The best catalog for YouTube and social media content creators. Unsubscribing after downloading does not remove the license from previously downloaded tracks.

**Artlist** ($199/year): Unlimited downloads, lifetime license for any track you download. Slightly more curated than Epidemic Sound — fewer tracks but higher editorial quality. The lifetime license is the key advantage: download tracks while subscribed, use them forever. Strong for cinematic and editorial content.

**YouTube Audio Library** (free): Google's free music library for YouTube videos. All tracks are free to use on YouTube with no Content ID issues. Quality varies widely; there are genuinely good tracks if you filter by mood and instrument. The go-to for budget-zero creators.

**Musicbed** ($19/month creator): Curated cinematic music, strong for commercial and documentary work. Higher average quality than Epidemic Sound; smaller catalog.

**Artgrid** ($149/year, sister platform to Artlist): Same license model as Artlist; focused on stock footage rather than music, but bundled options exist.

**Bandcamp / direct licensing from artists**: Some independent artists license directly through Bandcamp. Read their license terms — most are not automatically cleared for commercial YouTube use, but many artists are open to arrangement. Reach out directly. Better music, more friction.

### Content ID: What It Is and What Happens

Content ID is YouTube's system for identifying copyrighted material in uploaded videos. Rights holders (music labels, publishers, distributors) upload reference files to YouTube's database. When you upload a video, YouTube's system scans it against that database. If a match is found, a Content ID claim is placed on your video.

**What a claim does (by default)**:
- **Monetize**: The rights holder takes the ad revenue from your video. Your video stays up, you get nothing from ads on it.
- **Track**: The rights holder can see your viewership data.
- **Block**: In some cases, the video is blocked in certain countries (or globally) where the rights holder has distribution agreements.

**What a claim does NOT automatically do**: It does not take down your video, strike your channel, or result in a copyright infringement. A Content ID claim is not a copyright strike.

**Royalty-free services and Content ID**: The "royalty-free" services above handle Content ID differently:
- **Epidemic Sound**: Actively monitors and clears claims on behalf of their subscribers. If a claim appears, you submit a dispute through their platform and it's resolved. This is their explicit selling point.
- **Artlist**: Same — lifetime license includes Content ID clearance.
- **YouTube Audio Library**: By design, these tracks cannot generate Content ID claims on YouTube. Safe for YouTube; no guarantee for other platforms.
- **Independent music**: Even if you have a license, the rights holder may still have Content ID active. A license proves you're legal but doesn't stop the claim — you'd need to dispute it with documentation.

**How to dispute a claim**: If you receive a Content ID claim and you have a license, you dispute it through YouTube Studio → Content → Claims → dispute. Submit proof of your license (screenshot of your subscription, license document). Claims from Epidemic Sound and Artlist are typically resolved within 1–7 days.

### Music and Pacing: The Emotional Arc

A well-edited video has an emotional arc — it moves through different energy states. Music is the mechanism for creating that arc.

A typical structure:
- **Open quiet, build energy**: Start with atmospheric, quiet music to establish mood; transition to a higher-energy track as the video's momentum builds
- **Climactic moment**: Your most powerful footage matches your most impactful musical moment (a chorus, a build and drop, a key change)
- **Resolution**: Come back down — quieter music or silence for the ending

**Music transitions**: Abrupt music cuts usually feel like mistakes unless you're making a stylistic point. Three ways to transition between tracks:
1. **Hard cut on a beat**: Works if both tracks are in compatible tempo and key
2. **Fade out / fade in**: Sounds amateurish if both are present; works if you fade out fully before fading in
3. **Sound design bridge**: A sound effect or ambient noise covers the gap between tracks and masks the transition

**Silence as punctuation**: The absence of music is as deliberate as its presence. A moment of silence before a key reveal creates expectation. Silence after an emotional moment allows it to land. Don't fill every second with music.

## Common Mistakes

**Using popular songs.** Copyrighted commercial music will generate Content ID claims regardless of how you use it. Your video either loses revenue or gets blocked. Use royalty-free or license properly.

**Music too loud over voice.** Music that competes with dialogue is one of the most common errors. When someone is speaking, music should be felt, not heard — typically -20 to -30dB under voice. See [video-editing-basics.md](video-editing-basics.md) for the ducking workflow.

**Using the same track for the whole video.** A single track on loop for 8 minutes flattens the video emotionally. One track per section — or at minimum, vary the arrangement and energy within sections.

**Choosing music by genre, not emotion.** "Lo-fi hip hop" is a genre; "nostalgic and reflective" is what you're actually looking for. Search by how you want the viewer to feel.
