# POV and Daily Filming
*Wearable cameras, the continuous-capture mindset, and how to make first-person daily footage compelling*

---

## What Is It

POV filming is capturing first-person footage as you live — the camera sees roughly what you see, hands doing things, the world from your body's height and perspective. Daily filming is the practice of treating your life as a continuous documentary in progress, not waiting for interesting events to happen.

[vlogging-foundations.md](vlogging-foundations.md) covers the deliberate vlogging practice: picking up a camera with intention, composing shots, the 5-second B-roll hold rule. This file covers the different discipline of ambient and POV capture: wearable cameras designed to be forgotten, mount types, the mindset shift of continuous capture, and how to work with footage you didn't compose so much as collected.

## Core Concepts

### Wearable Camera Options

The wearable market has two distinct categories: action cameras (GoPro) optimized for athletic and outdoor use, and ambient clip cameras (Insta360 GO 3) optimized for effortless daily capture.

**GoPro Hero 13 Black ($400)**: The benchmark action camera. Waterproof to 10m without housing, excellent stabilization (HyperSmooth 6.0), records up to 5.3K 60fps. Largest battery of any GoPro. The primary use case is outdoor activity — surfing, cycling, hiking, farm work. Not ideal as a clip-on daily camera because it's bulky and the audio is unusable outdoors. Best combined with an external mic or a lapel mic going to a separate recorder.

**GoPro Hero 13 Mini ($200)**: Smaller and lighter, same sensor but fewer manual controls. Waterproof without housing. Good for activities where the Hero 13's size is a problem.

**Insta360 GO 3 ($340)**: The most practical daily carry camera. 26 grams, clips to a shirt collar or magnetic mount, records in 2.7K. The "Action Pod" doubles as a viewfinder and standalone camera with a flip-out screen. The defining characteristic: you forget it's there. Battery lasts about 45 minutes of continuous recording; the Action Pod extends this with a pass-through charging setup. Audio is acceptable for indoor capture; outdoor wind is a problem without a windscreen.

**Insta360 GO 3S ($380)**: Successor to the GO 3. Improved sensor (4K capture), better low-light performance, same form factor. The current recommendation for daily clip capture.

**DJI Osmo Action 5 Pro ($330)**: GoPro competitor with strong stabilization and better low-light than equivalently priced GoPros. Less established mount ecosystem.

### Mount Types and Placement

The mount determines what the footage looks like and how often you'll actually use the camera.

**Magnetic clip (Insta360 GO 3 / 3S native)**: The default for the GO 3. A magnetic clip on your shirt collar or lapel. The camera sits at chest-to-neck height, faces forward, and captures a first-person perspective with your hands visible in the lower third of the frame. This is the "I forgot I was wearing it" mount — it stays on all day.

**Chest mount (GoPro compatible)**: A harness worn across the chest, camera mounted at sternum level. Lower than the magnetic clip; the perspective shows more of your body. Good for hands-heavy work (cooking, building, mechanical work) where you want the viewer to see what your hands are doing. Uncomfortable over long periods; not for all-day wear.

**Head mount**: Camera at eye level, faces the direction you're looking. The truest POV perspective. Disorienting to watch for extended periods because of head movement. Best for short-duration, high-motion content (hiking, cycling) rather than daily documentation. Uncomfortable for all-day wear.

**Bike/handlebars mount**: For cycling footage specifically. Camera at handlebar height facing forward. The "road film" perspective. Also works on motorcycles, cars (suction mount on windshield or dashboard).

**Tripod / gorillapod for ground-up POV**: Not wearable, but relevant for crafts, cooking, and workshop content. A small flexible tripod placed on the workbench at working-hand height, angled up slightly, captures the cook's or builder's perspective. More intentional than wearable; less flexible.

### The Continuous-Capture Mindset

Deliberate vlogging (camera in hand, shooting with intention) and continuous capture are different disciplines and they require different thinking.

**Deliberate vlogging**: You pick up the camera when something worth capturing is happening. You compose the shot, hold for 5 seconds, get multiple angles. The quality of each clip is high. The coverage is episodic — you have great footage of specific moments and nothing between them.

**Continuous capture**: The camera rolls whether or not you think something interesting is happening. You're generating raw material without curation. The quality of individual clips is lower (framing is whatever the mount gave you). The coverage is dense — you have footage of the whole day.

The continuous-capture mindset requires a different relationship with footage. Most of it is unusable. You're looking for the moments that happened while the camera ran — the unscripted interaction, the process shot you'd never think to deliberately film, the moment of transition from one state to another. These often become the most authentic clips in a final edit precisely because they weren't composed.

**Practical constraints**:
- Battery: The GO 3S gets 45 minutes continuous; the GoPro Hero 13 gets 70–80 minutes. If you're out for 8 hours, you're either recharging (Hero 13 charges via USB-C while recording) or doing selective capture, not continuous
- Storage: At 4K, an hour of footage is 15–30GB depending on codec. An 8-hour day of 4K recording needs a terabyte. Use a lower resolution setting for ambient capture (1440p or 1080p) to manage storage
- Privacy and consent: Wearing a visible camera in public notifies people; a clip camera on your shirt collar is much less visible. Know your context

### What Makes First-Person Footage Compelling

Raw continuous POV footage is almost always unwatchable at full length. What makes it work as content is curation and structure.

**The ordinary moment captured unusually well**: A time-lapse of your morning commute is more watchable than your morning commute in real time. A 10-second clip of hands in soil — quiet, intimate, no narration — can carry weight that a talking-head explanation of garden work doesn't. POV footage works when it's either compressed (time-lapse, hyperlapse) or isolated to a specific moment that's visually specific and emotionally honest.

**Time-lapse and hyperlapse**: The GoPro Time Lapse mode and the Insta360 app both produce time-lapses from continuous capture. A 2-hour process compressed to 30 seconds — building something, cooking, a commute, a sunset — is one of the most accessible and watchable formats in daily vlogging. Shoot at 1 frame per 1–2 seconds for fast compression, 1 frame per 5–10 seconds for slower processes.

**Hands at work**: The most reliable category of compelling POV footage. Hands cooking, building, planting, writing, repairing — this is the material that makes process documentation interesting. The camera doesn't need to be doing anything clever; it just needs to be close enough to show what the hands are doing.

**The between-moments**: Footage of a car door closing, a door opening onto a new space, walking from one room to another — these transition clips are boring in isolation and become connective tissue in an edit. Continuous capture generates these naturally; deliberate filming doesn't because you never think to capture them.

### Day-in-the-Life Structure

The day-in-the-life format has a standard structure that works because it mirrors how people experience a day: a beginning with energy and anticipation, a middle with process and depth, an end with reflection or resolution.

**Three approaches:**

**Morning-to-night chronological**: Follow the day as it happened. The challenge is that real days have long slow stretches that kill pacing. Solve this with compression (time-lapses of slow sections) and narration (VO or to-camera commentary that moves the story forward faster than real time).

**Theme-based day**: The day is organized around one throughline — "everything I do before noon," "every meal I cooked this week," "a day on the property." The day-in-the-life format is the shell; the theme gives the editor a selection principle. Everything that serves the theme goes in; everything that doesn't, doesn't.

**Single-project focus**: Document one project across a day — building something, cooking an elaborate meal, doing a full maintenance job. The day-in-the-life is implied; the project is the story. This is the most structured approach and the one that most often produces compelling content from ordinary material.

### Audio with Wearables

Wearable camera audio is the hardest audio problem in video. The camera is on your body, not near your mouth; outdoors it's subject to wind; and you can't monitor levels while wearing it.

**GoPro built-in audio**: Usable indoors, unusable outdoors in any wind. The GoPro Hero 13 supports a media mod that adds a 3.5mm audio port and better mics — worth getting if you're doing interview or narration work with the GoPro.

**Insta360 GO 3S built-in audio**: Better than most action cameras for indoor clip capture. Still subject to cloth rustling if clipped to a lapel. Outdoors with any wind: use a foam windscreen.

**Separate audio recorder**: For any situation where you want quality audio separate from the wearable footage — record audio on a pocket recorder (Zoom H1n, $100) or the DJI Mic 2 with a separate receiver, then sync in post. More friction, significantly better results.

**The sync workflow**: Clap your hands (or use a clapper) once when starting a take — this creates a visible waveform spike in both the camera audio and the recorder that you can align in the NLE. After syncing, mute the camera's audio track and use the recorder track.

## Common Mistakes

**Recording everything, reviewing nothing.** Continuous capture produces footage you'll never watch if you don't build a review workflow. Set a rule: every 2–3 days, pull the cards, review at 2–4× speed, pull the 3–5 clips that are worth keeping, delete or archive the rest. Don't let footage accumulate without selection.

**Buying a wearable for "authentic" footage without developing an eye.** Authentic doesn't mean automatically compelling. The edit still requires selection, structure, and judgment. The camera's job is to generate raw material; the editor's job is to find the story in it.

**Neglecting the battery planning.** A wearable camera with a dead battery captures nothing. Know your camera's battery life, carry a USB-C powerbank for in-field charging, and develop a habit of charging overnight.
