# Storytelling for Video
*Structure, narrative arc, B-roll, and how to make a vlog feel like a story rather than a recording*

---

## What Is It

A recording is footage of something that happened. A story is footage arranged so the viewer cares about what happens next and feels something at the end. The difference is not production quality — it's structure and intent.

Most vlogs are recordings. The camera was on; things happened; it got edited down. The footage exists but there's no question the viewer is reading toward, no tension, no moment of change. The result is a video that people watch for a few minutes and abandon — not because the life being documented isn't interesting, but because it was never made into a story.

This file covers the structural decisions that turn footage into a story. The underlying frameworks (three-act structure, dramatic question, character arc) live in [story-structure.md](../writing/story-structure.md). [vlogging-foundations.md](vlogging-foundations.md) covers what to shoot. This file covers how to think narratively about what you're making before and after you shoot.

## Core Concepts

### The Dramatic Question in Nonfiction Video

Every story has a central question the viewer is reading — or watching — toward. In fiction, this is explicit. In nonfiction video, it often isn't stated, but it must exist.

**The question takes a form**: *Will X happen? Can Y be done? What happens when Z?* The viewer's engagement is their investment in the answer.

For a vlog, the question can be small: "Will this recipe work?" "Can I fix this thing?" "What does a day of doing X feel like?" Small is fine. What's not fine is no question at all — a video where nothing is at stake and nothing could go wrong.

**Setting the question in the first 30 seconds**: This is the hook's job. Don't open with a title card and music and then slowly set up context. Open with the question or the stakes: "I'm going to try to do X today, and I genuinely don't know if it's going to work." The viewer now has a reason to keep watching.

**The promise/payoff contract**: Whatever you imply in the opening, you must deliver by the end. A video that opens with a high-tension question and resolves it unsatisfyingly (or forgets to resolve it) trains viewers not to trust your hooks.

### Structure for Video: The Three-Act Skeleton

[story-structure.md](../writing/story-structure.md) covers the three-act structure in full. Applied to video:

**Act 1 — Setup and question** (~15–25% of video length): Establish the situation and state the question. Who are you, what are you doing, and why does it matter? The setup should be fast — viewers arrive with zero investment and abandon quickly. Get to the question before they leave.

**Act 2 — Process, complication, and tension** (~50–60% of video length): This is where most vlogs live exclusively, and where most vlogs fail. A series of things happening isn't Act 2 — Act 2 has escalation. Something should get harder, or a complication should arise, or the stakes should become clearer. Without escalation, the middle sags and the viewer drifts.

*The midpoint shift*: Something changes around the halfway point — a setback, a discovery, a decision point. This keeps the middle from being a flat line between opening and resolution.

**Act 3 — Resolution and reflection** (~15–25% of video length): Answer the dramatic question. What happened? What did it mean? This doesn't have to be grand — "it worked and here's what I learned" is a complete Act 3. What it can't be is missing: videos that end by simply stopping (the footage ran out) leave the viewer with nothing to carry away.

### The Hook — The First 30 Seconds

Retention data consistently shows the steepest viewer drop in the first 30 seconds. The hook's job is to answer the viewer's implicit question: "Is this worth my time?"

**Hook structures that work in video:**

**Open on the most interesting moment** (Tarantino structure): Start in the middle of the most compelling part of the video, then cut back to the beginning and build to it. "Here's what happened — let me show you how we got here." This works because the most interesting moment is usually buried in Act 2 or Act 3, not the setup. You show it first to earn the viewer's patience through the setup.

**State the problem explicitly**: "I tried to do X and it failed. This is what I'm going to do about it." The problem is the dramatic question made explicit. This works best for process, repair, and challenge content.

**The contrast open**: Show the end state and the start state in the first 15 seconds, without explanation. The contrast creates the question: "How did we get from that to this?" This is how most home renovation and cooking content opens.

**What doesn't work as a hook**: Music and title card. Long introduction of yourself to an audience that hasn't yet decided to stay. Explaining backstory before establishing stakes.

### B-Roll as Narrative

B-roll's function in [video-editing-basics.md](video-editing-basics.md) is described as visual variety — cutting over narration to avoid a static talking head. But B-roll has a narrative function beyond that: it can advance the story rather than merely illustrate it.

**Illustrative B-roll**: Footage of what's being talked about. You mention making pasta; there's a shot of pasta. This is fine but passive — it confirms rather than adds.

**Narrative B-roll**: Footage that reveals something the narration doesn't state. A shot of your hands hesitating before picking up a tool, while the narration says "I wasn't sure this would work" — the hesitation in your hands shows it. The B-roll is doing emotional work the words can't.

**Transitional B-roll**: Footage that moves the story through time. A time-lapse of setup, a sequence of arriving at a location, a series of close-ups showing a process proceeding. These compress time and give the edit momentum without narration.

**The B-roll scene**: A sequence of B-roll with no narration — just imagery and sound. When done well, these are the most cinematic moments in a vlog. Three to five shots, each 3–5 seconds, with natural sound. No talking. Let the images breathe. These moments create texture and signal that the creator knows how to be quiet.

### Voice-Over Narration

Narration recorded separately and cut over footage is one of the most powerful tools in video storytelling — and one of the most underused in vlogging because it requires writing.

**When to use it**: When the footage tells a story that the on-camera talking doesn't; when you need to compress time while maintaining narrative continuity; when you want a reflective tone that in-the-moment footage can't provide.

**Writing narration for video**: Narration written to be read sounds different from narration written to be heard. The rules:
- Short sentences. The voice needs to breathe and the viewer needs to absorb.
- Conversational, not formal. You're talking, not writing an essay.
- Narration should say what the footage can't show — not describe what's already visible.
- Let the footage speak when it can; use narration only when it adds.

**The narration draft process**: Watch the rough cut with the audio muted. Write down what story the images tell on their own. Then write narration that adds what the images can't convey — context, interiority, stakes, time compression. Narration that just describes what we're already seeing is redundant and reduces impact.

### The Emotional Arc

A video that stays in one emotional register for its entire length is a flat video. Emotional movement — quiet to energetic, uncertainty to resolution, tension to relief — is what makes a video feel like it went somewhere.

**Map the emotional beats before editing**: Watch your rough assembly. Note where you want the viewer to feel each emotion. If you have 20 minutes of footage and want to make a 6-minute video, the cutting question is: which clips advance the emotional arc?

**The energy curve**: Most watchable vlogs follow a curve — medium energy at the open, building through the middle, peak at the climax, then a deliberate comedown at the end. This isn't a rule, it's a pattern that works because it mirrors how we experience satisfying experiences.

**Relief and reward**: After tension — a moment of genuine lightness, success, or humor. These moments are disproportionately memorable. Viewers retain the beats where the tension broke more than the tension itself. Build in these moments deliberately.

---

### Editing for Story vs. Editing for Coverage

Most editing tutorials teach coverage editing: get the talking-head narration in order, add B-roll, clean up audio. This produces a competent video. Story editing is different.

**Story editing questions**:
- Does each section advance the dramatic question or deepen the viewer's investment?
- Is there a moment in the video where the stakes get higher? Where is it?
- What is the viewer feeling at the midpoint? Is it different from the opening?
- What do you want the viewer to think or feel after the video ends? Does the ending produce that?

**Cut for momentum, not documentation**: The instinct is to include everything that happened to be accurate. The story discipline is to include only what moves the story. A 3-hour day of footage where 90% of the story lives in 45 minutes should produce a 6–10 minute video, not a 30-minute documentary.

## Common Mistakes

**No dramatic question.** The viewer is watching footage of things that happened with no sense of what might happen next or whether it matters. State the stakes. Something must be at risk, even if it's small.

**Too much setup before the question.** Opening with 2 minutes of context and introduction before establishing what the video is about. Invert this: question first, context as needed after.

**The "wall of talking head."** Narration for an extended period with no B-roll, no visual change. The ear follows the story; the eye gets bored and the viewer leaves. Three to four minutes of talking head without visual variation is the outer limit for most audiences.

**Ending by stopping.** The footage ran out; the editor cut to black; the video ends. A story needs a resolution — however brief. One sentence of reflection, one final image, one moment that marks the video as complete rather than interrupted.

**Forcing drama.** Manufactured tension ("I don't know if this is going to work...!" — of course it works, the video exists) is transparent and erodes trust. Real stakes — even small, real ones — are more compelling than inflated fake ones.

## Resources

- *Every Frame a Painting* (YouTube, finished series): Video essays on film craft — pacing, editing rhythms, how directors create feeling. Each episode is a masterclass in visual storytelling applied to short-form video.
- *Story* by Robert McKee — the full framework; overkill for vlogging but the source of the principles that the shorter approaches derive from
- *Peter McKinnon* (YouTube) — the clearest current example of the story-edit approach in daily vlogging; study how his videos set a question, build it, and resolve it within 8–12 minutes
