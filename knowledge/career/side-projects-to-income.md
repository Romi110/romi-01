# Side Projects to Income
*Turning knowledge and skills into revenue — building optionality, not burning yourself out*

---

## What Is It

A side project that generates income is a second income stream built outside your job, using skills or knowledge you already have. The goal isn't to hustle yourself into the ground or build a startup — it's to create financial optionality: more choices about where, how, and whether you work.

The difference between a side hustle and a side project with staying power: one trades time for money directly (freelancing for dollars), the other builds an asset that eventually generates income without linear time input (a product, an audience, a system). Both are valid, but only the second one compounds.

This file is practical: what actually works, what skill profiles map to what income types, and how to start from zero without quitting your job.

## Why It Matters

A single income stream means your financial life has one point of failure — one employer, one salary, one decision that can change everything. A second income stream, even small, changes the psychology of work: you make decisions from more options, not fewer.

Stoic framing: this isn't about accumulating. It's about building resilience. Financial independence is a form of self-reliance — what Romi already values in food, energy, and practical skills. Income works the same way.

At 28, in tech, in NJ: you have the most valuable combination for this — high base salary (runway to experiment without desperation), technical skills (the highest-value category for independent work), and enough work experience to know what you're actually good at.

## Core Concepts

### The Income Type Spectrum

**Time-for-money (linear)**: freelance contracts, consulting by the hour, tutoring. You work, you get paid, you stop working and income stops. Fastest path to first dollar, but doesn't scale.

**Productized service (semi-linear)**: a defined scope, fixed price, repeatable delivery. "I build X for $Y, delivered in Z weeks." Less custom than freelancing, easier to sell repeatedly. Scales until you can hire or systematize.

**Digital products (asymmetric)**: something built once, sold many times. Templates, courses, e-books, tools, SaaS. Zero marginal cost per unit after creation. Slowest path to first dollar — requires an audience or distribution to sell — but scales without additional time.

**Content/audience (long-tail)**: writing, video, newsletters, podcasts. Builds an audience that becomes the distribution for everything else. Very slow to monetize. Only makes sense if you'd do it regardless — treating it as a primary income strategy from day one is how most people burn out.

**Equity in something (very long-term)**: advising for equity, building a product with a co-founder, angel investing. Riskiest, most illiquid, highest ceiling.

For most people starting from zero: freelance or productized service first (fastest to income), then use that runway to build something asymmetric.

### The Skill Audit — What's Worth Money

Not all skills are equally monetizable. The useful question isn't "what do I know?" but "what would someone pay to have done for them or learn from me?"

**High-value categories for a tech professional:**

| Skill | Direct income path | Asymmetric path |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Software development | Freelance contracts, code review | SaaS products, dev tools, templates |
| System design / architecture | Technical consulting, CTF advisory | Courses, architecture guides |
| AI/Claude integration | Build automations for small businesses | Prompt packs, agent templates, tools |
| Data skills (SQL, Python, analysis) | Analytics consulting, BI buildout | Templates, dashboards, courses |
| Writing clearly | Technical writing contracts | Newsletter, content, book |
| Teaching / explaining complex things | Tutoring, workshops | Courses, YouTube |

**Romi's specific profile:**
- Tech professional with AI experience (the hottest consulting category right now)
- Deep farming/self-reliance knowledge (unusual combination in tech — people building homesteads are hungry for this)
- Stoic/philosophy knowledge base (coaching-adjacent, content-adjacent)
- Systems thinking across domains

The AI + technical skills path is the highest near-term dollar return. The farming + self-reliance knowledge is the most differentiated and has a passionate audience that pays well for real expertise.

### What Actually Works vs. What Gets Sold

**What works:**
1. **Sell to people who already have money and already spend it.** Businesses pay more than individuals. B2B is easier to monetize than B2C. A small business paying $500/month for an automation you built beats selling a $20 template 25 times.
2. **Start with your existing network.** First clients almost always come from people who already know your work. LinkedIn, former colleagues, professional contacts. Cold outreach has a very low conversion rate.
3. **Do one thing first.** Most failed side projects try to build an audience, write content, sell a product, and offer services simultaneously. Pick one path and stay on it until you have your first $1,000, then your first $500/month.
4. **Charge more than feels comfortable.** Most people starting independent work charge too little. Rate yourself at what your outcome is worth to the buyer, not what your time feels worth to you.

**What gets sold but rarely works:**
- Drop shipping, print-on-demand, affiliate marketing — very low margins, extremely competitive, oversaturated with beginner guides that make it seem more accessible than it is
- Courses before you have an audience — a course with no distribution is a document no one reads
- Crypto/NFT adjacent income — the window for most people has closed; what remains is primarily extractive
- "Build an audience first" as a strategy — content creation before you have income means burning time for years before seeing returns

### The Practical Starting Path for Romi

**Phase 1 — First $1,000 (1–3 months)**

The fastest path to first dollar for a tech professional:

*Option A — AI/automation consulting for small businesses:*
Small businesses (contractors, restaurants, local services, real estate agents) have heard about AI and don't know how to use it. You do. A $500–1,500 project to set up a Claude-powered workflow, automate a repetitive task, or build a simple tool is a completely achievable scope. Reach out to 10 people in your network, explain what you can do in one sentence, offer a small scoped project.

*Option B — Technical freelance (known quantity):*
If you've shipped software, there are companies that will pay you to freelance on specific problems. Upwork has volume but low rates. Better path: direct outreach to companies you know or former employers, or contract through a technical staffing firm for 1099 work. Even 10 hours/month at $75–150/hr is $750–1,500/month.

**Phase 2 — $500/month recurring (3–12 months)**

Recurring means the income doesn't stop when you stop working that week. Two viable paths:

*Retainer model:* a small business paying you $500–1,500/month to maintain a system you built, manage their data, or be their "tech person on call." One retainer client is better than five one-off projects.

*Small product with distribution:* a Notion template, a Claude prompt pack, a workflow automation template sold through Gumroad. Requires distribution — but if you build an audience around the farming + tech combination (genuinely rare), even a small email list (500–1,000 people who care) converts to recurring product income.

**Phase 3 — Asset building (1+ years)**

Once the first income is flowing:
- Document the systems you've built for clients → productize them
- Write about what you've learned → attracts more clients and potentially audience
- Look for SaaS opportunities in the gaps you keep solving manually

---

### The Time Constraint Is Real

With a full-time tech job and evenings shared with a partner: 5–10 hours per week is the realistic maximum. This is enough for phases 1 and 2. It is not enough to build a startup.

**Protecting the constraint:**
- Don't say yes to scope creep. Defined deliverables protect your time.
- Asynchronous-first. Client communication by email and async video (Loom), not open-ended calls.
- One project at a time until you have cash flow established.

## Key Considerations

**The day job is the foundation, not the enemy.** A stable tech salary is the financial runway that lets you take intelligent risks on the side. Don't blow up the job for an income stream that makes $500/month. Build incrementally.

**$500/month recurring is the real milestone.** It's not retirement money, but it changes how you feel about your job. You have a second option. The psychological shift from that milestone is disproportionate to the dollar amount.

**The combination is the moat.** Tech skills are common. Farming knowledge is common. Someone who deeply understands both and can explain either clearly is unusual. Build the combination — it creates an audience that exists nowhere else.

**Taxes: 1099 income changes your tax situation.** Self-employment income is subject to self-employment tax (15.3%) on top of income tax. The silver lining: legitimate business expenses are deductible, and you can open a Solo 401k or SEP-IRA to shelter self-employment income — high limits, major tax advantage. ## Getting Started

1. List 5 skills you have that a business or specific person would pay for.
2. Pick the one with the highest $/hr potential and clearest "who pays for this."
3. Write one sentence describing what you do and for whom. Test it on 3 people.
4. Reach out to 10 people in your network. Not to sell — to say you're doing this and to ask if they know anyone who'd benefit.
5. Do the first project at whatever rate feels slightly uncomfortable to say out loud.

## Common Mistakes

**Waiting until it's "ready."** The product, the website, the portfolio — none of it matters before you have paying clients. First client always comes through a relationship, not a website.

**Charging by the hour for everything.** Hourly pricing caps your income and focuses the client on time rather than results. Productize: "I'll build X for $Y" is better than "$100/hour."

**Building a course before an audience.** A course is a distribution problem. Without an email list or audience that trusts you, a course sits unsold. Build the audience first, even if small.

**Saying yes to bad-fit clients because it's income.** Clients who want unlimited revisions, want to scope-creep, or who pay slowly are not worth the energy. The standard for a second client should be higher than the first, not lower.

**Burning out by trying to do it all at 110%.** A side project that costs you sleep and recovery isn't building optionality — it's just a second job. Sustainable pace, defined hours, or it doesn't last.
