# Supply Chain Resilience
*How global supply chains were built for efficiency and became fragile — where the vulnerabilities are and what you can actually do about them*

---

## What Is It

A supply chain is the full sequence of steps required to get a product from raw material to end consumer — mining, processing, manufacturing, assembly, shipping, distribution. Global supply chains are the invisible infrastructure of modern life: the reason a T-shirt costs $15, a phone costs $800 instead of $8,000, and you can buy fresh strawberries in January in New Jersey.

The problem: this infrastructure was optimized almost entirely for cost and efficiency, with essentially no redundancy. When it works, it's extraordinary. When it fails — pandemic, geopolitical disruption, natural disaster, war — the failure cascades in ways that are difficult to predict and slow to fix.

This file covers how we got here, where the specific vulnerabilities sit, and what resilience looks like at the personal and household level.

## Why It Matters

You experienced a preview of supply chain failure between 2020 and 2023: empty shelves, months-long waits for cars and appliances, semiconductor shortages, baby formula scarcity, toilet paper hoarding. These were disruptions to an otherwise functioning global system. The structural vulnerabilities that produced them haven't been fixed — they've been slightly reduced at the margins.

For someone building self-reliance: the goal isn't to opt out of the global supply chain entirely (that's neither practical nor necessary). It's to reduce your dependence on the most fragile nodes — the things most likely to fail, most difficult to substitute, and most consequential when they do.

## Core Concepts

### How Global Supply Chains Were Built — The Optimization for Efficiency

**Comparative advantage** (Ricardo, 1817): Countries should specialize in what they produce most efficiently and trade for the rest. This produces more total output than if everyone tries to make everything domestically. The theory is correct in a stable, peaceful world with reliable trade.

**The post-WWII trade order:** Bretton Woods, GATT (later WTO), and US-led globalization created the institutional framework for free trade. Tariffs fell, trade volumes exploded, and manufacturing migrated to wherever it was cheapest.

**Just-in-time (JIT) manufacturing:** Toyota's production system, refined in the 1970s–80s and adopted globally, minimized inventory. Parts arrive precisely when needed; warehouses are kept empty; capital isn't tied up in stock. This is extraordinarily efficient in normal conditions. In disrupted conditions, there's no buffer — any supply disruption immediately halts production.

**The China factor:** China's entry into the WTO (2001) opened the lowest-cost manufacturing base in history to global brands. Companies that didn't offshore lost cost competitiveness to those that did. Over two decades, manufacturing for thousands of product categories concentrated in China — not just assembly, but the supplier networks, specialized components, and tacit knowledge that make assembly possible.

**The result:** By 2020, the global supply chain was lean, efficient, geographically concentrated, and deeply interdependent — with almost no slack, almost no inventory buffers, and significant geographic concentration in a single country with increasing geopolitical tension with its largest customer.

### The Critical Chokepoints

**Semiconductors — the most acute vulnerability**

Modern civilization runs on chips. Cars (hundreds of chips each), appliances, phones, weapons systems, data centers, medical equipment — nothing complex works without semiconductors. And the most advanced semiconductor manufacturing is concentrated in one company, in one island, that China has explicitly claimed as its sovereign territory.

- **TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company)** fabricates ~92% of the world's chips at the most advanced nodes (sub-5nm). No one else can make leading-edge chips at scale.
- **ASML (Netherlands)** makes the extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines required to manufacture leading-edge chips. There is only one company in the world that makes these. Each machine costs ~$380 million and takes a year to build.
- **SMIC (China's domestic fab)** is roughly 1–2 generations behind TSMC and cannot yet make the chips that power AI or high-end consumer electronics.
- **The US CHIPS Act** is funding domestic fab construction (TSMC Arizona, Intel, Samsung Texas) but leading-edge domestic capacity won't be meaningful until 2027–2030.

**Rare earth minerals — China's upstream leverage**

Rare earth elements (REEs) — 17 metals including neodymium, dysprosium, cerium — are essential for electric motors (EVs, wind turbines), electronics, defense systems, and batteries. China produces ~60% of global REEs and processes ~85% of global supply. Even REEs mined elsewhere are typically sent to China for processing.

The US had almost no domestic REE processing capacity until recently (Mountain Pass mine in California was reopened; processing is still largely offshore). A supply disruption would affect EV motors, wind turbines, guided missiles, and consumer electronics simultaneously.

**Pharmaceuticals — a hidden vulnerability**

~80% of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) — the chemical compounds in medications — are manufactured in China or India. This includes generic drugs, antibiotics, blood pressure medications, insulin precursors, and many hospital essentials.

The US has almost no domestic API manufacturing. A disruption in Chinese or Indian pharma supply would affect hospitals, pharmacies, and medication availability within weeks.

**Food — more resilient but with fragile nodes**

The US produces enormous quantities of food domestically — this is a genuine strength. But:
- Fertilizer inputs (potash, nitrogen, phosphate) have significant geographic concentration — Russia and Belarus were major potash exporters before 2022 sanctions
- Pesticide and herbicide precursor chemicals are largely manufactured in China
- Seed technology has consolidated into a handful of companies; the diversity of crop genetics in commercial agriculture is lower than it should be
- Processing infrastructure has concentrated — four companies process ~85% of US beef; disruption to one major processing facility has outsized effects

**Energy — now a US strength, but with caveats**

The US became a net energy exporter (~2019) — a major structural change from the oil-dependent 1970s. But:
- Refined product infrastructure (refineries, pipelines) is aging and has been underinvested
- The electrical grid is a patchwork of regional systems with limited interconnection; it is increasingly stressed by weather events
- Natural gas is now exported as LNG in significant volumes — domestic prices are now connected to global LNG prices, which can spike with geopolitical events

### COVID as a Supply Chain Stress Test

The 2020–2023 disruptions revealed the specific failure modes:

- **Bullwhip effect:** Small demand shocks at the end of the supply chain get amplified as each upstream player adjusts orders. A 10% demand increase at retail became 40% order increases from manufacturers, 80% order increases from component suppliers — leading to massive over-ordering, then the inventory glut of 2022–23.
- **Single source dependency:** Products that had a single supplier — or that depended on a single component with a single supplier — halted entirely. Cars couldn't be finished because of a $5 chip. Baby formula supply collapsed because of FDA action against one of three domestic manufacturers.
- **Geographic concentration:** Lockdowns in Shenzhen didn't just affect Shenzhen — they affected every product that relied on components assembled there.
- **Logistics chokepoints:** Container shipping, port capacity, and trucking have thin margins and minimal surge capacity. When disrupted, congestion propagates through the entire network.

### The Reshoring Effort — Progress and Limits

Since 2020 (accelerated by the CHIPS Act, IRA, and bipartisan concern about China dependency), there has been genuine movement toward domestic manufacturing:

**Real progress:**
- TSMC fab in Arizona (2nm fab announced for 2028)
- Intel domestic expansion
- Battery gigafactories (Samsung SDI, LG Energy Solution, Panasonic) being built in the US
- Pharmaceutical API manufacturing incentives
- Ally-shoring to Mexico, India, Vietnam for lower-risk manufacturing

**The limits:**
- Semiconductor manufacturing expertise takes decades to develop — you can build the fab but the workforce and supplier ecosystem takes longer
- Unit costs of US-manufactured goods are higher than Asian equivalents — the economics of reshoring require sustained government support or tariffs
- Supply chains are interdependent — you can move final assembly but the sub-components often still come from the same sources
- A decade is the realistic timeline for meaningful domestic semiconductor independence, not 2–3 years

### Personal and Household Supply Chain Resilience

The practical question: what does this mean for your household, and what can you actually do?

**Food buffer — the highest-return preparation**

A household with 1–3 months of staple food supply has enormous resilience against both supply chain disruptions and personal financial shocks (job loss, medical expense, unexpected costs). This isn't a doomsday bunker — it's basic household inventory management.

- Staples with long shelf life: rice, lentils, oats, canned beans, dried pasta, canned tomatoes, cooking oils, salt, spices
- Rotate stock — first in, first out — so nothing expires unused
- Combined with a food garden ([farming/starting-from-scratch.md](../farming/starting-from-scratch.md)), a household can cover the majority of caloric needs through local production plus stored buffer

**Energy buffer**

A basic portable power station ([energy/portable-power-stations.md](../energy/portable-power-stations.md)) covers essentials (refrigeration, communication, medical devices, lighting) through a grid outage. A full solar + battery system removes the household from grid vulnerability entirely for normal use.

Galloway, NJ is within driving distance of multiple hurricane tracks and experienced significant grid disruption from Sandy (2012). Grid resilience is local and practical, not abstract.

**Medical supply buffer**

- A 60–90 day supply of any essential prescription medications (ask your doctor for a 90-day prescription)
- Basic first aid supplies beyond a typical medicine cabinet: wound care, trauma kit components, OTC antibiotics (fish antibiotics are the prepper workaround — legally sold, same compounds as human antibiotics, but not FDA-approved for human use; a judgment call)
- Knowledge of basic first aid and emergency response reduces dependence on overwhelmed medical systems during disruptions

**Reduce single points of failure in your own systems**

- Multiple income sources > single employer dependency
- Diverse food sources > single grocery store dependency
- Skills that enable substitution > helplessness when the usual option fails
- Relationships with neighbors who have different skills > individual isolation

**What not to stockpile**

Focus on: calories, water filtration, medical, energy, and communication. Skip: weapons (high cost, rarely decisive in realistic scenarios), exotic survival gear, anything that requires you to bug out (your home is your best asset). The realistic disruptions are: grid outage for days to weeks, supply shortages for specific categories for weeks to months, not societal collapse.

## Key Considerations

**Resilience is not the same as autarky.** You don't need to grow all your food, make your own clothes, and generate all your power to be resilient. You need enough buffer and enough skills that disruptions are inconveniences rather than crises.

**The reshoring trend is real but slow.** The US is genuinely trying to reduce the most dangerous supply chain vulnerabilities — semiconductors especially. This takes a decade or more to matter. Don't assume the problem is solved because the CHIPS Act passed.

**Corporate supply chains are already adapting.** Major manufacturers are moving from just-in-time to "just-in-case" — carrying more inventory, diversifying suppliers, nearshoring. This makes the system marginally more resilient at the cost of somewhat higher prices. This trend has legs.

**The most resilient individuals are embedded in communities.** A neighborhood where people know each other, have different skills, and have informal mutual aid relationships is more resilient than any individual household stockpile. See [civics/local-politics-civic-involvement.md](../nj/local-politics-civic-involvement.md).

## Getting Started

1. Inventory your household's critical dependencies: medications, food supply on hand, ability to function without grid power for a week. This takes 20 minutes and tells you immediately where your vulnerabilities are.
2. Build a one-month food buffer over the next 30 days — buy extra staples each grocery trip until you have 30 days of rice, lentils, oats, canned goods, cooking oil on hand.
3. Get a portable power station with enough capacity to run your refrigerator for 48 hours and charge essential devices. ~$300–500 for entry-level (EcoFlow River 2 Pro or similar).
4. If you take prescription medications, get a 90-day supply on hand.

## Common Mistakes

**Stockpiling without rotating.** Food stores that expire unused are wasted money and space. Buy what you eat, eat what you store, rotate continuously.

**Focusing on dramatic scenarios.** The realistic supply chain disruption is: a specific product category is unavailable for 4–12 weeks, prices are elevated, and substitution requires flexibility. Not: everything disappears and you need 5 years of food in a bunker.

**Treating this as a one-time preparation.** Resilience is a maintenance activity — rotating stock, testing backup power, keeping medication supplies current. A one-time preparation that isn't maintained degrades.
