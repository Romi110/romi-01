# Economic Statecraft
*Using economic tools as instruments of national power — the strategic framework that sanctions, export controls, and financial exclusion all operate within*

---

## What Is It

Economic statecraft is the deliberate use of economic tools — trade, investment, finance, technology access, and resource control — to achieve political and security objectives. It is coercion without the kinetic costs of war: compelling or deterring adversary behavior, punishing violations of international norms, rewarding alignment, and building dependency relationships that create leverage.

It is not the same as trade policy (which optimizes for economic outcomes) or development finance (which builds relationships) — though both overlap with it. The distinguishing characteristic: economic statecraft is deployed primarily to produce political effects. The economic outcome is secondary; the behavioral change in the target is the objective.

This file is the strategic framework that several more specific files operate within: [geopolitics/dollar-weaponization.md](dollar-weaponization.md) covers the dollar's specific role; [geopolitics/energy-geopolitics.md](energy-geopolitics.md) covers energy as leverage; [geopolitics/intelligence-and-power.md](intelligence-and-power.md) covers how economic espionage interacts with intelligence.

## Why It Matters

The 21st century's major power competition is being fought primarily through economic means. US semiconductor export controls on China, Russian gas supply to Europe, Chinese rare earth controls during territorial disputes with Japan, Iranian oil sanctions, Russian exclusion from SWIFT — these are the primary instruments of current great power conflict. Understanding economic statecraft is essential for understanding what is actually happening in geopolitics and where it is heading.

## Core Concepts

### Hirschman's Original Framework

Albert Hirschman's *National Power and the Structure of Foreign Trade* (1945) is the intellectual foundation. Hirschman observed that trade creates two kinds of leverage:

**Supply effect**: trading partners that depend heavily on a single supplier can be coerced by the threat of supply disruption. European natural gas dependence on Russia gave Russia leverage — until Europe diversified.

**Trade share effect**: if Country A accounts for a large share of Country B's total trade, Country B has strong incentives to accommodate Country A's political preferences to preserve the relationship. The asymmetric dependence creates political influence.

The implication: states can deliberately structure trade relationships to create dependency — and the target of this structuring can either accept the dependency (and the associated leverage) or diversify at economic cost. China's Belt and Road Initiative is Hirschman's insight applied at scale: create infrastructure dependencies that lock trading partners into Chinese supply chains, creating political leverage the host country cannot easily exit.

The US has run a version of this for decades through dollar denomination, market access, and technology dependence. The current conflict with China is partly a recognition on both sides that these dependencies have become tools of mutual coercion.

### Sanctions: What They Are and When They Work

Sanctions are the most common form of economic statecraft. They involve restricting trade, investment, financial transactions, or asset access for a target country, entity, or individual.

**Types of sanctions:**

*Comprehensive sanctions* target an entire country — trade embargo, asset freeze, financial exclusion. Cuba, North Korea, and Iran under full sanctions. These are maximally coercive but also maximally costly to implement (allies may not comply, alternatives emerge) and to the target's civilian population rather than its decision-makers.

*Targeted (smart) sanctions* focus on specific individuals, entities, or sectors. Freeze the assets of regime officials; ban specific goods; restrict specific banks. Designed to change behavior while limiting civilian harm. The theory: hurt the decision-makers, spare the population.

*Secondary sanctions* penalize third parties — non-US companies that do business with the sanctioned entity. These extend US enforcement power beyond US jurisdiction, forcing foreign banks and companies to choose between US market access and the sanctioned relationship. They are enormously effective and enormously unpopular with allies who see them as extraterritorial coercion.

**When sanctions work:**
The academic evidence (Hufbauer, Schott, Elliott) suggests sanctions succeed in achieving their stated objectives roughly 30-35% of the time — better than critics claim, worse than proponents assume. They work better when:
- The goal is modest (signaling displeasure, not regime change)
- The sender is a primary trade partner with few alternatives
- Implementation is multilateral (targets can't route around a single sender)
- The target has a domestic constituency that experiences the pain (a functioning economy that feels coercive pressure)

**When sanctions fail:**
- When the target can find alternative partners (China will buy Iranian oil)
- When the sender values the relationship too much to implement fully (agricultural exemptions undermine agricultural sanctions)
- When the target's decision-makers are insulated from the pain
- When the goal is regime change — sanctions have essentially never produced regime change; they've hardened authoritarian control and created political economies dependent on sanction evasion

**The Iran paradox**: 40+ years of comprehensive US sanctions have not changed Iranian behavior on nuclear development or regional intervention. The sanctions have hurt ordinary Iranians, enriched the Revolutionary Guards (who control the smuggling economy), and demonstrated the limits of coercion without viable military alternatives or genuine multilateral implementation.

### Export Controls and Technology Denial

Export controls restrict the transfer of goods, technology, and knowledge to foreign actors. The stated rationale is preventing military technology transfer; the strategic application is broader.

**Dual-use problem**: most advanced technology has both civilian and military applications. Semiconductors power both smartphones and precision weapons. Advanced machine tools manufacture both cars and missile components. Export control regimes (the US Export Administration Regulations, the Wassenaar Arrangement multilaterally) attempt to restrict the military applications without blocking civilian commerce — a distinction that becomes more difficult to maintain as technology advances.

**The US semiconductor campaign against China** (2022–present) is the most significant technology denial operation since the Cold War. The Biden administration used export control authority to:
- Block Chinese acquisition of advanced semiconductors (sub-7nm)
- Block Chinese access to equipment used to manufacture advanced chips (ASML's EUV machines)
- Block US persons from working in Chinese chip facilities

The objective is to prevent China from achieving the semiconductor manufacturing capability that would underpin military AI, hypersonics, electronic warfare, and space systems. The challenge: China is investing massively in domestic semiconductor production; the question is whether the controls can hold long enough to create a durable gap.

The second-order problem: export controls by a single country are ineffective if allies don't implement them. The US has pressured the Netherlands (ASML), Japan (Tokyo Electron), and others to align. But allied compliance depends on their economic interests, which are not identical to US security interests.

### Investment Screening

Foreign investment can transfer technology, create strategic dependencies, and enable intelligence operations. Investment screening regimes review foreign acquisitions and investments for national security risk.

**CFIUS** (Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States): reviews foreign acquisitions of US companies for national security implications. Has blocked Chinese acquisitions of semiconductor firms, hotel chains (for the intelligence value of their data), and telecom infrastructure. Has required divestiture of existing investments when security risks were identified.

**The TikTok problem**: TikTok is a case study in the intersection of investment screening, data security, and information operations. ByteDance (Chinese parent) collects data on 150M US users and operates an algorithm that shapes what 150M Americans see. The US government has been unable to force divestiture because ByteDance has resisted and the legislative/regulatory tools are imprecise. Theoretical risk: Chinese government access to the data; algorithm manipulation as an information operation. Counter-argument: no documented evidence of either happening at scale.

**Critical infrastructure scrutiny**: Chinese investment in ports (Long Beach, examined), telecom infrastructure (Huawei's 5G exclusion from US networks), power grid equipment, and farmland near military bases has triggered screening reviews. The concern is not just current ownership but the intelligence and potential disruption capabilities built into infrastructure during construction.

### Financial Exclusion

Control of financial infrastructure is the most powerful form of economic statecraft because it is hardest to route around — or was, until the weaponization became obvious enough to trigger diversification responses.

**SWIFT exclusion**: SWIFT is the messaging system that coordinates international bank transfers. Excluding a country from SWIFT effectively prevents it from conducting international financial transactions. Russia was partially excluded from SWIFT in March 2022. The impact was significant but incomplete: India, China, and others continued clearing transactions through alternative mechanisms, and Russia adapted to bilateral clearing arrangements.

**Correspondent banking**: most international financial transactions flow through a small number of major global banks (JPMorgan, HSBC, Citibank). These banks' dependence on US regulatory approval makes them unwilling to process transactions involving sanctioned entities — the threat of losing US banking licenses is existential. This "de-risking" extends US financial coercion well beyond the reach of formal sanctions.

**The dollar's structural advantage**: see [geopolitics/dollar-weaponization.md](dollar-weaponization.md) for full analysis. The key point: dollar denomination of global trade means that dollar-clearing transactions must pass through US-accessible financial infrastructure. This gives the US visibility and leverage over global financial flows that no other currency provides.

**The backlash dynamic**: every deployment of financial coercion accelerates efforts to build alternatives. Russia and China have developed alternatives to SWIFT. Central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) are being developed by 130+ countries partly to reduce dollar dependence. Saudi Arabia is accepting non-dollar payment for oil. The more aggressively the US weaponizes the dollar, the faster the alternative infrastructure develops.

### Resource Control and Critical Materials

States control access to critical materials as a form of leverage.

**Chinese rare earth controls**: China produces ~60% of global rare earths and processes ~85% — materials essential for permanent magnets in electric motors, wind turbines, EV drivetrains, and advanced weapons. China has used rare earth export restrictions during territorial disputes (Japan, 2010) as implicit coercion. The vulnerability is real; the US, Australia, and others are investing in alternative production precisely to reduce it.

**The critical minerals competition**: lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese — the materials for battery technology. China has dominant positions in processing and in African mining operations. The US Inflation Reduction Act (2022) included substantial incentives specifically to develop non-Chinese supply chains for these materials. This is economic statecraft in defensive mode: reducing dependency before it becomes leverage.

**Energy as leverage**: Russia's gas supply to Europe was the most dramatic demonstration of resource leverage in recent history — and of its limits. Supply leverage requires willingness to use it; using it destroys the relationship and accelerates the target's diversification. Russia weaponized gas supply, Europe diversified, and Russia lost its primary leverage instrument in Western Europe permanently.

### Coercive Economic Architecture

The advanced version of economic statecraft is not specific tools but the deliberate construction of structural dependencies:

**China's Belt and Road Initiative** builds infrastructure across Asia, Africa, and Latin America with Chinese financing, often at terms that create debt dependence. The Hambantota Port (Sri Lanka) was built on Chinese loans; when Sri Lanka couldn't repay, China took a 99-year lease. "Debt-trap diplomacy" — the deliberate creation of leverage through unsustainable financing — is contested as a systematic strategy but the dependency dynamics are real.

**The Quad and economic security coalitions**: the US, Japan, Australia, and India are building alternative supply chains specifically to reduce Chinese leverage in critical technology and materials. This is anti-Hirschman: deliberately creating redundancy to eliminate the dependency that creates leverage.

## Key Considerations

- Economic statecraft has a fundamental tension: the tools that create the most leverage also accelerate the target's efforts to build alternatives. The more the US weaponizes dollar access, the faster non-dollar settlement infrastructure develops.
- Multilateral sanctions are dramatically more effective than unilateral ones. A target can route around one sender; routing around a coordinated coalition is exponentially harder.
- Secondary sanctions are the US's most powerful tool and the most corrosive to alliance relationships. Allies comply because they have to; they resent it and build alternatives.
- The effectiveness of economic coercion depends heavily on the target's regime type. Democracies with real economies feel pressure quickly; authoritarian regimes can insulate their decision-makers and manage civilian pain.

## Resources

- *National Power and the Structure of Foreign Trade* — Albert Hirschman (1945). The original framework. Technically a dense economic text; the key chapters are accessible.
- *Economic Statecraft* — David Baldwin. The systematic academic treatment: definitions, historical cases, evaluation criteria.
- *The New Tools of Economic Warfare* — Elizabeth Rosenberg et al. (CNAS report). Current application, focused on US tools post-2014. Free online.
- *Chokepoints* — Edward Fishman. The most current account of how the US deploys financial and technology statecraft against China and Russia.
