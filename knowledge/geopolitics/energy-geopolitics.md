# Energy Geopolitics
*How oil, gas, and the energy transition shape which countries hold power*

---

## What Is It

Energy geopolitics is the study of how control over energy resources — oil, natural gas, nuclear, and increasingly critical minerals — determines geopolitical leverage. Countries that produce energy others depend on can convert that dependency into political influence, military deterrence, or economic coercion. Countries that lack domestic energy become structurally constrained in their foreign policy.

The core dynamic: modern economies run on energy. Whoever controls the supply, the price, or the infrastructure through which energy moves holds a form of power that can't easily be replaced in the short term.

## Why It Matters

The petrodollar system — oil priced in US dollars — is one of the structural foundations of US global dominance since 1974. Understanding it explains the Federal Reserve's power, why the US military maintains presence in the Middle East, why sanctions against Iran or Russia work (or don't), and why China's de-dollarization efforts are a direct strategic challenge. The energy transition is actively reshaping this — and the reshaping isn't clean or linear.

For personal context: energy prices affect inflation, which affects real wages and purchasing power. Geopolitical disruptions in oil or LNG (like the 2022 Ukraine war) ripple into gasoline prices, heating bills, and supply chains within months.

## Core Concepts

### The Petrodollar System

In 1971, Nixon ended the Bretton Woods gold standard — dollars could no longer be exchanged for gold at a fixed rate. This should have destroyed confidence in the dollar. It didn't, because in 1974 Kissinger negotiated a deal with Saudi Arabia: OPEC would price oil exclusively in US dollars, and the US would provide military security guarantees to the Gulf states.

The result: every country on Earth that imports oil must hold US dollars to buy it. This creates permanent demand for dollars worldwide, letting the US run trade deficits without the normal currency punishment, borrow cheaply, and project military power globally (financed by dollar demand). The petrodollar is the real basis of US hegemony — not democracy, not culture, not military alone.

Any move away from dollar-priced oil is a direct attack on this system. This is why Saudi Arabia flirting with yuan-priced oil sales to China in 2023 received US diplomatic attention immediately.

### OPEC and Cartel Mechanics

OPEC (Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries, founded 1960; OPEC+ added Russia and others in 2016) is an oil production cartel. Its power comes from spare capacity — the ability to increase or decrease production to influence global prices.

- **Price weapon**: 1973 oil embargo against the US (for supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War) caused a 4x price spike and US gasoline rationing. This demonstrated that oil dependency could be used as direct political coercion.
- **Cartel limits**: Members cheat on quotas constantly. Internal divisions (Iran vs. Saudi Arabia, ideological and sectarian differences) weaken unified action. The shale revolution reduced US dependence on OPEC imports dramatically after 2010.
- **OPEC+ leverage now**: Reduced compared to 1973-1990 peak, but still significant for setting a price floor. Saudi Arabia's fiscal break-even (~$80/barrel) effectively sets a soft floor below which they cut production.

### The LNG Revolution and European Vulnerability

Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) — natural gas chilled to liquid form for shipping — created a global gas market where previously gas was locked into regional pipeline networks. This matters because:

- **Russia's pipeline leverage**: Europe built deep dependence on Russian pipeline gas (40%+ of EU supply before 2022). This gave Russia veto power over European winters and made strong sanctions politically painful for European governments.
- **Ukraine war as energy shock**: Russia's 2022 invasion triggered a massive scramble. Europe fast-tracked LNG import terminals, signed long-term contracts with US/Qatar LNG suppliers, and accelerated renewables buildout. Russia lost its main European customer permanently.
- **US as LNG exporter**: The US became the world's largest LNG exporter in 2023. This is geopolitically significant — the US can now supply allies' gas needs, reducing Russian and Middle Eastern leverage over European and Asian allies.

### The Energy Transition and New Chokepoints

The shift from fossil fuels to solar, wind, and EVs doesn't eliminate energy geopolitics — it relocates it.

- **Critical minerals**: Solar panels need silicon and silver. Wind turbines need neodymium (rare earth). EV batteries need lithium, cobalt, and nickel. China controls 60%+ of global cobalt refining, 80%+ of rare earth processing, and significant lithium supply chains. Trading oil dependence (Middle East) for mineral dependence (China) is a structural risk if the transition happens faster than supply chains can diversify.
- **China's strategic position**: China built this dominance deliberately over 20 years through Belt and Road investments in African mining nations (DRC cobalt, Zambia copper, Zimbabwe lithium). The US, EU, and Japan are now scrambling to develop alternatives — but mineral processing infrastructure takes 10-15 years to build.
- **Petrodollar erosion**: As EVs displace oil demand (slowly — oil demand won't collapse for decades, but growth peaks), the structural demand for dollars weakens at the margin. This is a long-term process, not a near-term rupture.
- **Nuclear resurgence**: With recognition that renewables intermittency requires backup baseload, nuclear is back in political favor in France, UK, US, Japan, and even Germany is reconsidering. Small modular reactors (SMRs) are the new technology bet.

### The Gulf States' Transition Problem

Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, and Qatar know the oil era ends eventually. Their sovereign wealth funds (Saudi's PIF, UAE's ADIA and Mubadala) are how they're converting oil wealth into diversified assets before demand peaks. Saudi Vision 2030 is an attempt to build a post-oil economy. The challenge: political legitimacy in Gulf monarchies rests partly on oil-funded social spending. Without oil revenue, domestic politics become unpredictable.

## Key Considerations

**Oil demand won't collapse in a decade.** Even optimistic EV scenarios show oil demand peaking around 2030 and declining slowly — aviation, shipping, petrochemicals, and developing world growth absorb enormous volumes. The Middle East remains geopolitically central for at least another generation.

**Geography still determines energy leverage.** Straits of Hormuz (20% of global oil passes through), Strait of Malacca (most of Asia's energy imports), the Turkish Straits (Black Sea to Mediterranean) — physical chokepoints can't be routed around quickly. Military control of these chokepoints is a permanent strategic asset.

**Energy sanctions are a blunt instrument.** Sanctioning Russian oil pushed it to India and China at a discount. Europe paid more for the same volume elsewhere. Sanctions hurt Russia but didn't isolate it — they reshuffled trade flows. The lesson: energy sanctions work better against small producers than against large ones with alternative buyers.

**The transition benefits countries with renewable resources.** Sun, wind, and geothermal aren't geographically evenly distributed, but they're more widely distributed than fossil fuels. Morocco, Chile, Australia, and the US Southwest could become energy exporters via green hydrogen. Countries with both sun/wind AND mineral resources (Chile, Australia, DRC) have significant transition-era advantages.

## Getting Started

1. **Understand the petrodollar first** — [reserve-currency-history.md](reserve-currency-history.md) covers the Bretton Woods collapse and the mechanics that followed. The petrodollar is a direct continuation of that story.
2. **Read on the 1973 oil crisis** — it's the case study for how energy becomes a weapon. Daniel Yergin's *The Prize* is the definitive history.
3. **Track China's mineral strategy** — follow critical minerals news (lithium, rare earths, cobalt) to watch the transition geopolitics unfold in real time.
4. **Map the chokepoints** — look at a world map of the Strait of Hormuz, Strait of Malacca, Turkish Straits, and Suez Canal. The geography explains why the US maintains naval presence in those regions.

## Common Mistakes

**Thinking the energy transition ends geopolitics.** It relocates it from oil fields to mining regions and refining capacity. China's mineral dominance is the next version of the same game.

**Underestimating how long oil transitions take.** The 1973 oil shock didn't end oil dependence — it accelerated nuclear and North Sea development, but oil remained central. Peak oil demand in 2030 still means enormous oil trade through 2060+.

**Confusing the petrodollar with conspiracy.** The petrodollar system is documented policy, not theory. The 1974 Kissinger-Saudi deal and its mechanics are in declassified documents and mainstream economics literature.

**Missing the Gulf states' rational play.** Saudi Arabia isn't blind to transition risk — Vision 2030 and the PIF are serious attempts to diversify. Whether they succeed is uncertain, but it's not passivity.
