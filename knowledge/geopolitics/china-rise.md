# China's Rise
*How China became the world's second economy, where it's headed, and what it means for the global order you live inside*

---

## What Is It

China's rise is the defining geopolitical story of the past 40 years. From a largely agrarian economy with GDP per capita below sub-Saharan Africa in 1980, China grew into the world's second-largest economy (first by purchasing power parity), the world's largest manufacturer, largest trading nation, and a peer military competitor to the United States — in roughly one generation.

This file covers the model that produced that growth, the structural constraints China now faces, the specific flashpoints that matter (Taiwan, debt diplomacy, technology), and what the US-China rivalry means practically for someone in the US building a self-reliant life over the next 20 years.

## Why It Matters

The things you buy are largely made in China. The components in the things you buy — even if assembled elsewhere — often contain Chinese inputs. The financial markets you invest in are increasingly correlated with US-China relations. The supply chain vulnerabilities in [geopolitics/supply-chain-resilience.md](supply-chain-resilience.md) are primarily about China dependency. The dollar's reserve status discussed in [geopolitics/us-empire-decline-preparation.md](us-empire-decline-preparation.md) is being challenged largely by China-driven alternatives.

You don't need to have an opinion on China to need to understand it. It is structurally woven into the economy you live inside.

## Core Concepts

### The Growth Model — How It Actually Worked

China's growth was not a market miracle or a communist success story — it was a specific development model, borrowed in part from Japan and the Asian Tigers (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore), and executed at unprecedented scale.

**The core mechanism:**
1. **Suppress domestic consumption** — Keep wages low, currency undervalued, maintain high savings rates through limited social safety nets (people save because there's no pension or healthcare backstop)
2. **Direct capital toward industry** — State-controlled banks lend at below-market rates to state-owned enterprises (SOEs) and favored industries; capital misallocation is tolerated in exchange for scale and speed
3. **Export at competitive prices** — The undervalued currency makes Chinese exports cheap globally; the world buys; foreign exchange reserves accumulate
4. **Absorb technology** — Through joint venture requirements (foreign companies must partner with Chinese entities to access the market), IP theft, talent recruitment, and acquisition
5. **Infrastructure investment** — Build the physical infrastructure (roads, ports, rail, power) that makes manufacturing competitive

This model produced ~10% annual GDP growth for three consecutive decades — the most sustained rapid development in human history. It also created structural distortions that are now constraining further growth.

**What the model produces:** Enormous manufacturing capacity, large foreign exchange reserves, vast infrastructure, rising middle class — and also: massive debt (particularly at the local government and corporate level), over-investment in real estate and infrastructure relative to actual demand, demographic decline, and political authoritarianism that resists the institutional reforms needed to transition to a consumer-led economy.

### The Transition Problem China Can't Solve

Every successful export-led development model eventually needs to transition: once you've caught up to the technology frontier and wages have risen, you can't compete on cheap labor anymore. Japan made this transition (imperfectly — the 1990s "lost decade" was part of the adjustment). South Korea largely made it. China has not successfully made it, and the window may be closing.

**The middle income trap:** Countries routinely stall out at middle-income levels because transitioning from export-led to domestic consumption-led growth requires: higher wages (which reduces export competitiveness), social safety nets (which enable people to spend rather than save), strong rule of law and property rights (which enable entrepreneurship), and political liberalization (which China's leadership actively resists because it threatens party control).

**Debt:** China's total debt (government + corporate + household) is estimated at 280–300% of GDP. The local government financing vehicle (LGFV) problem is particularly acute: local governments, which fund themselves partly through land sales, borrowed massively through off-balance-sheet vehicles to build infrastructure and real estate. As property prices fall (Evergrande was the first visible symptom, not the last), land sale revenues collapse, and the debt becomes unpayable. Beijing has been managing this slowly rather than letting it blow up — but the debt doesn't disappear, it gets socialized.

**Demographics:** China is aging faster than almost any country in history — the consequence of the one-child policy. The working-age population peaked around 2015. By 2050, China will have roughly 400 million people over 60 — a dependency ratio that will strain the economy and the state for decades. The phrase in China: "getting old before getting rich." This is a structural constraint on growth, consumer spending, and military capacity that no policy easily reverses.

**Xi Jinping's consolidation:** Xi's reversal of Deng Xiaoping's liberalizing reforms — re-centralizing party control over business, cracking down on the technology sector (Ant Group, Didi, tutoring companies), elevating ideology over technocracy — has reduced the dynamism of the private sector that drove China's most innovative growth. This is a choice with costs: it trades economic efficiency for political control.

### Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)

China's infrastructure diplomacy program, launched 2013 — hundreds of billions of dollars in infrastructure loans and construction projects across Asia, Africa, Latin America, and Eastern Europe. Often described as "debt trap diplomacy."

**What it actually is:** Partially strategic (securing resource access, ports, and diplomatic influence), partially economic (keeping Chinese construction companies employed with excess capacity), partially genuine development finance. The "debt trap" narrative is contested — there's limited evidence of China deliberately seizing assets in exchange for unpayable debt (Sri Lanka's Hambantota port is the most cited case; evidence it was a planned trap is thin). But the loans are often opaque, non-concessional, and tied to Chinese labor and materials — which limits the development benefit to host countries.

**The strategic reality:** BRI gives China relationships, resource access (particularly African minerals and Middle Eastern oil), and alternative trade routes that don't depend on US-controlled sea lanes. It also gives China leverage over developing nations' votes in international institutions. This is genuine strategic competition with US influence, particularly in Africa and Southeast Asia.

### Taiwan — The Most Dangerous Flashpoint

Taiwan is the most consequential geopolitical risk in the world right now. A war over Taiwan would disrupt the global economy more severely than any event since World War II.

**Why Taiwan matters to China:** The Chinese Communist Party considers Taiwan a breakaway province whose reunification is a core party legitimacy claim. Xi Jinping has staked his legacy on it and has removed the implicit prior commitment to peaceful reunification without a timeline.

**Why Taiwan matters globally:**
- Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) fabricates ~90% of the world's most advanced semiconductors (sub-5nm chips). Every advanced AI chip, smartphone, military system, and data center depends on TSMC. There is no substitute that could be built in less than a decade.
- A Chinese takeover of Taiwan would give China effective control over the global semiconductor supply — leverage over every modern economy with no parallel in history.
- A war over Taiwan would trigger the most severe global supply chain disruption imaginable, potentially worse than the semiconductor shortage of 2021 by orders of magnitude, indefinitely.

**What a Taiwan conflict would look like:** Not necessarily a ground invasion (which China lacks the clear amphibious capability to execute against a defended island). More likely: blockade, missile strikes on infrastructure, economic coercion. Even a blockade scenario would halt semiconductor exports — TSMC has contingency plans to destroy fabs rather than let them fall into Chinese hands.

**Timeline:** Most serious analysts put the elevated risk window at 2027–2035. Xi has reportedly told the PLA to be ready by 2027. This doesn't mean war is inevitable or probable — deterrence remains strong, and a Taiwan war would also damage China enormously — but it is a meaningful tail risk, not a remote one.

**What you can do about Taiwan risk:** Almost nothing directly. Awareness helps with financial positioning: overconcentration in tech stocks that depend on TSMC (Nvidia, AMD, Apple, etc.) is a risk worth knowing about. The semiconductor reshoring efforts (TSMC fabs in Arizona, Intel expansion, CHIPS Act) are partly about this risk — they'll take a decade to matter.

### De-Dollarization and the US-China Currency Competition

China's most sustained geopolitical effort beyond military is reducing dollar dependency in global trade. Specific moves:

- **Petroyuan:** China has negotiated with Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states to price some oil exports in yuan. Progress is slow — the Saudis still prefer dollars — but the direction is clear.
- **CIPS (Cross-Border Interbank Payment System):** China's alternative to SWIFT, the US-controlled interbank messaging system. Still small relative to SWIFT but growing.
- **BRICS currency discussions:** Recurring proposals for a BRICS trade settlement currency or gold-backed instrument. So far more rhetoric than reality — India and China don't trust each other enough to share a currency.
- **Digital yuan (e-CNY):** China's central bank digital currency, partly designed to enable cross-border transactions that bypass the dollar system.

**The honest assessment:** The dollar is not being displaced in the near term. China's capital markets are not open enough, the yuan is not freely convertible, and no alternative has the depth and liquidity to replace dollar-denominated global trade and savings. But the direction of travel — a gradual, multi-decade erosion of the dollar's global share — is being actively driven by China, and the structural incentives for the rest of the world to reduce dollar dependency are real, especially after the US weaponized the dollar against Russia (SWIFT exclusion, asset freezes) in 2022, which alarmed every non-Western central bank.

### US-China Decoupling

Since roughly 2018 (Trump tariffs) through the current period, the US and China have been partially decoupling their economies — not fully, but in strategically important sectors.

**What's actually decoupling:**
- Semiconductors: US export controls on advanced chips and chip-making equipment to China (October 2022 and expanded since) are the most significant economic weapon deployed. China cannot currently produce advanced chips domestically; the export controls attempt to freeze that gap.
- Telecom: Huawei excluded from 5G networks in the US, UK, and most allied countries
- Investment: restrictions on US investment in Chinese AI, quantum computing, semiconductors
- Supply chains: government pressure and incentives to move manufacturing to "friend-shoring" countries (India, Vietnam, Mexico)

**What is not decoupling:** Consumer goods, basic manufacturing, many agricultural flows. China remains the world's factory for most non-sensitive goods and this doesn't change quickly.

**The implication for the next 20 years:** Two partially separate technology stacks are developing — a US-aligned tech ecosystem and a China-aligned one. Countries increasingly have to choose which stack to use for 5G, AI infrastructure, payment systems. This is the "splinternet" and splintered supply chain in slow motion.

## Key Considerations

**China is not going to collapse, but it is not going to dominate either.** The triumphalist narrative ("this is the Chinese century") and the collapse narrative ("property bubble will take it all down") are both wrong for the same reason: China's problems are real and structural, but China also has enormous resilience — 1.4 billion people, state capacity to manage crises, massive industrial base, and the political will to absorb pain that democratic systems often can't sustain.

**The most likely scenario:** A Chinese economy growing more slowly than the past 40 years, aging rapidly, geopolitically assertive but constrained by economic problems — a more difficult but still major power. A peer competitor that erodes US dominance without replacing it. A multipolar world rather than a Chinese century.

**For you specifically:** Semiconductor exposure, dollar concentration, supply chain dependency, and community resilience are the levers you can actually move. The Taiwan scenario is the tail risk worth knowing about but not worth panic-positioning against.

## Getting Started

1. Read *The Changing World Order* by Ray Dalio — covers the China-US power transition in the context of historical reserve currency cycles.
2. Follow the Semiconductor Industry Association (SIA) reports on chip supply chain geography — the most concrete and measurable version of this risk.
3. Assess your investment exposure to companies with heavy China manufacturing dependency or China revenue concentration (Apple: ~20% China revenue; Nike; many consumer brands).

## Common Mistakes

**Treating China as a monolith.** There are factions within the CCP, regional variation, and significant internal debate about economic policy. Xi's consolidation is real but so is the technocratic tradition he's suppressing. China is complex.

**Assuming the relationship is zero-sum.** US-China economic interdependence remains enormous even as decoupling proceeds. A full decoupling would damage both economies. Neither side wants that — the competition is about leverage, not destruction.

**Underestimating Taiwan risk.** Most people assume Taiwan will muddle through because it always has. The change in China's leadership posture under Xi is real; the previous "peaceful reunification, no timeline" position is functionally gone.
