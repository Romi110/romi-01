# Taiwan Strait
*The most consequential potential conflict of the next decade — a war that would disrupt global semiconductor supply overnight and force a US-China confrontation neither side has fully planned for.*

---

## What Is It

The Taiwan Strait is the 180km body of water separating Taiwan from mainland China. Taiwan is a self-governing democracy of 23 million people that Beijing considers a breakaway province. The People's Republic of China has never controlled Taiwan — the island has been governed independently since 1949 when Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalist government retreated there after losing the Chinese Civil War.

Taiwan matters geopolitically for two distinct reasons:

**Semiconductors**: Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) fabricates approximately 90% of the world's most advanced chips (below 5nm process). Every leading-edge processor — Apple's M-series, NVIDIA's AI GPUs, AMD's data center chips — is made in Taiwan. A conflict that disrupted TSMC's fabs would be the most severe supply shock in modern economic history, dwarfing COVID.

**Strategic geography**: Taiwan sits at the center of the First Island Chain — a string of islands from Japan through the Philippines that constrains Chinese naval access to the Pacific. Control of Taiwan would give China's navy unrestricted Pacific access, fundamentally changing the Indo-Pacific military balance.

The practical stakes: a Taiwan conflict — even a blockade without full invasion — would:
- Eliminate most of the world's advanced chip supply for 1-3+ years
- Trigger the largest financial sanctions ever imposed (against the world's second-largest economy)
- Crash global trade and financial markets
- Force every country to choose a side, accelerating bloc formation
- Potentially draw in the US militarily

This isn't inevitable, but it's the scenario serious strategists treat as the defining risk of the 2025-2035 decade.

## Core Concepts

### Why China Wants Taiwan

Beijing's position on Taiwan is not purely strategic — it's existential to the legitimacy of the Chinese Communist Party. Xi Jinping has repeatedly called "reunification" a historical inevitability and a prerequisite for the "great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation." Taiwan's existence as a prosperous democracy governed by ethnic Chinese is an implicit refutation of the CCP's claim that Chinese people need authoritarian governance to succeed.

Beyond ideology, Taiwan's strategic value is real:
- Pacific access for the PLAN (People's Liberation Army Navy)
- Removal of a US ally and potential basing location from China's near abroad
- Control of the semiconductor industry as geopolitical leverage

### The Military Balance

China has spent 30 years building military capabilities specifically designed for a Taiwan contingency:
- **Missile forces**: The PLA Rocket Force has thousands of ballistic and cruise missiles that can reach Taiwan and US bases in Guam, Japan, and South Korea within minutes
- **Naval buildup**: China now has the world's largest navy by ship count, with significant amphibious assault capability
- **Air force**: J-20 stealth fighters, long-range bombers, increasingly capable air-to-air missiles
- **Anti-access/area-denial (A2/AD)**: Designed to keep US carrier groups far enough from Taiwan that they can't effectively intervene

The US retains significant advantages: superior submarine fleet, more experienced carrier aviation, better C4ISR (command, control, communications, computers, intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance), and the alliance network (Japan, Philippines, Australia, South Korea).

The honest assessment: a Taiwan conflict in 2025-2030 is not a clear US win. Most war games conducted by CSIS, RAND, and US military planners result in massive losses on both sides. The US can likely prevent a successful invasion — at substantial cost — but a blockade is harder to counter.

### The Taiwan Strait as the World's Chokepoint

Taiwan processes ~90% of leading-edge chips (below 5nm). TSMC's fabs are clustered in Hsinchu, Taichung, and Tainan — all within range of Chinese missiles. The industry cannot be moved quickly:

- A new fab takes 3-5 years and $20B+ to build
- The specialized equipment (ASML's EUV lithography machines — 80% of which go to TSMC) is made in the Netherlands, takes 2 years to deliver, and can't be mass-produced faster
- The process knowledge — the "recipe" — is embedded in thousands of engineers who trained in Taiwan over decades

TSMC is building fabs in Arizona (US), Kumamoto (Japan), and Dresden (Germany). These will eventually reduce concentration risk. But the Arizona fab makes chips that are 2 generations behind TSMC's leading edge, and won't be at scale until 2027-2030 at the earliest. The window of maximum vulnerability is now through ~2030.

A conflict that damaged or halted TSMC's leading-edge fabs would create a chip shortage that makes the 2021-2023 shortage look trivial. Automotive, aerospace, consumer electronics, data centers, military systems — all halt simultaneously.

### Scenarios: What Could Actually Happen

**Scenario 1 — Blockade/Coercive pressure**: China establishes a naval blockade of Taiwan, cutting off imports and exports, without a full invasion. Designed to force capitulation without triggering a formal US military response. A blockade is an act of war under international law, but it's more ambiguous than missiles and landing craft. The US would face intense pressure to respond while avoiding a direct military confrontation with a nuclear power.

**Scenario 2 — Salami-slicing escalation**: China gradually escalates — more aggressive military exercises, seizure of smaller offshore islands (Kinmen, Matsu), increased air incursions — without triggering a bright-line US military response. Designed to shift facts on the ground incrementally. Already happening at low levels.

**Scenario 3 — Forced political resolution**: Economic coercion, combined with subversion and political influence operations, forces Taiwan's government to accept some form of "one country, two systems" arrangement. Requires no military action if successful. China's preference if feasible.

**Scenario 4 — Full invasion**: Combined arms assault across the strait — missile strikes, airborne forces, amphibious landing. Highest-risk, highest-reward scenario. Requires significant PLA success in neutralizing Taiwan's defenses and deterring US intervention within a narrow window. Most costly scenario for everyone. The scenario war planners focus on.

**Scenario 5 — Indefinite status quo**: Neither side forces the issue. Taiwan remains autonomous, both sides maintain ambiguity. This has been the stable equilibrium since 1979. It may continue indefinitely if neither side concludes the costs of action are acceptable.

### The 2027 Timeline Question

Chinese military analysts and US intelligence assessments frequently cite 2027 as a significant date. This is the 100th anniversary of the PLA's founding — a date Xi has referenced in the context of "rejuvenation" goals. It also aligns with when PLA modernization milestones are expected to be largely complete.

This does not mean war in 2027. It means China will have the capability to consider it by then, if political and strategic conditions favor it. Whether those conditions materialize depends on: Taiwan's political trajectory, US credibility and military posture, and internal Chinese politics.

The more important variable may be Taiwan's 2024 election results — which produced a third consecutive DPP (pro-independence) president — and Xi's domestic political standing. A leader facing internal pressure may be more likely to create external crises.

### US Strategic Ambiguity

For decades, US policy has been "strategic ambiguity" — selling weapons to Taiwan and committing to its security, without explicitly committing to military defense. The one-China policy acknowledges (but does not endorse) Beijing's position that Taiwan is part of China.

Biden made multiple statements suggesting the US would defend Taiwan militarily; the White House walked each one back. Trump's position has been more transactional — demanding Taiwan pay more for its own defense, implying the US commitment is conditional.

Strategic ambiguity was designed to deter both a Chinese attack (uncertainty about US response) and a Taiwanese declaration of independence (uncertainty about US support). Whether it still deters China effectively as China's military capability grows is genuinely contested among strategists.

### Economic Entanglement as Deterrent and Accelerant

The US-China economic relationship is the largest bilateral trade relationship in history. US and Chinese financial systems are intertwined. Major US technology companies derive 15-30% of revenue from China.

This entanglement is a deterrent — the mutual economic pain of a conflict is enormous. But it also creates leverage: whoever is more willing to absorb the economic pain has more coercive power.

If the US imposes SWIFT-style sanctions on China (as it did to Russia), China would respond with seizures of US corporate assets in China, export controls on critical minerals (China controls 60%+ of rare earth processing), and potential dumping of US Treasuries. The economic weapons are real on both sides.

## Key Considerations

**The TSMC self-destruction protocol**: Taiwan has reportedly prepared contingency plans to sabotage TSMC's fabs if a Chinese takeover appears inevitable. China is aware of this. It creates a situation where a successful Chinese military seizure of Taiwan might not deliver the semiconductor prize — the facilities would be destroyed. This is a significant deterrent to invasion specifically (less so to blockade).

**Japan is the most exposed US ally**: Japanese territory sits at the northern end of the First Island Chain. Japanese bases are critical to any US military response. China's missiles can reach every Japanese base. Japan's decision to rearm significantly in 2022-2024 is a direct response to this vulnerability — and a signal of how seriously Tokyo takes the Taiwan risk.

**Philippines re-engagement**: Under Marcos Jr., the Philippines reversed Duterte's pivot to China and granted the US access to four additional military bases, several facing Taiwan directly. This materially improves US military positioning for a Taiwan contingency.

**Korea complication**: Any Taiwan conflict risks North Korea using it as cover for provocations on the Korean peninsula, potentially drawing South Korean and US forces in two directions simultaneously.

**The nuclear dimension**: Both the US and China are nuclear powers. Any direct military confrontation carries escalation risk. This is the ultimate deterrent — and also the reason some analysts believe the US would not directly intervene militarily even if China invaded. The risk of nuclear escalation may constrain the US response in ways that conventional war games don't capture.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating it as a binary war/no-war question**: The more likely near-term scenarios are coercion and salami-slicing, not full invasion. The question isn't just "will there be a war" but "how much pressure does Taiwan face and how does that affect global systems."

**Underestimating the economic consequences of a non-military scenario**: A sustained blockade that stopped Taiwan's exports for 6 months would be economically devastating without a single US-China military exchange.

**Assuming US military intervention is certain**: US policy is deliberately ambiguous. A Trump administration's willingness to defend Taiwan militarily is genuinely less clear than previous administrations. China is almost certainly stress-testing this ambiguity.

**Confusing chip reshoring with solving the problem**: TSMC's Arizona fab is real progress. But it will take until 2028+ to produce leading-edge chips at scale, and it's one facility vs. dozens in Taiwan. Reshoring reduces risk; it doesn't eliminate it within the relevant timeframe.
