# Intelligence and Power
*How states gather, process, and weaponize information — and why intelligence shapes geopolitical outcomes more than most people realize.*

---

## What Is It

Intelligence in the geopolitical sense is the organized collection and analysis of information to give decision-makers an edge. It is not spying in the Hollywood sense — it is a systematic process: collect raw information, analyze it, produce assessments, deliver them to policymakers. The cycle repeats constantly.

Every major state maintains intelligence services. The differences are in collection methods, analytical depth, willingness to conduct covert operations, and how information gets weaponized beyond its defensive use.

## Why It Matters

Almost every major geopolitical outcome in the last century was shaped by intelligence — what was known, what was missed, and what was deliberately planted. The Cuban Missile Crisis was resolved partly because U-2 photos gave Kennedy certainty about what was in Cuba. The Iraq War was launched on intelligence assessments that were wrong. Russia's 2022 invasion calculations were based on their intelligence about Ukrainian will to fight — which turned out to be completely mistaken. The CIA's covert operations reshaped governments from Iran to Chile to Afghanistan.

Understanding the intelligence layer makes [geopolitics files](.) legible at a different depth. State decisions that look irrational from the outside often make sense given private intelligence — or are disasters because that intelligence was wrong.

## Core Concepts

### The Intelligence Cycle

The standard framework for how intelligence is produced:

1. **Planning and direction**: decision-makers (presidents, generals, ministers) identify what they need to know. "What is China's intention regarding Taiwan in the next 12 months?" This drives collection priorities.

2. **Collection**: raw information gathered through multiple means (see below). Signals intercepts, satellite imagery, human sources, open sources.

3. **Processing**: raw collection is often unusable directly. Encrypted signals need decryption. Foreign-language documents need translation. Satellite imagery needs interpretation.

4. **Analysis**: processed information is evaluated, cross-referenced, and turned into assessments. This is where intelligence agencies add value — not just collecting facts, but interpreting what they mean.

5. **Dissemination**: finished intelligence products (reports, briefings, the President's Daily Brief) are delivered to decision-makers.

6. **Feedback**: decision-makers act on intelligence and identify new questions, restarting the cycle.

The cycle is rarely clean. Intelligence failure usually happens at analysis (correct facts, wrong interpretation) or collection (gaps that weren't known to exist).

### Collection Methods: INT Disciplines

**HUMINT (Human Intelligence)**: information from people — recruited agents, walk-in sources, interrogations, diplomatic reporting. The CIA's core historical competency. HUMINT is expensive, slow, and irreplaceable: no satellite can tell you what a foreign minister believes, only what they say and do. The risk: human sources can be doubled (working for the other side) or fabricated.

**SIGINT (Signals Intelligence)**: intercepting communications — phone calls, emails, radio transmissions, data traffic. The NSA's domain. After WWII's Bletchley Park success (breaking the Enigma cipher), Western intelligence shifted heavily toward SIGINT. The Snowden revelations (2013) exposed the scope: the NSA was intercepting essentially all global digital communications. SIGINT can be collected at scale; HUMINT cannot.

**IMINT (Imagery Intelligence)**: satellite and aerial photography, now including commercial satellite constellations. U-2 overflights, KH-series reconnaissance satellites, and now Planet Labs commercial imagery have made it nearly impossible for a state to hide large military movements. The resolution of modern commercial satellite imagery (sub-meter) means that what used to be classified intelligence is now available to journalists and researchers.

**OSINT (Open Source Intelligence)**: information gathered from publicly available sources — news, academic publications, social media, satellite imagery available commercially, patent filings, company registrations. Bellingcat pioneered the use of OSINT to expose state deception (MH17 shootdown, Novichok poisoning suspects, Syrian chemical weapons). OSINT has become the fastest-growing intelligence discipline because the amount of public information is exploding.

**MASINT (Measurement and Signatures Intelligence)**: technical intelligence about physical phenomena — nuclear test signatures, chemical weapons traces, electromagnetic emissions. Specialized but critical for verification of arms control treaties.

In practice, finished intelligence products draw on all INT disciplines. An assessment of North Korean nuclear capability combines satellite imagery (IMINT) of construction activity, signals intercepts (SIGINT) of internal communications, technical analysis of detected seismic events (MASINT), and reports from intelligence officers in Beijing who recruit sources close to North Korean leadership (HUMINT).

### Major Intelligence Agencies

**CIA (Central Intelligence Agency)**: US foreign intelligence. Analysis and covert action. Does not operate domestically (that's the FBI). Known for Cold War operations: Iran 1953, Guatemala 1954, Bay of Pigs 1961, Afghan mujahideen 1980s, and more recently drone programs and rendition. The CIA's analytical directorate produces the President's Daily Brief.

**NSA (National Security Agency)**: US signals intelligence. The largest intelligence agency in the world by budget and personnel. Operates PRISM, XKEYSCORE, and global collection infrastructure. Also responsible for information assurance — protecting US communications.

**MI6 / SIS (Secret Intelligence Service)**: UK foreign intelligence. Long history of HUMINT operations. The "Five Eyes" alliance (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) shares intelligence at a depth no other partnership matches — a direct legacy of WWII.

**SVR / FSB (Russia)**: SVR is the foreign intelligence successor to the KGB's First Chief Directorate. FSB is the domestic counterintelligence successor to the KGB's Second Directorate. Russia is historically strong in HUMINT penetration — Soviet moles inside Western intelligence agencies (Kim Philby, Robert Hanssen) were catastrophic. Russian intelligence operations against US democracy (2016 election interference) were a SIGINT, HUMINT, and information operations hybrid.

**MSS (Ministry of State Security)**: China's primary civilian intelligence agency. China's approach emphasizes breadth: intelligence collection through students, researchers, businesspeople, diaspora communities, and cyber operations rather than traditional HUMINT case officers. The scale of Chinese economic espionage — theft of intellectual property — is estimated to cost the US hundreds of billions per year.

**Mossad**: Israeli foreign intelligence. Legendary for targeting the entire global adversary network: assassinations of nuclear scientists, the Entebbe rescue, killing PLO operatives in Operation Wrath of God after Munich. Small budget by Western standards; exceptionally effective for its size due to cultural proximity to adversaries in the Arab world and Iran.

### Covert Action

Intelligence agencies don't just collect information — they act on it covertly, attempting to shape events without attribution. Covert action is the most controversial intelligence function.

**Types of covert action:**

**Propaganda and information operations**: distributing favorable information, planting stories in foreign media, funding friendly publications, radio broadcasts (Radio Free Europe during the Cold War). The Soviet term was "active measures" — dezinformatsiya, forgeries, fabricated documents. Russia's modern information operations (Internet Research Agency, RT, Sputnik) are a direct continuation of this tradition.

**Political action**: financing political parties, candidates, or civil society groups abroad. The CIA funded Christian Democratic parties in Italy in the 1940s–50s to prevent Communist electoral victories. The National Endowment for Democracy does this more openly today.

**Paramilitary operations**: arming and training non-state actors. The CIA's support for Afghan mujahideen against the Soviet Union (Operation Cyclone) was the largest covert paramilitary program in history. The problem: paramilitary assets have their own interests and often outlive their utility (the mujahideen became the Taliban).

**Sabotage and assassination**: physical destruction of adversary assets. Stuxnet (attributed to US/Israel) was a cyberweapon that physically destroyed Iranian centrifuges — covert sabotage at scale. Mossad's assassination of Iranian nuclear scientists is covert action. The line between covert action and act of war is a legal and political construct, not a military one.

### Intelligence Failures: Why They Happen

The most famous intelligence failures reveal structural problems:

**Pearl Harbor (1941)**: the information existed — intercepts of Japanese diplomatic traffic showed war was coming. The failure was analytical and organizational: the signals weren't connected, the dots weren't joined, and the conclusion that Japan would attack American soil seemed too aggressive to accept.

**9/11**: similar structure. Individual CIA and FBI officers had pieces of the picture — two of the hijackers were known al-Qaeda members living in the US. The failure was sharing: CIA didn't tell FBI about the known operatives. Institutional silos killed the analysis before it was completed.

**Iraq WMD (2003)**: an analytical failure driven by political pressure and motivated reasoning. Evidence of WMD was weak; the analytical community felt pressure to confirm what policymakers wanted to hear. The dissents were buried. The result was the costliest intelligence failure in modern US history.

**Russia's 2022 Ukraine miscalculation**: Russia's own intelligence failed them catastrophically. The FSB told Putin that Ukraine would fall in days. Ukrainian will to resist and army effectiveness were systematically underestimated. The intelligence apparatus told leadership what it wanted to hear — a recurring failure in authoritarian states where delivering bad news is dangerous.

**The structural causes:**
- **Mirror imaging**: assuming the adversary thinks like you do
- **Politicization**: analysts feeling pressure to produce conclusions policymakers want
- **Groupthink**: once an assessment forms, it becomes difficult to challenge
- **Collection gaps**: you can't analyze what you didn't collect
- **Warning-fatigue**: crying wolf repeatedly makes genuine warnings less credible

### Information as a Weapon: Modern Disinformation

The weaponization of information goes beyond intelligence collection into active shaping of adversary beliefs. Modern information warfare:

**Social media manipulation**: Russia's Internet Research Agency ran operation in 2016 that amplified divisive content across Facebook, Twitter, and YouTube. The goal was not to elect a specific candidate but to deepen American polarization — to make the country harder to govern. This is the continuation of Soviet active measures with social media as the distribution mechanism.

**Deepfakes and synthetic media**: AI-generated video and audio that appears authentic. The threat is not just misleading content but the epistemic environment it creates — if anything could be fake, trust in all media erodes. This is the strategic outcome Russian information operations have always sought.

**Narrative control**: which framing of a conflict prevails affects diplomatic support, sanctions implementation, and public will. Russia's framing of Ukraine as a neo-Nazi state, China's framing of Taiwan as a domestic issue, the West's framing of Xinjiang as genocide — all of these are information operations competing for the dominant narrative.

**Economic espionage**: China's intelligence operations prioritize technology theft. Penetrating US defense contractors, universities, and technology companies to acquire R&D without the cost — this is information warfare with an economic vector. The value transferred is enormous; the attribution is difficult.

## Key Considerations

**Intelligence is not objective.** It is produced by human organizations with cultures, biases, and political pressures. Intelligence agencies in authoritarian states are especially prone to telling leadership what it wants to hear — which is why Putin's intelligence failure in Ukraine is predictable, not surprising.

**OSINT has democratized intelligence.** Bellingcat, The Intercept, and investigative journalists with satellite imagery subscriptions can now produce intelligence-quality analysis of state behavior. The information environment that states operated in for decades — where secrets stayed secret — has fundamentally changed.

**Counterintelligence is as important as collection.** For every CIA operation recruiting foreign agents, there's a counterintelligence operation trying to catch foreign agents inside US institutions. Robert Hanssen (FBI, Soviet asset for 22 years) and Aldrich Ames (CIA, Soviet asset) demonstrate the damage a single penetration can do.

**Plausible deniability is a feature, not a bug.** Covert action is preferred over overt action precisely because it avoids attribution. States act through proxies, cyber tools, and influence operations to pursue objectives they can't pursue openly.

## Getting Started

The best introduction to intelligence as a discipline is not textbooks but primary sources and case studies:

1. **Read the 9/11 Commission Report** — freely available. The definitive account of intelligence failure and what structural problems produce it.
2. **Follow Bellingcat** (bellingcat.com) — the best active demonstration of OSINT: how they geolocate events, verify claims, and expose state deception using public information.
3. **Read *Legacy of Ashes*** — Tim Weiner's history of the CIA. Pulitzer Prize-winning account based on declassified documents. Not flattering, but accurate.

## Common Mistakes

**Overestimating what intelligence agencies know.** The mystique is real but exaggerated. Intelligence agencies miss things constantly — their advantage is at the margin, not total knowledge.

**Underestimating the role of intelligence in history.** Most popular history of wars and crises focuses on military and political actors. The intelligence layer is usually invisible at the time and only surfaces decades later through declassification.

**Conflating intelligence with truth.** Intelligence is an assessment, not a fact. Assessments are wrong regularly. Treating an intelligence community conclusion as definitive is a mistake — see Iraq WMD.
