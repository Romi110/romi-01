# NJ Land Use
*How NJ controls what you can build, farm, and develop — zoning, variances, environmental overlays, and what it means for property decisions*

---

## What Is It

Land use law in New Jersey is a layered system. At the base is municipal zoning — your town controls what uses are permitted on what land. On top of that sit state and federal environmental overlays (Pinelands, Highlands, wetlands, floodplains) that restrict or modify what zoning alone would allow. Agricultural uses have their own set of protections and exemptions that cut across both layers.

Understanding land use before you buy or develop property in NJ is not optional. The wrong parcel can be unbuildable, unfarmable, or subject to restrictions that make your plans impossible — and those restrictions aren't always visible in a standard listing or title search.

---

## Why It Matters

For Romi's trajectory — moving toward land ownership in Atlantic County, possibly with farming and some energy infrastructure — land use determines whether a given property can actually support those goals. A parcel in the Pinelands Preservation Area cannot be subdivided or have new development added. A parcel with wetlands coverage may be 5 acres on paper but farmable on only 2. A zoning designation of "rural residential" permits a house but not a commercial agricultural operation.

Knowing how to read land use before you buy is the difference between buying land that serves your goals and buying a legal liability you can't use.

## Core Concepts

### Municipal Zoning: The Base Layer

Every municipality in NJ has a zoning ordinance that divides its territory into zones — typically residential (R-1, R-2, etc.), commercial, industrial, agricultural, and conservation. Each zone has a list of permitted uses (by-right), conditional uses (allowed with planning board approval), and prohibited uses.

**Key zoning designations relevant to farming and self-reliance in Atlantic County:**
- **AR (Agricultural-Residential):** The most useful designation for Romi's goals. Permits residential structures, agricultural operations, and often accessory structures (sheds, barns, greenhouses) by-right or with minimal approval.
- **R-1/R-2 (Residential):** Permits single-family homes, usually prohibits livestock and large-scale agricultural operations, limits accessory structures by size.
- **Conservation:** Often prohibits all development; may allow passive use only.

**Minimum lot sizes:** Zoning districts specify minimum lot sizes. In rural Atlantic County agricultural zones, minimums range from 1–5 acres. Pinelands zones impose their own minimums on top (often much higher).

**Accessory structures:** Sheds, greenhouses, barns, and similar structures are governed by zoning setback requirements (minimum distances from property lines) and sometimes by percentage-of-lot-coverage limits. Confirm before building.

### MLUL: NJ's Land Use Framework

New Jersey's Municipal Land Use Law (N.J.S.A. 40:55D) is the state statute that governs how municipalities zone and how property owners can challenge or modify those rules.

**Planning Board vs. Zoning Board of Adjustment:**
- The **Planning Board** handles subdivision applications, site plan approvals, and conditional use permits
- The **Zoning Board of Adjustment** handles **variances** — requests to deviate from zoning requirements

**Variances are the key mechanism** for getting a use or structure approved that doesn't comply with current zoning. Two types:
- **"d" variance (use variance):** Required when the proposed use isn't permitted in the zone at all. Hardest to get — requires proof that the property is "particularly suited" to the proposed use or that denying it would be unduly restrictive. Requires a supermajority of the zoning board.
- **"c" variance (bulk variance):** Required when the use is permitted but the structure doesn't comply with dimensional standards (setback, height, coverage). Easier to get — requires proof that the variance won't impair the zone's intent and that the hardship isn't self-created.

**Attorney review period** is not relevant here (that's for real estate contracts), but any variance application involving an agricultural use in a non-agricultural zone should involve a land use attorney.

### Agricultural Exemptions From Zoning

The Right to Farm Act and the MLUL together create meaningful exemptions for agricultural operations:

1. **Farm structures don't need site plan approval** in most cases — statewide exemption under MLUL for agricultural structures on farmland
2. **Agricultural fencing, ponds, and earthworks** are generally exempt from municipal building permit requirements (though NJDEP rules may still apply for earthworks near waterways)
3. **Farm stands and direct marketing** on agricultural land is permitted as an accessory use in most municipalities, even those where commercial uses would otherwise be restricted
4. **Right to Farm protections** prevent municipalities from using zoning to restrict sound agricultural practices on qualifying farms. See [nj-right-to-farm.md](nj-right-to-farm.md) for the full framework.

**Important:** These exemptions apply to legitimate agricultural operations. A structure that looks like a barn but functions as a commercial warehouse doesn't qualify.

### Environmental Overlays: Pinelands and Highlands

These are the most significant land use restrictions in NJ and operate independently of municipal zoning. They apply to specific geographic areas and can make otherwise buildable land effectively unbuildable.

**Pinelands (1.1 million acres, southern NJ):**
The Pinelands Comprehensive Management Plan (CMP) controls all development within the Pinelands National Reserve. See [nj-pinelands-regulations.md](nj-pinelands-regulations.md) for full detail. In summary: development intensity in the Pinelands is tightly controlled by management area designation, which ranges from near-total restriction (Preservation Area) to relatively permissive (Regional Growth Areas and villages). A parcel's management area designation is the first thing to check for any Atlantic County land.

**Highlands (800,000+ acres, northwestern NJ):**
Less relevant for Atlantic County, but the same principle applies: the Highlands Act creates a Conservation Zone (essentially no new development) and a Planning Zone (controlled development). Relevant for purchases in Bergen, Morris, Somerset, Passaic, Sussex, Warren, or Hunterdon counties.

### Wetlands

NJ has some of the most stringent wetlands protections in the US. The NJDEP Division of Land Resource Protection regulates freshwater wetlands under the Freshwater Wetlands Protection Act.

**Key rules:**
- A 300-foot buffer around Category One (exceptional-value) waters is regulated — no disturbance without a permit within that buffer
- A 150-foot buffer around other freshwater wetlands
- Wetlands delineation by a licensed wetlands scientist is required before any development or disturbance

**For farming:** Ground disturbance associated with normal farming practices (tilling, drainage ditching, irrigation) is generally exempt from wetlands permits for existing agricultural land. New agricultural operations converting non-farmed land that contains wetlands require careful analysis.

**Practical:** On any rural parcel purchase, get a wetlands delineation done by a licensed environmental consultant before closing. It costs $500–$2,000 and tells you what you actually have. Title searches and listing descriptions don't reliably capture wetlands impact.

### Floodplains

FEMA floodplain maps (Flood Insurance Rate Maps, FIRMs) designate flood zones. Properties in the 100-year floodplain (Zone A or AE) face:
- Mandatory flood insurance if the property has a mortgage
- Building restrictions (structures must be elevated above base flood elevation)
- Limited ability to add structures

**Check before buying:** FEMA's Flood Map Service Center at msc.fema.gov lets you check any address. Atlantic County coastal and inland areas both have significant floodplain coverage. NJ Pinelands flatlands drain slowly and have high flood risk along waterways.

### Net Metering and Energy Infrastructure

NJ's net metering rules are governed by the Board of Public Utilities (BPU) and are relevant to land use because solar array installations on agricultural land have their own rules.

**For agricultural/rural land:**
- Ground-mounted solar systems on farms may qualify for the NJ Solar Successor Program (SuSi) incentives and can often be sited without site plan approval if they serve the farm's energy needs
- Commercial-scale solar (net energy metering for grid export) requires BPU interconnection and often municipal site plan review
- The Right to Farm Act does not automatically exempt solar from zoning — it's a farm structure analysis, not a clean exemption

See the Energy section files for the solar system sizing and regulation detail. The land use consideration here is pre-siting zoning review before installation.

## Key Considerations

**The Pinelands designation is the most important variable in Atlantic County.** Before putting in an offer on any rural parcel, identify the Pinelands management area. This one data point determines what's possible more than any other factor. Use the Pinelands Commission mapping tool at pinelands.nj.gov.

**Wetlands delineation is the second thing to check.** Paper acreage and actual buildable/farmable acreage can differ significantly when wetlands are present.

**Municipal zoning ordinances are public and searchable.** Look up Galloway Township's ordinance before any purchase. The zoning map will tell you the zone; the ordinance text will tell you what's permitted in that zone.

**Agricultural exemptions have conditions.** They protect genuine agricultural operations with documented commercial activity. They don't turn any rural parcel into a by-right farm by claiming the exemption.

**Variances are uncertain.** The zoning board can say no. Don't buy a property assuming a variance will be granted — negotiate purchase price and contingencies around that uncertainty, or only buy land that works within its current zoning.

## Getting Started

1. **For any potential purchase:** Look up the address in the Pinelands Commission mapper (pinelands.nj.gov) — management area designation first
2. **Check the FEMA flood map** at msc.fema.gov for flood zone designation
3. **Pull the Galloway Township (or relevant municipality) zoning map** — free online at the municipal website
4. **Read the permitted use list for the zone** — confirm your intended use is permitted or understand the variance path
5. **Commission a wetlands delineation** on any rural parcel before closing

## Common Mistakes

**Buying rural land without checking Pinelands designation.** Atlantic County parcels in the Preservation Area can be farmable but not developable at all. Buying one to build a house on is a costly mistake.

**Relying on the seller's description of what's permitted.** Zoning and environmental restrictions run with the land, not with the seller's intentions. Verify independently.

**Assuming large acreage means farming is easy.** 10 acres of wetlands, floodplain, and forested buffer might have 2 usable acres. Always assess usable acreage, not total acreage.

**Not pulling permits for farm structures.** While many farm structures are exempt from site plan review, some still require building permits. Unpermitted structures create title problems when you sell and can require demolition.

**Ignoring setbacks on accessory structures.** Building a greenhouse or shed too close to a property line triggers a violation even if the agricultural use itself is permitted.
