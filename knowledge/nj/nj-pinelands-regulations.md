# NJ Pinelands Regulations
*The Pinelands Commission, what it controls, and what it means for any land decision in southern NJ*

---

## What Is It

The New Jersey Pinelands is a 1.1 million-acre region of pine-oak forest, wetlands, cedar streams, and aquifer recharge area in southern NJ — the largest area of open space on the Eastern Seaboard between Richmond and Boston. Congress designated it a National Reserve in 1978. New Jersey established the Pinelands Commission in 1979 and created the Comprehensive Management Plan (CMP) — a land use regulatory framework that governs what can be built, farmed, or developed within the Pinelands boundary.

For practical purposes: if your land is in the Pinelands, the Commission has authority over what you can do with it that supersedes municipal zoning in many respects. A municipality cannot simply rezone Pinelands land to allow development the CMP prohibits.

---

## Why It Matters

Galloway Township sits almost entirely within the Pinelands National Reserve. This is not a peripheral concern — it is the defining regulatory fact about land in Atlantic County. Understanding which management area a parcel falls in, and what that area permits, determines whether a property can be built on, subdivided, farmed commercially, or developed at all.

For Romi's goals — acquiring land with farming capacity and potentially building a home or structures — the Pinelands management area designation is the first question, not a detail to check later.

## Core Concepts

### The Pinelands National Reserve Boundary

The outer boundary of the Pinelands National Reserve encompasses portions of Atlantic, Burlington, Camden, Cape May, Cumberland, Gloucester, and Ocean counties. Most of Galloway Township falls within this boundary.

**The boundary has two nested zones:**
- **Pinelands National Reserve (outer boundary):** The entire 1.1 million acres. The CMP applies throughout.
- **Pinelands Area (inner boundary):** A more strictly regulated subset within the outer boundary.

Municipalities within the Pinelands must adopt land use ordinances that conform to the CMP. Conforming municipalities (Galloway is one) administer day-to-day land use approvals, but the Pinelands Commission reviews approvals for certain applications and has enforcement authority.

### Management Area Designations

The CMP divides the Pinelands into management areas, each with different development rules. This is the most important variable for any land decision.

**Preservation Area District (PAD)**
- The most restricted zone
- Covers the ecological core of the Pinelands — the most sensitive habitats, the highest aquifer recharge zones
- New development is essentially prohibited except for very limited agricultural structures and dwellings on existing lots of record
- Density: 1 dwelling per 32 acres for agricultural land; 1 dwelling per 10 acres for non-agricultural lots of record
- No new industrial, commercial, or major residential development

**Forest Area**
- Moderate restriction; the second-most-protected designation
- Low-density residential permitted (1 dwelling per 15 acres generally; some flexibility for clustered development)
- Agricultural uses permitted, including commercial farming
- Small-scale commercial uses may be permitted in existing villages
- No new shopping centers, industrial parks, or large-scale residential subdivisions

**Agricultural Production Area**
- Designed to protect existing farms and support agricultural operations
- Commercial farming is the primary use; residential is secondary
- Higher density agricultural housing permitted for farm workers
- Agricultural accessory uses (barns, greenhouses, processing facilities with limitations) permitted
- New non-agricultural development very limited

**Rural Development Area**
- Transition area: more development permitted than Forest or Agricultural areas, less than growth areas
- Residential at moderate densities; small commercial uses
- Still requires CMP conformance; not a free development zone

**Pinelands Villages and Towns**
- Historic settlement areas within the Pinelands boundary
- Higher development intensity permitted — residential, commercial, some light industrial
- Served by or capable of being served by public water/sewer in many cases
- Galloway Township has several recognized villages and towns within its boundaries (e.g., Pomona, Smithville area)

**Regional Growth Areas**
- Areas designated for higher-intensity development, typically on the Pinelands fringe
- Significantly more permissive than other management areas
- Often adjacent to existing development corridors

### How to Check Your Management Area

The Pinelands Commission provides a free online mapping tool: **pinelands.nj.gov** → Land Management → Parcel Report. Enter a block and lot (from the deed or tax records) to get the management area designation for a specific parcel.

This is the first due diligence step for any Atlantic County rural land purchase. Do it before the showing.

### Development Review Process

Applications for development in conforming municipalities go first to the local planning or zoning board. For applications that require Pinelands Commission review (called a "Pinelands development approval"), the Commission must concur.

**What requires Pinelands Commission review:**
- All applications in the Preservation Area District
- Certain applications in Forest and Agricultural areas above threshold density or size
- Applications for non-conforming uses
- Applications for Pinelands development certificates (PDCs — see below)

**Timeline:** Commission reviews can add 30–90 days to a standard local approval process.

### What You Can Build: Practical Rules

**Single-family residential dwelling on an existing lot:**
- In most management areas outside the Preservation Area, a dwelling on an existing conforming lot of record is permitted, subject to density and setback requirements
- Minimum lot sizes vary by management area; confirm with Galloway's zoning ordinance and the CMP

**Agricultural structures (barns, greenhouses, storage):**
- Generally permitted as accessory to a bona fide agricultural operation in Agricultural Production, Forest, and Rural Development areas
- Usually do not require Pinelands Commission review as stand-alone applications
- Must meet setback requirements

**Septic systems:**
- The CMP imposes standards on septic system design, particularly nitrogen loading — an issue unique to the Pinelands aquifer
- In the Preservation Area, engineered "alternative design" systems (peat filters, drip irrigation) are often required to meet nitrogen limits
- In other management areas, conventional septic is generally permitted with NJDEP compliance

**Wells:**
- Well placement must comply with setbacks from septic and from Pinelands surface water
- The CMP has specific water quality protection requirements for wells near cedar streams and other sensitive water resources

**Commercial uses:**
- Very limited outside Villages, Towns, and Regional Growth Areas
- Farm stands, farm markets, and agricultural tourism have more flexibility under the Right to Farm Act and CMP agricultural provisions
- Large commercial or industrial uses: essentially prohibited in core management areas

### What You Cannot Do

**Subdivide freely.** The CMP imposes minimum lot sizes per management area that prevent subdivision below those thresholds. A 10-acre parcel in the Forest Area cannot be subdivided into 5 two-acre lots.

**Add impervious cover without limit.** The CMP limits impervious coverage (parking, driveways, buildings) as a percentage of lot area, particularly in Agricultural and Forest areas. This affects what you can build.

**Clear native vegetation freely.** Land clearing in the Pinelands requires that clearing does not threaten Pinelands species or habitats. The Commission may require revegetation of disturbed areas.

**Convert farmland to residential without controls.** In the Agricultural Production Area, converting farmland to residential use is restricted to protect the agricultural character.

### Pinelands Development Credits (PDCs)

PDCs are the CMP's transfer of development rights mechanism. They allow development rights to be severed from land in the Pinelands core and used to increase density on land in the designated receiving areas (typically Regional Growth Areas).

**How PDCs work:**
1. A landowner in the Sending Area (typically Forest or Preservation Area) applies to the Pinelands Commission to have PDCs allocated based on their land
2. The sending parcel is deed-restricted from further development (the development right is sold)
3. The PDC is a certificate that can be sold to a developer who wants to build at higher density in a Receiving Area
4. Receiving Area municipalities require or allow PDCs to increase density above what would otherwise be permitted

**For Romi:** If you own Pinelands sending area land that has limited development potential anyway, PDCs represent a way to extract some value from the development rights you can't use. The PDC market has historically been thin but functions as a preservation mechanism. SADC farmland preservation is typically a more straightforward route to the same outcome.

### Pinelands and Agricultural Exemptions

The CMP includes significant provisions for agricultural uses:
- Agricultural development (structures, drainage, clearing for crops) is permitted in Agricultural Production Areas and Forest Areas without full Pinelands development review in most cases
- Greenhouses, barns, and storage structures for active farming operations are treated favorably
- The Right to Farm Act applies within the Pinelands
- Farmland preservation under SADC is compatible with Pinelands restrictions — in fact, Pinelands preserved land and SADC-preserved land often overlap

The key exception: even agricultural operations must comply with CMP surface water and groundwater protection rules, particularly for operations near the many cedar streams and wetlands in the Pinelands.

## Key Considerations

**Management area is destiny.** A parcel in the Preservation Area has fundamentally different possibilities than one in a Village or Regional Growth Area. No amount of creativity at the local level changes this — the Commission has veto authority.

**The Pinelands adds time, not just restriction.** For applications that require Commission review, expect 30–90 days added to any local approval timeline. Plan for this in any project schedule.

**Pinelands doesn't mean worthless.** Agricultural Production and Forest area land is valuable for farming, passive recreation, and habitat preservation. It's simply not developable in the conventional sense. Buying it with the expectation of farming or preservation rather than development is buying it correctly.

**The wetlands and surface water rules are layered on top.** NJDEP wetlands protections apply within the Pinelands independently of CMP rules. A parcel in a permissive management area may still have significant wetlands coverage that limits usable area. See [nj-land-use.md](nj-land-use.md).

**Galloway-specific note:** Most of Galloway Township's rural land falls in the Forest Area or Agricultural Production Area, with villages and growth areas concentrated along Route 9 and the Atlantic City Expressway corridor. Rural parcels off this corridor are almost certainly in restricted management areas.

## Getting Started

1. **Check any parcel you're considering** at pinelands.nj.gov before anything else — block and lot number from tax records, Parcel Report tool
2. **Read the Galloway Township land use ordinance** — the municipality's Pinelands-conforming ordinance translates CMP requirements into local rules
3. **Call the Pinelands Commission** with specific questions — their staff (609-894-7300) will answer parcel-specific questions. This is a free public service and they're responsive.
4. **Engage a NJ land use attorney** for any complex application — particularly anything involving the Preservation Area, subdivision, or a non-standard use

## Common Mistakes

**Buying a Pinelands parcel assuming it can be built on.** A 20-acre parcel in the Preservation Area may permit exactly one dwelling — the one you build when you buy it. Subdividing for future sale, adding commercial structures, or developing it further may all be prohibited.

**Relying on the listing description for land use information.** Real estate listings do not reliably disclose Pinelands management area designations or CMP restrictions. Verify independently.

**Assuming agricultural use is unrestricted.** The CMP's environmental protection rules apply to farms too — particularly for operations near surface water, for chemical/nutrient application, and for certain drainage modifications.

**Ignoring the PDC market.** If you own sending area land in the Pinelands with limited development potential, PDCs may have value you're not capturing. Worth understanding before deciding what to do with the land.

**Confusing Pinelands National Reserve with state park.** The Pinelands is not a park — it's privately and publicly owned land under a land use regulatory framework. You can own, farm, and build on Pinelands land within the rules. Millions of people live within the Pinelands boundary.
