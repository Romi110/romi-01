# NJ Homebuying Process
*The NJ-specific mechanics of buying a home — attorney review, the timeline, closing costs, and what's different from most states*

---

## What Is It

Buying a home in New Jersey follows a specific legal process that differs meaningfully from most other states. The most important difference: **NJ is an attorney state.** Both buyer and seller must be represented by licensed NJ attorneys. A real estate agent handles the negotiation; an attorney handles the legal transfer, contract review, and closing. This isn't optional or a luxury — it's required for a properly executed NJ real estate transaction.

Understanding the process from offer to close — each stage, its timeline, and what you control — is the preparation that prevents costly mistakes. The biggest mistakes in NJ homebuying happen not from bad luck but from not knowing what's supposed to happen next.

---

## Why It Matters

For Romi, homeownership is on the trajectory — financial independence, self-reliance, and building toward land with farming capacity in Atlantic County. The NJ process has quirks that catch buyers off guard: the 3-day attorney review period, the mortgage contingency clock, the real estate transfer fee, the mansion tax threshold, and the specific ways NJ property taxes flow through the closing.

A buyer who walks into this process knowing the stages, the costs, and the leverage points at each step is buying from a position of understanding rather than just hoping the agents and attorneys handle it.

## Core Concepts

### The Timeline at a Glance

A typical NJ purchase from accepted offer to closing takes **60–90 days**. The stages are:

1. Pre-approval → 0 days
2. Offer and accepted contract → Days 1–5
3. Attorney review period → Days 1–4 (runs concurrently with contract execution)
4. Inspection contingency → Days 5–15
5. Mortgage application and appraisal → Days 15–45
6. Title search → Days 20–50
7. Clear to close → Days 50–80
8. Closing → Days 60–90

Cash purchases compress this significantly (no appraisal, no mortgage contingency).

### Stage 1: Pre-Approval

Before making an offer, get a **pre-approval letter** from a lender (not pre-qualification — pre-approval involves actual income and credit review). Sellers in NJ won't seriously consider an offer without it.

**What lenders check:**
- Credit score (conventional loans: ideally 740+ for best rates; 620+ minimum)
- Debt-to-income ratio (DTI) — total monthly debt payments ÷ gross monthly income; NJ lenders prefer below 43%
- Employment history (2 years typical)
- Down payment source (seasoned funds — in your account 60+ days — are preferred)

**Down payment benchmarks:**
- 3–5% — Conventional loans with PMI, first-time buyer programs
- 10–20% — Avoiding PMI, stronger offer position
- 20%+ — Eliminates PMI, cleanest financing; PMI adds $50–$200/month on a $300,000 loan

**PMI:** Private mortgage insurance. Required when you put down less than 20%. It's insurance for the lender, paid by you. It cancels automatically when your loan balance reaches 78% of original purchase price (can request cancellation at 80%).

### Stage 2: Making an Offer

Offers in NJ are typically made on a standard NJ Realtors contract form. Key items:

- **Purchase price**
- **Deposit (earnest money)** — typically 1–2% of purchase price; paid to the seller's attorney's escrow account after attorney review
- **Contingencies:** mortgage contingency (protects you if financing falls through), inspection contingency (protects you if inspection reveals problems), and sometimes a home sale contingency (if you need to sell your current home first)
- **Closing date:** Proposed, typically 60–90 days out
- **Personal property inclusions:** Appliances, fixtures, window treatments — specify what stays

An accepted offer triggers the attorney review period immediately.

### Stage 3: Attorney Review (NJ-Specific and Critical)

This is the most important NJ-specific element of the process. **Every NJ residential real estate contract has a 3-business-day attorney review period** beginning when both parties receive a fully executed copy of the contract.

During attorney review:
- **Either party can cancel the contract for any reason, for any reason at all, with no penalty**
- Attorneys review the contract, propose modifications, and negotiate directly
- The deal is not binding until attorney review is completed (either by both attorneys signing off or by the review period expiring without disapproval)

**Why this matters:** Attorney review is your last no-cost escape hatch. If your attorney finds something wrong with the contract, or you simply change your mind in those 3 days, you walk away and get your deposit back. After attorney review closes, you're bound by the contingencies in the contract.

**What NJ attorneys negotiate during review:**
- Additional protections and representations
- Closing cost adjustments
- Title insurance obligations
- Certificate of occupancy requirements
- Radon and well/septic testing requirements (if applicable)

**Attorney fees:** NJ real estate attorneys for a standard purchase charge $1,000–$2,000. This is not optional; it is how NJ real estate law works. A closing without an attorney is not a properly executed NJ closing.

### Stage 4: Inspection Contingency

After attorney review, schedule a **home inspection** immediately. The inspection contingency window is typically 10–14 days from contract date and is spelled out in the contract.

**What a standard NJ home inspection covers:**
- Structure, foundation, basement
- Roof, gutters, chimney
- Electrical (panel, outlets, GFCI, smoke/CO detectors)
- Plumbing (supply, drain, water heater)
- HVAC (furnace, AC, ductwork)
- Attic insulation and ventilation

**NJ-specific additional inspections:**
- **Radon:** NJ has significant radon risk, particularly in northern counties but present statewide. A radon test (~$150) is standard. Acceptable levels: below 4.0 pCi/L; 4.0–8.0 pCi/L warrants mitigation; above 8.0 pCi/L is high priority. Mitigation (sub-slab depressurization) costs $800–$1,500 installed and is often negotiated as a seller concession.
- **Well and septic:** For rural/semi-rural properties on private water and sewer, these are separate inspections. See [nj-well-and-septic.md](nj-well-and-septic.md) for full detail. At minimum: water quality test (coliform, nitrates, arsenic, PFAS) and a septic inspection.
- **Oil tanks:** Older NJ homes frequently have buried heating oil tanks, some decommissioned, some still active. A buried oil tank that has leaked creates a NJDEP environmental remediation obligation that can run $20,000–$100,000+. Request any tank history from the seller and consider a soil probe test in older homes.

**Outcomes:** After inspection, you can request repairs, a price reduction, or a credit at closing. Sellers are not obligated to agree to anything — but significant defects shift negotiating leverage to the buyer. In a contingency purchase, if you can't reach agreement and the inspection contingency is properly exercised, you get your deposit back.

### Stage 5: Mortgage Application and Appraisal

If you're financing, your lender will order an **appraisal** (cost: $500–$800, paid by buyer). The appraisal:
- Establishes the home's market value for the lender
- Must meet or exceed the purchase price for the loan to proceed at the agreed amount
- If the appraisal comes in low, you either renegotiate the price, make up the difference in cash, or exit via the mortgage contingency

Simultaneously, your lender processes the full mortgage application. You'll be asked for:
- Pay stubs (last 2 months)
- W-2s and tax returns (last 2 years)
- Bank statements (last 2–3 months)
- Employment verification
- Explanation letters for any unusual deposits or credit inquiries

**Lock your rate early:** If rates are rising, lock your rate as soon as you have an accepted contract. Rate locks typically run 30–60 days. Discuss the lock period with your lender based on your expected closing date.

### Stage 6: Title Search and Title Insurance

The title company (engaged by your attorney) searches public records to confirm:
- The seller actually owns the property
- There are no liens, judgments, or encumbrances on title
- There are no easements, deed restrictions, or other title defects

**Title insurance:** NJ requires two title insurance policies in most financed transactions:
- **Lender's policy:** Protects the lender (required by the lender). Paid by the buyer.
- **Owner's policy:** Protects you as the buyer from pre-existing title defects. Optional but should be purchased every time. One-time premium at closing.

Title insurance costs in NJ are regulated. For a $400,000 purchase: owner's policy ~$1,500–$2,500, lender's policy ~$500–$1,000 additional.

**NJ-specific title issues to know:**
- The Pinelands and agricultural easements are title encumbrances — they appear in a title search but must be understood, not just noticed. See [nj-pinelands-regulations.md](nj-pinelands-regulations.md) and [nj-right-to-farm.md](nj-right-to-farm.md).
- Judgment liens against the seller attach to the property until resolved at closing
- Unpermitted work and open building permits are not always disclosed — a survey of the property and municipal records search can reveal them

### Stage 7: Closing

NJ closings are typically held in person at the title company or one of the attorneys' offices. Budget 2–3 hours.

**What you bring:**
- Cashier's check or wire transfer for closing funds (amount provided by the title company 24–48 hours before closing)
- Government-issued photo ID
- Any outstanding documents your lender requested

**What happens at the table:**
1. Lender funds the mortgage
2. Title company disburses funds to the seller (after paying off any existing mortgage, taxes, and liens)
3. Deed is recorded with the county clerk
4. You receive the keys

**Your closing costs:** Budget **2–5% of the purchase price** in buyer closing costs. Line items:
- Lender origination fee: 0.5–1% of loan amount
- Appraisal: ~$600
- Title insurance (lender + owner): $2,000–$3,500 for a $400,000 purchase
- Attorney fee: $1,000–$2,000
- Recording fees: $50–$200
- Prepaid items: First year of homeowners insurance, 2–3 months of property taxes escrowed, mortgage interest prepaid to end of month
- Homeowners association fees (if applicable)

**The NJ-specific costs that surprise buyers:**

**Realty Transfer Fee (RTF):** NJ charges a transfer fee on the sale of real property, paid by the **seller** but worth knowing in negotiations. For purchases over $1 million by non-exempt buyers, the RTF is paid by the buyer. Rates scale with price — at a $400,000 price, the seller pays roughly $2,400 in RTF.

**Mansion Tax:** A 1% tax on residential property purchases of **$1 million or more**, paid by the **buyer**. On a $1 million purchase: $10,000. On a $1.5 million purchase: $15,000. Not directly relevant at current price points but worth knowing as a threshold to plan around.

**Property tax proration:** NJ property taxes are billed quarterly in advance. At closing, they're prorated from the last payment date. You may receive a credit (if the seller has paid ahead) or owe a credit to the seller. Your attorney will calculate this.

## Key Considerations

**Do not skip the attorney.** NJ is an attorney state. Using only a real estate agent and skipping attorney review is leaving your most important leverage point on the table. A good NJ real estate attorney pays for themselves many times over.

**The mortgage contingency protects your deposit.** If your financing falls through after the mortgage contingency deadline, you can lose your deposit. Know your contingency deadlines and communicate with your lender constantly — if there's a problem, raise it before the deadline, not after.

**Atlantic County has unique considerations.** Rural and semi-rural parcels near Galloway may have private wells, private septic, Pinelands designations, and agricultural easements. Each requires specific due diligence beyond a standard suburban purchase inspection.

**Property taxes are not in the listing.** The listed annual tax is often outdated or reflects current assessment, not what you'll pay after a reassessment. Research the effective rate and current assessment independently before making an offer. See [nj-property-taxes.md](nj-property-taxes.md).

**Rate locks are perishable.** If you lock a rate and the closing is delayed, discuss extension options with your lender immediately. Extensions typically cost money or add to your rate.

## Getting Started

1. **Get pre-approved** — not pre-qualified — before looking at properties seriously. It defines your real budget and makes your offers credible.
2. **Engage an NJ real estate attorney** before you go under contract — most will be available on short notice, but don't scramble.
3. **Research effective property tax rates** in target municipalities — not just the assessed value in the listing.
4. **For any rural Atlantic County parcel:** check Pinelands designation (pinelands.nj.gov), check FEMA flood zone (msc.fema.gov), and budget for well/septic inspections.
5. **Save for closing costs** as a separate line item from your down payment — budget 3–4% of purchase price.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming attorney review is formality.** It's your best leverage. Your attorney can negotiate contract modifications, additional protections, and test requirements during those 3 days.

**Skipping the oil tank inquiry on older homes.** A buried oil tank leak in NJ creates a NJDEP remediation obligation. A seller may not know it exists. Ask, look for evidence, and consider a soil probe on pre-1985 homes.

**Not getting the well and septic inspected.** For rural properties, the standard home inspection doesn't cover these. They're separate and essential. A failing septic system costs $15,000–$40,000 to replace. Discovering this after closing is a disaster.

**Letting the mortgage contingency deadline pass.** If you're having financing trouble, raise it before the deadline. After the deadline, you could lose your deposit even if the financing ultimately falls through.

**Underestimating true monthly cost.** Mortgage payment + NJ property taxes + homeowners insurance + maintenance reserve. In Atlantic County, property taxes on a $400,000 home run $8,000–$12,000/year — $670–$1,000/month on top of your mortgage.
