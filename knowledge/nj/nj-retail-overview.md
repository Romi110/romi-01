# Running a Retail Business in NJ
*Legal structure, licensing, location, operations, and financials for a retail location in New Jersey*

---

## What Is It

This file covers the full operational picture of starting and running a physical retail business in New Jersey — from legal formation through day-to-day operations. NJ has specific requirements that differ from federal defaults and from other states, including mandatory sales tax registration, municipal licensing layers, and strict employee protections. Getting these right at the start costs less than fixing them later.

This is the overview. For taxes specifically, see [nj-business-taxes.md](nj-business-taxes.md).

---

## Why It Matters

NJ is one of the more complex states for small business compliance — multiple layers of licensing, a mandatory payroll tax system even for small operations, and one of the highest sales tax rates in the region (6.625%). Understanding the full picture before you open prevents the most common and expensive mistakes: selling without a Certificate of Authority, misclassifying employees as contractors, or signing a lease before verifying zoning.

## Core Concepts

### Legal Structure

The structure you choose affects taxes, liability, and complexity. For retail, the practical options are:

**Sole Proprietorship**
- No formation required; you operate under your own name or a DBA
- Personal liability: your personal assets are exposed to business debts and lawsuits
- Income reported on Schedule C; no separate business tax return
- Simplest, but offers no protection — not recommended for any retail with employees, inventory, or a lease

**LLC (Limited Liability Company)**
- Most common structure for small retail
- Protects personal assets from business liabilities (if operated properly — no commingling of funds)
- NJ LLC formation: file a Certificate of Formation with NJ DORES, $125 filing fee
- Default: taxed as a pass-through (profit flows to your personal return)
- Can elect S-Corp taxation if profits are high enough to justify it (roughly when net profit exceeds ~$50K and you're paying yourself a salary)
- Annual report: $75/year due by the anniversary of formation

**S-Corporation**
- Reduces self-employment taxes on profits above a reasonable salary
- More administrative overhead: separate payroll, quarterly filings, stricter requirements
- Worth considering when net profit consistently exceeds $80–100K
- NJ has a minimum CBT of $375/year for S-Corps regardless of profit

**Practical recommendation**: Form an LLC for almost all retail starting points. Revisit S-Corp election when income justifies the added complexity.

## Formation and Registration — The Full Checklist

### Step 1: Federal EIN
Apply at IRS.gov/ein — free, instant, takes 10 minutes. Required before any NJ registration. This is your business's tax ID number.

### Step 2: NJ Business Entity Formation
If forming an LLC: file online at njportal.com/dor/businessregistration or via NJBGS (NJ Business Gateway Services). $125 for LLC Certificate of Formation.

### Step 3: NJ Business Registration Certificate
Register with NJ Division of Revenue & Enterprise Services (DORES) at njportal.com/dor. This is separate from entity formation. Costs $0. Required before collecting sales tax, hiring employees, or opening a business bank account in NJ.

### Step 4: Certificate of Authority (Sales Tax)
Apply through NJ Division of Taxation. Required before making any taxable retail sale. Free. Once approved, you receive a Certificate of Authority — must be displayed at your location. See [nj-business-taxes.md](nj-business-taxes.md) for what you owe and when.

### Step 5: EIN + Business Bank Account
Open a dedicated business checking account before any transactions. Commingling personal and business funds pierces the LLC's liability protection.

### Step 6: Municipal Business License
**Every NJ municipality has its own licensing requirements.** There is no universal NJ business license — contact the clerk's office of the specific town where you're operating. Costs range from $50–$500/year. Some towns require annual renewal; some require inspections.

### Step 7: Certificate of Occupancy (CO)
Required before occupying any commercial space in NJ. Issued by the local construction department. Existing spaces may already have a CO — confirm it covers your use type. If you're changing the use (e.g., converting a warehouse to retail), you'll need a new CO. **Do not sign a lease or begin building out a space before confirming CO status.**

### Step 8: Zoning Verification
Retail use must be permitted under the local zone designation. Verify with the municipal planning/zoning office before leasing. Some zones permit retail with conditions (parking requirements, signage limits, hours of operation).

### Step 9: Sign Permits
NJ municipalities control signage heavily. Exterior signs typically require a sign permit from the local zoning board. Size, lighting, and placement are regulated. Get this approved before committing to a storefront design.

## Finding and Securing a Location

### Lease Fundamentals

**Triple Net (NNN) vs Gross Lease**
- NNN: you pay base rent + your share of property taxes, insurance, and maintenance. Common in retail. Your true monthly cost is base rent + NNN charges (often $3–8/sq ft/year extra).
- Gross/Modified Gross: you pay a flat rent and the landlord covers operating costs. Simpler; slightly higher base rent.
- NJ retail is mostly NNN. Model your costs accordingly.

**Key lease terms to negotiate**
- **Tenant Improvement (TI) Allowance** — Landlord contribution to buildout costs. Standard range: $20–50/sq ft in NJ. Push for this; it funds the initial buildout without depleting cash.
- **Free Rent Period** — 1–3 months free while you build out and ramp. Normal for first-time tenants in a new space.
- **Personal Guarantee** — Landlord will want you to personally guarantee the lease. Negotiate a "good-guy clause": if you close and vacate properly with X months notice, the personal guarantee ends at vacate date, not at lease expiration.
- **Co-tenancy clause** — If you're in a shopping center anchored by a major tenant, a co-tenancy clause lets you reduce rent or exit if that anchor leaves.
- **Assignment and subletting rights** — You want the right to assign the lease to a buyer if you sell the business.

**NJ-specific: NJ Commercial Lease Act**
NJ has limited tenant protections in commercial leases compared to residential. Everything is negotiated — the lease is the agreement. Get a commercial real estate attorney to review before signing. Budget $500–$1,500 for the review; it's worth it on a 5-year lease.

### Location Analysis

Key factors beyond foot traffic:
- **Parking** — NJ is a car-dependent state. Retail without accessible parking struggles. Check municipal parking requirements for your business type.
- **Neighboring businesses** — Complementary anchors (a gym near a protein supplement shop, a grocery near a specialty food retailer) drive traffic. Assess the full tenant mix.
- **Delivery access** — Loading zones, dock access, and delivery hours matter for inventory. Some downtown NJ locations have restrictions.
- **Demographics** — NJ has extreme demographic variation by town. Know your customer before committing to a location.

## Insurance

Retail requires multiple insurance types. Skipping any of these is a significant liability exposure.

**General Liability Insurance**
Required by most commercial landlords. Covers customer injury (slip-and-fall, product liability). Minimum $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate. Cost: ~$500–$2,000/year depending on business type and size.

**Commercial Property Insurance**
Covers your inventory, fixtures, and equipment against fire, theft, and weather. Particularly important given NJ's coastal storm risk. Cost varies by inventory value.

**Workers' Compensation Insurance (mandatory in NJ)**
**NJ requires workers' comp coverage for every employee, including part-time workers.** No exceptions. Penalties for non-compliance are severe. Purchase through a licensed NJ insurer or the State Insurance Fund (njcrib.com). Cost: ~1–4% of payroll depending on job type.

**Business Interruption Insurance**
Covers lost income if the business is forced to close temporarily (fire, flooding). Especially relevant in NJ given weather risk.

**Product Liability (if selling goods)**
Covers liability if a product you sell injures a customer. May be included in general liability; verify with your broker.

## Employees and Payroll

NJ has some of the strongest employee protections in the country. Know these before hiring.

### Payroll Requirements

**NJ Payroll Taxes (employer pays)**
- NJ Unemployment Insurance (SUI): 0.5–5.4% of wages up to $42,300 (2024 rate, adjusts annually)
- NJ Disability Insurance (SDI): 0.1% employer, 0.14% employee (withheld)
- NJ Family Leave Insurance (FLI): employee-paid only (withheld), 0.09% of wages

**Federal Payroll Taxes**
- FICA: 6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare (employer matches)
- FUTA: 6.0% on first $7,000 wages (reduced by SUTA credit)

**Tools**: Use Gusto, Rippling, or ADP for payroll. Gusto at ~$40/month + $6/employee is the right tool for a small retail operation — handles all NJ-specific filings automatically.

### NJ Minimum Wage (2024–2025)
- Standard minimum wage: $15.49/hour (indexed to CPI, adjusts January 1 each year)
- Tipped employees: $5.62/hour cash wage minimum if tips bring total to $15.49+
- Seasonal/small employer (under 6 employees): $13.73/hour (ending 2025 — rates converging)

### NJ WARN Act
If you have 100+ employees and lay off 50+, you must give 90 days written notice. Relevant if the business scales.

### Employee vs. Independent Contractor
NJ uses the **ABC Test** to classify workers. All three conditions must be met to classify someone as an independent contractor:
- A: The worker is free from control/direction by the hiring entity
- B: The work is outside the usual course of the business
- C: The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade or business

For retail, virtually anyone working in your store is an employee, not a contractor. Misclassification triggers back taxes, penalties, and interest.

## Point of Sale and Operations Systems

### POS Systems

**Square (USA)** — Free hardware entry point, 2.6% + $0.10 per swipe. Built-in inventory, employee management, reporting. Good for startups with <$500K annual sales where simplicity matters more than features.

**Clover (USA)** — More robust hardware ecosystem, better for higher volume. Monthly software fees ($15–$90/month depending on plan). Better for businesses with complex inventory or multiple employees clocking in/out.

**Lightspeed Retail (Canada)** — The step up for serious retail inventory management. Built for multi-SKU operations, purchase order management, supplier integration. ~$100/month. Worth it when product catalog exceeds a few hundred SKUs.

**Shopify POS** — Best if you're running both a physical location and an e-commerce store. Unified inventory between channels. ~$89/month + Shopify plan. The right choice if online sales are part of the model.

### Accounting

**QuickBooks Online** — ~$35–$75/month. Standard for small retail. Integrates with most POS systems. Required for any accountant you work with.
**Wave** — Free accounting software. Adequate for very simple operations; limited integrations.

The minimum setup: POS synced to accounting software so sales are automatically recorded. Doing this manually is a time drain and error source.

### Inventory Management

Track COGS (cost of goods sold) from day one. Most POS systems handle this. Key metric: **inventory turn rate** — how many times per year you sell through your total inventory. Industry averages:
- Grocery/consumables: 12–25x
- Apparel: 4–6x
- Specialty retail: 3–5x

Low turns mean capital is sitting on shelves. High turns mean you're selling fast (good) but may be stockout-prone (bad).

## Financial Fundamentals

### Retail Math to Know

**Gross Margin** = (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue
- Typical retail gross margins: 40–60% for specialty goods, 20–30% for commodity goods

**Break-Even Analysis**
Fixed monthly costs (rent + payroll + insurance + utilities) ÷ Average gross margin % = Monthly revenue needed to break even.

Example: $8,000 fixed costs / 50% gross margin = $16,000/month in sales to break even.

**CAC and LTV**
- Customer Acquisition Cost: what you spend per new customer (marketing spend / new customers)
- Lifetime Value: average spend per customer × average number of visits/year × number of years retained
- LTV must exceed CAC for the business to be profitable on a customer basis

### Cash Flow vs Profit
Retail businesses are frequently profitable on paper but cash-poor because:
- Inventory must be purchased before it sells
- Sales tax collected is held until remitted (not yours)
- Seasonal fluctuations hit cash before they hit the P&L

Maintain a cash buffer of 2–3 months of fixed costs before opening. Model cash flow monthly, not just profit.

## Getting Started — Launch Sequence

1. Choose legal structure and form entity (LLC: njportal.com/dor, $125)
2. Get EIN from IRS.gov/ein (free, 10 minutes)
3. Register with NJ DORES (njportal.com, free)
4. Identify target location; verify zoning before signing anything
5. Get Certificate of Authority from NJ Division of Taxation (free)
6. Sign lease with attorney review; negotiate TI allowance and good-guy clause
7. Apply for CO from local construction department
8. Apply for municipal business license
9. Get sign permit before installing signage
10. Set up business bank account (First Merchants, TD, or a local NJ community bank)
11. Purchase insurance: GL + property + workers comp (required before first hire)
12. Set up payroll (Gusto) before first pay period
13. Set up POS + accounting software integration
14. Open

## Common Mistakes

**Signing a lease before verifying zoning and CO.** The most expensive mistake. A lease is a binding contract. If the space can't be used for your purpose, you're still liable.

**Not getting a Certificate of Authority before first sale.** NJ can assess back taxes + 5% per month penalty + interest on all sales made without registration.

**Treating employees as contractors.** NJ enforcement is aggressive. If audited, you owe back FICA, SUI, SDI, FLI, plus penalties.

**Underestimating NNN costs.** Budgeting for base rent only and being surprised by $1,500/month in taxes, insurance, and CAM charges.

**Skipping workers' comp.** Even for one part-time employee. The fine for non-compliance is $5,000/day plus civil liability if the employee is injured.

**No cash reserve before opening.** Revenue ramps slower than expenses. The business needs 2–3 months of fixed costs in the bank before day one, not after.

## Resources

- **NJ Business Action Center** (business.nj.gov) — State's official business guide. Surprisingly useful. The "Starting a Business" checklist is thorough.
- **SCORE NJ** (score.org/nj) — Free mentoring from retired executives. Particularly valuable for retail operations and financial modeling if you've never run a business.
