# NJ Benefits and Programs
*The programs most NJ residents qualify for but never claim — unemployment, disability, family leave, property tax relief, food assistance, and more*

---

## What Is It

New Jersey administers a significant number of benefit programs funded through payroll taxes, state budgets, and federal matching funds. Most workers pay into these systems for years without knowing exactly what they're entitled to — or how to access them when the time comes. This file covers the programs most likely to be relevant to a working NJ adult: what they are, who qualifies, what they pay, and how to apply.

This is not about dependency — it's about knowing what you've already paid for and being able to access it when circumstances require.

---

## Why It Matters

You pay NJ state taxes, unemployment insurance (UI) contributions, Temporary Disability Insurance (TDI) premiums, and Family Leave Insurance (FLI) premiums out of every paycheck. These aren't charity — they're insurance programs you've funded. Not knowing how to access them is like paying for a service contract and not knowing how to file a claim.

Self-reliance includes knowing the systems around you and using them when they apply, without pride getting in the way.

## Core Concepts

### Unemployment Insurance (UI)

**What it is**: Wage replacement when you lose your job through no fault of your own (layoff, downsizing, company closure, sometimes resignation under certain circumstances).

**Who qualifies**:
- Worked in NJ and paid UI taxes on your wages
- Involuntarily unemployed (fired for cause generally disqualifies; layoff does not)
- Actively available for and seeking work
- Meet minimum earnings thresholds (base period — roughly the first 4 of the last 5 completed calendar quarters)

**What it pays**: 60% of your average weekly wage, up to a weekly maximum set annually (around $850–900/week as of recent years — verify current max at the time of claim). Paid for up to 26 weeks standard; extended benefits may be available during high unemployment periods.

**How to apply**:
1. File online at myunemployment.nj.gov or call 1-888-795-6672
2. Apply as soon as you lose your job — there's a waiting week, and delay costs you benefits
3. You'll need: SSN, employment history for past 18 months, employer addresses and contact info, reason for separation
4. After filing, you must certify every two weeks (confirm you're still unemployed and actively seeking work)

**Important**: If you're offered suitable work and decline it, you can lose eligibility. If you do any work during a week, report the earnings — they reduce but don't eliminate that week's benefit.

**Appeals**: If denied, you have 7 days to appeal. Most denials are for "misconduct" or "voluntary quit" — these can be contested. A separation that feels voluntary (mutual agreement, resignation under pressure) may still qualify if the circumstances were compelling.

### Temporary Disability Insurance (TDI)

**What it is**: Wage replacement when you can't work due to a non-work-related illness or injury (including surgery recovery, mental health treatment, etc.). This is a different program from workers' compensation, which covers work-related injuries.

**Who qualifies**: NJ employees who have paid into TDI through payroll deductions. (Self-employed can opt in voluntarily.) Must have a disabling condition certified by a healthcare provider. Cannot be receiving UI at the same time.

**What it pays**: 85% of average weekly wage, up to a weekly cap (currently around $1,025/week — verify at claim time). Benefits last up to 26 weeks per episode.

**How to apply**:
1. Notify your employer you'll be out
2. Physician must certify your disability on Form DS-1 (your doctor completes their section; your employer completes theirs)
3. Submit to the Division of Temporary Disability and Family Leave Insurance: myleavebenefits.nj.gov
4. Apply within 30 days of the start of disability; benefits won't be paid more than 30 days before application

**Pregnancy**: TDI covers the period you're medically disabled due to pregnancy and childbirth (typically 6 weeks for vaginal birth, 8 weeks for C-section). After the disability period ends, FLI kicks in for bonding (see below).

**Private plans**: Some employers have their own approved private TDI plans that replace the state plan. Check with your HR — if your employer has a private plan, you file with them, not the state.

### Family Leave Insurance (FLI)

**What it is**: Wage replacement while you're caring for a seriously ill family member or bonding with a new child (biological, adopted, or foster). This is separate from and in addition to TDI.

**Who qualifies**: NJ workers who've paid FLI premiums (deducted from paycheck). Must be bonding with a newborn, newly adopted, or newly placed foster child, OR caring for a family member with a serious health condition.

**What it pays**: 85% of average weekly wage, up to the same cap as TDI. Up to 12 weeks per year for bonding, 12 weeks for family caregiving (not combined — each has its own 12-week limit per benefit year).

**How to apply**: Same portal — myleavebenefits.nj.gov. Apply in advance when possible (for planned events like birth or adoption); for caregiving, apply when the need arises.

**FLI ≠ FMLA**: Federal FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act) protects your *job* (up to 12 weeks unpaid leave). FLI pays you *money* during leave. They can run concurrently. FMLA requires employer notice and applies to employers with 50+ employees. NJ Family Leave Act (NJFLA) extends job protection for employers with 30+ employees. Know which law applies to your employer — FLI payment is available regardless of employer size, but job protection depends on the employer size thresholds.

### ANCHOR (Affordable NJ Communities for Homeowners and Renters)

**What it is**: NJ's primary property tax relief program. Replaced the old Homestead Benefit. Provides a direct payment (not a deduction) to eligible homeowners and renters to offset property taxes.

**Who qualifies**:
- Homeowners: Must have owned and occupied your primary residence in NJ on October 1 of the benefit year. Income limits apply (currently around $250,000 for homeowners; verify annually). No age requirement.
- Renters: Must have rented a primary NJ residence on October 1. Lower income limit (~$150,000). Renters have always been included in this program but many don't know it.

**What it pays**:
- Homeowners: $1,500 (income under $150K) or $1,000 (income $150K–$250K)
- Renters: $450

**How to apply**:
- Online at anchor.nj.gov or by phone at 1-888-238-1233
- You'll receive an ANCHOR ID and PIN in the mail once you're on the tax rolls
- Applications open in the fall for the prior tax year; deadline is typically around January 31
- File annually — it doesn't auto-renew

**Key**: Many people miss this because they don't receive the mailer or don't know renters qualify. Apply every year you're eligible.

### Senior Freeze (Property Tax Reimbursement)

**What it is**: For NJ residents 65+ (or disabled receiving Social Security Disability), this program reimburses the *increase* in your property taxes over a base year. Once established, if your taxes go up, the state pays the difference.

**Who qualifies**:
- Age 65+ or receiving Social Security Disability
- Have lived in NJ for at least 10 years, and in your current home for at least 3 years
- Income limits (typically around $150,000 — verify annually)
- Must apply and establish a "base year" — the year you first qualify sets your baseline property tax amount

**Why it matters**: In Atlantic County where property taxes are high and can increase significantly, this program can save thousands over time. It's most valuable if you plan to stay long-term.

**How to apply**: File Form PTR-1 (first-time applicants) or PTR-2 (continuing) with the NJ Division of Taxation. File by October 31 each year.

### Veterans and Disabled Veterans Property Tax Exemptions

**Veterans exemption**: NJ veterans who served during specified periods and were honorably discharged receive a $250/year property tax deduction. Apply at your local tax assessor's office (in Galloway: Galloway Township Tax Assessor).

**Disabled veterans exemption**: Veterans with service-connected disability rated at 100% by the VA are exempt from *all* property taxes on their primary residence in NJ. This is one of the most significant financial benefits in NJ for eligible veterans — property tax bills in Galloway can be $6,000–$12,000+/year.

Apply with: DD-214, VA disability rating letter, and a notarized application at the local assessor's office.

**Surviving spouses** of veterans may also qualify — rules vary. Check with the assessor.

### SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program)

**What it is**: Federal food assistance program (formerly food stamps) administered by NJ's Division of Family Development. Provides an EBT card loaded monthly for grocery purchases.

**Who qualifies**:
- Gross income at or below 185% of federal poverty level (for NJ residents — check current figures)
- Net income requirements also apply
- Citizenship/immigration status requirements
- Working-age adults without dependents may have work requirement obligations

**What it pays**: Varies by household size. Average benefit for a single person is roughly $200–300/month; family of four roughly $600–700/month (these are approximations — the actual amount depends on income and household composition).

**How to apply**:
- Online at mynjhelps.gov
- Or at your county Board of Social Services — in Atlantic County, this is the Atlantic County Department of Human Services (609-345-3243)
- You'll need: proof of identity, residency, income, expenses (rent, utilities), and household composition

**Who might not know**: SNAP is commonly associated with very low-income households, but after income spikes from the pandemic, the income thresholds are higher than many people assume. If you've had a job loss or reduced income, checking eligibility is worth doing.

### Medicaid / NJ FamilyCare

**What it is**: Health insurance for NJ residents who meet income requirements. For NJ, this includes adults up to 138% of the federal poverty level and children/families at higher thresholds.

**Who qualifies**: Low-to-moderate income NJ residents. Income limits are lower than SNAP — this is a program for those without access to employer-sponsored or marketplace insurance.

**How to apply**: mynjhelps.gov — the same portal as SNAP. Applications are processed together.

**For most working adults with employer insurance**: Not directly applicable, but relevant if you experience a gap in coverage (job loss, change of employment, etc.). Medicaid can fill gaps if your income drops below threshold.

### NJ Earned Income Tax Credit (NJEITC)

**What it is**: State-level version of the federal Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC). If you qualify for the federal EITC, you automatically qualify for the NJ version.

**What it pays**: NJ's EITC is a percentage of the federal credit — currently 40%. If your federal EITC is $1,000, NJ adds another $400.

**Who qualifies**: Working individuals and families below income thresholds that vary by filing status and number of children. For 2024, a single filer with no children qualifies up to ~$18K income; the range increases significantly with children.

**How to apply**: Claimed on your NJ state income tax return (NJ-1040). If you already claim the federal EITC, make sure you're claiming the state version too — many people don't know the NJ version exists.

## Key Considerations

**All these programs have deadlines**: UI has a waiting week and benefit year limits. ANCHOR applications close annually. Senior Freeze has an October 31 deadline. TDI has a 30-day application window. Missing a deadline often means missing a year of benefits.

**Apply before you need it (when possible)**: If you're expecting a baby, understand the TDI + FLI sequence before the birth. If you're 65, set a reminder to look at Senior Freeze the year you qualify.

**Overpayments are serious**: If you receive UI or TDI while working and don't report income, that's fraud and creates an overpayment you're required to repay plus potential penalties. Report all income accurately during claims.

**Check annually**: Benefit amounts, income limits, and program rules change. ANCHOR payments changed significantly from year to year in recent history. Verify current figures each year when applying.

**Taxes on benefits**: UI benefits are taxable income at the federal level (NJ does not tax UI). TDI and FLI are generally not taxed at the NJ level. ANCHOR payments are generally not taxable. Track what you receive for tax filing purposes.

## Getting Started

1. **ANCHOR**: If you're a homeowner or renter in NJ, check anchor.nj.gov. If you've been in NJ for at least one full tax year, you may already have a benefit year waiting for you to claim.
2. **Assess your payroll deductions**: Look at your pay stub. You're paying into UI, TDI, and FLI now. Know that these exist so you can activate them when needed.
3. **Veterans**: If you or your household has any veteran status, contact the Galloway Assessor's office and ask about exemptions. A 100% disabled veteran who doesn't know about the full exemption is leaving potentially $10,000+/year unclaimed.
4. **Job change or loss**: The moment you lose a job, file for UI. Don't wait. The waiting week already costs you — further delay costs more.
5. **Tax filing**: When you file NJ taxes, check whether you qualify for NJEITC and/or other NJ tax credits. Tax prep software handles this if you use it; if you file manually, review NJ-1040 instructions.

## Common Mistakes

**Not applying for ANCHOR as a renter**: Renters have been eligible since the program changed — most renters don't know. If you rent your primary home in NJ and your income is under ~$150K, you qualify for $450/year by just filing.

**Waiting to file UI**: The waiting week is mandatory — it starts when you file, not when you lost your job. Delay just loses you money. File the same week you become unemployed.

**Confusing TDI and workers' comp**: TDI is for non-work-related disabilities. Workers' comp is for injuries/illnesses caused by work. They're separate programs filed through different channels.

**Missing the FLI window for bonding**: You can use FLI for bonding up to one year after the child's birth, adoption, or placement. You don't have to take it immediately — but many parents don't know it's available after the birth, and it expires if not used within that year.

**Not establishing a Senior Freeze base year**: If you qualify at 65 and don't file, you don't establish a base year. The program is most valuable over time — the earlier you start, the lower your base year taxes, and the more protection you accumulate as taxes rise. File the first year you're eligible even if the benefit seems small.
