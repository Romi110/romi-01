# NJ Employment Law
*The laws NJ employers get burned on — what you must know before hiring anyone in New Jersey*

---

## What Is It

New Jersey has some of the most employee-protective employment laws in the country. Federal law sets the floor; NJ consistently exceeds it. For a small business owner, this means obligations that don't exist in most other states: mandatory earned sick leave, near-universal anti-discrimination protections, family leave that goes beyond FMLA, and an independent contractor test so strict that it's effectively a presumption of employment.

This file covers the laws that cost NJ employers money and legal exposure when ignored — not to be alarmist, but because the gap between what a business owner assumes applies and what actually applies in NJ is significant.

---

## Why It Matters

NJ's Division of Civil Rights, Department of Labor, and private plaintiff attorneys actively enforce these laws. The penalties for earned sick leave violations start at $100/day. Misclassifying an employee as a contractor can result in back taxes, penalties, and civil suit. Non-compete agreements are largely unenforceable. Understanding these laws before you hire determines whether your first employee is an asset or a liability.

---

## Core Concepts

### The ABC Test: Employee vs. Independent Contractor

NJ uses the **ABC test** — one of the strictest contractor classification standards in the US. Under NJ law, a worker is presumed to be an **employee** unless the hiring party can prove **all three** of the following:

**A** — The worker is free from control and direction in performing the work, both under the contract and in practice.
**B** — The worker performs work that is *outside the usual course of business* of the hiring entity, OR the work is performed *outside all places of business*.
**C** — The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business.

The B prong is what catches most businesses. If you hire someone to work in your retail store, they're doing work that is inside your usual course of business — they fail the B test and are an employee by law, regardless of what your contract says.

**Consequences of misclassification:**
- Back payment of unemployment insurance contributions (employer + employee share)
- Back payment of TDI (Temporary Disability Insurance) and FLI contributions
- Penalties from NJ DOL: up to 25% of wages owed
- Potential civil suit under wage payment laws
- IRS payroll tax liability at the federal level

**Legitimate 1099 contractors in a retail context**: true specialists who aren't doing core retail work — a one-time IT consultant, an accountant, a photographer for a campaign. Not regular workers filling ongoing operational roles.

### Earned Sick Leave (NJ Earned Sick Leave Act, 2018)

Every NJ employer, regardless of size, must provide earned sick leave to employees working in NJ. No minimum employee count.

**Accrual:** 1 hour of sick leave for every 30 hours worked. Cap of 40 hours per benefit year.

**Use:** Employees can use it starting on the 120th day of employment. It can be used for:
- The employee's own or a family member's illness, injury, or preventive care
- School or childcare closures due to public health emergency
- Domestic or sexual violence (safe leave)

**Carryover:** Up to 40 hours carry over to the next benefit year. You can elect to pay out unused leave at year-end instead of carrying it over.

**Notice:** Employees must give advance notice if the need is foreseeable (medical appointments, etc.). For unforeseeable illness, they must notify you as soon as possible.

**Record-keeping:** Maintain records of hours worked and sick leave accrued/used for 5 years.

**Penalty for violation:** $100–$1,000 per employee per violation. The NJ DOL enforces this actively and there is a private right of action (employees can sue you directly).

**Practical setup:** Use payroll software (Gusto, QuickBooks Payroll, ADP) that tracks sick leave accrual automatically. Publish a written sick leave policy and provide the NJ-required notice to employees at hire.

### NJ Law Against Discrimination (NJLAD)

The NJLAD is broader than federal anti-discrimination law (Title VII) in two important ways:
1. **No employee minimum.** Title VII applies to employers with 15+ employees. The NJLAD applies to **every employer**, including a sole proprietor with one employee.
2. **More protected classes.** In addition to federal protected classes (race, color, religion, sex, national origin, disability, age 40+), NJLAD adds: ancestry, marital status, civil union status, domestic partnership status, affectional or sexual orientation, gender identity and expression, atypical hereditary cellular or blood trait, and liability for service in the armed forces.

**What this means in practice:**
- Hiring decisions, terminations, promotions, and any adverse employment action must be based on legitimate business reasons — not any protected characteristic
- Workplace harassment based on any protected class is actionable — even by a single employee against another in a small shop
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding are explicitly protected — you must provide reasonable accommodations
- Retaliation against someone who complains about discrimination is independently illegal

**Liability:** There is no damages cap under the NJLAD. Claims can include back pay, front pay, compensatory damages, punitive damages, and attorney fees. Small business owners are personally liable (not just the entity) in some circumstances.

**Practical steps:**
- Develop a simple written anti-harassment/discrimination policy and have employees acknowledge it
- Document every employment decision (hire, fire, discipline) with a business reason
- If an employee complains about harassment or discrimination, take it seriously and document your response

### NJ Family Leave Act (NJFLA) and Family Leave Insurance (FLI)

**NJ Family Leave Act (NJFLA):**
- Applies to employers with **30+ employees** (broader than FMLA's 50+)
- Eligible employees can take up to 12 weeks of **unpaid, job-protected leave** per 24-month period to care for a family member or bond with a new child
- You must hold the employee's position (or an equivalent position)
- Does not apply to the employee's own serious health condition (that's covered by the federal FMLA)

**NJ Family Leave Insurance (FLI):**
- A state-run paid leave program funded by employee payroll deductions
- Employees can receive up to 85% of their average weekly wage (capped at 70% of the statewide average weekly wage) for up to 12 weeks
- Employers do not pay FLI premiums — employees do through withholding
- But you must withhold the correct amount and remit it to the state
- Employees can use FLI concurrently with NJFLA leave

**Temporary Disability Insurance (TDI):**
- NJ requires employers to maintain TDI coverage for employees — either through the NJ State Plan (via payroll withholding) or an approved private plan
- Provides wage replacement when an employee can't work due to a non-work-related illness, injury, or pregnancy
- Employer contribution: a small percentage of taxable wages (varies annually)
- Significant: pregnancy is covered. An employee on maternity leave will likely use TDI for the first 6–8 weeks, then FLI for bonding

### NJ WARN Act

Federal WARN requires 60 days notice before mass layoffs for employers with 100+ employees. NJ's WARN Act is more aggressive:

- Applies to employers with **100+ employees** (same threshold)
- Requires **90 days notice** (vs. federal 60)
- Applies to layoffs of **25+ workers** (vs. federal 50)
- **Penalty:** 1 week of severance per year of employment, mandatory, regardless of any contractual arrangement, if proper notice isn't given
- 2023 amendment added mandatory severance even when full notice is given for qualifying mass layoffs — NJ is one of a small number of states with mandatory severance requirements

Applies only at scale. Relevant if you ever have a larger operation or are acquiring/being acquired.

### Non-Compete Agreements in NJ

NJ courts apply a reasonableness test to non-competes but are skeptical of them, especially for:
- Low-wage employees
- Employees who were laid off (not terminated for cause)
- Agreements with no geographic or time limitation
- Agreements that prevent someone from earning a living in their field

As of 2025, NJ has been moving toward broader non-compete restrictions (bills introduced in the legislature multiple times). The practical current state: broad non-competes are not reliably enforceable. Narrow, time-limited, geographically specific agreements protecting legitimate business interests (customer lists, trade secrets) have a better chance.

**What does work:**
- **Non-solicitation of customers** — agreeing not to solicit specific customers you worked with
- **Non-solicitation of employees** — agreeing not to recruit co-workers
- **Confidentiality/NDA** — protecting actual trade secrets and confidential business information

For a retail business: skip the non-compete, use a confidentiality agreement for anyone with access to pricing, vendor relationships, or business systems.

### Wage and Hour Laws

NJ minimum wage (2025): **$15.49/hour** for most employers. Tipped workers: $5.62/hour cash wage (if tips bring total to at least $15.49; if not, employer makes up the difference).

**Overtime:** 1.5x regular rate for hours over 40/week. No daily overtime in NJ (some states have daily rules; NJ doesn't).

**Pay frequency:** Must pay employees at least twice per month (bi-weekly is standard). Withhold and remit taxes on each pay cycle.

**Wage deductions:** You cannot deduct from wages for breakage, cash register shortages, or customer walkouts unless the employee consented in writing and the deduction doesn't bring wages below minimum wage.

**Tip pooling:** NJ allows tip pooling among front-of-house employees. You cannot include managers or owners in a tip pool.

**Record-keeping requirement:** 5 years for payroll records including hours worked, wages paid, deductions.

### Unemployment Insurance

NJ unemployment claims are experience-rated — the more former employees who file successful claims, the higher your UI tax rate goes. Your rate starts at around 0.4% and can rise to over 5% of taxable wages for employers with repeated layoffs or separations.

**What increases your rate:**
- Laying employees off (even temporarily)
- Employees quitting "for cause" (hostile workplace, non-payment of wages, etc.)
- Firing employees without documented legitimate business reason

**What doesn't increase your rate:**
- Employees who voluntarily quit without good cause attributable to you
- Seasonal employees who knew employment was temporary

**Practical:** Document all terminations. "It wasn't working out" is not a documented reason. "Attendance policy violation — 3 written warnings on X/X/X dates" is.

### Workers' Compensation

NJ requires all employers with employees to carry workers' compensation insurance. No exceptions based on company size.

- Purchase through a licensed NJ insurer or the State Insurance Fund
- Premium is based on payroll and job classification (retail is relatively low risk vs. construction)
- Covers medical expenses and partial wage replacement for work-related injuries
- Failure to carry: civil and criminal penalties, and personal liability for injured employees

**Where to get it:** Retail workers' comp is standard coverage — any commercial insurer (Travelers, Hartford, CNA) or your business insurance broker. Bundle it with general liability for a BOP (Business Owner's Policy) discount.

## Key Considerations

**NJ assumes employee status.** The ABC test puts the burden on you to prove contractor status. When in doubt, treat the worker as an employee.

**Small employers are not exempt from most NJ laws.** Earned sick leave: 1 employee. NJLAD: 1 employee. TDI: 1 employee. Workers' comp: 1 employee. The federal "small employer" exemptions largely don't exist at the NJ level.

**Retaliation is separately actionable.** If an employee complains about a wage violation, safety issue, or discrimination, and you fire them shortly after, that's retaliation — separately illegal from whatever the underlying complaint was.

**Handbooks are not optional.** A simple employee handbook with policies on: sick leave, anti-harassment, attendance, social media, and confidentiality is standard risk management. It doesn't need to be 50 pages — it needs to be signed and dated.

**Payroll software is not optional.** Manual payroll in NJ with its multiple withholdings (NJ income tax, SDI, FLI, UI) is error-prone. Gusto, QuickBooks Payroll, and Paychex all handle NJ-specific withholdings automatically and generate the required reports (WR-30 quarterly wage report).

## Getting Started

1. **Before first hire:** Register for a NJ employer ID at the NJ Division of Revenue. Required to withhold and remit NJ income tax and payroll taxes.
2. **Set up payroll software** — Gusto is the modern small business standard (~$40/month base + per-employee fee). It handles NJ withholdings, generates W-2s, and files required state reports.
3. **Get workers' comp insurance** — Required before day one. Bundle with your BOP.
4. **Draft an offer letter and simple handbook** — Two documents: offer letter with title, salary, at-will status; handbook with sick leave policy, harassment policy, attendance expectations.
5. **Complete I-9 on day one** — Federal requirement; must have acceptable identity/work authorization documents in hand.
6. **Report new hire within 20 days** — NJ new hire reporting to the NJ Child Support Institute (newjerseychildsupport.com). Required for all new and re-hired employees.

## Common Mistakes

**Calling someone a contractor when they do the same work as employees.** Fails the ABC B prong immediately. The label on the agreement doesn't control; the actual work does.

**Not tracking sick leave accrual.** "We'll deal with it if they ask" is not a policy. The NJ DOL expects records. Track it in your payroll system from day one.

**Firing someone the week after they file a complaint.** The timing alone creates a retaliation inference. Document business reasons and create a paper trail before taking adverse action.

**Using a form employee handbook from another state.** NJ has specific required policies that don't exist federally. Get a handbook template from a NJ employment attorney or an NJ-compliant HR platform like Gusto.

**Not posting required notices.** NJ requires specific posters in the workplace: Earned Sick Leave, NJLAD, Wage and Hour, Workers' Comp, UI. The NJ DOL provides these free at njdol.com. Failure to post is a fine.
