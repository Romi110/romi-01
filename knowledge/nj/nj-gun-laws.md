# New Jersey Gun Laws
*What NJ actually requires to own, purchase, transport, and carry firearms — step by step*

---

## What Is It

New Jersey has among the most restrictive firearms laws in the United States. This file covers what you need to know as a resident: the documents required to purchase, the permits needed to carry, what's legal to own, how to transport lawfully, and what happens if you get it wrong. NJ gun law is dense, and the cost of a mistake is high — some violations are fourth-degree crimes (felony-level in most states).

This is practical and accurate as of 2026, but law changes — verify current statute or consult an NJ attorney for anything involving actual legal exposure.

---

## Why It Matters

If you own or want to own firearms in NJ, not knowing the rules isn't a defense. NJ is a state where otherwise law-abiding gun owners have been arrested for transport violations, magazine capacity violations, and failure to have the right paperwork. The system is navigable — but you have to know it before you act, not after.

Self-reliance includes knowing your legal rights and limits around lawful defense tools.

## Core Concepts

### Firearms ID Card (FID)

The Firearms Purchaser Identification Card (commonly called the FID or FPIC) is the baseline document every NJ resident who wants to own a firearm needs. Without it, you cannot legally purchase a long gun (rifle or shotgun) in NJ, and cannot legally own any firearm.

**What it covers**: Purchasing rifles, shotguns, and shotgun/rifle ammunition. Also required for handgun purchases (along with a separate permit — see below).

**How to apply**:
1. Apply through your local police department (the department in the municipality where you reside). In Galloway, this is the Galloway Township Police Department.
2. Complete the SP-642 application (NJ State Police form). As of recent law changes, applications are now submitted through the NJ Firearms Application Registration System (FARS) — an online portal at firearms.njportal.com. You no longer submit paper to the station in most cases.
3. Consent to a background check (state + federal) and mental health records check.
4. Submit two references (non-family members) who will be contacted.
5. Pay the application fee (~$5 for FID, $2 per handgun permit — though fees have changed; verify current amounts at application time).
6. Fingerprints: Required for your first FID application. Go through an Identogo or similar NJ-approved fingerprinting provider. Schedule separately; costs ~$60–70.

**Timeline**: Legally required to be processed within 30 days (60 days for initial applicants requiring fingerprints). In practice, many departments take longer — 60–120 days is common, some longer. There's no practical enforcement mechanism for delays. Apply early.

**Duration**: FID cards do not expire. Once issued, you keep it permanently. It is a physical card.

**Denial**: If denied, you have 30 days to appeal to the Superior Court in your county.

### Permit to Purchase a Handgun

For every handgun purchase — whether from a licensed dealer or a private sale — you need a separate Permit to Purchase a Handgun (also called a "pistol permit" or P2P). This is in addition to the FID.

- Apply through the same FARS online system.
- Each permit covers one handgun purchase.
- Permits expire 90 days after issuance (one 90-day extension available upon request).
- A new permit is required for each handgun purchase — there's no bulk permit.
- You must already have an FID to apply for a handgun permit.

**Private sales**: In NJ, all firearm transfers (including private sales, gifts between non-immediate family) must go through a licensed dealer (FFL) for a background check. No private hand-to-hand sales without dealer involvement. Exceptions exist for immediate family transfers (parent to child, spouse to spouse) — but even these have documentation requirements.

### Carry Permit (Permit to Carry a Handgun)

NJ's carry permit is technically "shall-issue" following the 2022 Supreme Court decision (*Bruen*), meaning the state can no longer require you to demonstrate a "justifiable need" beyond what any law-abiding citizen has. In practice, NJ responded by adding extensive new requirements that make it difficult and expensive, though not impossible.

**Requirements (as of 2026)**:
- Must be 21+
- Must have FID and be otherwise legally eligible to own firearms
- Must complete a state-approved firearms training course (at least 16 hours, including range qualification)
- Must submit endorsements from four reputable individuals who are not family members
- Must pass a background check
- Must not have any disqualifying criminal, mental health, or domestic history
- Liability insurance is required (at least $300,000 — a controversial requirement currently under legal challenge)
- Submit through FARS; fees apply

**Where you can't carry (even with a permit)**: NJ added extensive "sensitive places" restrictions post-Bruen that are actively being litigated. As of the current writing, restricted places include (but are not limited to): schools, government buildings, hospitals, bars and restaurants that serve alcohol, entertainment venues, public parks, libraries, museums, and more. These restrictions have been challenged in court — the legal landscape is shifting. Verify current restricted places status when applying or before carrying.

**Reality check**: Getting a carry permit in NJ is significantly more burdensome than most states but is now legally possible. Expect the process to take months and cost several hundred dollars including training. But for a committed, law-abiding applicant, it's achievable.

### What You Can Own — Permitted Firearms

NJ restricts what firearms can be owned, not just how they're acquired:

**Handguns**: Legal to own with FID + P2P. Standard semi-automatic pistols and revolvers are generally permitted.

**Rifles and shotguns**: Legal with FID. Standard bolt-action, lever-action, semi-automatic configurations are generally permitted.

**"Assault firearm" restrictions**: NJ has an assault weapons ban. A firearm meets the definition if it is a semi-automatic that meets certain criteria:
- Semi-automatic rifle with a fixed magazine capacity exceeding 15 rounds, OR
- Semi-automatic rifle that can accept a detachable magazine AND has two or more of the following: folding/telescoping stock, pistol grip, bayonet mount, flash suppressor, grenade launcher
- Similar criteria for pistols and shotguns

Common rifles like the AR-15 (in standard configuration) are generally banned under this definition. NJ-compliant versions exist (fixed stock, no pistol grip, 10-round mag) but verify the specific configuration before purchase.

**Magazine capacity**: 10-round limit. Magazines holding more than 10 rounds are illegal to possess in NJ. This applies even if you purchased them legally in another state. If you move to NJ with high-capacity magazines, you are required to surrender, sell out of state, or render them permanently inoperable.

**Hollow-point ammunition**: Legal to purchase and possess at home or at the range. Legal to transport directly to/from a range. Carrying hollow-points while otherwise transporting a firearm (e.g., in your car going somewhere other than a range) may create legal exposure — this is a gray area. Many NJ gun owners avoid carrying hollow-points outside the home to stay conservative.

**Suppressors/NFA items**: Generally illegal in NJ. No suppressor ownership, no short-barrel rifles or shotguns, no machine guns.

### Transport Rules

How you transport a firearm in NJ is heavily regulated. Getting this wrong is a crime.

**The legal transport standard for a handgun**:
- Unloaded
- In a closed and fastened case, or locked container, or in the trunk
- Not accessible from the passenger compartment
- Traveling directly to/from a range, gun dealer, gunsmith, or other legally permitted destination

**For rifles/shotguns**: Unloaded, broken down in a case, in trunk or area inaccessible from passenger compartment.

**"Traveling" protection**: The federal Firearm Owners Protection Act (FOPA) provides some protection for travelers passing through NJ with legally owned firearms — but this applies only to through-travel (not staying overnight), and the firearm must be legal in both origin and destination states. NJ does not extend this protection broadly, and NJ courts have interpreted it narrowly. Do not rely on FOPA for in-state activity.

**Common transport mistake**: Having a loaded handgun in the center console or glove box — this is illegal in NJ regardless of whether you have a carry permit (though with a carry permit, the rules change for what you're authorized to carry on your person).

### Safe Storage

NJ law requires safe storage of firearms when not in use if minors are present or may be present in the home. Practically, a gun safe or lock box is the standard approach. This is also common-sense self-reliance: a firearm that can be accessed by unauthorized people (including during a break-in) is a liability.

NJ's "Childproof Handgun" law also requires that any handgun sold in NJ after a certain trigger date must be a "personalized" (smart) gun if/when such technology is certified — a long-standing and contentious provision currently under legal revision.

## Key Considerations

**Everything is interconnected**: You need the FID before you can get a handgun permit. You need both to purchase a handgun. You need additional training to apply for a carry permit. Don't try to shortcut the sequence.

**Reciprocity**: NJ does not recognize carry permits from any other state. If you have a permit from another state, it does not apply in NJ. If you're visiting from another state and carrying, you are violating NJ law.

**Penalties**: Unlawful possession of a weapon in NJ is a second-degree crime (potential 5–10 years prison). Transport violations are fourth-degree crimes. These are serious charges — NJ prosecutors have pursued these cases against otherwise law-abiding people. Know the rules.

**FARS system**: The online application system has improved the process vs. the old paper system. Use it at firearms.njportal.com. If you have questions, call your local PD's records bureau.

**Police department contact for applications**: In Galloway, contact the Galloway Township Police Department records bureau. They process FID/P2P applications locally and can answer questions about the process timeline.

## Getting Started

**Step 1 — Apply for your FID** (if you don't have one):
1. Go to firearms.njportal.com
2. Create an account and complete the SP-642 application online
3. Schedule fingerprints through Identogo (identogo.com) — select NJ State Police firearms
4. Complete your mental health waiver (part of the application)
5. Provide two references (they'll be contacted by email or phone)
6. Wait — follow up with your local PD records bureau after 30 days if no update

**Step 2 — Apply for a Handgun Permit** (once FID is issued or in hand):
- Same FARS system, different application type
- Each permit is per-handgun; apply for as many as you're buying

**Step 3 — Purchase**:
- From a licensed NJ dealer: bring FID + handgun permit, complete Form 4473 (federal), background check runs at point of sale, wait for approval
- Wait period: NJ has a 7-day waiting period for handgun purchases

**Step 4 — Safe storage**: Get a secure location before the firearm comes home.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying before your paperwork is approved**: The permit must be in hand before purchase. A dealer cannot legally sell without it.

**Transporting loaded**: Even in a case, a loaded firearm in transport (outside of a carry permit context) is illegal. Load only at the range.

**Bringing magazines from other states**: High-capacity magazines are illegal in NJ regardless of where they were purchased. Don't bring them across the border.

**Assuming online purchases ship to your door**: No — any firearm purchased online must be shipped to a licensed NJ dealer (FFL), who handles the transfer and charges a fee (~$25–50). You pick it up from the dealer with your paperwork.

**Letting a permit expire**: Handgun permits expire in 90 days. If you don't complete the purchase in time, you need a new permit.
