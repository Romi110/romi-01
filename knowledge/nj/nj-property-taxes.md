# NJ Property Taxes
*Highest in the US — how assessment works, how to fight it, and how to legally reduce what you pay*

---

## What Is It

New Jersey has the highest effective property tax rate of any state in the US: median effective rate around 2.2–2.4% of assessed value. The average NJ homeowner pays over $9,000/year in property taxes. In Atlantic County specifically, property taxes on a median home run $5,000–$8,000/year depending on municipality.

Understanding how NJ property taxes work — how assessment is calculated, when you can appeal, what exemptions and programs exist — is essential for anyone who owns or plans to own property in NJ. The difference between an incorrect assessment and a correct one can be $1,000–$3,000/year permanently.

---

## Why It Matters

Property taxes in NJ are a fixed annual cost that:
- Directly affects the real cost of homeownership (must be factored into any buy vs. rent calculation)
- Are fully deductible for NJ income tax purposes (unlike the federal $10,000 SALT cap)
- Can be appealed if your assessment is too high — and in NJ, they frequently are
- Can be reduced through exemptions and programs that many eligible homeowners never claim

If you own a home in Galloway Township, Atlantic County, and never appeal your assessment, you may be paying tax on an inflated value without knowing it.

## Core Concepts

### How NJ Property Tax Is Calculated

The formula: **Assessed Value × Tax Rate = Annual Tax Bill**

But this is less simple than it looks because both sides of the equation have layers.

**Assessed Value:**
- NJ law requires municipalities to assess property at 100% of true market value. In practice, most municipalities assess at a percentage of market value — the **Assessment Ratio** (also called the Chapter 123 ratio).
- Each county has an **Equalization Ratio** set annually by the NJ Division of Taxation. In Atlantic County, this ratio fluctuates but is often in the 60–90% range — meaning assessed value is typically 60–90% of actual market value.
- The equalization ratio is what's used in tax appeals to determine whether your assessment is fair (see below).

**Tax Rate:**
- Set annually by your municipality based on the budget divided by the total assessed value of all property in the township
- Atlantic County 2024 breakdown (approximate for Galloway Township): municipal rate ~$0.50, county rate ~$0.55, school rate ~$1.20 per $100 of assessed value — roughly $2.25–$2.40 per $100 total
- School district taxes are typically the largest component (50%+ of the total bill) — property taxes fund NJ public schools heavily

**Example:** Home with assessed value of $300,000 at 2.35% effective rate = $7,050/year in property taxes

### The Equalization Ratio and What It Means for Appeals

When appealing a property tax assessment, NJ doesn't require your assessed value to exactly equal market value. Instead, the **Chapter 123 rule** says your assessment is acceptable if it falls within 15% of the county's equalization ratio applied to your market value.

Example:
- County equalization ratio: 80%
- Your home's market value (determined by recent sales of comparable homes): $400,000
- Acceptable assessed value range: 80% × $400,000 = $320,000, ±15% → $272,000–$368,000
- If your assessed value is above $368,000, you have grounds to appeal

This matters because you don't need to prove your assessment equals market value — only that it exceeds the ratio corridor. A good comparable sales analysis can establish this.

### Appealing Your Property Tax Assessment

**When:** The annual appeal deadline in NJ is **April 1** of the tax year (or May 1 if the municipality is undergoing a revaluation). For added assessment notices: 45 days from the date of notice.

**Where:** File with the Atlantic County Board of Taxation (for property assessed under $1 million). For higher-value properties or if you lose at the County level, appeal to the NJ Tax Court.

**How to build your case:**
1. Get your property record card from the municipal assessor's office — it shows the data they used (square footage, number of rooms, condition, features). Errors here are common and can be corrected without a formal appeal.
2. Pull recent sales (last 12 months) of comparable properties in your neighborhood from Zillow, Realtor.com, or the county records. "Comparable" means similar size, age, style, condition.
3. Calculate the implied assessment ratio for each comp: (assessed value of comp) ÷ (sale price of comp). If your home's ratio is higher than the comps, you have a case.
4. File Form A-1 (real property appeals form) with the County Board of Taxation before April 1. Filing fee: $5 for residential under $150,000 assessed value; $25 for higher.

**Outcome:** The County Board will schedule a hearing (informal or formal). Many appeals settle before the hearing. A successful appeal reduces your assessed value and generates a refund of overpaid taxes.

**Hiring a tax appeal attorney:** Contingency fee is standard — typically 33% of the tax savings in the first year. For a large overpayment, this can be worth it. For smaller cases, self-represented appeals work well given the informal nature of County Board hearings.

**When to seriously consider appealing:**
- Your home's assessed value is significantly higher than recent sale prices of similar homes
- Your municipality recently completed a revaluation (values often spike; appeals spike with them)
- You purchased your home and the sale price was lower than your assessed value (this is direct evidence)

### ANCHOR Program (Affordable NJ Communities for Homeowners and Renters)

**ANCHOR** (formerly the Homestead Benefit) is NJ's largest property tax relief program. It provides a direct benefit payment to homeowners and renters — not a deduction, an actual payment.

**Homeowners (2024 benefit year):**
- Income under $150,000: $1,500 benefit
- Income $150,000–$250,000: $1,000 benefit
- Must have owned and occupied your NJ principal residence on October 1 of the tax year
- File annually (NJ Division of Taxation sends notifications)

**Renters:**
- Income under $150,000: $450 benefit
- Must have rented a principal residence in NJ on October 1 of the tax year

**How to file:** Online at anchor.njit.gov or by phone. Application period typically opens in the fall. Many eligible homeowners never file and leave this money unclaimed.

**The math:** A Galloway homeowner earning $120,000 who doesn't file ANCHOR is leaving $1,500/year on the table. Over 10 years: $15,000.

### Senior Freeze (PTR — Property Tax Reimbursement)

For homeowners 65+ or disabled, receiving Social Security:
- Income limit (2024): $150,000/year
- Freezes property taxes at a base year amount — the state reimburses any increase above that base
- Designed so seniors on fixed incomes aren't taxed out of their homes as values rise

Not directly relevant at 28, but relevant for parents or family members who own NJ property.

### Other Exemptions and Programs

**Veterans Deduction:** $250/year property tax deduction for any NJ veteran who was honorably discharged. Wartime veterans or surviving spouses may qualify for a full exemption.

**Disabled Veterans:** Full property tax exemption for veterans with a 100% service-connected disability. One of the most significant property tax benefits available.

**Senior/Disabled Deduction:** $250/year deduction for residents 65+ or permanently disabled with income under $10,000 (excluding Social Security). Limited but available.

**Farmland Assessment (New Jersey Farmland Assessment Act):** Agricultural land can be assessed at its value for farming purposes rather than development value — massively lower in most cases. Requires 5+ acres actively devoted to agricultural or horticultural use generating at least $500/year in agricultural income. Relevant if you have or plan to have a working farm in NJ. See [nj-right-to-farm.md](nj-right-to-farm.md) for the related protections.

### Galloway Township and Atlantic County Specifics

**Atlantic County Equalization Ratio:** Has historically run in the 70–90% range; check the current year at nj.gov/treasury/taxation.

**Galloway Township Tax Assessor:** Office at 300 E Jimmie Leeds Road, Galloway. Property record cards, exemption applications, and informal review requests are handled here. An informal conversation with the assessor before filing a formal appeal can sometimes resolve obvious errors without the formal process.

**Atlantic County Board of Taxation:** Handles formal appeals. Located at 5901 Main Street, Mays Landing. The County tax appeal process is relatively accessible — hearings are informal, no attorney required.

**NJ ANCHOR benefits for Atlantic County:** File at anchor.njit.gov. Atlantic County has a relatively high participation rate compared to other counties but still leaves significant unclaimed benefits annually.

**Key local numbers to know:**
- Galloway Township Tax Collector: manages billing and payment questions
- Galloway Township Assessor: manages valuations, exemptions, and informal appeals
- Atlantic County Board of Taxation: formal appeals

## Key Considerations

**The assessment is not automatically correct.** Municipalities complete formal revaluations infrequently (every 10–20 years in many towns). Between revaluations, assessed values drift away from market reality. Checking your assessment annually against comparable sales costs nothing.

**Appeal every year if you're over-assessed.** The $5 filing fee and 2 hours of work for a successful appeal can save $1,000+/year permanently (or until the next revaluation). Once you win an appeal, the lower assessed value sticks — you don't have to re-argue it annually.

**ANCHOR is not automatic.** You must file. The state sends notifications but many homeowners miss them. Bookmark anchor.njit.gov and file every fall.

**Property taxes are the biggest variable in NJ housing cost.** A $400,000 home in Galloway paying 2.35% effective rate costs $9,400/year in taxes. A $400,000 home in a lower-rate township costs $7,500. Over 30 years at the same purchase price, the difference in taxes alone is $57,000. Know the effective rate before buying.

**NJ property taxes are fully deductible on your NJ state return** (not subject to the federal $10,000 SALT cap on state income tax filings). This partially offsets the cost for higher earners.

## Getting Started

1. **Find your current assessed value:** Look up your property on the Atlantic County tax records at atlanticcountynj.gov or the Galloway Township tax portal.
2. **Check the current equalization ratio** at nj.gov/treasury/taxation.
3. **Pull 3–5 comparable recent sales** in your neighborhood. Calculate the implied assessment ratio for each. Compare to yours.
4. **If over-assessed:** Request an informal review from the Galloway assessor first. If not resolved, file Form A-1 before April 1.
5. **File ANCHOR** every fall at anchor.njit.gov if you meet the income threshold.

## Common Mistakes

**Assuming the assessment is accurate.** It often isn't, particularly if you bought your home in a rising market after the last revaluation, or if the assessor's property data has errors (wrong square footage, wrong number of bathrooms, non-existent improvements).

**Missing the April 1 appeal deadline.** It's firm. Miss it by a day and you wait another year.

**Not filing ANCHOR.** The #1 missed benefit in NJ. $1,000–$1,500/year left unclaimed because people assume it happens automatically.

**Confusing tax rate with assessment.** A low tax rate on a high assessed value can cost more than a high rate on a correctly assessed value. The effective rate (taxes ÷ market value) is the number that matters.

**Selling without checking for prorations.** At closing in NJ, property taxes are typically prorated from the last payment date. Understand how your outstanding tax liability factors into your net proceeds before signing.
