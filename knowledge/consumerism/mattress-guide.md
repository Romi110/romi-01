# Mattress Guide
*How mattresses are built, what the construction means for how they feel, and how to evaluate any mattress*

---

## What Is It

A mattress buying guide focused on construction — the layers, materials, and engineering that determine how a mattress performs. For specific brand recommendations and price tiers, see [sleep-environment.md](sleep-environment.md), which covers the buying decision thoroughly. This file covers the why behind those recommendations: what's inside a mattress and how to evaluate one you've never encountered.

## Why It Matters

The mattress industry is built on confusing jargon, inflated specs, and brand names that tell you nothing about construction. Understanding the layers lets you assess any mattress — not just the brands in current reviews — and evaluate claims like "7-zone pressure relief" or "cooling gel memory foam" on their actual merits.

## Core Concepts

### The Layer System

Every mattress has the same basic structure: a support core at the bottom, comfort layers in the middle, and a cover on top. Quality, thickness, and material of each layer determine the feel and durability.

**Support core**: provides structural support and prevents bottoming out. The largest component by volume. Two types:
- **Coil systems** (innerspring/hybrid): coil springs under compression support body weight. More breathable than foam, more durable under heavy use.
- **Foam base**: high-density polyfoam (2.0+ lb/ft³) or latex foam forming the structural layer. Common in all-foam mattresses.

**Comfort layers**: the transition between the support core and the sleeper's body. These layers determine initial feel — whether the mattress feels soft, responsive, pressure-relieving, or hot. Typically 2–4 inches total.

**Cover / ticking**: the fabric exterior. Mostly aesthetic, but organic cotton covers breathe better than synthetic. Quilted covers add a thin additional comfort layer.

---

### Coil Types

Not all springs are equivalent. Coil type significantly affects feel, motion isolation, and longevity.

**Pocketed coils (individually wrapped)**: each coil is in its own fabric pocket, independent of adjacent coils. Benefits: much better motion isolation (one person moving doesn't disturb the other), conforms to body curves independently, quieter. The current standard for quality mattresses. Look for high coil counts (800+ in a queen) with thicker gauge steel (14–16 gauge — thicker gauge = more durable).

**Bonnell coils (interconnected hourglass)**: traditional connected coil system. The original innerspring design. When one coil moves, adjacent coils move with it — poor motion isolation. Still used in entry-level and hotel mattresses. Appropriate for single sleepers or budget contexts; not ideal for couples.

**Offset coils**: like Bonnell but with flat-top coils that hinge more smoothly. Better motion isolation than Bonnell, not as good as pocketed. Mid-tier innerspring design, less common in new mattresses.

**Micro coils (comfort layer)**: very small-diameter coils used in the comfort layer of hybrid mattresses, not the support core. Adds breathability and a specific "coil-on-coil" feel that fans describe as bouncy and responsive without the sinkage of foam.

---

### Foam Types

**Memory foam (viscoelastic foam)**: slow-response foam that conforms to body shape under heat and pressure, then slowly returns. The signature feel: you sink in, it hugs. Benefits: excellent pressure relief, very good motion isolation. Drawbacks: retains heat, "stuck" feeling during position changes. Density matters: 4+ lb/ft³ is quality memory foam; 3 lb/ft³ and below degrades faster and sleeps hotter.

**Cooling gel / copper infusion / graphite infusion**: additives to memory foam marketed for temperature regulation. The research is mixed. Gel beads and gel layers have modest cooling benefit in the first hour of contact but don't address the fundamental heat-retention of viscoelastic foam. Open-cell foam processing does more for breathability than additives. Don't pay a premium for cooling gel over quality foam.

**Latex foam**: the most durable foam type. Natural latex (Talalay or Dunlop process) is responsive — it pushes back rather than slowly conforming. More breathable than memory foam. Hypoallergenic. Heavy. Talalay process: more consistent, softer, better pressure relief. Dunlop: denser, firmer, more durable. Natural latex is more expensive than synthetic; blended latex is in between.

**Polyfoam (polyurethane foam)**: the lowest-quality foam, used as filler or base layers. Density below 1.8 lb/ft³ is the sign of a cheap mattress that will sag within 2–3 years. High-density polyfoam (2.0+ lb/ft³) is acceptable as a support base layer. If the primary comfort layer is polyfoam, the mattress is commodity-grade.

---

### Mattress Types

**Innerspring (traditional)**: pocketed or Bonnell coil support core with thin comfort layers. Breathable, durable, responsive. The comfort layer is thin — you feel the support system more directly. Good for people who sleep hot or like a firmer, more traditional feel. Entry-level innersprings use Bonnell; better innersprings use pocketed coils.

**Hybrid**: pocketed coil support core + substantial foam comfort layers (2"+ of memory foam, latex, or micro coils). The most versatile mattress type — combines coil durability and breathability with foam pressure relief and motion isolation. Currently the most popular category for quality mattresses. Most of the recommended brands in [sleep-environment.md](sleep-environment.md) are hybrids.

**All-foam**: foam support base + foam comfort layers (memory foam, latex, polyfoam in various combinations). No coils. Better motion isolation than innerspring, worse breathability than hybrid. Pure memory foam all-foam mattresses sleep the hottest. All-latex is the exception — all-latex mattresses breathe well and are durable.

**Latex**: the premium all-foam option. Natural latex support core + latex comfort layers. Most durable mattress type available. Significantly heavier than other types (a queen natural latex mattress weighs 90–130 lbs). The most responsive and breathable foam mattress. More expensive than comparable hybrids.

### Firmness for Body Type and Sleep Position

The firmness-support relationship is specific to the sleeper's weight and position. A mattress that feels firm to a 130 lb person may feel soft to a 220 lb person on the same mattress.

**By sleep position:**
- **Back sleeper**: needs lumbar support — hips shouldn't sag. Medium to medium-firm (5–7 on a 1–10 scale).
- **Side sleeper**: shoulder and hip must sink to keep spine neutral. Medium to medium-soft (4–6). The most common source of shoulder and hip pain is a mattress too firm for a side sleeper.
- **Stomach sleeper**: hips must not sink (causes lumbar hyperextension). Firm to medium-firm (6–8). The rarest sleep position and the most demanding for spinal alignment.
- **Combination sleeper**: medium (5–6) as the balance point.

**By body weight:**
- Under 130 lbs: softer mattress than the position recommendation — less weight means less sinkage, so what's labeled "medium" feels firm.
- 130–230 lbs: use position recommendation as stated.
- Over 230 lbs: firmer than position recommendation — more weight compresses comfort layers more, so labeled "medium-firm" may feel medium.

---

### Edge Support

Edge support is how much the perimeter of the mattress compresses when sat on or slept near. Poor edge support means rolling off the edge of the sleeping surface — the effective sleeping area shrinks. Important for couples (both people using the full width) and for sitting on the edge to put on shoes.

Better edge support comes from: reinforced perimeter coils, foam encasement around the coil perimeter, or high-density foam edges. All-foam mattresses typically have worse edge support than hybrids. Ask specifically about edge support or check review sites (Sleepopolis, Mattress Clarity) that test this directly.

---

### How to Use a Trial Period

Every mattress worth buying now offers a 100–365 night sleep trial. This is genuinely useful — a mattress feels different in the store (5 minutes, clothes on, nervous) than after 2 weeks of actual sleep.

**Week 1–2**: adjustment period. Many people experience more pressure than expected or find the mattress firmer or softer than anticipated. This is normal — your body is adjusting. Don't evaluate yet.

**Week 3–4**: the real data. Does the pressure at your shoulder or hip wake you? Is your lower back pain better or worse than before? Do you feel heat building up? Are you waking up from your partner's movement?

**Week 6–8**: if you still have doubts at this point, they're probably valid. Initiate the return. Most companies make this process easy.

**Common reasons to return**: shoulder or hip pressure from a mattress too firm for your position (side sleepers especially), lower back discomfort from a mattress too soft, heat retention, or motion transfer disturbing sleep.

## Key Considerations

**Sag and body impressions**: all foam and comfort layers compress over time. An impression of up to 1.5 inches is considered normal under most warranties; above that qualifies for replacement claims. High-density foams and pocketed coils resist this better than low-density foam and Bonnell coils.

**Partner differences**: if you and a partner have significantly different sleep positions, weights, or temperature preferences, a split firmness option (two twin XLs in a king frame) or a mattress with strong edge-to-center range (some brands sell different firmness zones) may be worth considering.

**NJ humidity**: New Jersey summers are humid — a mattress that sleeps hot in a dry climate will sleep hotter here. Favor breathable construction (hybrid over all-foam memory foam, cotton cover over synthetic) if heat is a concern.

## Common Mistakes

**Trusting the showroom feel.** Five minutes lying on a mattress fully clothed, nervous, in bright lighting, next to a salesperson tells you almost nothing about how you'll sleep on it. Use trials aggressively.

**Buying on brand recognition alone.** Sealy, Serta, and Simmons (the "S brands") dominate retail floor space through long-standing dealer relationships, not product quality. Their mid-range products are often outperformed by DTC brands at the same price point.

**"Orthopedic" and "chiropractic-approved" claims.** These terms have no regulated meaning. They're marketing labels, not medical endorsements. Evaluate the construction, not the claim.

**Ignoring the foundation.** A good mattress on a sagging box spring or a slatted bed frame with gaps above 3 inches will sag faster and void some warranties. Confirm your foundation is flat and supportive before the mattress arrives.
