# Camera Buying Guide
*Mirrorless vs. DSLR, sensor size, used vs. new, and the right camera for content creation, travel, and farm documentation*

---

## What Is It

A dedicated camera buying guide for the moment after the phone. [video/vlogging-foundations.md](../video/vlogging-foundations.md) explicitly recommends starting with a smartphone and adding gear only when you've hit a specific limitation. This file is for when that limitation is real: low-light performance, better autofocus, interchangeable lenses, or the ability to shoot professional-quality 4K video.

---

## Core Concepts

### Why Mirrorless Won

As of 2024–2025, the camera market has consolidated around mirrorless interchangeable lens cameras (ILCs). The distinction matters because lens investment follows the camera body — buying lenses for a dying mount is expensive.

**DSLR (digital single-lens reflex)**: uses a physical mirror that flips up when you shoot, directing light to an optical viewfinder. The mirror mechanism adds size, weight, and moving parts. Canon and Nikon both officially ended new DSLR development in 2022 and 2023 respectively.

**Mirrorless**: no mirror. Light goes straight to the sensor always. The viewfinder (if present) is electronic — showing you a live preview of what the sensor sees, with exposure and color preview in real time. Advantages: smaller, lighter body; faster and more accurate autofocus (sensor-based AF is better than phase detect through a mirror); better video because the sensor is always live; shorter flange distance enables smaller lenses.

**The practical implication**: buy mirrorless. The Sony E-mount, Fujifilm X-mount, Nikon Z-mount, and Canon RF-mount are the mounts with long-term futures. Micro Four Thirds (Panasonic, OM System) is still viable for smaller bodies and a wide lens ecosystem.

---

### Sensor Size

The sensor is the most important hardware variable. It determines low-light performance, depth of field, and lens design — and determines which lenses work on the body.

**Full-frame (36×24mm)**: the largest common sensor. Best low-light performance (more area to collect light), shallowest depth of field, largest and heaviest lenses. Sony A7 series, Nikon Z6/Z7, Canon EOS R6. Price premium is significant — bodies start at $2,000+. Necessary only if you're shooting professionally in very low light or printing large.

**APS-C (crop sensor, ~23×15mm)**: approximately 1.5× smaller than full-frame. The sweet spot for most users. Better low-light than phone; smaller and lighter than full-frame; mature lens ecosystems; bodies in the $700–1,500 range. Sony A6x00 series, Fujifilm X-series, Canon R10/R50.

**Micro Four Thirds (17.3×13mm)**: smaller sensor, smaller bodies and lenses. Worse low-light than APS-C but a significant size and weight advantage — the system preferred by travel videographers and documentary filmmakers who carry cameras all day. Panasonic G9 II, OM System OM-5.

**For a first dedicated camera**: APS-C is the right tier. Full-frame is unnecessary overhead; Micro Four Thirds is worth considering if weight is the primary constraint (farm and outdoor work, travel).

### Recommended Cameras

#### Content Creation / Vlogging

**Sony ZV-E10 II ($900)**: APS-C, E-mount, designed for video-first. No viewfinder (keeps it light and small), flip-out touchscreen, 4K 60fps, excellent subject-tracking autofocus. The dedicated vlog camera from Sony. Right for someone whose primary use is talking-to-camera and daily vlog footage. Lenses: Sony's APS-C E-mount lineup or adapted full-frame E-mount glass (works with crop).

**Fujifilm X-S20 ($1,300)**: APS-C, X-mount, excellent video, 6.2K open gate (flexible framing in post), outstanding autofocus (eye tracking), 7-stop IBIS. Better stills than the ZV-E10 II, more versatile. The best all-around APS-C camera for someone who shoots both video and photography. Fujifilm's film simulations (in-camera color profiles mimicking classic film stocks) are distinctive and reduce post-processing time.

#### Travel and All-Purpose

**Fujifilm X-T30 II ($900 new, $450–600 used)**: APS-C, X-mount, compact body, excellent image quality, good video (4K 30fps). The best travel camera at this price. Lacks IBIS (relies on lens stabilization or steady technique) and the autofocus is not as aggressive as the X-S20. The used case is strong — the X-T30 II is 2+ years old and available well below new pricing.

**Sony A6700 ($1,400)**: APS-C, E-mount, Sony's current flagship APS-C. Best-in-class autofocus (the AI-based subject recognition tracks birds, cars, insects — used as a benchmark). 4K 60fps, 5.5-stop IBIS, excellent low-light. The most capable APS-C camera. Expensive for a first camera; better as a second body after you've used APS-C and know what you want.

**Sony A6400 (used, $350–500)**: Older APS-C E-mount camera, discontinued but widely available used. The flip-up screen made it popular for vloggers when released. Autofocus is excellent for its age — face and eye tracking still outperforms many current competitors. No IBIS, 4K with a crop. The best value entry into Sony's E-mount ecosystem on a budget. If budget is the constraint and you want Sony glass, start here.

#### Farm and Outdoor Documentation

**OM System OM-5 ($900)**: Micro Four Thirds, smaller and lighter than APS-C equivalents, IP53 weather and dust sealing (genuine outdoor protection), 7.5-stop IBIS (the best stabilization in any camera body). For outdoor, all-weather use — farming, hiking, garden documentation — the OM-5's weather resistance and stabilization are practical advantages over most APS-C options. The smaller sensor is a trade-off in low light.

**Fujifilm X-T5 ($1,700)**: APS-C, X-mount, 40MP sensor (the highest resolution in this class), weather sealed to -10°C, compact body. For someone who wants to document a farm with archival quality images — the 40MP files allow massive prints and heavy cropping. Overkill for vlogging; right for photography-first use.

### The Used Camera Market

Cameras depreciate faster than most people expect. A camera released 3 years ago at $1,200 is typically available used for $450–600 in excellent condition. The sensor, autofocus processor, and image quality are largely identical — firmware has been updated and known issues addressed. This is one of the best categories for buying used.

**Where to buy:**
- **KEH Camera** (keh.com): The oldest and most trusted used camera dealer in the US. Conservative grading (their "Bargain" grade often looks Like New). Good warranty on purchases.
- **MPB** (mpb.com): European origin, now US. Well-organized, accurate grading, good selection. Competitive with KEH.
- **B&H Photo Used / Adorama Used**: Large US camera retailers with used departments. Reliable, physical stores in NYC.
- **eBay**: More variable grading, but the largest selection and often the lowest prices. Buy from sellers with 98%+ feedback and look for listings with clear photos of the actual item, not stock photos. Stick to eBay's Money Back Guarantee for protection.
- **r/photomarket and r/videography**: Community sales, often from enthusiasts upgrading who take care of their gear. Typically honest grading; no buyer protection.

**What to look for in used listings:**
- Shutter count (cameras have rated shutter life; most are rated 100,000–200,000 actuations): request or ask for shutter count via EXIF data
- Physical condition of the lens mount and hot shoe
- Sensor dust (request a test shot against a blue sky at f/16)
- Firmware version — can be updated after purchase

**Used value picks:**
- Sony A6400 (2019): $350–450. Exceptional used value for Sony E-mount entry.
- Fujifilm X-T30 (2019): $300–400. Outstanding image quality, highly portable.
- Sony A7 III (2018): $1,200–1,400. Full-frame entry, still excellent in 2026, available at 40% below original retail.

### Lenses: What Actually Matters

The lens affects image quality more than the body. A good lens on a budget body outperforms a bad lens on an expensive body.

**Start with the kit lens**: most cameras bundle a zoom lens (typically 16-50mm or 18-55mm f/3.5-5.6). Kit lenses are often dismissed but are genuinely capable — learn on the kit lens before buying more glass. The limitation is maximum aperture (f/3.5-5.6), which matters in low light.

**The first prime lens upgrade**: a 35mm f/1.8 (on crop sensor, roughly equivalent to 50mm full-frame "normal" view) or a 50mm f/1.8 equivalent. Advantages over a zoom: larger maximum aperture (f/1.8 lets in 10× more light than f/5.6 at the same focal length), shallower depth of field for portraits and detail shots, usually sharper, typically cheaper. A 35mm f/1.8 for Sony E-mount ($450), Fuji XF 35mm f/2 ($400), or their cheaper alternatives ($150–200 from Sigma and Samyang/Rokinon) are practical upgrades.

**For vlogging specifically**: a wide-angle lens (10-20mm on crop) is more useful than a telephoto for close-range talking-to-camera work. The Sony 11mm f/1.8 ($550) is the standard recommendation for Sony E-mount vlogging.

**For farm and nature documentation**: a versatile mid-telephoto zoom (70-300mm equivalent) enables plant, insect, and wildlife detail shots at distance without disturbing the subject.

**Don't buy lenses speculatively.** Identify the specific shot you can't get with what you have, then buy the lens that solves it. Lens collections built without clear purpose become expensive clutter.

### Video Specs: What Matters vs. Marketing

**4K resolution**: standard on all current mid-range cameras. Worth having, even if you deliver in 1080p — the extra resolution allows cropping and stabilization in post.

**4K 60fps**: smoother motion, enables 2× slow motion in a 30fps timeline. Not universal at this tier — Sony A6400 shoots 4K at 30fps only; X-S20 and A6700 do 4K 60fps.

**10-bit color**: more data in the color channel, better for color grading in post. Matters for professional video work. The Sony A6400 shoots 8-bit; most current cameras at $900+ support 10-bit. 10-bit vs 8-bit makes almost no visible difference unless you're doing heavy color grading — don't let it drive the purchase decision at entry level.

**Log footage (S-Log, F-Log, etc.)**: flat, low-contrast recording mode that preserves dynamic range for grading in post. Requires color grading to look correct. Useful for professional output; creates more post-production work. For casual vlogging, shoot in a regular picture profile or a hybrid like Fujifilm's Eterna Cinema — less editing complexity, still looks good.

**Overheating**: some cameras shut down during extended 4K recording. The Sony A6400 overheats in about 30 minutes of continuous 4K. The X-S20 includes a heat-dissipating design that effectively eliminates overheating. For long interviews or event recording, check real-world overheating data before buying.

**IBIS (In-Body Image Stabilization)**: the gyroscope system in the camera body that compensates for hand shake. Significant for handheld video. The A6400 lacks IBIS; the X-S20 and A6700 have it. Without IBIS or a gimbal, handheld 4K footage at walking speed is noticeably shaky.

### Accessories

**Extra batteries**: always buy a second battery at minimum. Sony NP-FZ100 (full-frame), NP-FW50 (older APS-C A6xxx), Fuji NP-W235. Budget for one additional from a reputable third-party (Wasabi Power is reliable; avoid $5 no-name batteries). Cameras typically get 300–400 shots per charge; video use drains faster.

**Memory cards**: UHS-I SD cards (V30 speed rating) are sufficient for 4K video at 30fps and stills. UHS-II is faster (matters for burst shooting) and more expensive. CFexpress is only necessary for very high bitrate video formats. For normal use: a Samsung Pro Plus or SanDisk Extreme Pro in UHS-I V30, 128GB, is sufficient.

**Camera bag**: for a mirrorless with 1–2 lenses, a small sling (Peak Design Sling 6L at $80, or the Wandrd Roam 9L at $100) works well for travel and farm use. Avoid bags that scream "expensive camera inside."

**ND (Neutral Density) filter**: for outdoor video, an ND filter reduces light coming in so you can maintain the 180° shutter rule (see vlogging-foundations.md) in bright sun without overexposing. A variable ND ($40–80 for a quality one from Tiffen or Breakthrough Photography) is more practical than fixed NDs. Not needed for stills.

## Getting Started

1. **Identify the actual gap.** Before buying, name the shots you can't get with your phone: low-light portraits, fast-moving subjects, shallow depth of field close-ups, long focal lengths. The gap determines the lens requirement, which informs the body choice.

2. **Pick the ecosystem.** Sony E-mount has the largest APS-C lens ecosystem. Fujifilm X-mount has the most developed crop-sensor glass with exceptional image quality. Don't mix ecosystems early — commit to one and build glass within it.

3. **Buy used for the body, careful with used lenses.** Camera bodies depreciate quickly and show condition in the shutter count. Lenses hold value better and are harder to evaluate — buy from a reputable dealer (KEH, MPB) rather than eBay for glass unless you know what to look for.

4. **Start with kit lens + one prime.** Get the body with the kit zoom, learn with it for 3 months, then identify which focal length you keep returning to. Buy the prime in that focal length.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying full-frame as a first camera.** Full-frame bodies and lenses are more expensive, heavier, and larger. The image quality advantage over good APS-C is marginal in the contexts most people actually shoot. APS-C until you've outgrown it.

**Prioritizing megapixels.** For web and social media output, 24MP (the standard) is more than sufficient — you'll never use the resolution. Prioritize autofocus, IBIS, video specs, and ergonomics over megapixel counts.

**Not researching autofocus before buying.** Autofocus quality varies significantly across manufacturers and generations. For vlogging (subject tracking while you move) and wildlife/sports (moving subjects), autofocus is the most important performance variable. Sony and Canon have the best subject-tracking AF in 2025–2026. Read actual AF tracking tests, not spec sheets.

**Buying the cheapest lens.** A $99 no-name lens will produce noticeably worse results than a $300 Sigma or $400 native lens. Lenses outlast bodies — invest there. One good lens beats three mediocre ones.

**Shooting in Auto and wondering why images aren't better than a phone.** A mirrorless camera in full Auto mode doesn't look dramatically different from a phone. Learning Aperture Priority (control depth of field) and understanding ISO/shutter trade-offs is the actual upgrade from a phone.
