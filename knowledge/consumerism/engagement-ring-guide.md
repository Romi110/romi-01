# Engagement Ring Guide
*How to buy a ring intelligently: budget, settings, metals, shapes, and where to source*

---

## What Is It

A buying guide for an engagement ring — covering the decisions in order and the tradeoffs at each step. The goal is to spend your money on what matters (cut quality, stone size, durable metal) and not on what doesn't (marketing, brand names, arbitrary conventions).

See [lab-diamonds.md](lab-diamonds.md) for the full breakdown on natural vs lab-grown stones. This file assumes you've read it.

---

## Why It Matters

Engagement rings have one of the highest markup structures in consumer goods — 200–400% retail markup is normal at mall jewelers. The 2-month-salary rule was invented by De Beers in 1947 as a marketing strategy and repeated until it became cultural fact. There is no correct price for a ring. There is a price that makes sense for your situation, and then there's whatever jewelry stores prefer you spend.

Spending $4,000 on a well-chosen lab diamond in a well-made setting produces a ring that is visually indistinguishable from a $20,000 natural diamond ring. Understanding the decisions means you capture the full quality at the price that reflects actual value.

## Core Concepts

### Budget Framework

Set a number you're comfortable spending, then divide it: roughly 80% for the stone, 20% for the setting. Settings from quality online vendors run $500–$1,500 for most styles. What's left buys the stone.

Reasonable ranges and what they produce with a lab oval or round:
- $2,000–$3,000: 1.2–1.5ct lab diamond, good cut, VS2 clarity — excellent on a finger
- $3,000–$5,000: 1.5–2.5ct lab, excellent cut — noticeably substantial
- $5,000–$8,000: 2.5–4ct lab, ideal cut, higher clarity — genuinely impressive

The 2-month salary convention is marketing. Ignore it.

### Stone Shape

Shape is the first decision because it affects everything else — carat pricing, setting style options, and how the ring looks on the hand.

**Round brilliant** — Most sparkle, most standardized, most expensive per carat. 58 facets optimized for light return. The benchmark every other shape is compared to. Buy Excellent or Ideal cut.

**Oval** — Elongates the finger, 20–30% cheaper per carat than round, very popular. Face-up looks larger than a round of identical carat weight because of the elongated surface area. Can show a "bow-tie" shadow in the center — inspect the 360° video closely and avoid stones where it's dark or distracting.

**Cushion** — Soft rounded corners, vintage feel, less expensive than round. Comes in "cushion brilliant" (more sparkle) and "cushion modified" (chunkier facet pattern). Specify which when ordering.

**Emerald** — Step-cut facets, long rectangular, very modern and architectural. Lower sparkle than brilliant cuts but distinctive. Requires higher clarity because the large flat facets show inclusions easily — target VS1 or better.

**Princess** — Square shape with brilliant-style facets. Less expensive than round. The corners are the weakest point; look for a setting that protects them (four prongs on the corners, or a bezel).

**Pear and marquise** — Elongating shapes, less common, distinctive. Both can show bow-tie shadows — same inspection rule as oval.

### Settings

**Solitaire** — One stone, no side stones, prong set. Timeless, maximizes stone visibility, easiest to clean. The most flexible if tastes change — you can add a wedding band without complexity.

**Halo** — Center stone surrounded by a ring of smaller diamonds. Makes the center stone appear 20–30% larger. More surface area to collect dust; harder to clean well. Can look dated faster than solitaire.

**Pavé band** — Smaller diamonds set into the band shank. Adds sparkle along the ring, not just the center. More maintenance — small stones can loosen over time.

**Bezel** — Metal rim wraps fully around the stone. Most secure setting, most modern look, least sparkle (blocks some light from entering). Good for active lifestyles. No prongs to snag on clothing.

**Three-stone** — Center plus two side stones. "Past, present, future" symbolism. Allows mixing shapes (oval center with tapered baguette sides is a classic combination).

**Hidden halo** — Halo sits beneath the center stone, visible from the side but not the top. More subtle than a full halo while still adding presence.

### Metal Selection

**Platinum** — Densest, most durable, hypoallergenic, naturally white (no plating needed). Develops a patina over time that some people prefer and others polish out. Significantly heavier than gold — noticeable on the hand. Most expensive option, roughly 30–40% premium over 18k white gold. The practical choice if longevity and no maintenance is the priority.

**18k White Gold** — 75% pure gold, 25% alloy (palladium, silver, zinc). Rhodium-plated for the white finish — that plating wears off every 1–3 years and needs re-plating (~$50–$100). Without rhodium, 18k white gold has a slight warm tone. Strong, good weight, less expensive than platinum.

**14k White Gold** — 58.5% gold, more alloy. More durable than 18k (harder metal), less prestigious, most affordable option. Same rhodium situation. Good choice for an active lifestyle where the ring takes knocks.

**Yellow Gold** — 18k or 14k. No rhodium needed — the color is the natural alloy color. The warmth of yellow gold pairs with some stone shapes (oval, cushion) better than white metal, and makes lower-color diamonds (H, I) look whiter by contrast. Currently very popular and genuinely timeless.

**Rose Gold** — Pink from copper content. Trendy in the 2010s, still aesthetically valid. The copper makes it harder than yellow gold at the same karat. No rhodium. Consider whether it fits the long-term aesthetic — it's more distinctive than yellow.

### Certification

Always buy a certified stone. For lab diamonds, IGI is the dominant certifier. For natural diamonds, GIA. See [lab-diamonds.md](lab-diamonds.md) for the full breakdown on IGI vs GIA grading standards.

Never buy a stone certified only by EGL (European Gemological Laboratory) — their grading is notoriously inconsistent and overstates quality.

## Sourcing Channels

**Online vendors** — Best pricing, best selection, worst for hands-on evaluation. Mitigated by 360° video.
- *James Allen* — Large inventory, excellent 360° video, competitive pricing, 30-day return
- *Brilliant Earth* — Lab and natural, strong ethical sourcing story, slightly higher prices
- *Whiteflash* — Specialized in ideal-cut round brilliants, highest-quality photographs, smaller inventory
- *Blue Nile* — Largest inventory, reasonable pricing, less differentiation in product quality presentation

**NYC Diamond District (47th Street, Manhattan)** — 90 minutes from NJ. A full city block of wholesale and retail dealers. Advantages: you can negotiate, compare stones side-by-side, and access natural diamond inventory at prices well below mall retail. Disadvantages: predominantly natural diamonds (lab selection is thin), requires knowing what you're looking at (bring a loupe or a knowledgeable friend), pressure-sales environment at some booths. Best for: natural stone buyers who want to negotiate or want to see stones in person before committing. Don't go without a clear spec (shape, carat range, color/clarity target, certification requirement).

**Local independent jewelers** — Personal service, you can see the ring before it's final, easier custom work. Higher markup than online. Worth visiting to try on settings and understand what styles look good on the hand — then price-compare online.

**Mall chain stores (Kay, Zales, Jared)** — Avoid. The markup is the highest in the industry, quality control is inconsistent, and the sales pressure is designed to prevent comparison shopping. Nothing about the product justifies the premium over online vendors.

## Key Considerations

**Ring size matters — measure twice.** A finger size off by half a ring size is a $50–$100 resize. Fingers change size with temperature, time of day, weight, and hydration. Size in the afternoon when fingers are warmer and slightly larger.

**Photo vs. in-person** is a real gap. Rings in photos with studio lighting and macro lenses look different than rings on a finger in ordinary light. 360° video is better than photos. Your own hand in your own light is better than both.

**The proposal timeline.** Custom rings take 4–8 weeks. In-stock rings from online vendors typically ship in 1–2 weeks. Build in buffer.

**Wedding band compatibility.** Think about what band will sit next to the engagement ring. Solitaires are the most band-flexible. Curved or contoured bands designed to nest around a specific setting add cost and complexity later.

**Resale and insurance.** Buy an appraisal after the ring is complete (replacement cost appraisal, not resale). Add a jewelry rider to your renter's/homeowner's insurance or get a standalone policy from Jewelers Mutual.

## Common Mistakes

**Prioritizing carat weight over cut.** A 1.5ct diamond with a poor cut returns less light than a well-cut 1.2ct. Cut determines sparkle. Carat determines weight. Don't sacrifice cut to chase a round number.

**Buying from mall chains.** The markup is 200–400% over cost. The stone quality is often mediocre. The financing deals lock you into a payment structure that costs more total. Nothing about the purchase is favorable.

**Not considering the total system.** The ring finger has a wedding band next to it within a year. A solitaire in a simple 4-prong setting is easiest to pair. A thick halo with a complex profile may not sit flush against any straight band.

**Ignoring return policy.** Buy from vendors with at least a 30-day return. You need to see it on the hand.
