# Custom Ring Design
*Designing a ring from scratch: finding the right maker, the design process, and what to specify*

---

## What Is It

A custom ring is designed from scratch — your specifications, not an off-the-shelf catalog. It can be as simple as a standard solitaire in a metal and width that doesn't exist in stock, or as involved as an original design no one has made before. The process involves a jeweler (or CAD service) taking your brief through design, model, and casting.

This file covers how to navigate that process: who to use, what to say, and what to approve at each step.

---

## Why It Matters

Custom isn't automatically more expensive than retail. A bench jeweler charging $800–$1,500 for labor to set a stone you source separately can produce a ring for less than a comparable retail setting. You also get exactly what you want rather than the closest thing the catalog offers.

The self-reliance angle is real: designing something rather than buying it requires you to understand what you're making — metal behavior, prong geometry, proportions. That knowledge makes you a better buyer even if you never go custom again.

## Core Concepts

### Who Makes Custom Rings

**Bench jeweler** — A craftsperson who works at the bench: wax carving or casting, stone setting, finishing by hand. True custom work: they take your sketch or reference photos and build the piece. Wide quality range — a great bench jeweler produces better results than most online vendors; a mediocre one produces worse. Ask to see examples of their work in the specific style you want.

**CAD/casting shop** — Uses computer-aided design software (Rhino3D or Matrix) to design the ring digitally, then prints a wax or resin model on a 3D printer, casts in metal, and sets the stone. More precise than hand-carved wax, easier to make revisions, and the CAD file can be reused. Most modern custom jewelers use CAD/casting even if they finish by hand. This is now the dominant method for custom work.

**Online custom platforms** — Vendors like Brilliant Earth, Frank Darling, or independent Etsy jewelers who offer semi-custom options (modify their catalog) or full custom through a digital consultation. Useful when you want custom without the complexity of finding a local jeweler.

**NJ/NYC bench jewelers** — NYC has the largest concentration of bench jewelers and diamond setters in North America, concentrated in the Diamond District but spread across the garment district and Brooklyn. NJ has independent jewelry studios. For full custom, visiting the maker matters — you want to see their portfolio and talk through the design in person.

### The Design Process

The sequence regardless of who you use:

1. **Design brief** — Written or visual description of what you want: style (modern, vintage, minimal), metal, stone shape and approximate size, prong or bezel, band width, any specific details. Include what you don't want.

2. **Reference images** — A Pinterest board or folder of rings you like is more useful than a verbal description. Show examples of what you like and what you don't.

3. **Sketch or CAD rendering** — The jeweler produces a drawing or 3D render. You review and request changes. Expect 1–3 revision rounds before approval. Don't approve a render you're not happy with — changes after casting are expensive.

4. **Wax model or 3D print** — A physical version of the ring before metal is poured. This is where you see the actual scale and geometry. Hold it, look at it in light, check the prong spacing. Easier to revise here than after casting.

5. **Casting and setting** — Metal is poured, the stone is set, the ring is finished. The bench jeweler sets the stone by hand — this step is where craftsmanship matters most. A well-set stone is secure and flush; a poorly set one can allow the stone to shift or is at risk of falling out.

6. **Final finishing** — Polishing, rhodium plating (if white gold), final inspection.

### What to Specify

These are the decisions that shape the ring. Have answers before the first conversation with a jeweler.

**Metal and karat** — See [engagement-ring-guide.md](engagement-ring-guide.md) for the full breakdown. Platinum and 18k white gold are the most common for custom work.

**Prong count and style** — 4-prong shows more of the stone, looks lighter and more modern. 6-prong is more secure and traditional. Prong shape: claw (pointed, classic), round (softer, popular), flat-top (modern). For oval and cushion shapes, specify split prongs or a basket — they better match the shape.

**Shank width** — The band width in millimeters. Typical ranges: 1.5–2mm for delicate (more common for oval/cushion), 2–2.5mm for moderate, 2.5–4mm for a statement band. Wider bands are more comfortable on thick fingers; very narrow bands can feel insubstantial on large hands.

**Shank profile** — The cross-section shape of the band. Flat (looks wide), half-round (domed top, flat bottom — most common), comfort fit (rounded inside for wearability), knife-edge (V-shaped top, dramatic). Comfort fit is worth specifying if the ring will be worn daily.

**Stone seat height** — How high the stone sits above the finger. High setting: more light enters the pavilion, more sparkle, catches on clothing. Low/bezel: secure, modern, barely protrudes. Mid-height with a basket setting is the most common balanced option.

**Surface finish** — High polish (mirror), brushed/matte (subtle texture), hammered (artisan look), or a combination (polished face, brushed sides). High polish is easiest to re-finish if it scratches; matte is harder to restore but looks intentional when it wears.

**Edge details** — Milgrain: a tiny beaded border along the edge of the setting, looks vintage and handmade. Plain edge looks clean and modern. Harder to add after the fact — specify at design stage.

**Side stones** — Any diamonds or stones along the band or flanking the center stone. Common options: tapered baguettes (modern), pavé (small round brilliants set into the metal), channel set (stones flush in a channel), half-eternity (side stones halfway around the band). All add to cost and maintenance complexity.

## Cost Structure

Custom ring pricing has two components: the stone (which you source separately or through the jeweler) and the labor/setting.

**Labor and setting (typical ranges):**
- Simple solitaire (4-prong, plain band): $400–$800
- Moderate complexity (halo, pavé band, or unique shank): $800–$1,500
- High complexity (intricate vintage, multi-stone, hand-engraving): $1,500–$3,000+

**Markup on stones through a jeweler:** If the jeweler sources the stone, expect 20–40% above wholesale. You can supply your own stone from an online vendor — many jewelers accept this. Confirm before assuming.

**CAD fee:** Some shops charge $100–$300 for the CAD file and wax model separately. Ask whether this is included.

**Total cost comparison:** A custom solitaire with a 1.5ct lab oval (sourced from James Allen for ~$1,200) + $800 setting labor = $2,000 total. The same configuration at a retail chain: $4,000–$6,000 or more.

## Key Considerations

**See their work, not just photos.** Ask to see finished rings in person, not just renders. CAD renders look good even for mediocre shops. Finished rings reveal actual craftsmanship in prong work, polish quality, and stone setting precision.

**Get everything in writing.** Design specs, metal weight, estimated timeline, total cost, what happens if you're unhappy with the result. A good jeweler will provide a written quote that matches what's in the CAD render.

**Supply your own stone if pricing is a concern.** Most bench jewelers accept customer-supplied stones. This lets you shop the diamond separately and get the best price on both components. Confirm the jeweler is comfortable with this and whether it changes the warranty on setting work.

**Timeline is longer than you expect.** CAD revision rounds + wax approval + casting + finishing = 4–8 weeks minimum for a well-made ring. 10–12 weeks for complex designs. Build in buffer.

**The proposal deadline trap.** Don't let a hard proposal date rush you into approving a render or wax model you're not fully happy with. Rushing this decision produces a ring you'll look at for decades.

## Common Mistakes

**Approving the CAD without seeing a wax model.** 3D renders look different from physical wax models, which look different from the final metal ring. Insist on a wax or resin physical model for any complex design before casting.

**Specifying style from Pinterest without understanding what drives it.** "I want something vintage" is a direction; it doesn't tell a jeweler how wide the shank is, what prong style, what finish, or what metal. Get specific.

**Choosing a jeweler based on price alone.** Stone setting is skilled manual work — bad stone setting means a stone that shifts, catches, or falls out. Check portfolios, ask about their setting experience specifically, and don't choose the cheapest quote for complex work.

**Not asking about resizing policy.** Rings need to be resized. A bezel-set ring, a channel-set ring, or a ring with very thin shoulders are harder to resize. Know the limitations before you commit to a design.
