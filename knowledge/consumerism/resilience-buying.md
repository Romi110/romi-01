# Resilience Buying
*What to own before the shelves go empty — purchases that reduce your dependence on fragile supply chains*

*Synthesized from: supply-chain-resilience.md × buy-it-for-life.md × quality-life-upgrades.md*

---

## What Is It

Resilience buying is a purchasing framework that asks a specific question: **which things you own or fail to own most expose you to supply chain disruption?** It's different from both BIFL (which optimizes for lifetime cost) and emergency preparedness (which focuses on acute crisis). Resilience buying sits at the intersection: durable goods that reduce your ongoing dependency on the supply chain, chosen specifically because disruptions to that chain are predictable.

The supply chain threat is not exotic. Between 2020 and 2023, the US saw empty shelves, months-long appliance waits, baby formula scarcity, and car chip shortages — from a single pandemic disruption in an otherwise functioning global system. The structural vulnerabilities (semiconductor concentration in Taiwan, pharmaceutical ingredients in China, fertilizer from Russia, JIT inventory with no buffer) have not been fixed. A second disruption — geopolitical escalation, another pandemic, a major natural disaster, conflict in a critical manufacturing region — follows the same playbook.

This file identifies the specific categories where targeted purchasing now creates meaningful buffer. It's not a bunker-building guide. It's an answer to the practical question: **given what supply chains can fail, what should you own?**

---

## Why It Matters

The BIFL philosophy already solves one version of this problem: if your knife, boots, and tools don't need replacing, you're not dependent on supply chains for replacements. But BIFL optimizes for lifetime cost — resilience buying adds a second filter: *what happens if this category becomes unavailable or very expensive for 3–18 months?*

For someone building self-reliance in NJ: the disruptions you'll actually face are tier-1 (grid outages, specific product shortages, price spikes) and occasionally tier-2 (extended regional disruption from a major storm or infrastructure failure). Tier-3 collapse scenarios are not the planning target — tier-1 and tier-2 are both realistic and have happened in living memory (Sandy 2012, COVID supply chain 2020–2022).

The purchases in this file pay off in three ways: they buffer disruptions when they happen, they reduce ongoing dependence on fragile supply nodes, and — in BIFL categories — they compound financially over a lifetime.

## Core Concepts

### The Framework: Four Exposure Categories

Every supply chain exposure you have falls into one of these:

1. **Consumables you regularly replace** — food, medications, cleaning supplies, hygiene products. If these disappear for 8 weeks, you feel it within days.
2. **Durable goods you'll eventually need to replace** — appliances, tools, clothing. If the replacement isn't available or costs 3x the normal price, you're exposed.
3. **Infrastructure goods that reduce your dependency** — water filtration, energy generation, food production. These don't replace things — they reduce how much of category 1 and 2 you need at all.
4. **Skills and tools that enable substitution** — the ability to repair, produce, or do without. A broken appliance is a crisis for someone without tools; an inconvenience for someone who can fix it.

Resilience buying addresses all four. The sequence matters: consumable buffers first, infrastructure second, tools third.

### Food: The Highest-Return Category

The supply chain's most immediate consumer-facing failure mode is food: specific categories disappear from shelves, prices spike 30–50%, substitutes aren't available at normal scale.

**Pantry buffer — the first investment**

A 60–90 day pantry buffer requires no special equipment and no lifestyle change. Buy extra staples each grocery run until you have a rolling buffer. Vegetarian-focused staples with 1–5 year shelf life:

| Item | Shelf Life | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| White rice (sealed) | 25–30 years | Brown rice goes rancid; white stores longer |
| Red/green/black lentils | 5+ years | Core protein source; 25g protein per 100g dry |
| Dried chickpeas, black beans, pinto beans | 5+ years | Rotate; older beans take longer to cook |
| Rolled oats (airtight container) | 1–2 years | Breakfast base |
| Dried pasta | 2 years | Semolina-based stores well |
| Olive oil | 18–24 months | Buy fresh; don't overbuy |
| Coconut oil | 2–5 years | Longer shelf life than olive oil |
| Salt | Indefinitely | Also useful for preservation |
| Honey | Indefinitely | Crystallizes but doesn't spoil; antibacterial |
| Apple cider vinegar | Indefinitely | Also useful for fermentation and cleaning |
| Whole spices (cumin, coriander, turmeric, etc.) | 3–4 years | Whole spices store far longer than ground |
| Nutritional yeast | 1–2 years | B12 source for vegetarian buffer |
| Ghee (sealed) | 1 year at room temp, 3 years frozen | High calorie density, long shelf life |

Store in a cool, dark place. Rotate continuously — first in, first out. At 60-day buffer, this costs roughly $200–$400 to establish and minimal ongoing cost to maintain.

**Food production infrastructure**

The pressure cooker is the most important single appliance for food resilience. An Instant Pot or stovetop pressure cooker converts dry beans and lentils (the core protein buffer) in 20–35 minutes — critical when you're using stored staples constantly. It also handles grains, stocks, and preserved vegetables efficiently. The stovetop version (Presto or Kuhn Rikon) works without electricity; the Instant Pot doesn't.

For longer disruptions: fermentation extends the life of vegetables and produces probiotics cheaply. A 2-liter crock and two pounds of salt converts cabbage to sauerkraut that keeps for months. See [farming/food-preservation.md](../farming/food-preservation.md) for the full system.

**What not to stockpile**: freeze-dried "survival" food at $5–$8 per serving. It's expensive, nutritionally limited, and unnecessary when staples cost $0.50–$2 per serving. The prepper food industry sells fear, not function.

### Water: The Overlooked Gap

Most households in NJ have 0–2 days of stored water. Municipal water supply interruptions from infrastructure failure, contamination events, or storm damage are not unusual. A 2-week buffer is achievable cheaply.

**Storage**:
- WaterBOB Emergency Bathtub Bladder (~$30) — fills a standard bathtub with 100 gallons. Enough for 2 people for 25 days at 4 gallons/day (drinking + sanitation). Fill it before a known storm. The cost of the option is $30.
- 5-gallon food-grade containers (~$10 each): 4–6 of these provide a permanent backup that doesn't require storm anticipation

**Filtration — the more important investment**:

Stored water runs out. Filtration capacity doesn't. Two options at different price points:

- **Berkey Big Berkey** (~$330): Gravity-fed countertop filter, no electricity, no installation, no plumbing. Two Black Berkey elements filter to 0.2 microns — removes bacteria, parasites, heavy metals, pharmaceuticals, chlorine. Rated for 6,000 gallons before element replacement. Works on tap water, lake water, river water. This is the most supply-chain-resilient water solution available: no replacement filters on a quarterly schedule, no power requirement, no installation. The BIFL answer to water filtration.
- **LifeStraw Community** (~$75): Lower upfront cost, 26,000 gallons of filtration capacity, no electricity. More appropriate as a backup or vehicle-stored option than a primary.
- **APEC ROES-50 Reverse Osmosis** (~$200): If you're not concerned about grid power, this is the definitive under-sink solution for ongoing drinking and cooking water quality. Less resilient in an extended power outage (requires pressure).

The Berkey is the recommendation here specifically because it doesn't depend on electricity, plumbing, or ongoing supply of proprietary replacement filters.

### Energy: Buffer and Independence

Covered in depth in [energy/portable-power-stations.md](../energy/portable-power-stations.md) and [energy/diy-renewable-energy.md](../energy/diy-renewable-energy.md). The resilience buying angle:

**Tier 1 — Portable power (immediate impact, ~$300–700)**

An EcoFlow River 2 Pro (768Wh, ~$400) or similar powers: refrigerator for 12–24 hours, phone charging for 50+ cycles, internet router for 3–5 days, lights indefinitely with LED. In a grid outage — the most common NJ disruption — this covers essentials. Recharges from wall, car, or solar panel.

**Tier 2 — Solar input (eliminates grid dependency for recharging)**

A 200W portable solar panel (~$200) + the above power station means the unit recharges from sun in 3–5 hours. Now the system is autonomous indefinitely for the loads above. This is the minimum viable energy independence setup for under $600 total.

**Tier 3 — Full system (see energy files)**

A proper home solar + battery bank removes the household from grid vulnerability for most loads. The BIFL angle: solar panels and LiFePO4 batteries have 25-year lifespans with minimal maintenance — this is infrastructure, not a consumer product.

**The BIFL-resilience intersection on energy**: Hand tools (Knipex, Wera, Klein) work without power. A carbon steel or cast iron skillet works on a gas burner, camp stove, or open fire — your All-Clad induction-only pan doesn't. When buying cookware for resilience, ensure everything you own works with non-electric heat. A stovetop pressure cooker is more resilient than an Instant Pot.

### Medical: The Pharmaceutical Vulnerability

~80% of active pharmaceutical ingredients are manufactured in China or India. This is the supply chain risk most people carry personally but rarely think about as an inventory problem.

**Prescription medications**: Request 90-day supplies from your doctor. Most insurers cover this. A 90-day supply on hand means a 3-month buffer against shortage or disruption. For essential medications (blood pressure, thyroid, insulin, psychiatric), this buffer is non-negotiable.

**First aid infrastructure**:

Beyond the standard drugstore first aid kit, the quality-life-upgrades file identifies the critical gap: a real trauma kit vs. a collection of bandages.

- **MyMedic MyFAK Pro** (~$100): tourniquet, hemostatic gauze, trauma dressings, CPR mask, SAM splint. The kit that actually contains a serious injury. Combined with a free Stop the Bleed course (2 hours, widely available), this covers the injuries most likely to be life-threatening when professional help is delayed.
- **CAT Tourniquet** (~$30): The standard for serious extremity bleeding. One for the home, one for the car.
- **Standard OTC buffer**: 90-day supply of ibuprofen, acetaminophen, antihistamines, antidiarrheal, antacids. These are cheap, store well, and become expensive or unavailable during disruptions.

### Tools: The Enabler of Substitution

The tools section of the BIFL file is also the resilience section. The underlying logic: a tool that lets you repair something extends its life indefinitely; a household without tools is dependent on the supply chain for every replacement.

**Essential toolkit for supply-chain resilience**:

- **Knipex Cobra pliers + combination pliers** (~$80 total): Every plumbing repair, every fixture, every pipe fitting. If a plumber can't come for 3 weeks, you need these.
- **Wera screwdriver set** (~$50): Every appliance repair, every electrical connection, every furniture assembly.
- **Klein wire strippers and lineman's pliers** (~$60): Electrical repairs. In an extended outage, you may need to rewire connections.
- **Estwing framing hammer** (~$40): Drywall, framing, everything.
- **Leatherman Wave+** (~$100): Field repair of anything small. 25-year warranty. The single most versatile tool you carry.
- **Hand saw** (~$30): Works without power. Silky makes the benchmark.
- **Headlamp: Petzl Actik Core** (~$60): USB-C rechargeable, 450 lumens. You will need to work in the dark during a power outage.

The BIFL property here matters specifically for resilience: when replacement is unavailable, the tool that never needs replacing is worth more than its list price.

**What the tools enable**:

- Plumbing leak → fixed without waiting for a plumber
- Appliance failure → diagnosed and potentially repaired vs. replaced
- Electrical issue → handled safely vs. left until an electrician is available
- Structural damage from storm → contained immediately

### Clothing and Footwear: The Replacement Cycle Risk

Cheap clothing and footwear require replacement every 1–3 years. In a supply chain disruption where specific goods become unavailable or cost 3–5x normal price for an extended period, a household dependent on constant replacement feels this acutely.

The BIFL approach — buy once, maintain, repair, resole — is also the resilience approach. One pair of Red Wing Iron Rangers resoled at year 8–10 doesn't expose you to a footwear supply disruption. Ten pairs of $30 shoes bought over 10 years does.

Priority investments from a resilience lens:
- **One quality waterproof jacket** (Barbour waxed cotton or Patagonia) — rewaxable/repairable indefinitely; never needs replacing
- **Durable work boots** (Red Wing or Danner) — resoleable; the cobbler exists locally and doesn't depend on international shipping
- **Merino wool base layers** (Smartwool or Icebreaker) — naturally antibacterial, temperature-regulating, durable; reduces washing frequency and extends wear life
- **Heavy cotton or canvas work pants** (Carhartt) — repair-friendly, patch-friendly, no synthetic coating to degrade

The guiding question for any clothing purchase: *can this be repaired locally if the manufacturer is unavailable?*

### What Not to Buy

**Elaborate freeze-dried survival food** — expensive, nutritionally narrow, and unnecessary when staples work. The prepper food industry exists to monetize anxiety.

**AR-15s and extensive weapons stockpiles** — the realistic disruption scenarios don't require them, and they create other risks. The FEMA baseline and the supply-chain-resilience file both recommend against over-indexing on weapons.

**Single-use preparedness items** — chemical light sticks, flares, single-use water pouches. Prefer reusable, maintainable systems (Berkey over water pouches, headlamp over light sticks).

**Smart home devices with cloud dependencies** — a Nest thermostat or Ring camera becomes useless if the cloud service goes down or the company fails. Prefer dumb appliances or locally-controlled smart home systems.

**Complex electronics as resilience investments** — the more chips, the more fragile. A mechanical watch keeps time during an outage. A digital watch with a replaceable battery keeps time if you have the battery. A smartphone without power keeps time for nothing.

## Prioritized Buying Sequence

If you're starting from zero, the order of operations for maximum resilience per dollar:

**Month 1 (under $200)**
1. Begin building a 30-day pantry buffer — add $30–50 in staples per grocery run
2. WaterBOB bathtub bladder (~$30)
3. CAT tourniquet (~$30) + Stop the Bleed course (free)
4. 90-day supply of critical OTC medications (~$30–50)

**Month 2 (under $400)**
5. Portable power station: EcoFlow River 2 Pro or equivalent (~$400)
6. Complete the 60-day pantry buffer

**Month 3 (under $400)**
7. Berkey Big Berkey water filter (~$330)
8. MyMedic MyFAK Pro first aid kit (~$100)

**Quarter 2 (under $600)**
9. 200W solar panel for power station (~$200)
10. Core tool kit: Knipex pliers + Wera screwdrivers + Klein electrical (~$190)
11. Leatherman Wave+ (~$100)

**Ongoing (as replacements are needed)**
- Replace footwear with resoleable BIFL options (Red Wing, Danner)
- Replace clothing with repairable, durable options (Carhartt, Barbour, Patagonia)
- Replace cookware with stovetop-compatible BIFL options (Lodge cast iron, stovetop pressure cooker)

**Total invested for meaningful resilience**: under $1,500 over 6 months.

## Key Considerations

**The Berkey is the highest-leverage single purchase in this list.** Water filtration without power or plumbing is the gap most resilience-minded people overlook. Food you can manage for weeks; water you cannot manage for days. The Berkey solves this permanently.

**Stovetop compatibility matters more than it seems.** When buying cookware, always verify it works on gas, camp stove, or open fire — not just electric or induction. An Instant Pot is more convenient; a stovetop pressure cooker is more resilient. For a one-time decision that lasts 30 years, choose the more resilient option.

**The BIFL lens and the resilience lens almost always agree.** The products that don't need replacing are the products that don't create ongoing supply chain dependency. When the two lenses point at different choices, the resilience lens should win for essential categories (food, water, tools, footwear, energy).

**Rotation is maintenance.** A pantry buffer not rotated is wasted money and false security. A fire extinguisher not checked is dangerous. A portable power station never charged will fail when needed. Resilience is not a one-time purchase — it's a maintenance system. Build rotation into normal grocery shopping and a quarterly equipment check into your calendar.

**Skills amplify the value of every purchase here.** A trauma kit without the knowledge to use it covers only the basic injuries. Fermentation knowledge multiplies the value of a ceramic crock. The ability to sharpen tools means your tools last indefinitely rather than just longer. See the resources below and the farming and health files for the skills side.

## Getting Started

The single action that unlocks most of this: **do an inventory audit**. Walk through your home and answer:
1. How many days of food do you have on hand if you don't shop for 2 weeks?
2. How many days of water?
3. What do you do if the power goes out for 5 days?
4. What do you do if a prescription medication is unavailable for 2 months?
5. What tools do you have for home repairs if a contractor isn't available for 4 weeks?

The gaps in your answers are your buying list. Most people discover they have 3–5 days of food, no real water backup, no power backup, a 30-day medication supply, and no meaningful tools. This audit takes 20 minutes and generates a specific, actionable list.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating this as a one-time project.** A pantry buffer that isn't rotated degrades. A portable power station that isn't tested may not work when needed. Resilience is a maintenance activity.

**Optimizing for dramatic scenarios.** The realistic scenario is 2–4 weeks of product unavailability in a specific category, elevated prices, and inconvenience. Not societal collapse. Buy for the realistic scenario, not the cinematic one.

**Buying complexity when simplicity is more resilient.** A sophisticated water filtration system that requires replacement filters from one manufacturer is less resilient than a Berkey that uses elements lasting 3+ years. A solar generator that requires proprietary charging cables is less resilient than one using standard XT60 connectors. Prefer simple, standardized, locally-serviceable systems.

**Skipping the skills side.** A first aid kit doesn't know how to use itself. Seeds don't plant themselves. Fermentation vessels don't ferment automatically. Every physical investment in resilience has a corresponding skill investment. The equipment without the skill is half an investment.
