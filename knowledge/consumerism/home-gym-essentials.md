# Home Gym Essentials
*Removing the gym as a dependency — owning your practice by owning the equipment, built specifically around kettlebell and mace training.*

---

## What Is It

A home gym is not a commercial gym reproduced at smaller scale. It's a deliberate set of equipment that supports a specific practice, fits your space, and removes every possible barrier between you and training.

For kettlebell and mace work, the equipment footprint is small, the cost is modest, and the durability is essentially infinite. Cast iron doesn't wear out. A quality kettlebell bought today will still be in use in 30 years. The payoff is removing the gym commute, the membership fee, and every logistical excuse that compounds into skipped sessions.

This file is specific to Romi's actual practice. It doesn't cover barbell powerlifting, cardio machines, or building a full commercial gym equivalent. It covers exactly what's needed for kettlebell, mace, bodyweight, and conditioning work.

---

## Why It Matters

Discipline over motivation means removing friction from the path of right action. A gym membership adds: commute time (20-40 min round trip), schedule dependency (gym hours), social friction (waiting for equipment), and the mental barrier of leaving the house. A home setup eliminates all of it. The 6am session happens because walking to the garage or spare room is the total commitment required.

The math: a decent gym membership in NJ runs $40-80/month = $500-1,000/year. A complete home setup for this practice costs $600-1,200 once, then nothing.

## Core Concepts

### The Foundation: Flooring First

Before buying anything else, solve the floor. Dropping kettlebells on hardwood or tile cracks the floor and damages the bells. Working without cushion on concrete fatigues joints.

**Horse stall mats** — The only answer. 4'x6', 3/4" thick, solid rubber. Available at Tractor Supply Co. or any farm supply store for $40-50 each. Stack two side-by-side = 4'x12' platform. Heavy (approximately 100 lbs each), odorous initially (air out for a week), essentially indestructible. Cut to size with a utility knife. Nothing else comes close at this price.

**Foam interlocking tiles** — Cheaper, easier to install, not adequate for heavy kettlebell drops. Use only if you never drop the bell from overhead.

Buy the horse stall mats. Two is enough to start.

### Kettlebells

**Cast iron vs. competition:**

*Cast iron*: Size varies with weight — a 32kg cast iron bell is larger than a 16kg. The handle diameter may differ between brands. Powder-coated cast iron is the standard. Good for home use where you're not switching weights mid-set constantly.

*Competition kettlebells* (also called sport kettlebells): Same exact size and handle diameter regardless of weight. Plastic or steel color-coded by weight. If you want consistent technique development across weight increases — the bell always sits in the same position on your forearm — competition is better. Slightly more expensive.

**Recommended brands:**
- **Kettlebell Kings** — Best value competition kettlebells. Quality control is consistent. Handle finish is good. Powder coat holds up. Ships direct.
- **Rogue Fitness** — Excellent cast iron. Built to last decades. Slightly premium price but genuinely quality. The "E-Coat" finish is superior to standard powder coat.
- **Rep Fitness** — Good quality, competitive pricing. Solid alternative to Rogue.
- **Avoid**: No-name Amazon brands. Handle finish and casting quality are inconsistent. A poorly cast handle tears up your hand.

**What weights to start with:**

Most men training consistently: 16kg (35 lbs) for high-rep skill work, 24kg (53 lbs) as primary working weight, 32kg (70 lbs) for heavy strength work. This covers 90% of programming needs.

If you're earlier in training: start with 16kg and 24kg. Add 32kg when you can press and swing 24kg for multiple heavy sets.

A single 24kg competition bell is a legitimate starting point if budget is limited. You can do almost everything with one bell — swings, cleans, presses, goblet squats, Turkish get-ups.

**Cost**: Competition bells run $1.50-2.00/kg typically. A 24kg bell = ~$48-65. A set of 16/24/32 = ~$140-200 total.

### Steel Mace

**What to buy:**

**Set For Set** — The standard recommendation. Good quality, consistent weight, reasonable price. The handle finish holds up to chalk and heavy use. Available in 7lb, 10lb, 15lb, 20lb, 25lb, 30lb.

**Steel Mace Warrior** — Similar quality, similar price. Legitimate alternative.

**Starting weights:**
- Beginners: 7lb is genuinely challenging for mace-specific movements (360s, barbarian squats, mills). Don't skip it.
- Intermediate: 10lb for most complex movements, 15lb for two-handed power work.
- The mace is deceptively demanding because of the offset center of mass. Ego check your weight selection.

**Cost**: $40-70 per mace.

### Pull-Up and Bodyweight Infrastructure

**Wall-mounted pull-up bar** — The best option for a dedicated space. Bolt directly into studs. Holds bodyweight plus rings plus a weight vest. Rogue, Titan Fitness, and Iron Age make good versions. $80-150. Takes 30 minutes to install.

**Doorframe pull-up bar** — If wall mounting isn't possible. Fits most standard doorframes without hardware. **Iron Gym Total Upper Body** (~$30) or **Stamina Doorframe** (~$35). Not ideal for loading with a weight vest or rings due to leverage on the frame, but functional for bodyweight pulls.

**Gymnastic rings** — One of the most underrated purchases in bodyweight training. Ring push-ups, ring rows, ring dips, ring support holds — all dramatically harder and more effective than bar equivalents. Hang from any pull-up bar or outdoor beam.
- **Rogue Wood Rings** (~$70): The standard. 28mm diameter, smooth, correct width. Wooden rings grip better than plastic in sweaty conditions.
- **NOSSK Twin-loop** (~$30): Budget option that performs well. Nylon straps, plastic rings — adequate if you don't want to spend on Rogue.

**Cost for pull-up bar + rings**: $100-220 depending on wall-mount vs. door.

### Conditioning Tools

**Jump rope** — The most calorie-efficient conditioning tool for a small space. A quality speed rope is worth buying once.
- **RPM Session 3** (~$70): Ball bearings, adjustable cable, the standard for double-unders. Will last years.
- **Rx Smart Gear** (~$45): Good quality, popular in CrossFit circles. Handles well.
- **Avoid**: Basic plastic jump ropes. They kink, handle poorly, and make learning double-unders frustrating.

**Ab wheel** — One of the most effective core tools. $15-25. Any cast iron or steel version works. Avoid plastic ones that crack under load.

### Optional Additions (in order of value)

**Parallettes** (~$60-100): Low parallel bars for L-sits, planche work, push-up variations. Rogue or Rep Fitness. Not essential but high-value for bodyweight work.

**Weight vest** (~$80-150): Adds load to bodyweight work without buying heavier kettlebells. **Hyperwear SteelBell** or **5.11 TacTec** for quality. Useful for pull-up progressions, vest carries, farmer's walks.

**Plyo box** (~$60-120): 3-in-1 wood box (20/24/30"). Box jumps, step-ups, depth drops. Build your own from plywood for $40 in materials.

**Foam roller** (~$25-40): Recovery, not training. **TriggerPoint GRID** is the standard. Don't buy cheap foam — it compresses within months.

## Complete Setup by Budget

**Minimal ($300-400)**
- Horse stall mats x2: ~$90
- One competition kettlebell (24kg): ~$65
- Steel mace (7lb and 10lb): ~$100
- Doorframe pull-up bar: ~$35
- Jump rope: ~$45

This handles the complete practice. Add rings and a second kettlebell as budget allows.

- Horse stall mats x2: ~$90

- Steel mace: 7lb, 10lb, 15lb: ~$150
- Wall-mounted pull-up bar: ~$120
- Rogue wooden rings: ~$70
- RPM jump rope: ~$70

- Parallettes: ~$80

## Key Considerations

**Space**: A 4'x6' mat footprint is the minimum. A 4'x12' (two mats) is comfortable for swings and get-ups. You don't need a full room — a garage corner, basement section, or covered outdoor space works.

**Outdoor training**: Cast iron kettlebells can stay outside in a covered area without issue. Bring them in if temperatures will freeze (water expansion in any casting cracks). A tarp is enough protection for mild NJ weather.

**Chalk**: Liquid chalk (Friction Labs or Bear Komplex) for indoor use — no mess, same grip as block chalk. Block chalk outdoors. Grip strength isn't the point of kettlebell training; a slipping bell is a safety issue and interferes with technique.

**Sound**: Dropping kettlebells on horse stall mats is still loud. In an apartment, this matters significantly. Consider neighbors and time of day. In a house or garage, it's a non-issue.

## Getting Started

1. Buy two horse stall mats from Tractor Supply. Air them outside for a week.
2. Order a 24kg competition kettlebell from Kettlebell Kings and a 7lb + 10lb steel mace from Set For Set.
3. Get a doorframe pull-up bar and an RPM jump rope.
4. Train for 3 months. Then evaluate what you're missing — which will be almost nothing.

Don't over-buy before knowing your practice. The 24kg bell and two maces will occupy you for months.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying cheap kettlebells**: A poorly cast handle tears skin and makes clean technique impossible. Spend the extra $20-30 and get Kettlebell Kings or Rogue.

**Skipping the flooring**: Training on unprotected hardwood or concrete is worse for joints and will eventually damage the floor. The mats are the first purchase.

**Over-buying weights early**: You don't need 10 kettlebells. You need two or three that match your current strength level. The goal is mastery of movement, not access to every weight.

**Buying a cheap jump rope**: Learning double-unders on a kinked plastic rope is genuinely miserable. The RPM or Rx rope is $40-70 and the difference is immediate.

**Not chalking up**: A wet palm turning on a kettlebell handle is a safety issue. Liquid chalk in a small bottle costs $15 and solves the problem.
