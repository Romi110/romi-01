# Home Water Filtration
*What's actually in your water, which filters remove it, and why the Pinelands water profile changes the calculus*

---

## What Is It

Home water filtration is the practice of treating water at the point of entry or point of use to remove contaminants before drinking, cooking, or bathing. The right filter depends entirely on what's in your water — which varies by source (well vs. municipal), location, and infrastructure age.

This file covers filter types, what each removes, NJ Pinelands-specific water characteristics, and how to choose based on your actual water rather than marketing claims.

## Why It Matters

Water quality directly affects health and is the most fundamental self-reliance input. In the Pinelands specifically, well water has a distinctive profile that most generic filtration guides don't address. Buying the wrong filter (a water softener for Pinelands water, which is already soft) wastes money; buying the right one (iron and tannin filter for Pinelands well water) solves the actual problem.

## Core Concepts

### Know Your Water First

No filter recommendation is valid without knowing what you're filtering. The two starting points:

**Municipal water (Galloway / Atlantic County area)**: treated by Atlantic County Utilities Authority (ACUA). Your annual Consumer Confidence Report (CCR), required by federal law and mailed to all customers, lists all detected contaminants and their levels. Request it if you haven't received it. Common municipal concerns: chlorine/chloramine (disinfection byproduct taste/odor), disinfection byproducts (trihalomethanes), and occasional lead from older pipes or fixtures.

**Well water (Pinelands properties)**: not tested or treated by any municipality. Entirely your responsibility. **Test before you filter.** A comprehensive well water test ($150–250 from a certified NJ lab) covers coliform bacteria, nitrates, pH, hardness, iron, manganese, arsenic, lead, and VOCs. The NJ Department of Health recommends testing private wells annually for bacteria and nitrates, and every 3 years for a full panel. Without testing, you're guessing.

---

### NJ Pinelands Water Profile

Pinelands water has a specific chemistry that differs from most of the country:

**Soft and slightly acidic**: the Kirkwood-Cohansey aquifer underlies the Pinelands — sandy, highly permeable soil with very little mineral content. Water is naturally soft (low calcium/magnesium) and slightly acidic (pH 5.5–6.5). This means: **water softeners are not needed** in the Pinelands. Installing one on already-soft water wastes salt and money.

**Low pH (acidity)**: acidic water is corrosive — it leaches copper and lead from plumbing. If your home has copper pipes with lead solder (pre-1986 construction) and well water with pH below 6.0, lead contamination is a real concern. An acid neutralizer (calcite filter) raises pH and protects your plumbing.

**Iron and manganese**: common in Pinelands well water. Signs: orange/brown staining in sinks and toilets, metallic taste. Iron above 0.3 mg/L is the EPA action level. An iron filter (birm or manganese greensand) or air injection system addresses this before a carbon filter.

**Tannins**: organic compounds from decomposing vegetation in the sandy organic Pinelands soil. Result: tea-colored water that's not harmful but stains fixtures and tastes earthy. A tannin filter (anion exchange resin) removes them. Often the most noticeable water quality issue in the Pinelands.

**Low TDS (Total Dissolved Solids)**: Pinelands water is naturally very low in minerals — typically 30–100 mg/L TDS vs. the national average of ~150 mg/L. Reverse osmosis is often overkill for Pinelands municipal or well water unless specific contaminants justify it.

### Filter Types and What They Remove

**Activated carbon (pitcher, faucet-mount, under-sink)**
Removes: chlorine, chloramines, taste/odor compounds, some VOCs, some pesticides, some heavy metals (lead, copper at point of use if NSF/ANSI 53 certified).
Does NOT remove: nitrates, fluoride, most heavy metals, TDS, bacteria, hardness.
Best for: municipal water with chlorine or taste issues.

*Pitcher (Brita, PUR, ZeroWater)*: $25–40, convenient, filters need replacement every 2–3 months. ZeroWater uses a 5-stage filter that includes ion exchange and removes nearly all TDS — the closest a pitcher gets to RO. Good for renters.
*Under-sink carbon block (Aquasana AQ-5200, iSpring FC25)*: $50–100 for the filter, better flow rate than pitcher, higher contaminant removal, 6-month filter life.

**Reverse Osmosis (RO)**
Removes: 95%+ of TDS, heavy metals, nitrates, fluoride, arsenic, most VOCs, some bacteria.
Does NOT remove: chloramines (requires carbon pre-filter, which RO systems include), volatile gases.
Produces: waste water — 3–4 gallons of rejected water per gallon of filtered water.
Best for: well water with multiple contaminants, or if lead, arsenic, or nitrates are detected.

*Good systems*: iSpring RCC7 (7-stage, under-sink, $200), APEC ROES-50 ($200), A.O. Smith 4-stage ($250). Add a remineralization filter (the last stage in many systems) to restore pH and minerals removed by RO — especially important for daily drinking water.

*For Pinelands water*: RO is often unnecessary unless testing reveals specific elevated contaminants. Acidic Pinelands water at low TDS doesn't need TDS removal — it needs pH correction and iron/tannin management if those are the issues.

**Berkey (gravity-fed countertop)**
Removes: bacteria, viruses, protozoa, chlorine, heavy metals, pharmaceuticals, VOCs, fluoride (with add-on PF-2 elements), pesticides.
Does NOT require: electricity or water pressure.
Self-reliance case: the only filter type that works completely off-grid. Two Black Berkey elements filter 3,000 gallons each (6,000 gallons per pair) — at 1 gallon/day per person, that's 8+ years of drinking water per filter set. Stores water in the upper chamber (2.25–6 gallons depending on model).

*Models*: Berkey Travel (1.5 gal), Big Berkey (2.25 gal, most popular for 1–4 people), Royal Berkey (3.25 gal), Crown Berkey (6 gal). Price: $250–430 depending on size. The investment pays back quickly vs. bottled water.

*NJ consideration*: Berkey removes iron and manganese but works better when pre-filtered — run iron-heavy well water through a sediment pre-filter before the Berkey. They also reduce (but don't reliably eliminate) all nitrates — if nitrates are the primary concern, confirm with testing before relying on Berkey alone.

**Whole-house (point of entry)**
Treats all water entering the home — drinking, bathing, laundry. Installed at the main line.

*Sediment filter (Big Blue 20")*: removes particles, sediment, and rust. First stage for any whole-house system. Change every 3–6 months. ~$100 for housing + cartridges.
*Carbon block whole-house*: removes chlorine from all water, including shower water (chlorine absorbed through skin during long showers is a legitimate concern). ~$150–200 for housing.
*Iron filter (birm or air injection)*: addresses iron and manganese before they reach any fixture. Required if iron above 0.3 mg/L. Air injection systems ($500–1,500) work by oxidizing dissolved iron so it can be filtered out.
*Acid neutralizer (calcite tank)*: raises pH of acidic water to prevent pipe corrosion. Critical for Pinelands well water below pH 6.5 with copper plumbing.

A Pinelands well water whole-house sequence for iron + acid + tannins: sediment → acid neutralizer → air injection iron filter → tannin filter → carbon block. Each stage is separate; cost ranges $1,500–3,000 installed.

**Water softener**
Ion exchange removes calcium and magnesium (hardness). Used throughout NJ north of the Pinelands where municipal water from the Delaware River or Raritan aquifer is harder (150–300 mg/L TDS, 100+ mg/L hardness).

**Not needed for Pinelands well water** — Pinelands water is already very soft. Installing a softener in Galloway is a waste of salt and money. If you're in northern NJ or on certain municipal supplies, get a hardness test first.

### Self-Reliance Priority: The Berkey Case

For a homesteading/self-reliance context, a Berkey countertop filter is uniquely valuable: it requires no electricity, no plumbing connection, and no water pressure. During a power outage, flood, or grid disruption, a Berkey filters pond water, rainwater, or suspect municipal water using gravity alone.

A Big Berkey ($280) + two Black Berkey elements handles a household's drinking and cooking water indefinitely with filter replacement every 2–3 years. It's the one filter that works when infrastructure fails.

This is complementary to, not a replacement for, a whole-house system. The Berkey handles drinking water; the whole-house system handles laundry, showers, and appliance protection.

## Getting Started

1. **Get your water tested** before buying any filter. For municipal: pull your CCR. For well: hire a certified NJ lab (NJ DEP maintains a list at nj.gov/dep/watersupply).
2. **Identify the actual problem**: chlorine taste → carbon filter. Iron staining → iron filter. Acidic well water → acid neutralizer. Unknown contaminants → comprehensive test first.
3. **Decide on scope**: drinking water only (under-sink or Berkey) vs. whole-house protection (point of entry system).
4. **For a Pinelands well**: test pH, iron, manganese, tannins, coliform, nitrates. These are the high-probability issues. Sequence the treatment based on results.
5. **Register for your municipal CCR** if on city water — it's free and required to be provided annually.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying a filter before testing.** A water softener on Pinelands water doesn't help. An RO system when you only need a carbon filter wastes water and money. Test first.

**Trusting the NSF certification claim without checking which standard.** NSF/ANSI 42 certifies taste/odor removal only. NSF/ANSI 53 certifies contaminant removal (lead, VOCs). NSF/ANSI 58 certifies RO systems. A filter "NSF certified" for standard 42 doesn't reduce lead — check the specific certification.

**Forgetting filter replacement schedules.** A carbon filter past its rated capacity stops removing contaminants and may release what it's absorbed. Set a calendar reminder.

**Berkey with hard water or iron without pre-filtration.** Iron and heavy minerals clog the Black Berkey elements prematurely. A $15 sediment pre-filter extends element life significantly.
