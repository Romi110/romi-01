# Fabric Guide
*How to identify quality cloth, care for what you own, and make it last*

---

## What Is It

Fabric literacy is knowing what you're buying when you buy clothes. Most people read a tag, see "100% cotton," and think that settles it — it doesn't. There are grades of cotton separated by decades of durability. There's cashmere sold for $40 that falls apart in a season and cashmere sold for $400 that lasts a decade. The difference is almost always in the raw material and how it was processed, not the label.

This file covers the five major fabric categories worth understanding: cotton, wool, linen, cashmere, and leather. For each: what it is, what the quality spectrum looks like, how to identify good versus poor, and how to care for it.

## Why It Matters

Two items that look identical at purchase — same style, similar price — can have wildly different lifespans based on the fabric. A $70 cotton tee from a quality brand can outlast ten $20 tees from a fast-fashion house. The cost-per-wear math in [wardrobe-building.md](wardrobe-building.md) only works if you can identify which items will actually deliver the wear count.

Fabric knowledge also makes you harder to upsell and easier to shop: you read a label, feel the cloth, and know whether the price is justified — no brand name required.

## Core Concepts

### Cotton

Cotton's quality is determined almost entirely by fiber length (staple length). Longer fibers spin into finer, stronger, smoother yarn. Shorter fibers are cheaper to grow and process but pill faster, feel rough sooner, and fade unevenly.

**The quality spectrum:**

| Grade | Fiber Length | Examples | What to expect |
|-------|-------------|----------|----------------|
| Regular (commodity) | Short staple | H&M, Zara basics | Pilling within months, rough after washing |
| Combed cotton | Mid-length, sorted | Better basics, Uniqlo | Noticeably smoother, 2–3x the lifespan |
| Supima / Pima | Long staple (~38mm) | Buck Mason, Sunspel | Very soft, pills minimally, holds color |
| Egyptian (genuine) | Extra-long staple | Expensive shirts | Silky hand feel, extremely durable; note: most "Egyptian cotton" claims are false |

**How to identify quality cotton at purchase:** Stretch a small section of the fabric and release. Low-quality cotton stays slightly distorted; quality cotton snaps back immediately. Also pinch and feel — cheap cotton has a papery, thin texture; good cotton has body and some heft.

**Care:** Cold wash, low heat dry or hang dry. Heat degrades cotton fibers over time. Turn graphic tees inside-out to protect prints. Do not use fabric softener on technical cotton — it coats the fibers and reduces breathability.

### Wool

Wool is the most varied fabric category. The differences between a $30 acrylic-blend sweater labeled "wool" and a $200 merino knit are enormous and worth understanding.

**Types:**

**Merino** — Fine-fibered wool from Merino sheep. The fiber diameter is measured in microns; the standard is 18–22 microns (finer = softer and less itchy, but more delicate). Quality merino sits at 17–19 microns. Weight matters: 170–200g is a genuine mid-layer; 130g is sheer/layering weight. Merino is the best base layer and mid-layer fabric available — temperature-regulating, naturally odor-resistant, machine-washable at quality grades. Uniqlo's merino is a legitimate product at the price.

**Lambswool** — First shearing from young sheep, very soft. Used in knitwear. Slightly coarser than merino but very warm and durable. A lambswool sweater from a quality brand (Johnstons of Elgin, William Lockie) is a 20-year garment with care.

**Shetland** — From Shetland sheep. More texture than merino — deliberately rustic. Worn by people who want a visual and tactile warmth. Itchy against bare skin; usually worn over a base layer.

**Cashmere** — See below. It warrants its own section.

**Tweed and worsted** — Heavier, woven wool for jackets and trousers. Quality is in the tightness of the weave and the weight. Harris Tweed is the benchmark for heavy woven wool; Italian mills (Vitale Barberis Canonico, Loro Piana) for suiting fabric.

**Quality signals for wool:** Look at the fiber count on the label (two-ply vs. single-ply). Two-ply means two strands twisted together — more durable and holds shape better. Genuinely fine wool (merino, cashmere) won't prickle against the back of your hand. If it does, the fiber is too coarse or the grade is low.

**Care:** Cold machine wash on wool cycle or hand wash. Never wring — roll in a towel to extract water. Lay flat to dry; hanging a wet sweater stretches it permanently. Shave pilling with a fabric shaver (not scissors). Store folded, not hung — hanging wool shoulders distort over time.

### Linen

Linen is made from flax plant fibers. It's the oldest textile in continuous use — and for NJ summers, it's the only fabric that keeps you genuinely cool in humid heat. Linen is breathable because the fibers are hollow; sweat passes through rather than being trapped.

**Quality spectrum:** Unlike cotton, linen quality is more about origin and weave than fiber grade. Belgian linen (from the Lys River valley) is the benchmark. Irish linen is also premium. Most cheap linen is sourced from China — it's finer on specs but the fabric has less body and feels lighter in a way that doesn't hold up.

**What quality linen feels like:** Some texture and body. Cheap linen is thin and floppy — it wrinkles badly and looks cheap within an hour of wearing. Quality linen wrinkles too (this is unavoidable and part of the look) but the wrinkles fall out with body heat and look intentional rather than sloppy.

**The wrinkle question:** Linen wrinkles. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something. The answer is to either embrace it (casual summer shirts, pants) or to choose linen blends (linen/cotton) for a slightly less prone-to-wrinkling result at the cost of some breathability.

**Care:** Machine wash cold, line dry or low-heat tumble dry. Do not over-dry — linen dried completely in a hot dryer becomes brittle at the creases. A slightly damp iron on the linen setting removes wrinkles completely if needed.

### Cashmere

Cashmere deserves its own section because the quality range is enormous and the cheap version is almost a different product entirely.

Cashmere fiber comes from the undercoat of Cashmere goats. High-quality cashmere uses fibers 14–16 microns in diameter; low-quality cashmere (and there is a lot of it) uses shorter, coarser fibers that are combed from less controlled sources. The difference is immediately apparent: good cashmere is impossibly soft and doesn't pill aggressively; cheap cashmere starts pilling within weeks.

**Grades:**

- **Grade A (or Grade 1):** Fiber length 34mm+, diameter 14–15.5 microns. From inner Mongolia. What Loro Piana, Brunello Cucinelli, and quality Scottish mills use.
- **Grade B:** Shorter fiber, slightly coarser. Used by mid-tier brands — still nicer than most wool but noticeably different in hand.
- **Unmarked/cheap cashmere:** Short, coarse fiber. What fills $30–$60 cashmere sweaters. Pilling within weeks is the tell.

**Ply:** Two-ply cashmere (two threads twisted together) is significantly more durable than single-ply. Single-ply cashmere is not worthless but requires more careful handling.

**Price as a signal:** Cashmere is expensive to produce correctly. A $60 "cashmere" sweater is almost always compromised — short fiber, mixed with other materials, or Grade C. Genuine quality cashmere starts around $150–200 for a basic knit at a brand like Uniqlo's best tier; real premium starts at $300+. The brands in [fashion-brands.md](fashion-brands.md) (Loro Piana, Brunello Cucinelli, Drake's) are what the top tier looks like.

**Care:** Hand wash in cold water with a dedicated wool/cashmere wash (Woolite or Soak). Never machine wash unless the label explicitly says so and you're willing to accept some pilling acceleration. Dry flat. Fold and store in a breathable container — do not compress in plastic bags. Cedar blocks deter moths; this matters more than most people realize.

### Leather

Leather quality is more misunderstood than any other fabric — because the naming is deliberately confusing and the cheap versions are engineered to look identical to quality until they fail.

**The hierarchy:**

**Full-grain leather** — The outermost layer of the hide, with the natural grain intact. The most durable leather that exists. Resists moisture, develops patina rather than cracking, can be resoled and repaired. Red Wing Iron Ranger boots, Alden shell cordovan, quality wallets and belts. Scratches and scuffs at first; develops a unique surface over years of use.

**Top-grain leather** — Full-grain with the surface sanded down to remove imperfections. More uniform appearance, less natural texture, slightly weaker fiber structure. Still genuine quality leather — just slightly below full-grain. Used in quality dress shoes and some jackets.

**Genuine leather** — Industry term for lower layers of the hide bonded with adhesive. This is the entry-level tier and the point at which longevity drops sharply. It looks like leather, feels similar initially, and peels and cracks within 2–5 years. The word "genuine" is not a quality claim — it just means real animal material rather than synthetic.

**Bonded leather** — Scraps and dust from leather production bonded with polyurethane. Not meaningfully leather. Usually found in cheap furniture and very cheap accessories. Fails quickly.

**PU/vegan leather** — Synthetic. The quality range here is actually wide: cheap PU cracks like bonded leather; quality Ultrasuede or Piñatex alternatives can be durable. But they are not leather.

**How to identify full-grain leather:** Natural grain pattern should be visible and vary slightly across the surface (not uniform). The edge of the piece should show layered fibers, not a clean cut synthetic. It will smell like leather (not chemical). Run your fingernail across it — full-grain shows a slight mark that fades; bonded shows a cut or scuff that doesn't recover.

**Care:** Clean with a damp cloth as needed. Condition twice a year with a dedicated leather conditioner (Leather Honey, Bick 4). This prevents drying and cracking. For suede, brush with a suede brush to maintain nap; use a water repellent spray before first wear. For waxed cotton (Barbour-type jackets), re-wax every 1–2 years with Barbour wax.

## Key Considerations

**Blends are not a downgrade.** A 80% merino / 20% nylon blend is often more durable than pure merino without meaningfully sacrificing softness. Merino-nylon socks, for example, last significantly longer than pure merino. Know what the blend is doing before judging it.

**Care labels are conservative.** "Dry clean only" on a wool jacket often means "we don't want liability for your home washing." Many such items can be carefully hand-washed. "Machine wash cold" means the item can handle a machine — not that hot wash is fine. Err toward gentler care than the label minimum.

**Fabric weight is underrated.** A 200g cotton tee is a different product from a 160g cotton tee at the same price. Weight correlates with longevity and shape retention. Brands that list weight (Buck Mason, Sunspel) are usually doing so because it's a selling point.

**Pilling is not just a cheap-fabric problem.** Any wool or cashmere will pill where there is friction — under the arms, at the collar, cuffs. Even expensive sweaters pill. The difference is how quickly: quality fiber resists pilling for years; cheap fiber starts in weeks. A fabric shaver handles pilling when it does occur — this is normal maintenance, not a sign of failure.

## Getting Started

You don't need to rebuild your wardrobe to apply this. Start with:

1. **Read the labels on what you currently own** — identify what's actually cotton vs. cotton-blend vs. synthetic. Start feeling the difference.
2. **Buy one quality wool or cashmere item next winter** — a merino crewneck from Uniqlo (around $70) is a low-cost reference point for what quality wool feels like.
3. **Learn to identify full-grain leather** on your next shoe or belt purchase — check the edge of the leather at the sole or on the back of the belt.
4. **Start caring for what you own** — condition leather twice a year, wash wool on cold. The lifespan difference from correct care alone is significant.

## Common Mistakes

**Trusting marketing names.** "Egyptian cotton," "genuine leather," "Italian wool" — these terms have minimal legal definition and are routinely applied to substandard products. Judge by feel and by the signals above, not the name.

**Washing everything the same way.** Hot machine wash destroys wool, shrinks quality cotton, and degrades leather finishes. Two minutes reading a care label and using cold water is the single highest-leverage maintenance habit.

**Buying cheap cashmere.** It looks the same for a month. Then it pills everywhere and you've spent $60 on something you hate wearing. Better to buy one quality item less often.

**Ignoring pilling.** A pilled sweater is not finished — it looks bad but can be restored with a fabric shaver in 10 minutes. Most people throw away perfectly good sweaters they could've saved.
