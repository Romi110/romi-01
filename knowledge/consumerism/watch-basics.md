# Watch Basics
*Mechanical vs. quartz, how movements work, brand tiers, and the case for buying one good watch*

---

## What Is It

A wristwatch is one of the few consumer objects where craft, longevity, and daily utility fully intersect. Understanding what's inside the case — how different movements work, what complications do, why steel type matters — makes the buying decision legible rather than brand-driven. This file covers the fundamentals of watch selection from a BIFL and craft perspective.

## Why It Matters

At 28, a well-chosen watch is a 30-year decision. Mechanical watches don't become obsolete, don't require software updates, and are fully repairable. A $500 automatic purchased today may outlive a $1,500 smartwatch by decades. The self-reliance angle is real: a mechanical watch doesn't need a battery, a charger, or a network. It runs on your movement.

## Core Concepts

### Movement Types

The movement is the engine inside the watch. Everything else — case, dial, hands, strap — is packaging around it.

**Quartz (battery-powered)**: a battery sends electrical current through a quartz crystal that vibrates at 32,768 Hz. A circuit divides that frequency to drive a stepping motor that moves the seconds hand in ticks. Accuracy: ±15 seconds per month. Requires a battery replacement every 1–3 years. Virtually maintenance-free otherwise.

The case for quartz: more accurate than mechanical by an order of magnitude, cheaper, lighter, and requires no skill to maintain. A Seiko 5 quartz at $60 keeps better time than a Rolex Submariner.

**Mechanical (manual-wind)**: a mainspring wound by hand stores energy and releases it through a gear train regulated by the escapement. The balance wheel oscillates (commonly 28,800 times/hour = 8 beats/second), dividing time into countable units. Accuracy: ±10–30 seconds per day depending on movement quality and regulation. Requires winding every 1–3 days. Servicing every 5–7 years (cleaning, lubrication, regulation).

**Automatic (self-winding)**: same as mechanical but with a rotor — a semicircular weight that swings freely and winds the mainspring as you move your wrist. Worn regularly, an automatic winds itself. Left still for 24–48 hours, the power reserve depletes and it stops. Accuracy: same as manual-wind.

**Solar / Eco-Drive / Kinetic**: solar converts light to electricity stored in a capacitor; Kinetic converts wrist motion to electricity. Accuracy of quartz with minimal battery intervention. Citizen's Eco-Drive technology is the most developed — some models run a year in the dark on a full charge.

---

### Why Mechanical Matters

The case for a mechanical watch isn't accuracy — quartz wins that. It's craft, longevity, and the absence of obsolescence.

A mechanical watch built to modern tolerances from quality steel is maintainable indefinitely. The parts are machined metal; there's nothing to degrade beyond lubricant (which services address). A 1960s Omega Seamaster can be serviced today and run for another 60 years. No smartwatch will be functional in 2085.

The movement visible through a display caseback is a miniature mechanical system: the rotor, the bridges, the balance wheel oscillating — it's visible engineering operating in real time. For someone drawn to systems and craft, this has a different quality than an app on a wrist.

### Complications

A "complication" is any function beyond basic timekeeping.

**Date**: the most common complication. Quick-set date (adjustable without cycling through 12 hours) vs. date that requires cycling — the latter is an annoyance on months shorter than 31 days. Quick-set is standard on any watch above the budget tier.

**GMT / dual time**: a fourth hand pointing to a second time zone. Useful for frequent travelers or anyone with regular cross-timezone communication. The Rolex GMT-Master popularized this; the Seiko SKX series brought it to accessible price points.

**Chronograph**: a stopwatch function built into the watch. Two pushers — start/stop and reset. Useful for timing; complex movement with more failure points. For casual use, rarely necessary.

**Power reserve indicator**: displays remaining mainspring tension. Useful on manual-wind watches. Rarer on automatics since you can tell by looking at it whether it's running.

**Moon phase**: tracks the lunar cycle. No practical use for most buyers; highly decorative.

**Rule of thumb**: buy only the complications you'll actually use. Each complication adds movement complexity and potential failure points. Date is universally useful. GMT is useful if you need it. Chronograph is often underused.

### Case and Crystal Materials

**Case:**
- **Stainless steel (316L, 904L)**: the standard. Corrosion resistant, durable, takes a polish or brushed finish. 904L (used by Rolex) is more corrosion resistant and harder to machine — a genuine quality difference, not just marketing. 316L is the standard for the rest of the industry and completely adequate.
- **Titanium**: lighter (~40% lighter than steel), hypoallergenic, slightly less scratch resistant. Seiko, Grand Seiko, and tool watch brands use titanium for dive watches where weight matters.
- **Gold/gold-filled**: solid gold is extremely heavy and expensive; gold-filled (PVD coating) scratches through eventually. For most buyers, these are occasional-wear decisions, not daily watches.

**Crystal:**
- **Mineral**: treated glass. Affordable, scratches more easily than sapphire, but chips rather than shatters.
- **Sapphire**: synthetic corundum (hardness 9 on Mohs scale, just below diamond). Scratch-resistant, cracks rather than scratches under normal conditions. Anti-reflective coating on sapphire is standard on quality watches. Any watch above ~$200 should have sapphire crystal.
- **Acrylic (Hesalite)**: plastic crystal used on vintage watches and some intentional vintage-spec watches (Omega Speedmaster Professional). Scratches easily but polishes out; doesn't shatter.

**Water resistance**: expressed in meters, though the rating refers to static pressure testing, not depth.
- 30m / 3 ATM: splash resistant only. Don't swim with it.
- 50m / 5 ATM: can handle swimming, not diving.
- 100m / 10 ATM: recreational swimming and snorkeling.
- 200m / 20 ATM: suitable for scuba diving.
- "Water resistant" without a rating: effectively splash-proof only.

### Brand Tiers

**Entry (under $200):**
- **Seiko 5 automatic**: the benchmark entry automatic. In-house movement (7S series), accurate to ±15 sec/day, stainless, sapphire crystal on newer models. $80–150. Hard to beat for a first automatic watch.
- **Orient**: Japanese manufacturer, owned by Seiko. Their Bambino dress watch (~$100–150) and Mako dive watch (~$100) use in-house movements. Solid value.
- **Citizen Eco-Drive**: the reference solar watch. Charges in any light, runs indefinitely. Accurate, reliable, various styles from $100–300.

**Mid-range ($200–600):**
- **Seiko Presage**: upgraded finishing, in-house movements, enamel or textured dials, sapphire crystals. The $250–400 tier is the sweet spot for genuine craft at an accessible price.
- **Tissot PRX**: Swiss-made, ETA Powermatic 80 movement (80-hour power reserve, ±10 sec/day), Integrated bracelet design derived from the 1970s original. ~$325–375. One of the best value propositions in the category.
- **Tissot Le Locle**: dress automatic, Swiss Elaboré grade movement, decorated movement visible through caseback. ~$325.
- **Hamilton Khaki Field**: military-inspired, American brand (Swiss-made), ETA-based movement, highly legible, durable. ~$400–500. Genuine BIFL watch.

**Upper mid-range ($600–2,000):**
- **Longines**: Swiss COSC-adjacent movements, strong heritage (the movement inside many Omega watches is Longines-derived), excellent value. Master Collection (~$1,200–1,500) is the benchmark.
- **Omega Seamaster 300**: the serious tool watch entry. Co-Axial escapement (lower friction, longer service intervals), METAS certification (more rigorous than COSC), magnetic resistance. ~$5,000 new but available pre-owned from ~$2,500.
- **Grand Seiko** (pre-owned): Japan's domestic premium brand, produced to tolerances that rival Swiss maisons. Spring Drive movement (accuracy: ±1 sec/day) is a hybrid of mechanical and quartz regulation. Pre-owned Grand Seiko entries from ~$1,500–2,000 represent extraordinary value.

**Luxury (above $3,000):**
- **Rolex, Omega, IWC, Jaeger-LeCoultre**: genuine quality steps in movement finishing, materials, and tolerances — but also significant brand premium. The pre-owned market for these is well-established and prices are relatively transparent.
- **Patek Philippe, A. Lange & Söhne, Vacheron Constantin**: the apex of mechanical watchmaking. Movements finished to a different standard. Heirloom objects. Out of scope for a first watch discussion.

### New vs. Pre-owned

Watches depreciate differently from most goods:
- A $500 Seiko Presage bought new loses value like any consumer good
- A $5,000 Rolex Submariner holds value and often appreciates — the pre-owned market is efficient and liquid
- A $1,500 Grand Seiko bought pre-owned is available at 40–50% of new retail

The pre-owned market is legitimate and well-organized. **WatchBox, Crown & Caliber, and Hodinkee Shop** (US) are reputable grey market dealers with authentication and warranty. **Chrono24** is the global marketplace — equivalent to eBay but watch-specific with escrow protection. **r/watchexchange** for community sales.

For the mid-range tier ($500–2,000), buying pre-owned often gets you a significantly better watch for the same money as buying new at the tier below.

## Key Considerations

**Service interval**: mechanical watches need service every 5–7 years — disassembly, cleaning, lubrication, regulation. Cost: $200–500 for a mid-range watch, $800–1,500 for a Rolex. Factor this into the true cost of ownership.

**Strap systems**: most watches come on a bracelet or leather strap but accept third-party straps. NATO straps ($20) and aftermarket leather ($40–80 from brands like Hirsch or Sinn) change the character of a watch significantly. The lug width is the key measurement (20mm, 22mm are most common).

**The one-watch philosophy**: one well-chosen watch worn daily develops a patina, fits perfectly, and becomes part of how you present yourself. Five watches worn occasionally never develop this. The considered luxury approach — not the collector approach.

**The heirloom question**: a mechanical watch is one of the few consumer objects you can plausibly pass to the next generation. Factor this into the decision. A $400 Hamilton Khaki Field that runs for 50 years with one service is a different purchase than a $400 fashion watch that's irrelevant in 5.

## Getting Started

1. **Determine the category**: dress watch (thin, metal bracelet or leather strap, dark dial) vs. field/tool watch (legible, durable, water resistant) vs. sport/dive watch (200m+ WR, rotating bezel). The use case guides the design.
2. **Set the budget honestly**: the $200–400 range has genuinely good watches (Tissot PRX, Seiko Presage). There's no urgency to spend $2,000 on a first watch.
3. **Handle it before buying**: watch fit and weight are highly personal. A watch that looks right online may feel wrong in hand. Local jewelers and authorized dealers often allow try-ons even when not purchasing.
4. **Start with a reputable brand at any tier**: Seiko, Tissot, Hamilton, Orient all make watches that hold up to daily wear, can be serviced, and represent genuine mechanical craft.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying a fashion brand watch at a significant price.** Fossil, MVMT, Daniel Wellington — these are fashion objects with quartz movements that don't warrant their price. The money spent on a $300 MVMT buys a Tissot PRX with a Swiss mechanical movement and 200-year brand heritage.

**Over-complicating the first watch.** A clean three-hand watch (hours, minutes, seconds) wears better and fits more contexts than a chronograph or complicated dial. Add complications later if actually needed.

**Ignoring the bracelet/strap.** A great dial on a poor bracelet feels cheap. Factory bracelets on sub-$300 watches are often the first thing worth upgrading. A $30 NATO strap from Crown & Buckle transforms the wearing experience.

**Buying to flip**: watch collecting as investment speculation requires significant knowledge of the market and ties up cash. Buy to wear.
