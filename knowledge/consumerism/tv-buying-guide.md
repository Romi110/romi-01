# TV Buying Guide
*Panel types, specs that matter, specs that don't, and how to pick a TV without being misled*

---

## What Is It

Modern TVs are sold with an overwhelming number of specs, most of which are marketing noise. Understanding what's actually behind the screen — how different panel technologies work, which picture quality factors are real, and what manufacturers inflate — lets you make a good decision rather than a well-marketed one.

This file covers the technology behind TV screens, what each spec actually means, and how to navigate the current market. Monitor buying for a desk setup is a different decision and not covered here.

## Why It Matters

A TV is a high-cost item you use daily for years. The difference between a $600 and $1,500 TV is real, but the difference between a $1,500 and $2,500 TV is often marketing rather than picture quality. Knowing the technology means you spend where the quality gap is genuine and skip where it isn't.

## Core Concepts

### Panel Technology — What Actually Creates the Picture

This is the most important variable in picture quality. Everything else is secondary.

**OLED (Organic Light Emitting Diode)**
Each pixel in an OLED generates its own light and can turn completely off. This produces true black — not "very dark gray," but zero light output. Contrast is functionally infinite. Colors near black are rendered with accuracy no LCD can match.

Two OLED variants matter now:
- **W-OLED** (LG Display's design, used in LG and many Sony OLEDs): white OLED subpixel with color filter layer. The dominant OLED design for the past decade.
- **QD-OLED** (Samsung Display, used in Samsung and Sony): blue OLED layer + quantum dot color conversion. Produces wider color volume and higher peak brightness than W-OLED. Better for HDR highlights and brighter rooms.

Downside: OLED can't match LCD's peak brightness in full-screen scenes (the sun in a daylight shot, a white document). For very bright rooms, this matters. For typical evening TV viewing, it doesn't.

**Mini-LED LCD**
Traditional LCD technology — liquid crystals blocking a backlight — but with thousands of small LEDs as the backlight instead of a few hundred. More LEDs means more local dimming zones (the ability to dim specific areas of the screen independently), which improves contrast far beyond regular LCD. Samsung Neo QLED, TCL QM8, LG QNED are the main players.

Mini-LED is the right pick for very bright rooms where OLED's brightness ceiling is a real limitation. Contrast is still worse than OLED for dark room viewing — backlight bleed around bright objects on dark backgrounds (haloing) is visible in the best mini-LED sets at close viewing distance, though it's improved substantially.

**Regular LCD (IPS and VA panels)**
Budget and mid-range LCD without mini-LED backlighting. Two panel subtypes:
- **IPS**: wide viewing angles, accurate color, but mediocre contrast (~1,000:1 native). Fine for bright rooms and wide seating arrangements.
- **VA**: better contrast (~3,000–6,000:1) than IPS, narrower viewing angles. Better for dark-room viewing.

Regular LCD is the right technology for secondary TVs, bedrooms, and budget situations. Not what you want for a primary living room screen if picture quality matters.

**MicroLED**
Individual microscopic LEDs per pixel — OLED-like contrast without organic materials (no burn-in risk, higher sustainable brightness). Currently exists only in very large formats (89"+) at prices above $10,000. Mention it to know it exists; it's not a realistic purchase for most buyers yet.

### HDR — What It Means and What It Doesn't

HDR (High Dynamic Range) is real when done right and meaningless when done wrong. The format matters more than the label.

**HDR10**: the base standard. Uses static metadata — the mastering engineer sets tone mapping parameters for the whole film, and the TV applies them uniformly. Widely supported. Minimum bar. Most 4K content supports it.

**Dolby Vision**: dynamic metadata — tone mapping adjusts scene by scene (or frame by frame). A dark scene is optimized differently than a bright outdoor scene. Results in noticeably better HDR rendering. Netflix, Disney+, Apple TV 4K all support it. If choosing between two TVs, Dolby Vision support matters.

**HDR10+**: Samsung's alternative to Dolby Vision. Also dynamic metadata. Less content support than Dolby Vision. Effectively the same concept, lesser ecosystem.

**What makes a TV capable of real HDR**: brightness. A TV needs to hit at least 600 nits peak brightness to use HDR meaningfully. 1,000+ nits for genuine HDR impact in a bright room. Many cheap "HDR-compatible" TVs hit 300-400 nits — technically able to decode the signal, but not bright enough to render the highlights HDR requires. This is the most common HDR lie in TV marketing.

### Resolution

**4K (3840×2160)** is the current standard for any TV 43" or larger. Content exists, the price premium over 1080p is minimal at most sizes, and the improvement in perceived sharpness at normal viewing distances is real above 55".

**8K**: content doesn't exist outside a handful of demo clips. 8K upscaling is mildly beneficial at extreme sizes (75"+) from close distances. Not a reason to buy a TV in 2025. Ignore.

**1080p**: fine for smaller TVs (32-43") in secondary rooms where viewing distance is close. Costs less and the resolution difference is undetectable at normal distances on small screens.

### Refresh Rate

**60Hz**: adequate for all traditional TV content (film at 24fps, TV at 30/60fps). Not a limitation for most watching.

**120Hz (native)**: meaningful for two use cases: (1) gaming at 120fps from a PS5 or Xbox Series X, and (2) motion interpolation (the TV invents intermediate frames to make motion smoother). Native 120Hz is what you need for gaming; interpolation is a processing trick applied to 60Hz and lower content.

**240Hz claims on budget TVs**: almost always motion interpolation of a 60Hz or 120Hz panel, not a native 240Hz display. Doesn't mean the panel refreshes 240 times per second. This is standard spec inflation.

**The "soap opera effect"**: motion interpolation makes film content look like cheap daytime TV — movement is unnaturally smooth, the cinematic look disappears. Turn it off. Every TV has a setting to disable it (TruMotion on LG, MotionFlow on Sony, Auto Motion Plus on Samsung). Leave it off for film and TV; it's occasionally useful for sports.

---

### Gaming-Specific Features

If a TV will also be used with a PS5 or Xbox Series X, these specs matter:

**HDMI 2.1**: required for 4K/120Hz signal input from a current console. Standard HDMI 2.0 caps at 4K/60Hz. Verify the TV has at least two HDMI 2.1 ports — some manufacturers quietly include only one HDMI 2.1 port alongside three HDMI 2.0 ports.

**VRR (Variable Refresh Rate)**: syncs the TV's refresh rate to the console or GPU's output frame rate. Eliminates screen tearing (the horizontal tear artifact when frames don't align). Look for HDMI Forum VRR; G-Sync compatible and FreeSync Premium are also common. This matters for gaming; irrelevant for regular TV viewing.

**Input lag**: the delay between the source signal and what appears on screen. Game Mode on most modern TVs achieves 10–15ms, which is imperceptible. Above 20ms starts to be noticeable in fast-paced games. Check rtings.com for measured input lag in game mode — don't rely on specs.

**ALLM (Auto Low Latency Mode)**: the TV detects when a console is connected and automatically switches to game mode. Convenience feature, not a quality differentiator.

### Size and Viewing Distance

Most people buy too small. The correct size for 4K content at a given viewing distance:

`Optimal screen size (inches) = viewing distance (inches) ÷ 1.5`

At 9 feet (108 inches): 108 ÷ 1.5 = **72 inches**
At 8 feet (96 inches): 96 ÷ 1.5 = **64 inches**
At 7 feet (84 inches): 84 ÷ 1.5 = **56 inches**

These numbers feel large but reflect what's actually needed to resolve 4K detail at those distances. The calculation uses the premise that 4K pixel density is worth buying when you can actually distinguish individual pixels — closer than this and you can; farther and you can't.

### Specs That Are Marketing Noise

**Dynamic contrast ratio** (1,000,000:1, 10,000,000:1, etc.): measures the difference between the brightest white and darkest black across different scenes with the backlight dimmed — not simultaneously. Native contrast ratio is the meaningful number. Dynamic contrast ratios are effectively infinite on any TV and meaningless as a comparison.

**"AI" picture processing**: marginally useful upscaling. Not a purchase driver. Every TV has it now.

**Built-in speakers**: thin, flat TVs produce poor audio physics. Uniformly bad on all flat panels. Budget for a soundbar or receiver from the start.

**"Local dimming" on regular LCD TVs with few zones**: 6 or 12 dimming zones on a regular LCD produces obvious blooming (bright halos around bright objects on dark backgrounds). Genuine local dimming requires hundreds or thousands of zones. Mini-LED with 500+ zones is meaningful; regular LCD's handful of zones is not.

### Brand and Tier Summary

**Best picture (OLED):**
- LG C-series — best price-to-performance OLED, W-OLED panel, strong picture and processing
- Sony A80L/A95L — QD-OLED with Sony's XR processing (better motion handling); costs more
- LG G-series — MLA panel (brighter than standard W-OLED, closer to QD-OLED); premium over C-series
- Samsung S90C/S95C — QD-OLED; excellent color volume, competitive with Sony A95L

**Bright room / HDR highlight performance (Mini-LED):**
- TCL QM8 — best value in mini-LED; aggressive pricing, competitive picture
- Samsung QN90B/QN95 — strong mini-LED, excellent gaming features
- Sony X95L — Sony processing advantage; most expensive in the tier

**Budget / secondary rooms (regular LCD):**
- TCL 5-series, Hisense U6/U7 — acceptable picture at low cost; correct expectations

## Key Considerations

**Burn-in on OLED**: modern OLEDs (2020+) have substantially improved resistance. The risk is real but often overstated for typical mixed TV use. The scenarios that cause burn-in: displaying a static image or logo for 8+ hours daily (news channel bug, sports ticker, video game HUD in a static position for hundreds of hours). Normal streaming, film, and varied game use poses low risk. If you're considering using a TV as a permanent PC monitor, burn-in risk is higher.

**Calibration**: factory settings are tuned for showroom brightness — oversaturated, too bright, wrong color temperature. Switch to "Movie" or "Cinema" mode immediately. Disable motion smoothing. Reduce backlight to match room conditions. A professional ISF calibration ($300–500) extracts the maximum from a high-end panel; worthwhile on a $1,500+ set you'll use for a decade.

**Room conditions matter as much as panel choice**: OLED in a pitch-dark room is unambiguously the best picture. OLED in a bright room with sunlight directly on the screen may disappoint compared to a bright mini-LED. Match the technology to the actual environment.

## Getting Started

1. **Measure the room**: determine your primary seating distance, then use the size formula. Buy for the calculated size, not what feels reasonable in a showroom.
2. **Assess the room lighting**: consistently dark → OLED is the right choice. Very bright with windows → mini-LED.
3. **Set a budget tier**: OLED starts around $800 for 55" (LG C-series on sale), $1,200–1,500 for 65". Mini-LED (TCL QM8) at $700–900 for 65". Regular LCD for secondary rooms at $300–500.
4. **Check rtings.com** for the specific model you're considering: measured contrast, brightness, input lag, color accuracy. More reliable than manufacturer specs.
5. **After purchase**: switch to Movie mode, disable motion smoothing, set brightness to the room level. Done.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying on contrast ratio numbers from the spec sheet.** Dynamic contrast ratios are fabricated as comparison metrics. Use rtings.com measured native contrast.

**Buying a "240Hz" budget TV expecting native 240Hz.** It's a 60Hz or 120Hz panel with interpolation. Irrelevant for content quality.

**Ignoring room conditions.** The best OLED in the wrong room underperforms a mid-range mini-LED in the right room.

**Skipping the soundbar budget.** Built-in TV speakers are always the weak point. Budget $150–400 for even a basic soundbar; the audio improvement per dollar spent is far higher than upgrading the panel tier.

**Buying based on brand name alone.** LG, Sony, and Samsung all make garbage budget TVs alongside excellent flagship models. The model line matters more than the brand.

## Resources

- **rtings.com** — independent, measured TV reviews with standardized testing. The only reliable source for objective picture quality data. Check any TV here before buying.
- *HDTVtest* (YouTube) — Vincent Teoh's channel; technically rigorous calibration-focused reviews. The best video format for understanding what measured specs actually look like on screen.
- *The Wirecutter / NY Times TV picks* — practical recommendations by tier; useful for shortlisting, not for understanding the technology.
