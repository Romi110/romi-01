# Generator Buying Guide
*Inverter vs. conventional, watt sizing, fuel types, and what NJ storm history actually requires*

---

## What Is It

A generator is a fuel-powered device that produces AC electricity independently of the grid. The buying decision has more meaningful variables than most major appliances — inverter vs. conventional, watt sizing, fuel type, and how it integrates with your home's circuits all affect whether the generator actually solves the problem when you need it.

For the portable power station (battery) alternative, see [energy/portable-power-stations.md](../energy/portable-power-stations.md) — that file covers when a battery station is the right choice. This file covers fuel-based generators.

## Why It Matters

Atlantic County averages 1–2 extended outages per year from hurricanes, nor'easters, and summer storm lines. After Superstorm Sandy (2012), parts of South Jersey were without power for 2+ weeks. A generator bought impulsively the day before a storm — underpowered, wrong fuel type, unfamiliar — is significantly less useful than one sized correctly and tested in advance.

## Core Concepts

### Inverter vs. Conventional Generator

This is the most important distinction. The technology inside determines output quality, noise, fuel efficiency, and price.

**Conventional (open-frame) generator**: the engine runs at a fixed speed (3,600 RPM for 60Hz output) regardless of load. Produces electricity directly from the alternator. Louder (65–75 dB at 25 feet). Less fuel efficient at partial load. Typically available in higher wattages (3,000–15,000W) at lower cost per watt.

Output quality: the sine wave is "dirty" — voltage and frequency fluctuate slightly. Adequate for resistive loads (lights, water pumps, refrigerators). May damage or shorten the life of sensitive electronics (TVs, computers, phone chargers, medical equipment) over time.

**Inverter generator**: the engine speed varies with load — runs slower under light loads, throttles up under heavy loads. Generates raw DC power, then electronically inverts it to clean AC (total harmonic distortion < 3% vs. 20–25% for conventional). Significantly quieter (50–59 dB at 25 feet — similar to a conversation). More fuel efficient at partial load. More expensive per watt. Generally limited to 1,000–4,500W.

Output quality: "clean power" — safe for all electronics. The right choice for anything with a microprocessor: phones, laptops, TVs, CPAP machines, modern variable-speed refrigerators.

**Which to buy for a household**: an inverter generator at 2,000–3,500W covers refrigerator, freezer, lights, and device charging with clean power and reasonable noise. A conventional generator at 5,000W+ is needed for well pumps, window AC units, or whole-house capability — but accept the louder operation and dirtier power.

### Watt Sizing

Sizing a generator requires knowing the running watts and surge watts of everything you intend to power simultaneously.

**Running watts (continuous)**: what the device uses once running steadily.
**Surge watts (startup)**: the brief peak drawn when a motor starts. Can be 2–6× the running watts for compressor-driven loads.

**Common household loads:**

| Load | Running Watts | Surge Watts |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Refrigerator (full-size) | 150–200W | 600–800W |
| Chest freezer | 100–150W | 400–600W |
| Window AC (8,000 BTU) | 900W | 1,800W |
| Window AC (12,000 BTU) | 1,200W | 2,400W |
| Well pump (1/2 HP) | 900W | 2,100W |
| Well pump (1 HP) | 1,500W | 4,500W |
| LED lighting (10 bulbs) | 80W | 80W |
| Laptop | 60W | 60W |
| Phone charger | 20W | 20W |
| CPAP (no humidifier) | 30–40W | 50W |
| CPAP (with humidifier) | 70–100W | 120W |
| Sump pump (1/3 HP) | 500W | 1,300W |

**Sizing approach**: add up the running watts of everything you'd run simultaneously. Then check that the generator's surge rating handles the largest single surge in that list. You don't add surges — only one motor starts at a time.

**Example for a NJ household (no AC)**: refrigerator (150W) + freezer (120W) + lights (80W) + CPAP (40W) + phone/laptop (80W) = 470W running. Largest surge: refrigerator at 800W. A 1,000W inverter generator handles this with headroom.

**With window AC**: add 1,200W running + 2,400W surge. Now need 2,000–3,000W running capacity, and the generator must handle a 2,400W surge when the AC compressor kicks on. A 2,200W inverter generator is marginal; a 3,500W inverter or 5,000W conventional is comfortable.

**With a well pump**: the 1 HP well pump at 4,500W surge requires a generator rated 5,000W+ surge. Inverter generators typically max at 4,500W surge (Westinghouse iGen4500). A conventional 5,000W+ open-frame generator handles well pumps reliably.

### Fuel Types

**Gasoline only**: the most common and least convenient for emergency use. Gasoline degrades: untreated gas goes stale in 30 days, ethanol-blended gas (E10, standard at NJ pumps) absorbs moisture and separates. With fuel stabilizer (STA-BIL), it stores for 12 months. During a major storm, gas stations lose power too — filling stations are unavailable when you need fuel most. Always store 5–10 gallons of stabilized gas before storm season.

**Dual-fuel (gasoline + propane)**: the most practical choice for emergency use. Propane (LP) stores indefinitely — a 20 lb barbecue tank stores safely for years. You don't need to rotate it or add stabilizer. Propane is available even when gas stations are down (hardware stores, Costco, U-Haul). Trade-off: most dual-fuel generators produce 10–15% less power on propane vs. gasoline.

For an emergency-preparedness context, dual-fuel is the clear recommendation. Run propane for most outage use; keep gasoline as backup if propane runs out.

**Natural gas (standby generators only)**: standby generators can be piped to your home's natural gas line — infinite fuel supply as long as the gas utility is running (which it almost always is, even during power outages). No fuel storage required. The major advantage of standby systems.

**Tri-fuel**: some generators add natural gas as a third fuel option. Useful if you want standby-like capability from a portable unit, but these are rare and expensive.

### Specific Generator Recommendations

**Honda EU2200i** — 1,800W running / 2,200W surge. The industry benchmark inverter generator. 48–57 dB (quieter than a conversation at 25 feet). 0.95-gallon tank with 3.2–8.1 hour runtime at varying loads. ~1,060 lbs of fuel efficiency edge. ~$1,100 new. The reason professionals use this: it simply doesn't fail. A 10-year-old EU2200i bought used still runs reliably. Pairs with a second EU2200i in parallel (Honda parallel cable) to double output to 3,600W.

*Best for*: essential loads only — refrigerator, freezer, lights, devices, CPAP. Not enough for AC or well pump alone.

**Yamaha EF2200iS** — Honda's closest equivalent. 1,800W running / 2,200W surge. Nearly identical specs, comparable quality, typically $50–100 cheaper. Less common resale market than Honda.

**Champion 3500-Watt Dual Fuel Inverter** (46539) — 3,500W running / 4,000W surge, dual-fuel (gas + propane), inverter technology. ~$600–700 new. Significantly cheaper than Honda at nearly twice the output. Heavier and louder (52 dB vs. Honda's 48 dB). Reliability is good for the price. The practical choice for someone who wants dual-fuel + inverter + window AC capability at an accessible price.

**Westinghouse iGen4500** — 3,700W running / 4,500W surge, inverter, remote start. ~$600–700. Handles everything including the 2,400W surge from a 12,000 BTU window AC. A strong value at this wattage tier.

**Honda EU7000iS** — 5,500W running / 7,000W surge, inverter. The premium large inverter. ~$4,000 new. Overkill for most households; right for well pump + AC + everything else simultaneously with clean power throughout.

**Generac GP6500 (conventional open-frame)** — 5,500W running / 6,500W surge. ~$700. Loud (76 dB), dirty power, no inverter. Handles well pumps and AC reliably. The budget choice when output wattage matters more than noise or power quality.

### Safe Operation

These are not optional considerations — generators kill people in NJ during every major storm event:

**Carbon monoxide**: a generator produces CO equal to hundreds of cars. Run only outdoors, minimum 20 feet from any door, window, or vent. CO is colorless and odorless. It kills quickly without warning. Install CO detectors on every floor of your home. Never run a generator in a garage — even with the door open, CO enters the living space.

**Fuel storage**: store gasoline in approved metal safety cans (Eagle, Justrite) with fuel stabilizer. Maximum residential storage: 25 gallons in NJ. Keep away from heat sources, flames, and direct sunlight. Rotate annually.

**Electrical connection**: plugging a generator directly into a wall outlet (backfeeding) is illegal and lethal — it energizes the grid lines that utility workers are trying to repair. Proper connection options:
- **Extension cords**: run from generator to individual appliances. Safe, always legal, no installation required. Use heavy-duty 10-gauge cords rated for outdoor use.
- **Transfer switch / interlock kit**: installed by a licensed electrician ($200–500 labor) on your main panel. Lets you power specific circuits from the generator safely. The right long-term solution.
- **Portable power station hybrid approach**: run the generator to charge an EcoFlow Delta Pro, which powers sensitive loads through its inverter. The generator handles the heavy charging; the PPS provides clean power to electronics.

### Standby Generators

A standby generator is permanently installed, connected to your home's main electrical panel, and starts automatically within seconds of a power outage. Runs on natural gas or propane piped directly.

**Brands**: Generac dominates the residential market (roughly 70% share). Kohler and Briggs & Stratton are strong alternatives. Cummins for commercial grade.

**Cost**: $2,500–6,000 for the unit + $3,000–8,000 installation (electrical panel work, gas line, permits, concrete pad). Total: $5,000–15,000 depending on size and complexity. NJ requires permits and inspections.

**When it makes sense**: you have medical equipment requiring power, a home office with revenue at stake, or you've experienced enough multi-day outages that the premium has obvious payback. For a standard household, a portable generator + fuel supply handles NJ outage frequency at a fraction of the cost.

## Getting Started

1. **List your critical loads** — refrigerator, freezer, CPAP, well pump, sump pump. Calculate running watts and identify the largest surge.
2. **Decide on AC** — window AC pushes you from a 2,000W to a 3,500W+ generator. Make this decision first.
3. **Choose dual-fuel if possible** — the propane advantage for emergency use is significant. Pay the modest premium.
4. **Size with headroom** — don't buy a generator you'll run at 95% capacity. Run it at 60–70% for fuel efficiency, longer runtime, and reduced wear.
5. **Test it before the storm** — run the generator under load once before you need it. A generator that won't start in November was also not starting in September when you could have fixed it.
6. **Store propane now** — a 20 lb tank lasts 6–8 hours at heavy load on a dual-fuel generator. Have two tanks minimum before storm season.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying based on price per watt without accounting for fuel type.** A $400 gas-only generator needs fresh stabilized gas rotated regularly. A $600 dual-fuel generator eliminates that problem. The difference is often worth it.

**Underestimating surge requirements.** A 2,000W generator that can't start a refrigerator's compressor is useless when you need it. Always size for surge, not running watts.

**Running the generator near the house or in the garage.** The number of CO deaths in NJ after major storms is not abstract. 20 feet, outdoors, away from all openings.

**No transfer switch or interlock.** Running extension cords to individual appliances is safe but inconvenient. A $300 electrician visit for an interlock kit is money well spent for long-term outage capability.

**Not running it before a storm.** The generator has been in the garage for 18 months. The fuel is stale. The oil is low. You discover this at 9pm during a nor'easter. Start and load-test the generator once at the beginning of hurricane season every year.
