# Experiences Over Things
*The research is clear: spending on experiences produces more lasting happiness than spending on objects — and the reasons why explain how to spend better.*

---

## What Is It

"Experiences over things" is the finding, consistently replicated in psychology research since the early 2000s, that experiential purchases — travel, concerts, meals, skill-building courses, adventures — produce more durable happiness than material purchases of equivalent cost.

This isn't a moral claim. It's an empirical one. The research explains *why* it's true, which tells you when it applies and when it doesn't — because some objects genuinely do provide lasting happiness, and some experiences are wastes of money.

This file is the philosophical grounding for the entire consumerism section. Understanding it changes how you decide where money goes.

---

## Why It Matters

You have finite money and finite time. Both are being spent constantly on objects, experiences, services, and entertainment. Most people have no framework for these decisions — they respond to marketing, status anxiety, and hedonic impulse. The result: houses full of things that provide no ongoing happiness, while experiences that would produce lasting memory and connection go unfunded.

The Stoic frame: Seneca's *vita brevis* — life is short not because days are few but because we waste them. Money spent on things that produce no lasting value is time converted to clutter. Money spent on experiences that deepen skill, relationship, or perspective is time well converted.

## Core Concepts

### Why Experiences Outlast Objects: The Research

**Hedonic adaptation**: When you buy something new, it produces a spike of happiness. Within weeks or months, the object becomes part of your baseline and you no longer notice it. A new couch becomes just the couch. A new car becomes just the car. The happiness boost fades as the object is absorbed into ordinary life.

Experiences are largely immune to hedonic adaptation because they exist in memory rather than in the environment. You don't habituate to the memory of hiking in Iceland or the meal in a restaurant you'll never return to. The memory remains distinct rather than becoming background.

**Van Boven & Gilovich (2003)** — the foundational study: participants consistently reported greater happiness from experiential purchases than material ones when looking back. Critically, *anticipated* happiness from experiences was also higher — the period of looking forward to an experience is itself pleasurable, giving experiences a "double return" (anticipation + memory).

**Social currency**: Experiences are easier to share and talk about than objects. "I hiked the Palisades last weekend" creates connection; "I bought a new espresso machine" doesn't. Shared experiences with others create shared reference points that deepen relationships.

**Identity integration**: Experiences become part of who you are in a way that possessions don't. You identify as someone who has traveled, who has trained in a specific way, who has done a specific thing. Objects don't modify identity in the same way unless they enable practice (a good guitar isn't just an object if you actually play it).

**The "story" value**: Experiences, especially difficult or unusual ones, produce better stories. A trip that went wrong generates more value — more learning, more to tell — than a trip that was frictionlessly luxurious.

### When Objects Do Provide Lasting Happiness

The research isn't "objects are worthless." It's a statistical claim. Objects that produce lasting happiness share a pattern: **they enable or extend experiences or practice.**

A good guitar that you play every day is not just an object — it's a practice. A quality backpack that gets you into the outdoors is not just an object — it's an enabler. The kettlebells in your home gym are not just objects — they're the equipment for a daily practice that produces years of physical returns.

The failure mode is objects as substitutes for experience: buying the expensive camera instead of learning to see photographs. Buying the beautiful running shoes and not running. Buying the cookbook collection and not cooking from them.

**The test for an object purchase**: Does this enable or extend a practice or experience? Or is it a consumption item — something I'll use passively and then habituate to? 

Objects that pass the test: [buy-it-for-life](buy-it-for-life.md) tools, training equipment, instruments, quality kitchen tools. Objects that fail it: decorative items, convenience gadgets, status goods that signal rather than enable.

### What Makes an Experience Worth Spending On

Not all experiences compound equally. The highest-return experiences share several features:

**Skill-building experiences**: You leave with a capability you didn't have. A cooking class in a specific cuisine, a foraging walk, a craft course, a training camp — the experience produces a skill that continues to generate value. Compare to a luxury hotel stay that produces a pleasant memory but no capability.

**Physically demanding experiences**: Discomfort and effort create stronger memories and clearer identity markers. A challenging hike is remembered more vividly and produces more satisfaction in retrospect than an easy one. Research on "peak-end rule" (Kahneman): the most intense moment and the final moment determine how an experience is remembered — which is why difficult endings (summit, finish line, the last mile) produce disproportionate memory value.

**Novel experiences**: The brain encodes novelty more deeply than repetition. Doing something for the first time, visiting somewhere new, attempting something outside your current capability — all produce richer memory encoding than repeating familiar experiences.

**Shared experiences with people you care about**: These compound in two directions — the experience itself and the relationship. A trip with your partner, a meal with close friends, a shared challenge — these create reference points in the relationship that strengthen it. "Remember when we..." is the currency of long relationships.

**Experiences connected to your values**: Romi's core values are self-reliance, learning, physical health, Stoicism. Experiences that connect to these — travel that builds perspective, physical challenges, learning trips, time in nature — compound against the whole system. An experience that feels vaguely fun but doesn't connect to anything you're building doesn't compound.

### How to Budget for Experiences

**Make it a line item**: Experience spending that isn't budgeted gets crowded out by object spending, which is constant and marketed. Create an explicit "experience" budget category — treated the same as rent and groceries. Even $200-300/month creates significant experience spending over a year.

**Front-load payment where possible**: Pay for experiences in advance. Research shows that paying upfront and then "consuming for free" increases enjoyment of the experience — the bill isn't hanging over you while you're in it. Book travel early (better prices + the anticipation dividend of looking forward to it for months).

**Prioritize high-anticipation experiences**: If you're choosing between two experiences, weight the one you'll look forward to longer. A trip booked 3 months out produces 3 months of anticipatory happiness. A dinner reservation booked yesterday produces none.

**Invest in experiences that are hard to access**: Scarcity creates value in memory. A meal at a restaurant you had to wait months for is remembered differently than a convenient dinner. An experience that required effort to make happen is valued more than one that didn't.

**Don't waste money on passive experiences**: Going to a movie or concert you're mildly interested in, eating at a mediocre restaurant, going on a trip with no plan — these produce weak memories and weak happiness returns. Be selective. Fewer, better experiences beat constant moderate ones.

### The "Good Life" Spending Audit

Once a year or so: look at what you spent money on in the previous 12 months. For each significant purchase, ask:

1. **Do I still think about this?** (experiences continue to provide return as memories; objects don't)
2. **Did this change how I see myself or what I can do?** (identity formation)
3. **Did it deepen a relationship?** (social compound)
4. **If I had to do it over, would I spend the money the same way?** (honest retrospective utility)

This audit slowly shifts spending toward what actually worked — more accurate than predicting in advance.

## Key Considerations

**The experience premium for some objects is real**: An $800 tent that enables 3-day backcountry trips every season for 10 years is producing experience value. Don't dismiss the object because "experiences over things" sounds like a universal rule — it's a statistical tendency, not an absolute.

**Luxurious experiences don't automatically compound**: A $2,000 luxury hotel produces comfort and convenience but not necessarily more lasting happiness than a $150 guesthouse where something memorable happened. The variables that make experiences valuable (novelty, challenge, connection, skill) are not purchased by price.

**Don't use this as a justification for impulse experiences**: "It's an experience, not a thing!" can rationalize poor spending just as easily as object hoarding. The question is whether the experience is worth spending on — does it meet the criteria above? A spontaneous $400 "experience" that you'll barely remember in a month is worse than a $400 object you'll use daily.

**Time is the real constraint**: Most people aren't purely money-constrained — they're time-constrained. An experience that requires a week of vacation and costs $1,000 isn't competing with a $1,000 object. It's competing with other things you could do with that week. Evaluate experiences against both time and money.

**The Stoic angle**: Negative visualization (premeditatio malorum) has a positive application here. Before spending on an experience, ask: if I'm 70 and looking back at this moment, will I wish I'd done this? Marcus Aurelius's writing is full of the awareness of time's passage. Experiences, more than objects, are the answer to the question "did I actually live?"

## Getting Started

**Near term (this month)**:
- Write down three experiences you've wanted to have but keep deferring. Pick one and book it — even if just the first commitment (a deposit, a date set aside).
- Look at your last 3 months of discretionary spending. What percentage went to experiences vs. objects? Is that ratio what you'd choose deliberately?

**Annually**:
- Set an experience budget at the start of the year. Earmark it. Protect it from getting absorbed into other spending.
- Plan at least one experience that requires physical challenge, one that teaches something new, and one shared with your partner.

**The practice**:
- Before major object purchases, ask: could this same money produce a more memorable experience? Not as a block on all object spending — but as a genuine check.

## Common Mistakes

**Treating experiences as rewards**: "I'll do that trip when I'm more settled / when work slows down / when I have more money." Experiences deferred indefinitely are experiences not had. The conditions are never perfect. Book it.

**Passive entertainment as experience**: Watching 4 hours of TV, scrolling content, going to a mediocre show — these are entertainment, not experiences in the value-producing sense. They're fine in moderation but don't produce lasting returns. Don't confuse leisure with experience.

**Expensive experience = better experience**: The correlation between cost and memory/happiness for experiences is weak. Some of the most valuable experiences are cheap or free: a difficult hike, a conversation that changed how you think, learning a skill from someone who knows it.

**Neglecting the objects that enable your best experiences**: This framework can overcorrect. If buying a $300 pack means you actually go hiking regularly instead of occasionally, the object is producing enormous experience value. Don't let "experiences over things" stop you from buying the equipment for your best life.
