# Everyday Carry
*The objects you interact with 50–100 times a day — wallet, pen, knife, notebook — earn their quality more than anything else you own.*

---

## What Is It

Everyday carry (EDC) is the set of objects you carry on your person daily. They're not glamorous purchases. But because of raw usage frequency, the quality gap between a bad version and a good version compounds across years of daily friction.

A wallet you open 20 times a day is touched 7,300 times a year. A pen you reach for constantly either writes smoothly or skips. A notebook either makes you want to write in it or it doesn't. These aren't luxury items — they're tools with the highest possible usage rate. They deserve the same attention as any other tool.

The frame: buy the best version of each item once, carry it until it fails or you die, and stop thinking about it.

---

## Why It Matters

EDC sits at the intersection of two principles: BIFL (buy it for life — see [buy-it-for-life](buy-it-for-life.md)) and the idea that your physical environment shapes your mental state in subtle, constant ways. Poor tools are a low-grade daily drag. Good tools disappear into your hands and let you focus on the thing you're actually doing.

The total cost of a well-considered EDC kit is low. You're not buying luxury goods — most of these are $30–150 per item. The return on that investment is disproportionate because of usage frequency.

## Core Concepts

### Wallet

The wallet is opened and closed more than almost anything else you carry. Criteria that matter: slim enough not to distort your pocket or back, durable enough to last a decade, holds what you actually need (not what you might theoretically need someday).

**Slim bifold / cardholder** — Most people carry 3-5 cards and cash occasionally. A cardholder serves this cleanly.
- **Bellroy Note Sleeve** (~$100): Slim, quality leather, note compartment, holds 4-8 cards — probably the best all-around option. Bellroy's leather holds up well.
- **Bellroy Card Sleeve** (~$80): Even slimmer, 2-8 cards, minimal. Best if you're fully digital for cash.
- **Horween Leather Bifold** (Saddleback, Vintage, ~$50-80): Full-grain leather that patinas and lasts 20+ years with basic care. Classic.

**Ridge Wallet** (~$95): Aluminum card case with money clip. Extremely slim, holds 1-12 cards, indestructible. The money clip is functional. No leather. Best for someone who goes fully digital for cash.

Skip: anything with faux leather, excessive card slots, or built-in RFID blocking theatre (genuine RFID theft is rare; it's mostly marketing).

### Pen

A pen you reach for constantly should write the first time, every time, without shaking or testing it on the corner of the page.

**Lamy Safari** (~$30): The standard recommendation for good reason. Steel nib, reliable ink flow, comfortable grip, interchangeable cartridges or converter for bottled ink. Buy the all-black or safari green. Refillable — essentially permanent.

**Pilot Metropolitan** (~$20): Similar quality, slightly more office-appropriate aesthetic. Excellent steel nib. Reliable.

**Tactile Turn Bolt Action Pen** (~$80-100): Machined aluminum or titanium, uses common Parker refills (Schneider Gelion for best writing). For someone who wants metal and tactile satisfaction. BIFL without question.

**Retractable choice**: **Pilot G2** ($2) is the best disposable. For BIFL: the **Tactile Turn** above or the **Karas Kustoms Render K** (~$65) — machined metal, uses G2 refills.

Carry one pen. Know where it is. Don't own 20 pens of varying quality.

### Notebook

A notebook you don't want to write in sits unused. One you do want to write in gets used, which is the entire point.

**Field Notes** (~$12 for 3-pack): The standard pocket notebook. 3.5x5.5", 48 pages, fits in a back pocket. Rugged enough for real use. Runs out quickly, which is a feature — finishing a notebook is satisfying. Buy the kraft or original. Skip the limited editions.

**Leuchtturm1917 A5** (~$25): For more sustained writing, a full A5 hardcover. Numbered pages, index in front, quality paper. Better than Moleskine at the same price point. Dotted grid is the most versatile ruling.

**Baron Fig Confidant** (~$18, pocket-sized): Beautiful paper, sturdy cover, lay-flat binding. Slightly more premium feel than Field Notes.

Pick one pocket-sized and one desk-sized. Use both completely before buying replacements.

### Pocket Knife

A pocket knife isn't just for survival scenarios. It's the most useful daily tool: opening boxes, cutting rope, food prep, removing tags, scraping stickers. You reach for it more than you expect to.

**Victorinox Alox Pioneer** (~$50): The ultimate BIFL pocket knife. Swiss Army knives made with aluminum scales (not plastic). Knife, bottle opener, can opener, screwdrivers. Slim enough to forget in your pocket. Has been made the same way for decades. Will outlast you.

**Victorinox Classic SD** (~$15): Smallest viable Swiss Army option. Fits on a keychain. Blade, scissors, file, screwdriver, toothpick. For people who want minimal weight and the blade is secondary.

**Spyderco Tenacious** (~$70): If you want a proper lockback folding knife rather than a Swiss Army. G10 handle, reliable mechanism, good steel. Holds an edge. For harder use.

**Benchmade Bugout** (~$150): Premium lightweight folding knife. Axis lock (excellent), superb steel (S30V), feather-light. The knife to buy if you want one knife for life and are willing to spend for it.

NJ note: carrying a knife with a blade over 5 inches is prohibited; most pocket knives are well under this. A single-bladed folding knife is legal for general carry.

### Keychain

Keys become a jangling mess without management. Goals: fewer keys than you think you need (audit what actually opens what), and a small multitool on the ring.

**Orbitkey** (~$45): Stacks keys flat between leather layers. Eliminates key jangle, sits flat in pocket. Looks clean.

**KeySmart** (~$20): Similar concept, aluminum construction, more modular (can add USB drives, bottle openers). More rugged than Orbitkey.

**Gerber Shard** (~$8): Titanium keychain tool — pry bar, flat/Phillips drivers, bottle opener, wire stripper. Adds almost no weight, solves problems constantly.

**Leatherman Style PS** (~$25): Keychain-sized multi-tool (TSA compliant — no blade). Pliers, screwdrivers, scissors, file. More capable than the Shard but bulkier.

### Bag

The bag is the container for everything above — and for a laptop, gym clothes, or whatever the day requires.

**Aer Day Pack 2** (~$185): The best everyday carry backpack for a tech professional. 14.8L, organized laptop compartment, water bottle pockets, quality zippers, clean look. Doesn't look like a hiking bag or a school bag. Handles daily abuse. Many people carry this for 5+ years.

**Bellroy Transit Backpack** (~$250): Similar positioning to Aer but more polished. Luggage handle pass-through. Good for people who travel with it.

**Topo Designs Daypack** (~$139): More casual/outdoorsy aesthetic. Rugged canvas/nylon. Better if you want one bag that moves from city to trail.

**Sling bag option** — for lighter days: **Aer City Sling** (~$80), one-shoulder, 7L. Phone, wallet, keys, water bottle, a paperback. Minimal friction day use.

## Key Considerations

**Audit what you actually carry before buying**: Most people carry 2-3 cards, not 12. Most people use their knife rarely. Most people write in a notebook inconsistently at best. Match what you buy to actual behavior, not aspirational behavior.

**One of each**: The whole point of EDC is that you carry the same thing every day. Two wallets, three knives, five notebooks means decision fatigue and nothing gets used enough to know if it's right. Pick one of each, carry it for 6 months, then evaluate.

**Aesthetic matters**: You should like looking at the things you carry. This isn't vanity — an object you're proud to take out gets used more, cared for better, and replaced less. A pen you enjoy writing with gets used; a pen you're indifferent to sits in a drawer.

**Repairability**: Knives can be sharpened. Fountain pens can be cleaned and refilled indefinitely. Leather goods can be conditioned and resoled. Check repair paths before buying anything expensive.

## Getting Started

Audit your current carry. For each item ask: does it function well every time? Do I like using it? Would I replace it with the same thing?

Replace failures first. Start with the wallet if yours is bulky or falling apart. Then the pen if you're always hunting for one that works. Then the notebook if you're not writing in it.

Don't buy everything at once. Each piece should earn its place through use, not purchase.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying too much**: EDC culture trends toward gear accumulation. The goal is minimum viable carry, not maximum capable carry. More items = more weight = more friction = less likely to carry it.

**Prioritizing aesthetics over function**: A beautiful notebook with paper that bleeds through on both sides isn't a good notebook. Test before committing to expensive versions.

**Ignoring knife maintenance**: A dull knife is more dangerous than a sharp one (requires more force, more likely to slip). Learn to maintain an edge. A Spyderco Sharpmaker or Lansky system handles most pocket knives.

**Over-investing in cheap-feeling objects**: The Ridge Wallet is excellent. A $6 Amazon card sleeve is not, because you'll replace it in 18 months. The cost difference is small; the quality difference is not.
