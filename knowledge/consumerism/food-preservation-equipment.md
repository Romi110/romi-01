# Food Preservation Equipment
*Pressure canners, water bath canners, dehydrators, and vacuum sealers — what to buy for each method*

## Core Concepts

### Pressure Canner

The pressure canner is the most consequential piece of preservation equipment — it's the only method that safely processes low-acid foods (vegetables, beans, soups, stocks) for long-term shelf storage. Water bath canning cannot achieve the 240°F required to destroy botulism spores in low-acid foods. This is not a preference; it's food safety science.

**The equipment distinction**: a pressure canner is not a pressure cooker. They're different tools. A modern Instant Pot (even the "canning" version) is not USDA-tested or recommended for pressure canning low-acid foods. Use an actual pressure canner.

**All American Pressure Canner**: the BIFL choice. Made in Wisconsin since 1930. All-metal construction — the lid seals metal-to-metal, no rubber gasket. This is the key advantage: no gasket means no replacement parts. The All American runs indefinitely with minimal maintenance. Available in 15.5 qt ($230–260), 21.5 qt ($300–320), 25 qt ($340–360), and 41.5 qt ($450–500) sizes.

*Which size*: The 21.5 qt holds 7 quart jars or 19 pint jars per batch — the right size for a serious home garden. The 15.5 qt (7 quarts or 10 pints) works for smaller batches. The 41.5 qt is for serious production volumes. If you're canning a full summer's worth of tomatoes, beans, and stock, get the 21.5 qt.

*Weight note*: when full, a 21.5 qt All American weighs 20+ lbs. Requires a sturdy stovetop. Do not use on glass-top/ceramic ranges (too heavy; may crack the surface). Gas and standard electric coil ranges only.

**Presto Pressure Canner**: the accessible entry point. Aluminum construction with a rubber gasket lid. 16 qt ($85–100) and 23 qt ($100–120). The gasket requires periodic replacement (every few years, ~$8–12) — a minor inconvenience but a real one. Presto is made in the US, has been in continuous production for decades, and is a reliable choice for occasional use. If you're canning once or twice a year, Presto is fine. If you're canning weekly through a long harvest, the All American's maintenance-free construction justifies the premium.

**Both**: any pressure canner purchased should be used with USDA-tested recipes and processing times only. Never improvise canning times for low-acid foods. The Ball Complete Book of Home Preserving and the USDA Guide are the authoritative references.

### Water Bath Canner

For high-acid foods only: tomatoes (with added lemon juice or citric acid), fruit jams and jellies, pickles, and salsa. Processing at 212°F (boiling water) is sufficient to destroy pathogens in high-acid environments.

**Equipment needed**: a large pot with a rack that keeps jars off the bottom, and a lid. That's it.

**Dedicated water bath canner**: Granite Ware 33 qt (blue speckled enamel, $30–35) with an included jar rack. This is the standard. Holds 7 quart jars or 9 pint jars per batch. Works perfectly. Buy this.

**Alternate approach**: any large stockpot (12+ quarts for pints, larger for quarts) with a canning rack or even a folded towel on the bottom works. The dedicated Granite Ware canner is a minor convenience, not a requirement.

**Essential accessories** (one canning kit covers all of these, $15–20):
- **Jar lifter**: heavy-duty tongs designed to grip jar necks. Required for safely lifting hot jars from boiling water.
- **Canning funnel (widemouth)**: routes food into jars without contaminating the rim.
- **Headspace tool / bubble remover**: a flat plastic stick with headspace measurements marked. Runs around the inside of the jar to release air bubbles, then measures headspace accurately.
- **Lid wand (magnetic)**: lifts jar lids from the hot water where you're softening them. A fork works too.

**Jar lids**: always use new lids for each canning batch — the sealing compound on the flat lid is one-time use. Bands (the screw rings) are reusable indefinitely if not bent or corroded. Ball and Kerr lids are interchangeable (same parent company). Generic lids from Amazon have inconsistent failure rates; stick with Ball/Kerr.

**Mason jars**: Ball and Kerr widemouth jars are the standard. Widemouth is more versatile: works for canning, fermenting, freezing (leave 1-inch headspace), and dry storage. Regular mouth works for high-acid canning where the narrow opening doesn't matter. Don't buy the cheapest off-brand jars — the rim geometry affects sealing. Genuine Ball/Kerr jars can be reused indefinitely if not chipped or cracked.

### Dehydrator

Dehydrating removes moisture to below 10%, preventing microbial growth. Results are shelf-stable at room temperature for 1–2 years. Concentrated flavor, lightweight, minimal storage space. Best uses: herbs, fruits, vegetables, jerky, and for making backpacking or long-term storage foods.

The difference between a good and poor dehydrator: horizontal vs. vertical airflow, and temperature control.

**Excalibur (rectangular, horizontal airflow)**: the benchmark dehydrator for serious home use. The fan is at the back, drawing air horizontally across each tray uniformly. All trays dry at the same rate — you don't need to rotate them. Temperature control ranges 95°F–165°F. 

*5-tray ($150–175)*: 8 sq ft of drying space. Right for herbs, occasional fruit, moderate batches.
*9-tray ($250–280)*: 15 sq ft of drying space. The right size when dehydrating large harvests — a full herb garden, several pounds of tomatoes, or large batches of fruit leather. Used by professional herb driers and commercial small-scale food producers. If you're growing seriously, this is the model.

The Excalibur is the only dehydrator worth buying for production use. Everything else is a compromise.

**Cosori Premium (rectangular, rear fan)**: the mid-range option. $80–130, decent temperature control (95°F–165°F), stainless steel trays. Not quite as even as Excalibur but significantly better than round-tray stackable units. A reasonable choice if the Excalibur price is prohibitive.

**Nesco/American Harvest (round stackable trays, top or bottom fan)**: $40–80. The entry point. The round design with top or bottom heat produces uneven drying — the center tray dries faster than the outer tray edges. You need to rotate trays during drying sessions. Fine for small batches of herbs or occasional fruit; inadequate for production-scale use. Don't buy this if you're drying more than a handful of herbs annually.

**Temperature guidance**:
- Herbs: 95–115°F (preserve volatile oils; higher heat cooks away the compounds that matter)
- Fruit: 135°F
- Vegetables: 125–135°F
- Jerky: 160°F (poultry) or 145°F+ (beef, minimum for pathogen safety)

### Vacuum Sealer

Vacuum sealers remove air from bags before sealing, extending shelf life of dry goods significantly and enabling freezer storage without freezer burn.

Two fundamentally different technologies:

**Edge/clamp sealer (FoodSaver and similar)**: the open end of a bag is inserted into the machine, which vacuums air out through the opening and then heat-seals the bag closed. The bag must be the brand's proprietary channel bag (textured on one side to allow air flow during suction). Liquid or moist foods must be frozen first — the machine can't seal liquid-filled bags without pulling liquid into the pump.

*Best for*: dry goods (grains, nuts, dehydrated foods), portioned frozen meat and fish, cheese, bulk purchases. For most home users, this covers 90% of needs.

*FoodSaver V4840*: the current standard model. Moist/dry settings, built-in roll storage and cutter, handheld sealer port for jar attachments (very useful — vacuum seals regular and widemouth mason jars for dry goods storage). ~$100–130.

*Jar attachment*: for the FoodSaver, the accessory jar sealers ($10 for widemouth + regular mouth set) are extremely practical. They vacuum-seal mason jars for storing spices, herbs, nuts, and grains without buying specialty bags. A jar of vacuum-sealed coffee lasts months without degradation.

**Chamber vacuum sealer**: the bag — and all its contents, including liquids — is placed inside a sealed chamber. The machine evacuates the entire chamber, then seals the bag while still under vacuum. When the chamber re-pressurizes, atmospheric pressure compresses the bag around the food. Result: near-100% air removal, works with liquids and liquid-adjacent foods (marinated meats, pickles, soups, sauces, wet brines).

*Best for*: home canners who want to vacuum-seal pickles or marinated vegetables; liquid-containing foods; serious meat processing.

*Avid Armor USV32*: the entry-level chamber sealer, ~$300–350. The jump from $130 (FoodSaver) to $300 is significant but chamber sealers use any smooth bag (much cheaper per bag than brand-specific channel bags — chamber bags cost $0.05–0.10 each vs. $0.30–0.50 for FoodSaver bags). Over time, the bag cost savings recoup the machine cost difference.

*VacMaster VP215*: the mid-range commercial step up, ~$500. More durable, faster cycle, handles thicker bags. Right for anyone sealing meat in volume (butchering whole animals, large harvest processing).

**Recommendation for a home garden / food preservation context**: start with a FoodSaver V4840 plus jar attachment. It handles dehydrated foods, dry goods, and frozen items. Upgrade to a chamber sealer if you're regularly sealing wet-marinated foods, pickles, or liquid-rich preparations.

### Fermentation Equipment

Fermentation (lacto-fermentation specifically) requires almost no specialized equipment, but a few items meaningfully reduce friction.

**Mason jars**: widemouth pint and quart jars are the standard fermentation vessel. No special equipment beyond the jar and a lid arrangement that allows CO2 to escape without letting oxygen in.

**Airlock lids**: replace the standard lid with a two-part airlock (common in homebrewing) that allows CO2 out while blocking oxygen in. Masontops makes mason jar fermentation lids ($15–20 for 4). Standard lids "burped" daily work equally well — the airlocks are a convenience, not a requirement.

**Fermentation weights**: small glass weights that keep vegetables submerged below the brine surface. Important because surface exposure to oxygen causes kahm yeast (harmless white film) and can cause mold. Masontops glass weights ($10–15). A small ziplock bag filled with brine works as a free alternative.

**Fermentation crock (ceramic)**: traditional European-style fermentation vessel for larger quantities (gallon+) of sauerkraut or kimchi. Weck, Humble House, and Ohio Stoneware make quality crocks. $50–150. The moat-seal design (water fills a channel around the lid, blocking oxygen) is elegant and passive. Worth buying once you're fermenting regularly at volume; unnecessary for starting out.

## Getting Started

**Year one, minimal investment**:
- Granite Ware water bath canner + canning kit ($50)
- Ball mason jars, widemouth quarts and pints, 24-count ($30–40)
- Ball/Kerr lids, 3 boxes ($15)

This enables fermenting, water bath canning of tomatoes and pickles, and basic cold storage — sufficient for a first harvest year.

**Year two, add dehydrating and pressure canning**:
- Excalibur 5-tray ($150–175)
- Presto 23 qt pressure canner ($100) — or go straight to All American 21.5 qt ($310) if planning to can seriously long-term

**Year three, add vacuum sealing**:
- FoodSaver V4840 + jar attachment ($110–140)

**When to add a chamber sealer**: when you're regularly processing whole animals, large batches of marinated foods, or find yourself going through enough FoodSaver bags that the per-bag cost adds up.

## Common Mistakes

**Using a pressure cooker for pressure canning.** A modern pressure cooker (Instant Pot, stovetop) has not been USDA-tested for safe canning times. Internal headspace, heat distribution, and depressurization rates differ from pressure canners. Don't use them for canning low-acid foods.

**Reusing flat canning lids.** The sealing compound is one-time use. Reused lids fail to seal and may not be obvious until a jar fails months later. Always new lids. Bands are reusable.

**Buying a round-tray dehydrator for serious use.** Uneven drying means constant tray rotation and inconsistent results. If you're drying more than a few batches per year, the Excalibur's horizontal airflow pays back immediately in time saved.

**Drying herbs at high temperature.** Herbs dried above 115°F lose volatile oils — the compounds that make them worth growing and drying. The lowest possible dehydrator temperature for herbs, for longer times, preserves quality.

**Skipping the jar attachment for FoodSaver.** It's $10 and enables vacuum-sealing mason jars, which is one of the most practical home storage applications — especially for dehydrated herbs and spices.
