# Fine Jewelry Fundamentals
*Hallmarks, metals, gemstones, care, insurance, and what actually holds value*

---

## What Is It

The underlying knowledge that makes all jewelry decisions legible: what the numbers stamped inside a ring mean, why some stones survive daily wear and others don't, how to care for pieces by material type, and what you can realistically expect to recover if you sell.

This is the reference layer. The ring-buying decisions are in [engagement-ring-guide.md](engagement-ring-guide.md); this file is the vocabulary and fundamentals that make those decisions make sense.

---

## Why It Matters

Without this knowledge, you're dependent on whatever a jeweler tells you. With it, you can verify the quality of what you're looking at, ask informed questions, and avoid being oversold on features that don't matter or sold underweight on ones that do.

## Core Concepts

### Karat vs Carat

These sound identical but measure different things and apply to different materials:

**Karat (k or kt)** — Purity of gold. 24 karat = 100% pure gold. Gold is too soft at pure for most jewelry, so it's alloyed:
- 10k = 41.7% gold (417 stamp) — durable, least expensive, common in US mass-market jewelry
- 14k = 58.5% gold (585 stamp) — the US standard, good balance of purity and durability
- 18k = 75% gold (750 stamp) — the international standard for fine jewelry, more pure, slightly softer
- 22k = 91.7% gold (916 stamp) — common in Indian and Middle Eastern jewelry; very soft, not ideal for prong-set stones
- 24k = 99.9% pure (999 stamp) — too soft for most jewelry; used for bars, coins, some bangles

The alloy metals determine color: silver and palladium lighten gold toward white; copper warms it toward rose or yellow. The gold content is fixed by karat; the alloys vary.

**Carat (ct or ctw)** — Weight unit for gemstones. 1 carat = 0.2 grams. 100 points = 1 carat (so a 0.75ct stone is "75 points"). "Total carat weight" (ctw or TW) is the combined weight of all stones in a piece — a useful number but easy to inflate with many small low-quality stones.

### Metal Hallmarks

Stamps on jewelry identify purity and sometimes origin:

| Metal | Common stamps | Meaning |
|-------|--------------|---------|
| 10k gold | 10k, 417, 10kt | 41.7% gold |
| 14k gold | 14k, 585, 14kt | 58.5% gold |
| 18k gold | 18k, 750, 18kt | 75% gold |
| Sterling silver | 925, STG, Sterling | 92.5% silver |
| Platinum | PT950, Plat, 950Pt | 95% platinum |
| Palladium | 950Pd, PALL | 95% palladium |

A piece with no stamp may be gold-filled (a thin gold layer over base metal), gold-plated, or simply unsigned — older estate pieces sometimes lack stamps. Gold-filled is marked GF; gold-plated is marked GP or HGE (heavy gold electroplate). Neither is fine jewelry.

### Gemstone Families

Hardness matters for rings because they take daily abrasion. Mohs scale measures scratch resistance (1=talc, 10=diamond):

**Precious stones (historical designation, now marketing)**
- Diamond — 10 Mohs. Scratch-resistant; can chip from hard impact despite hardness. The only common gemstone appropriate for an everyday ring without concern.
- Ruby and sapphire — 9 Mohs. Same mineral (corundum), different trace elements. Extremely durable. Sapphires come in every color; "sapphire" without a color modifier means blue.
- Emerald — 7.5–8 Mohs. Durable enough for careful daily wear but more fracture-prone than ruby or sapphire due to inclusions. Protect from hard knocks.

**Durable semi-precious stones (ring-appropriate)**
- Alexandrite — 8.5 Mohs. Color-change gem (green in daylight, red/purple in incandescent light). Extremely rare and expensive natural; lab versions are available.
- Spinel — 8 Mohs. Often historically confused with ruby. Comes in red, pink, blue, violet. Excellent hardness, undervalued.
- Aquamarine and other beryls — 7.5–8 Mohs. Blue-green, durable enough for rings if protected.
- Garnet — 6.5–7.5 Mohs depending on variety. Acceptable for rings with some care.

**Softer stones (pendants, earrings, occasional wear rings)**
- Opal — 5.5–6 Mohs, fragile, water-sensitive. Stunning but not for everyday rings.
- Pearl — 2.5–4.5 Mohs, organic, soft. Earrings and necklaces only.
- Turquoise — 5–6 Mohs, porous, should not be worn in water.
- Tanzanite — 6–6.5 Mohs. Only found near Mount Kilimanjaro; beautiful blue-violet but not durable enough for rings.

**Lab-created versions** of rubies, sapphires, emeralds, and alexandrite are physically identical to natural. Lab sapphires are an undervalued alternative to lab diamonds for colored stone rings — excellent hardness, vibrant color, significantly less expensive than natural.

### Precious Metals Compared

| Metal | Hardness | Hypoallergenic | Maintenance | Cost |
|-------|---------|----------------|-------------|------|
| Platinum | Soft (develops patina) | Yes | Occasional polish | Highest |
| 18k white gold | Medium | Not always (nickel in alloy) | Rhodium re-plate 1–3yr | High |
| 14k white gold | Harder than 18k | Not always | Rhodium re-plate | Moderate |
| 18k yellow/rose gold | Medium | Generally yes | Minimal | High |
| Sterling silver | Soft | Generally yes | Polishing, anti-tarnish | Low |
| Palladium | Medium | Yes | Minimal | Moderate |

Nickel allergy is common (estimated 10–15% of people). White gold often uses nickel as a whitening alloy. If nickel allergy is a concern, choose platinum, palladium, or confirm the jeweler uses a palladium-based white gold alloy.

## Jewelry Care by Material

### Gold and Platinum

Basic maintenance: warm water, mild dish soap, soft toothbrush. Ultrasonic cleaners are safe for gold and platinum with diamonds; avoid for softer stones or pieces with significant inclusions.

Avoid: chlorine (pools, hot tubs) — weakens gold alloys and prongs over time. Remove before swimming. Bleach and harsh chemicals similarly degrade prongs.

White gold needs periodic rhodium re-plating when the white finish wears to the underlying yellow. A jeweler does this in 15–30 minutes for $50–$100.

Platinum scratches and develops a patina (matte finish) with daily wear. This is considered desirable by many — a jeweler can polish it back to mirror finish. The metal doesn't lose mass when it scratches (it displaces); gold does lose small amounts.

### Diamonds

Diamonds attract oils and grease — fingerprints, hand lotion, cooking oils — which reduce brilliance. Clean weekly if worn daily. Safe for ultrasonic cleaners. The stone itself is unaffected by chemicals; the setting (prongs, metal) is what to protect.

### Colored Gemstones

Ruby and sapphire: similar care to diamonds, ultrasonic safe if no fracture-filling treatment (ask the jeweler if the stone is clarity-enhanced).

Emerald: most natural emeralds are fracture-filled with oil to improve clarity — ultrasonic cleaner can remove the filler. Hand-clean with mild soap only.

Opal, pearl, turquoise, coral: clean with a damp cloth only. No soap, no ultrasonic, no chemicals. Keep opals away from extreme temperature changes.

### Silver

Tarnishes from sulfur compounds in the air. Clean with silver polish or a polishing cloth. Anti-tarnish storage strips in jewelry boxes extend the time between cleanings. Not ideal for everyday rings — too soft and too much maintenance.

## Insurance

A homeowner's or renter's policy typically covers jewelry up to $1,500–$2,500 for theft or loss, with a deductible. Fine jewelry exceeding that needs either a scheduled rider or a standalone policy.

**Option 1: Jewelry rider on homeowner's/renter's** — Schedule the piece at its appraised replacement value. Covers theft, loss, accidental damage. Usually adds $20–$50/year per $1,000 of value. Convenient; goes with your existing policy.

**Option 2: Jewelers Mutual** — The specialist insurer for jewelry. Covers loss, theft, accidental damage worldwide. No deductible on some policies. Roughly 1–2% of replacement value annually. More comprehensive than most riders.

**Appraisal requirement:** Both options require a professional appraisal. Get a "replacement value appraisal" (what it costs to replace with a comparable piece at retail), not a "fair market value appraisal" (what you'd get selling it). The replacement value is higher and is what the insurance pays out. Update the appraisal every 3–5 years.

## What Holds Value

**Natural diamonds** — Modest resale; wholesale buys at 40–60% below retail and resells below retail. A $5,000 natural diamond engagement ring is unlikely to fetch more than $1,500–$2,500 if sold. Not an investment, but not zero.

**Lab diamonds** — Near-zero resale value. Production is essentially unlimited; the secondary market has collapsed. See [lab-diamonds.md](lab-diamonds.md).

**Colored gemstones** — Variable. Fine rubies and sapphires (no heat treatment, Burma or Kashmir origin) have held value well. General "semi-precious" stones at retail prices are poor resale candidates.

**Gold** — The metal weight holds value at spot price. A 14k ring weighing 5 grams has ~$130 in gold at $2,000/oz spot. That's the floor; the setting labor adds no resale premium in most cases.

**Signed pieces** — Tiffany, Cartier, Van Cleef & Arpels, Bvlgari retain a brand premium at auction and through specialist dealers. A Tiffany solitaire can resell for 40–60% of retail. Estate versions of signed pieces sometimes sell above retail for discontinued designs.

**Estate and vintage jewelry** — Buying from estate sales or auctions often yields 30–50% below equivalent-quality retail. The trade-off: pieces need evaluation (has it been repaired, are stones original, is the metal tested), and rings need resizing. A skilled jeweler can authenticate and resize for $50–$200.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing gold-filled with solid gold.** Gold-filled has 5% or more gold by weight bonded to a base metal core — it wears through over years. Solid 14k or 18k is throughout. The hallmark tells you: GF = filled, no suffix on a karat stamp = solid.

**Ignoring stone durability for an everyday ring.** An opal or tanzanite ring worn daily will show damage within months. Hardness matters for rings in a way it doesn't for earrings or pendants.

**Skipping insurance after a purchase.** A $4,000 ring costs ~$60–$80/year to insure properly. Not insuring it is a large, unnecessary risk.

**Getting an appraisal from the selling jeweler.** The selling jeweler has an incentive to appraise high (flatters the purchase, inflates perceived value). Get an independent appraisal from a GIA-certified gemologist with no financial relationship to the piece.
