# Estate Planning Basics
*The legal documents that protect your assets and your people if you're incapacitated or dead — most people at 28 have none of them.*

---

## What Is It

Estate planning is the process of deciding what happens to your money, property, and medical care if you can't make decisions yourself — whether from death or incapacitation. It's not just for the wealthy and old. It matters the moment you have a partner, meaningful assets (401k, home, savings), or preferences about your own medical care.

The documents involved:
- **Will** — who gets your property when you die
- **Beneficiary designations** — who gets specific financial accounts (often overrides the will)
- **Durable Power of Attorney** — who manages your finances if you're incapacitated
- **Healthcare Proxy / Healthcare POA** — who makes medical decisions if you can't
- **Living Will / Advance Directive** — what medical interventions you do or don't want

None of these are complicated to set up. Most people just never do it.

---

## Why It Matters

Without these documents, NJ law decides everything for you. "Intestate succession" — dying without a will — follows a fixed formula that may not match your actual wishes. A partner you're not married to gets nothing by default. Your 401k goes to whoever is named on the beneficiary form, which may be an ex or a deceased parent if you never updated it. Your family makes medical decisions based on what they think you'd want, which is often wrong.

You also have no say in what happens during incapacitation — a stroke, a coma, a serious accident. Without a POA and healthcare proxy, your family needs a court order to access your accounts or make decisions, which takes months and costs money.

This is not morbid planning. It's infrastructure. You build it once, update it at life milestones, and it sits in the background doing nothing — until it matters enormously.

## Core Concepts

### The Will

A will specifies who inherits your property. Key things to include:
- **Beneficiaries** — who gets what (partner, family, specific amounts)
- **Executor** — who administers the estate (manages the process, pays debts, distributes assets)
- **Residuary clause** — catches anything not specifically named ("the rest goes to...")
- **Guardianship** — if you have children, who raises them

What a will does NOT control: assets with named beneficiaries (retirement accounts, life insurance, joint bank accounts with right of survivorship). Those pass outside the will entirely.

In NJ, a will must be signed in front of two witnesses who are not beneficiaries. It does not need to be notarized to be valid, but a "self-proving affidavit" (witnessed and notarized statement) speeds up probate. A handwritten "holographic" will is not valid in NJ.

**Probate**: When you die, your estate goes through probate — a court process that validates the will and oversees asset distribution. NJ probate is relatively simple and inexpensive compared to states like California. Still takes time (months). Assets with beneficiary designations or held jointly bypass it entirely.

### Beneficiary Designations — The Most Important and Most Neglected

Your 401k, IRA, Roth IRA, and life insurance all have beneficiary forms. These designations override your will completely. If you named your parents as beneficiaries on your 401k at 22 and never updated it, they get the money — regardless of what your will says, regardless of whether your partner is your primary heir.

**Primary beneficiary**: first in line.
**Contingent beneficiary**: inherits if the primary predeceases you.

Always name both. Always keep them current. Review after every major life event: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, death of a named beneficiary.

**Per stirpes vs per capita**: How the money divides if a beneficiary dies before you. Per stirpes means their share passes to their children. Per capita means it divides among surviving beneficiaries. Usually per stirpes is the right choice if you're naming family.

### Durable Power of Attorney (DPOA)

Authorizes someone to manage your finances if you're incapacitated — pay bills, manage investments, sell property, file taxes. Without it, your family needs court-ordered guardianship, which takes months and is expensive.

"Durable" means it remains valid if you become incapacitated (a regular POA becomes void). This is what you want.

In NJ, a DPOA must be signed before a notary and two witnesses. It becomes effective immediately upon signing unless you specify it's a "springing" POA (only activates upon incapacitation — harder to invoke in practice).

Choose someone you trust completely. An attorney-in-fact under a DPOA has broad authority. This is not a role for someone with poor financial judgment or conflicting interests.

### Healthcare Proxy (Healthcare POA)

Names a person to make medical decisions for you if you can't communicate. Different from the financial DPOA — this is purely for medical situations.

Your healthcare proxy can authorize or refuse treatment, access your medical records, and speak to your doctors. Without one, medical providers fall back on NJ's default hierarchy: spouse, adult children, parents, siblings. A partner you're not married to has no legal standing by default.

In NJ, the healthcare proxy document is called an "Instruction Directive" or "Proxy Directive." Sign it before two witnesses (who are not your healthcare proxy, related to you, or your heirs).

### Living Will / Advance Directive

Specifies your actual medical wishes — what interventions you do or don't want. Distinct from the healthcare proxy (which names a person) — this is a document that expresses your preferences directly.

Key decisions to address:
- Resuscitation (CPR, defibrillation)
- Mechanical ventilation
- Artificial nutrition and hydration
- Palliative/comfort care preferences

In NJ, the advance directive combines the proxy designation and the instruction directive into one document. The NJ Commission on Legal and Ethical Problems in the Delivery of Health Care provides a standard form.

## Key Considerations

**Unmarried partners**: NJ law does not automatically recognize an unmarried partner as your heir or medical decision-maker. Without explicit documents, they have no legal standing. If you're in a committed relationship without marriage, this is the highest-urgency item.

**Digital assets**: Your estate planning should address passwords, crypto wallets, and online accounts. A separate "digital estate" document (not the will — it shouldn't contain passwords in a public court document) listing account access, stored with your other estate documents, handled by your executor.

**Joint ownership**: Assets held jointly with right of survivorship (JTWROS) pass automatically to the surviving owner, outside probate, regardless of your will. Common for bank accounts and real estate between spouses. Understand what you hold jointly vs individually.

**NJ estate tax**: NJ eliminated its estate tax in 2018 but still has an inheritance tax. Spouses and children are exempt. Siblings pay 11-16%. Unrelated people (unmarried partners) pay 15-16%. If you have a long-term partner you're not married to, this matters for larger estates.

**When to update**: After marriage or divorce, birth or adoption of a child, death of a named beneficiary or executor, major change in assets (buying a home), moving states.

## Getting Started

**This week — free and immediate:**
1. Log into every retirement account (401k, Roth IRA) and check the beneficiary designation. Update if wrong.
2. Check life insurance beneficiaries.
3. Have a conversation with your partner about who you'd each want making decisions for the other.

**Within the next month:**
1. Use NJ's advance directive form (free, downloadable from NJ Department of Health) for the healthcare proxy and living will. Sign with two witnesses. Give a copy to your partner and your doctor.
2. Draft a basic will. Three options:
   - **DIY with Nolo or Trust & Will** (~$100-200 online): appropriate for simple estates with no minor children, no complex assets
   - **Local estate attorney** (~$300-600 for a simple will in NJ): recommended if you own property, have significant assets, or want to ensure it's airtight
   - **Legal aid** (if income-eligible): NJ has free services
3. Draft a DPOA — same attorney visit, or Nolo includes it.

**After buying a home:** revisit everything. Property ownership changes the estate picture significantly. An attorney visit at that point is worth the cost.

## Common Mistakes

**Relying on the will for retirement accounts**: The beneficiary designation on your 401k is a separate legal instrument that overrides the will. People write careful wills and then discover their retirement account — often their largest asset — still lists someone from 2015.

**Not naming a contingent beneficiary**: If your primary beneficiary predeceases you and there's no contingent, the account goes into your estate and through probate. Simple fix: always name a backup.

**Choosing the wrong executor**: The executor manages the estate, deals with creditors, and distributes assets. It's paperwork and decisions, not just a title. Choose someone organized and trustworthy, not just the family member who expects it.

**Assuming a partner has legal standing without documents**: In NJ, an unmarried partner has no default legal authority over your medical care or your estate. This is the gap estate planning closes.

**Treating it as done forever**: Estate documents need to be reviewed every 3-5 years and after major life events. A will written at 28 may be completely wrong by 35.

## Resources

- **NJ Advance Directive form** — available free from NJ Department of Health; the standard form is legally valid and covers healthcare proxy and living will in one document
- **Nolo's *Plan Your Estate*** — thorough book that covers all the concepts without requiring an attorney first; useful for understanding what you're signing before you sign it
- **Trust & Will** (trustandwill.com) — online platform for wills, POAs, and trusts; good for simple estates; NJ-specific documents
- **NJ State Bar Lawyer Referral Service** (njsba.com) — find a local estate attorney; a single consultation for a simple will and POA runs $300-600 in NJ
