# Life Insurance
*When you need it, how much, which type, and why the industry sells you the wrong thing*

---

## What Is It

Life insurance pays a lump sum (the death benefit) to your named beneficiaries when you die. That's the entire function. Everything else — cash value, investment components, living benefits — is layered on top of this core function, usually at your expense.

The fundamental question for any life insurance decision: does someone depend financially on your continued income? If yes, buy insurance to replace that income when you're gone. If no, you don't need it.

At Romi's current stage (28, no kids, partner not solely dependent on your income), life insurance is low priority — covered briefly in [finance/insurance-basics.md](insurance-basics.md). This file exists for when circumstances change, or to understand the product well before a salesperson does.

## Why It Matters

Life insurance is one of the most aggressively sold financial products in existence. The commission structure is designed to push permanent insurance (whole life, universal life) over term insurance, because permanent products generate 5–10x higher commissions. Understanding what you actually need — and the math behind why — is the defense.

The cost of getting this wrong: buying the wrong product in your 30s can cost $50,000–$200,000 over 20 years in unnecessary premiums and opportunity cost of money not invested.

## Core Concepts

### When You Actually Need It

Life insurance insures against one specific risk: the financial hardship your death creates for people who depend on your income.

**You need life insurance if:**
- A partner cannot maintain their standard of living without your income
- You have children who depend on you
- You have a mortgage on a property owned jointly
- You have a business partner with shared obligations
- You have parents or family members financially dependent on you

**You don't need life insurance if:**
- You're single with no dependents
- Your partner has independent income and no shared debt
- You have enough liquid assets that your death wouldn't create financial hardship

At 28 with no children and a working partner: you're likely in a gray zone. The questions to answer: could your partner maintain the mortgage or rent alone? Would your death create a financial crisis, or just a difficult adjustment?

**The trigger for Romi:** buying a home together (shared mortgage obligation) or having children — either one makes life insurance non-optional.

### Term Life Insurance — What You Should Buy

Term life insurance is the simplest financial product: pay a fixed premium for a defined period (the term), and the insurer pays the death benefit if you die during that period. If you outlive the term, the policy expires with no payout.

That's it. No investment component. No cash value. Just insurance.

**Why term is almost always the right answer:**
- Pure insurance for the lowest possible premium
- Forces clarity: you're buying coverage for the years you actually need it (raising children, paying down mortgage, building wealth)
- Lets you invest the premium difference yourself, in better vehicles than any insurance company offers

**The types of term:**

**Level term (what you want):** Premium is fixed for the entire term — same monthly payment from year 1 to year 20. Predictable, simple, the default choice.

**Annual renewable term:** Premium increases every year as you age. Cheap at 25, expensive at 45. Only useful if you need coverage for a very short period and know it.

**Return of premium (ROP) term:** Pays back your premiums if you outlive the term. Sounds appealing; the premium is 30–50% higher. The math almost never works — you're better off buying standard term and investing the difference.

**Choosing your term length:**

Match the term to the period you need coverage:
- Youngest child finishes college: 20–25 years if you have a newborn
- Mortgage payoff: match the mortgage term (15 or 30 years)
- Financial independence date: if you plan to have enough invested that your death doesn't create financial hardship, that's your endpoint

Most 28-year-olds buying term should buy **20-year level term**. By 48 with 20 years of investing behind you, the financial dependence your family has on your income is typically much reduced.

**How much coverage:**

The standard recommendation is **10–12x your annual income**. The logic: the death benefit, invested conservatively at 4–5% annual return, produces income that replaces your salary indefinitely for your beneficiaries.

| Annual income | 10x coverage | 12x coverage |
|--------------|-------------|-------------|
| $100,000 | $1,000,000 | $1,200,000 |
| $120,000 | $1,200,000 | $1,440,000 |
| $150,000 | $1,500,000 | $1,800,000 |

Add your mortgage balance to this number if you have one — the death benefit should cover both income replacement and debt payoff.

**What it costs:**

A healthy 28-year-old male, non-smoker, $1,000,000 / 20-year level term: approximately $35–55/month. This is cheap because you're young, healthy, and statistically unlikely to die in the next 20 years. Rates increase meaningfully every 5 years of delay.

Buy term early. The rate you lock in at 28 holds for the full 20 years — you don't pay 48-year-old prices when you're 48.

### Whole Life Insurance — What You're Sold Instead

Whole life is permanent insurance: it never expires, the premium is fixed for life, and it builds "cash value" — a savings component that grows over time.

**How it works:**
- Your premium is split: part funds the death benefit, part goes into a cash value account
- The cash value grows at a guaranteed low rate (typically 2–4%) plus possible dividends from the insurer
- You can borrow against the cash value or surrender the policy for its cash value
- At death, the insurer pays the death benefit (and typically keeps the accumulated cash value)

**Why it's heavily sold:**
An insurance agent selling you a $1,000/month whole life policy earns roughly $6,000–12,000 in first-year commissions. The same agent selling $50/month term earns roughly $200. The product exists in the form it does largely because of this commission structure.

**The honest case for whole life:**
- Permanent coverage: if you have lifelong dependents (a disabled child, a lifelong financial obligation), term insurance that expires creates real risk
- Forced savings: for people who will not invest otherwise, the automatic premium and cash value serves as a savings mechanism
- Estate planning tool for very high net worth individuals: at estate tax thresholds ($13M+ in 2024), whole life inside an irrevocable trust has legitimate uses

**The case against for most people:**
- Cost: a $1,000,000 whole life policy for a 28-year-old costs $700–1,200+/month vs $40–55/month for the same death benefit in term
- Cash value returns are low: 2–4% guaranteed, with dividends uncertain, vs 7–10% historical average for equity index funds
- The cash value is not your money until you surrender or borrow (at interest) — it's the insurer's until you die, at which point they keep it and pay only the death benefit
- Complexity and surrender charges: early surrender of whole life policies often results in substantial losses in the first 10–15 years

**The "buy term and invest the difference" math:**

Assume: 28-year-old, $1,000,000 coverage, 20-year comparison

| | Whole life | Term + invest the difference |
|--|-----------|----------------------------|
| Monthly premium | $900 | $45 term + $855 invested |
| Death benefit | $1,000,000 | $1,000,000 (term) |
| After 20 years (alive) | Cash value ~$150,000–200,000 | ~$520,000 invested (7% avg) |
| After 20 years (dead) | Beneficiary gets $1,000,000; insurer keeps cash value | Beneficiary gets $1,000,000 (term) + $520,000 in investments |

In virtually every realistic scenario over a 20-year horizon, buying term and investing the difference produces more wealth and provides equal or better protection.

### Limited-Pay Whole Life (10-Pay / 12-Pay)

A variation worth understanding separately because it gets sold aggressively and has a narrower legitimate use case than standard whole life.

**What it is:** permanent whole life insurance where you pay premiums for only 10 or 12 years instead of for life. After the payment period, coverage continues forever with no further premiums. The death benefit and cash value growth continue until death.

**The trade-off:** to compress lifetime premiums into 10–12 years, the annual premium is significantly higher than standard whole life. A $1,000,000 10-pay policy for a 28-year-old costs approximately $8,000–12,000/year ($667–$1,000/month) vs $700–1,000+/month for standard whole life (which you'd pay for life) vs $40–55/month for term.

**The pitch:** "pay for 10 years, covered forever, cash value accumulates faster." True on all counts — but it doesn't fix the underlying problems with whole life.

**The cash value reality on 10-pay:**

| Year | 10-pay cash value (approx) | Term + invest same $10k/year |
|------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| Year 5 | ~$25,000–35,000 | ~$61,000 (7% avg) |
| Year 10 (paid up) | ~$100,000–130,000 | ~$138,000 |
| Year 20 | ~$200,000–280,000 | ~$394,000 |
| Year 30 | ~$350,000–500,000 | ~$786,000 |

The cash value compounds at 3–4% guaranteed (plus non-guaranteed dividends from mutual insurers like MassMutual or Guardian). The investment alternative compounds at whatever the market does — historically ~7% real. The gap widens over time.

**Where 10-pay has a legitimate case:**

*Business succession planning:* buy-sell agreements funded by life insurance often use 10-pay because the business needs a finite, defined premium payment period tied to the ownership structure — not a lifetime premium.

*Insuring children:* some parents buy small 10-pay policies on children (typically $50,000–$100,000 face value) to lock in insurability regardless of future health, with a fully paid-up policy at age ~18–28. The premiums are small ($30–80/month for a child), the guaranteed insurability has real value if the child develops a condition that would otherwise make them uninsurable, and the policy transfers to them. This is one of the more defensible uses.

*Legacy / estate planning at high net worth:* if estate taxes are a concern (currently $13M+ per individual), a 10-pay policy inside an irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) can fund the tax liability. The compressed payment period means the trust is funded efficiently over 10 years. Not relevant at Romi's current wealth level.

**What 10-pay is not:**

Not a retirement savings vehicle. Agents often pitch it as "tax-free retirement income" via policy loans. The mechanics are real — you can borrow against cash value tax-free — but the 3–4% underlying return means you're borrowing your own underperforming savings. A Roth IRA with index funds produces more money with less complexity and equivalent tax treatment on withdrawal.

Not a replacement for term during the years you actually need income replacement coverage. If you have dependents and a mortgage, a $500,000 10-pay policy at $600/month provides less protection than a $1,000,000 term policy at $45/month. The death benefit is half; the cost is 13x higher.

**The company matters for whole life:** mutual insurance companies (MassMutual, Guardian, Northwestern Mutual, New York Life) return dividends to policyholders, which improve cash value performance. Stock companies (MetLife, Prudential) return profits to shareholders — policyholder dividends are lower or absent. If you're evaluating whole life or 10-pay, mutual company policies with strong dividend histories are meaningfully better than stock company products.

**The honest summary:** 10-pay is the most financially defensible form of whole life insurance because it eliminates lifetime premium obligation and accelerates cash value. It still doesn't beat term + index funds for wealth accumulation. Its legitimate uses are narrow: business succession, locking in insurability for children, and estate planning at high net worth. For a 28-year-old building wealth, these edge cases don't apply yet.

### Universal and Variable Life — Variations on the Same Problem

**Universal life (UL):** Similar to whole life but with flexible premiums and death benefit. The flexibility sounds useful until you understand that the cost of insurance rises every year inside the policy — if returns are low, the cash value gets consumed by rising internal costs, and the policy can lapse.

**Variable universal life (VUL):** The cash value is invested in mutual fund-like subaccounts. Higher upside potential, but now you have market risk inside an insurance product with high fees. You're combining the worst features of insurance (inflexible premiums, death benefit structure) with the worst features of investing (high-cost funds with no tax-loss harvesting). Most VUL policies have internal expense ratios of 2–4% — before the mutual fund expense ratios on top.

**Indexed universal life (IUL):** Cash value tied to an equity index (S&P 500) with a floor (you don't lose principal) and a cap (you don't get full upside). Sold as "market upside with no downside." The caps are typically 8–12% in good years vs actual S&P returns of 20–25% — you give up significant upside for the floor protection, while paying high insurance expenses.

**The pattern:** each product adds complexity to justify higher costs. None of them beat the simple alternative of term + index funds for a person building wealth in their 20s–40s.

### Riders — What's Worth Adding

Riders are add-ons to a base policy that modify coverage. Most are not worth paying for. Two are.

**Waiver of premium rider:** If you become disabled and can't work, the insurer waives your premium — your coverage continues without payment. Low cost ($5–15/month). Worth adding.

**Conversion rider:** Allows you to convert term to permanent insurance without medical underwriting at a future date. Useful if you develop a health condition during the term that would make new insurance uninsurable or very expensive. Often included at no additional cost on good term policies — verify before purchasing.

**Not worth it for most people:**
- Accelerated death benefit (terminal illness): often included free
- Accidental death rider: you're already getting the death benefit; this only pays extra for accidental death specifically — unnecessarily narrow
- Return of premium: discussed above — poor math

### How to Buy

**Online comparison platforms:** Policygenius, SelectQuote, and similar aggregators pull quotes from multiple insurers at once. Good for getting ballpark numbers and comparing term options. Free to use.

**Independent broker:** a broker who works with multiple insurers (not a captive agent for one company) can compare across the market, explain underwriting nuances, and find the best rate for your health profile. For larger policies ($1M+), worth using.

**Underwriting process:** medical exam for most policies above $500,000 — height, weight, blood pressure, blood panel. Some "no-exam" policies exist but cost more and have lower coverage limits. At 28 and healthy, the medical exam should produce the best rate class (preferred plus or preferred).

**Best insurers for term (current reputation):** Banner Life, Pacific Life, Protective Life, Haven Life (online-first, no exam for qualifying applicants), Ladder (flexible coverage). Don't choose based on brand recognition — compare actual rates for your age, health, and coverage amount.

**NJ specifics:** NJ requires a 10-day "free look" period — you can cancel a new policy within 10 days of receiving the policy document for a full premium refund. This is your opportunity to read the actual policy carefully, not just the illustration you were shown during the sales process.

## Key Considerations

**Name your beneficiaries correctly and update them.** The life insurance benefit goes to the named beneficiary regardless of your will. An ex-partner named as beneficiary from 10 years ago gets the money — your estate documents don't override this. Review beneficiaries every time your life changes: marriage, divorce, children, death of a beneficiary.

**Don't conflate insurance and investing.** They solve different problems. Insurance replaces income when you die. Investing builds wealth while you live. Products that try to do both (whole life, VUL, IUL) do both poorly compared to doing each separately.

**Your need decreases as wealth builds.** At 28 with $50,000 invested, your death creates enormous financial risk for dependents. At 48 with $1M invested, the portfolio partially replaces the income insurance was providing. The right amount of coverage decreases as your net worth grows — this is why term is appropriate: it expires around the time you need it less.

**Health is your most valuable underwriting asset.** Rates are set at issue and locked in for term policies. A $1M policy bought at 28 at preferred plus rates costs $40/month. The same policy bought at 35 after a borderline health finding costs significantly more. Buy when you're young and healthy.

**Don't let the conversation start with "what product do you want"** — it should start with "what risk are you trying to cover and for how long." If a financial professional leads with product recommendations before understanding your situation, that's a signal to slow down.

## Getting Started

1. Answer the dependency question honestly: does anyone rely on your income in a way that would cause real financial hardship if you died tomorrow? If no, nothing to do now.
2. Trigger events: partner income dependence, home purchase with shared mortgage, first child — any of these means buy term within the next 6 months.
3. Get quotes on Policygenius for $1M / 20-year level term. Takes 10 minutes.
4. If quotes are favorable, apply. The medical exam is simple — it comes to you.
5. Add waiver of premium rider. Verify conversion rider is included.
6. Name beneficiaries. Set a calendar reminder to review beneficiaries every 3 years.

## Common Mistakes

**Buying through the employer group plan as your only coverage.** Group term through an employer is often cheap but limited ($1–2x salary), not portable (it ends when you leave), and not enough. Use it as a supplement, not a foundation.

**Over-insuring.** Buying $3M in term when $1M is sufficient is wasted premium. The coverage goal is financial need replacement, not leaving a windfall.

**Underinsuring because whole life was sold instead.** A $500,000 whole life policy that costs $800/month does less than a $1,000,000 term policy that costs $45/month. Many people are underinsured because the product they bought ate the premium budget.

**Not reviewing after major life changes.** Coverage bought before children may not be enough after. Coverage bought before a home purchase doesn't account for the mortgage.

**Lapsing a policy unintentionally.** Missing premiums causes lapse. Auto-pay from a stable account. If you're in financial hardship, contact the insurer before missing a payment — most have grace periods and hardship options.
