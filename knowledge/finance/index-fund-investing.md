# Index Fund Investing
*How to build wealth passively — own the market, pay almost nothing, beat most professionals*

---

## What Is It

An index fund is a fund that tracks a market index — a predefined list of companies, like the S&P 500 (the 500 largest US companies) or the total US stock market. When you buy one share of an S&P 500 index fund, you own a tiny slice of all 500 companies at once. The fund isn't managed by someone picking stocks — it just mirrors the index mechanically.

The insight behind index investing: markets are very hard to beat consistently, because prices already reflect what millions of informed buyers and sellers think a stock is worth. Trying to outsmart the market costs money (in fees and taxes) and usually loses. Owning the market, at the lowest possible cost, is the rational default.

## Why It Matters

Romi at 28, in tech, with a steady income — the most powerful variable now is time. A dollar invested at 28 is worth roughly 5–7x more at 65 than a dollar invested at 45. The gap between a good investment strategy (low-cost index funds) and a bad one (high-fee active funds, trying to pick stocks) compounds for decades. Getting this right early and leaving it alone is worth more than any stock tip or clever trade.

This isn't about getting rich fast. It's about applying Stoic logic to money: do what's in your control (save, minimize costs, stay consistent), let go of what isn't (market timing, returns, macro).

## Core Concepts

### How Index Funds Work

An index fund buys the same stocks, in the same proportions, as its target index. If Apple is 7% of the S&P 500, the fund holds 7% Apple. When the index changes (a company gets added or removed), the fund rebalances. No human decides which stocks to buy — it's rules-based. This eliminates manager error and drastically reduces costs.

**ETF vs mutual fund**: Both can track an index.
- ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund): trades on an exchange like a stock, can be bought/sold any time during market hours. Lower minimum investment. Examples: VTI, VOO, FXAIX.
- Mutual fund: bought/sold at end-of-day price directly through the fund company. Often requires a minimum (Fidelity and Schwab offer $0 minimums). Examples: VTSAX, FSKAX.

For most people starting out, ETFs and index mutual funds at Fidelity/Schwab/Vanguard are functionally identical. Use whichever your account supports.

### Expense Ratio — The Number That Actually Matters

The expense ratio is the annual fee the fund charges, expressed as a percentage of your investment. On $10,000 invested:
- 1.0% expense ratio → $100/year, every year, forever
- 0.03% expense ratio → $3/year

Sounds small. Over 30 years at 7% average returns, the difference between a 1% fund and a 0.03% fund on a $10,000 initial investment is roughly $14,000 in lost wealth — just from fees.

Target expense ratios under 0.10%. The best index funds (VTI, VOO, FSKAX, SWTSX) are 0.01–0.04%.

### What Indexes to Track

**Total US Market** — owns every publicly traded US company, large to small. Most diversified single US fund.
- ETF: VTI (Vanguard) or SCHB (Schwab)
- Mutual fund: VTSAX (Vanguard), FSKAX (Fidelity)

**S&P 500** — owns the 500 largest US companies. Overlaps ~85% with total market. Either works.
- ETF: VOO (Vanguard), SPY (State Street), IVV (iShares)
- Mutual fund: VFIAX (Vanguard), FXAIX (Fidelity)

**International** — owns companies outside the US. The US is ~60% of global market cap; owning international reduces concentration risk.
- ETF: VXUS (Vanguard total international), VEA (developed markets only)
- Mutual fund: VTIAX (Vanguard)

**Bonds** — lower return, lower volatility. Used to dampen portfolio swings, especially approaching retirement.
- BND (Vanguard total bond market ETF)

**Simple default**: VTI + VXUS (or just VT, which is both in one fund) covers the entire global stock market at near-zero cost.

### Account Types — Where You Hold the Funds

The account wrapper determines the tax treatment. Same fund, different account = very different tax outcome.

**401(k)** — employer-sponsored, pre-tax. Contributions reduce your taxable income today. Tax is paid when you withdraw in retirement. 2025 contribution limit: $23,500. Always contribute at least enough to get the full employer match — that's a 50–100% instant return.

**Roth IRA** — individual account, after-tax. You pay tax now, but all growth and withdrawals are tax-free in retirement. 2025 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). Income phase-out starts at $150k (single) / $236k (married). Best account type for people who expect to be in a higher bracket later.

**Traditional IRA** — like 401k but individual. Pre-tax, pay on withdrawal. Less advantageous than Roth at lower income levels, but useful if over the Roth income limit.

**HSA (Health Savings Account)** — triple tax advantage: pre-tax contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses. If you invest the balance (most HSA custodians allow this), it becomes a stealth retirement account. Requires an HDHP health plan.

**Taxable brokerage** — no tax advantage, full flexibility. Use after maxing tax-advantaged accounts. Subject to capital gains tax when you sell.

**The order of operations**:
1. 401(k) up to employer match (free money first)
2. Max Roth IRA ($7,000/year)
3. Max 401(k) ($23,500/year)
4. HSA if eligible
5. Taxable brokerage with remaining savings

### Dollar-Cost Averaging

You don't need to time the market. Invest a fixed amount on a fixed schedule (e.g., $500 on the 1st of every month) regardless of whether the market is up or down. When prices are low, you buy more shares. When prices are high, you buy fewer. Over time this smooths out volatility and removes the psychological burden of "is now a good time to invest?"

The evidence strongly favors automatic, consistent investing over trying to pick entry points. Most people who wait for the "right time" wait too long and miss years of compounding.

### The Case Against Stock Picking

Not opinion — math. Over any 15-year period, roughly 90% of actively managed funds underperform a simple S&P 500 index fund after fees. Professionals with full-time research teams, proprietary data, and sophisticated tools still lose. The reason: fees. A fund charging 1% annually needs to beat the market by 1% just to break even. Consistently doing that, year after year, is near-impossible.

Individual stock picking adds concentration risk on top of this — if you put 20% of your savings into one company and it drops 60%, that's a decade of progress erased.

Index funds win not because of special insight, but because they stop trying to outsmart the market and minimize the only thing you can control: costs.

## Key Considerations

**Volatility is the cost of admission.** The S&P 500 has dropped 20%+ roughly every 5–7 years. In 2008–2009, it fell 57%. Every one of those drops looked permanent in the moment. Every one recovered. The investors who sold locked in the loss. The ones who held (or kept buying) came out ahead. Long-term investing requires sitting with discomfort.

**Asset allocation depends on time horizon.** If you're 28 and investing for 35+ years, 100% stocks is defensible — you have time to recover from crashes. At 55, bonds provide a cushion because you can't wait out a crash. A rough heuristic: subtract your age from 110 to get your stock percentage. Adjust based on risk tolerance.

**Rebalancing.** If you hold 80% stocks / 20% international and stocks surge, you might drift to 90% / 10%. Rebalance annually by selling the overweight and buying the underweight, or by directing new contributions toward the underweight.

**Tax-loss harvesting.** In a taxable account, if a position is down, you can sell it, take the capital loss (which offsets gains elsewhere), and immediately buy a similar fund. Vanguard's VTI and iShares' ITOT track similar indexes — selling one and buying the other is valid tax-loss harvesting.

**Don't over-optimize.** Fidelity did a study (oft-cited, disputed in detail, but plausible in conclusion) finding their best-performing accounts belonged to people who had forgotten they had them. The most dangerous move is checking your portfolio during a crash and selling.

## Getting Started

1. **Open an account.** Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard — all are excellent. Fidelity has the best UI and no account minimums. Open a Roth IRA first if you're eligible.

2. **Fund the account.** Link your checking account and transfer money. You can start with whatever you have — $100 is fine.

3. **Pick a fund.** For simplicity: VTI or FSKAX for US total market. If you want international exposure, add VXUS or FZILX (Fidelity zero-fee international). That's a complete portfolio.

4. **Set up automatic contributions.** Monthly auto-invest removes decision friction. Set it and don't watch it.

5. **Max your 401(k) match first.** Before funding the Roth IRA, make sure you're capturing the full employer match in your 401(k). This comes before everything else.

6. **Leave it alone.** Seriously. The biggest return-killer is tinkering.

## Common Mistakes

**Waiting for the "right time."** The right time is now. Every year you wait costs compounding years you can't get back.

**Picking thematic or sector ETFs.** Clean energy, AI, metaverse, cannabis — these are marketing products, not investing strategies. They're usually launched at the peak of a trend, charge high fees, and rarely outperform the total market long-term.

**Checking too often.** Looking at your portfolio weekly (or daily) during a crash leads to panic selling. Quarterly is plenty. Yearly is fine.

**Holding cash "until things calm down."** Things don't calm down before prices recover — prices recover before it feels calm. By the time news is positive, the market has already priced it in.

**Not accounting for employer match.** A 50% match on the first 6% of salary is a 3% salary bonus you're leaving on the table if you skip it.

**Confusing performance with skill.** Any individual stock or sector fund that outperformed last year will be presented as proof of investing genius. It's usually luck. Reversion to the mean is real.

## Resources

- *The Little Book of Common Sense Investing* — John Bogle. The founder of Vanguard makes the complete case for index funds in under 200 pages. The best starting point.
- *A Random Walk Down Wall Street* — Burton Malkiel. Academic rigor on why markets are hard to beat. More comprehensive than Bogle; read this second.
- *JL Collins — The Simple Path to Wealth*. Free blog series (and book). More accessible tone than Malkiel, equally solid. Good for the "just tell me what to do" reader.
- *Morningstar Fund Screener* (morningstar.com). Free tool to compare expense ratios, returns, and risk ratings for any fund. Use it to vet anything before buying.
