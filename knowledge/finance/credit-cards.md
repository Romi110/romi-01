# Credit Cards
*The tier system, which cards earn what, and the decision logic for building a wallet that actually pays you*

---

## What Is It

Credit cards in the US fall into a hierarchy: no-fee commodity cards, mid-tier fee cards, and premium cards. Each tier exists for a different type of user. The mistake most people make is either avoiding fee cards out of reflex or paying for a premium card whose benefits they don't use.

The second layer is the rewards currency question: cash back (simple, liquid) vs. transferable points (more complex, higher ceiling). This file maps the landscape, ranks the cards within each tier, and gives clear decision logic for when to move up.

---

## Why It Matters

A well-chosen credit card stack generates $500–$2,500+/year in real value with no change in spending behavior — just routing existing spend through the right cards. At Romi's income level and spending pattern (tech professional, business owner, travel, restaurants), the cards in the right tier pay for themselves multiple times. The wrong stack — or no stack — leaves that money on the table permanently.

**One rule above all:** Pay the statement balance in full every month. Credit card rewards math assumes zero interest paid. A single month of carrying a balance erases months of rewards at a 24% APR. This file assumes discipline here.

## Core Concepts

### The Rewards Currency Decision

Before picking cards, decide which currency system you're operating in:

**Cash back:** 1–2% back on purchases. Simple, liquid, no redemption skill needed. The right choice if you never want to think about points.

**Transferable points:** Points that can be moved to airline and hotel programs. Higher ceiling (flights and hotels at 1.5–10 cents/point vs. cash back's 1 cent/point), but requires intentional redemption. Three major transferable currencies:
- **Chase Ultimate Rewards (UR):** Transfers to United, Southwest, Hyatt, British Airways, Air France/KLM, and others. Hyatt is the standout — high redemption value for hotels.
- **Amex Membership Rewards (MR):** Transfers to Delta, Air France/KLM, Hilton, Marriott, British Airways, and others. More airline partners than Chase; hotel side is weaker.
- **Capital One Miles:** Transfers to Air Canada, Turkish Airlines, Avianca, Wyndham, and others. Fewer partners than Chase/Amex but improving; also redeemable at 1 cent/mile flat against travel charges.

**The right default for Romi:** Transferable points (Chase UR or Amex MR), prioritizing Chase because of the Hyatt transfer partner — one of the best values in the system — and because Chase cards cover more spend categories effectively.

### Tier 0 — No Annual Fee (Foundation)

These earn real rewards with zero cost. The floor of any stack.

**Chase Freedom Unlimited (CFU)**
- Earn: 1.5% cash back on everything; 3% on dining and drugstores; 5% on travel booked through Chase
- Annual fee: $0
- The value: Paired with a premium Chase card (Sapphire Reserve or Preferred), these "cash back" points convert to full Chase Ultimate Rewards points — making this card a UR earning machine with no fee
- Standalone: Still the best no-fee card for flat-rate spend
- **Who gets it:** Everyone with a Chase premium card, as the catch-all spend card

**Chase Freedom Flex**
- Earn: 5% on rotating quarterly categories (gas, Amazon, grocery, PayPal, etc. — changes quarterly); 3% dining and drugstores; 1% everything else
- Annual fee: $0
- The value: Same UR upgrade trick as CFU. The rotating 5% categories can be high-value if you plan around them (Q4 is often Amazon/PayPal — useful for holiday spend)
- **Who gets it:** People who want to maximize rotating categories and are willing to track them

**Citi Double Cash**
- Earn: 2% on everything (1% on purchase, 1% on payment)
- Annual fee: $0
- The value: Flat 2% is the best simple cash back rate available with no fee; transfers to Citi ThankYou Points (mediocre partner list) if you have a Citi Premier
- **Who gets it:** People who want maximum simplicity and cash back, not points

**Discover it Cash Back**
- Earn: 5% on quarterly rotating categories (gas, grocery, restaurants, Amazon alternating); 1% everything else; **Cashback Match first year** — Discover matches all cash back earned in the first 12 months
- Annual fee: $0
- The value: The first-year match makes this effectively 10%/2% for year one — one of the best signup offers with no fee, no minimum spend requirement
- **Who gets it:** As a no-fee add for year one, then evaluate whether the rotating categories are worth tracking

### Tier 1 — Mid-Fee Cards ($95–$99/year): The Core Stack

These pay for themselves with one or two specific benefits. For most people, this is the right home.

**Chase Sapphire Preferred (CSP) — $95/year**
- Earn: 3x dining, 3x streaming, 3x online grocery (non-Instacart); 2x travel; 1x everything else; 5x on Chase Travel portal
- Signup bonus: Typically 60,000–80,000 UR points ($750–$1,000 in travel value via Chase portal; more via transfer)
- Key benefits:
  - Full Chase Ultimate Rewards access — points transfer to all partners (Hyatt, United, Southwest, British Airways, etc.)
  - $50/year hotel credit through Chase Travel portal (nets the fee to $45)
  - Primary rental car insurance (not secondary — actual coverage without filing with your personal auto first)
  - Trip cancellation/interruption insurance up to $10,000/trip
  - No foreign transaction fees
- **The math:** 60,000 UR signup bonus = 3 nights at a Hyatt Category 4 property (~$750 cash value at 1.25 cents/point minimum; often worth 2 cents+ via transfer = $1,200+ value). That's 12+ years of annual fees in year one.
- **Who gets it:** The right starting point for anyone entering the points game. Best first premium card. Upgrade to Reserve later if spend justifies it.

**Capital One Venture Rewards — $95/year**
- Earn: 2x miles on everything; 5x on hotels and cars booked via Capital One Travel
- Signup bonus: Typically 75,000 miles ($750 against travel; ~$1,125+ via transfer)
- Key benefits:
  - $100 Global Entry/TSA PreCheck credit (every 4 years)
  - No foreign transaction fees
  - Miles transfer to airline partners (Air Canada Aeroplan, Turkish Airlines Miles&Smiles, Avianca, Wyndham)
  - Simple 2x flat rate — no category management
- **The math:** $95 fee offset partially by Global Entry credit. 2x on everything is a clean earn rate for non-category-optimized spend.
- **Who gets it:** Someone who wants a single card, no tracking, transferable miles, and simplicity. Also useful for people whose spend doesn't cluster in dining/travel categories that Sapphire Preferred rewards more.

**American Express Blue Cash Preferred — $95/year**
- Earn: 6% on US grocery (up to $6,000/year, then 1%); 6% on select US streaming; 3% on transit and US gas; 1% everything else
- Signup bonus: $250 statement credit after minimum spend
- Key benefits:
  - The 6% grocery rate is the highest in the market for a no-complexity card
  - $84/year Equinox+ credit (useful if you use Equinox; otherwise worthless)
  - $0 intro fee first year, then $95
- **The math:** If you spend $500/month on groceries ($6,000/year), the 6% earns $360 in cash back. Net of the $95 fee: $265 profit from groceries alone, plus streaming and gas.
- **Who gets it:** Households with significant grocery spend. Not a points card — cash back only. Best combined with a Chase UR card for travel categories.

**Citi Premier — $95/year**
- Earn: 3x hotels, 3x air, 3x restaurants, 3x grocery, 3x gas; 1x everything else
- Signup bonus: Typically 60,000 ThankYou Points ($600 cash; higher via transfer)
- Key benefits:
  - $100/year hotel credit (one booking of $500+ per year)
  - ThankYou Points transfer to Air France/KLM, Turkish Airlines, Avianca, and others
  - The broadest 3x category coverage of any $95 card — five categories
- **The math:** The five 3x categories cover most of normal spending. ThankYou Points have a narrower transfer network than Chase/Amex but Turkish Airlines Miles&Smiles is a high-value sweet spot (cheap Star Alliance redemptions).
- **Who gets it:** Citi loyalists or people who want diversification into a third points currency. Not the first choice over Chase, but not bad.

### Tier 2 — Premium Cards ($250–$695/year): Pay for Themselves With Benefits

These have fees that look alarming but are structured to break even or exceed cost through credits and perks. They require using the credits. If you don't travel or don't use the credits, they're expensive.

**Chase Sapphire Reserve (CSR) — $550/year**
- Earn: 3x dining anywhere; 3x travel (after the $300 credit); 10x hotels and rental cars via Chase Travel portal; 1x everything else
- Signup bonus: Typically 60,000 UR points (same pool as Preferred)
- Key benefits that matter:
  - **$300 annual travel credit:** Automatically applied to any travel purchase. Nets the effective fee to $250. This credit is the broadest in premium cards — gas, Uber, flights, hotels, parking all trigger it.
  - **Priority Pass Select:** Unlimited airport lounge access for you and guests at 1,300+ lounges worldwide. A Priority Pass membership alone is $429/year. This alone covers the fee delta over the Preferred.
  - **$100 Global Entry/TSA PreCheck credit** every 4 years
  - **Primary rental car insurance** up to $75,000
  - **Trip cancellation/interruption insurance** up to $10,000/person
  - **Travel Emergency Evacuation** up to $100,000 — medical evacuation coverage that most people don't realize exists until they need it
  - 1.5 cents/point through Chase Travel portal (vs. 1.25 cents on Preferred)
  - No foreign transaction fees
- **The math after credits:**
  - $550 fee − $300 travel credit = $250 net cost
  - Priority Pass value: $429/year (if you use lounges)
  - Net-net: You're getting paid ~$179 to hold the card if you use lounges, vs. paying $250 if you don't
- **CSP vs. CSR decision:** If you take 3+ flights per year and use airport lounges, CSR wins. If you fly 1–2 times per year and don't care about lounges, CSP at $95 is better. The break-even: use the Priority Pass 2–3 times/year and the Reserve pays for itself.
- **Who gets it:** Regular travelers (3+ trips/year), people who want lounge access, people who want the highest travel protections.

**American Express Platinum — $695/year**
- Earn: 5x on flights booked directly with airlines or through Amex Travel; 5x on prepaid hotels via Amex Travel; 1x everything else
- Signup bonus: Typically 80,000–150,000 MR points (150,000 offers appear via referral/targeted)
- Key benefits:
  - **$200 annual airline fee credit:** Select one airline; covers incidentals (seat upgrades, checked bags, in-flight food). Note: does not cover flight purchases directly.
  - **$200 hotel credit:** Prepaid hotels through Amex Fine Hotels + Resorts or The Hotel Collection (2-night minimum). More restricted than Chase's $300.
  - **$200 Uber Cash:** $15/month + $35 in December, deposited as Uber Cash monthly. Only useful if you actively use Uber/Uber Eats.
  - **$240 digital entertainment credit:** $20/month toward Disney+, ESPN+, Hulu, Peacock, NYT, SiriusXM. Must be used monthly — doesn't accumulate.
  - **$155 Walmart+ credit:** Annual membership reimbursed. Useful if you shop Walmart/use Paramount+.
  - **$100 Saks Fifth Avenue credit:** $50 Jan–Jun, $50 Jul–Dec.
  - **Centurion Lounge access:** Amex's own premium lounges (not Priority Pass) at major airports — quality is higher than most Priority Pass lounges, particularly food and drinks. Limited locations.
  - **Priority Pass Select:** Same as CSR.
  - **Marriott Bonvoy Gold status** and **Hilton Honors Gold status** automatically
  - **$100 Global Entry credit**
  - **TSA PreCheck credit** (separate from Global Entry)
  - No foreign transaction fees
- **The math:** The $695 fee looks brutal. Breaking it down:
  - $200 airline credit (usable)
  - $200 Uber Cash (usable if you Uber)
  - $240 digital entertainment (usable if you have these services)
  - $155 Walmart+ (usable)
  - $100 Saks (if you shop there)
  - Centurion Lounge (variable value)
  - Total theoretical value: $895–$1,200+ in credits, before any points
  - **The catch:** These credits require active management. Many expire monthly. People who don't track them leave $300–400 on the table and are actually paying for the card.
- **MR points vs. UR points:** Amex MR's best partners are Air France/KLM Flying Blue (often great for Delta flights at lower rates than Delta SkyMiles), British Airways Avios, and ANA (incredible business class value). For hotel points, Marriott and Hilton transfers are available but inefficient.
- **Who gets it:** Frequent flyers who can use 5x on airfare (high airfare spend), people who specifically want Centurion Lounge access, people who will actively use the monthly credits. At Romi's current stage — unless flying regularly for work — this card doesn't justify itself yet.

**Capital One Venture X — $395/year**
- Earn: 10x on hotels and rental cars via Capital One Travel; 5x on flights via Capital One Travel; 2x on everything else
- Signup bonus: 75,000 miles (typically)
- Key benefits:
  - **$300 annual travel credit:** Applied to Capital One Travel portal bookings only — less flexible than CSR's $300 (which covers any travel charge)
  - **10,000 anniversary bonus miles:** Every year at renewal = $100 in travel value, effectively reducing the net fee to $295 − $300 credit = ~$0 net after credits
  - **Priority Pass Select** + access to Capital One Lounges (newer, high-quality lounges at limited airports)
  - **$100 Global Entry/TSA PreCheck credit**
  - 2x on everything — the highest flat rate on a travel card
  - No foreign transaction fees
- **The math:** $395 − $300 travel credit − $100 anniversary bonus miles = effectively free to $5 cost per year. Comparable lounge access to CSR at a lower effective cost.
- **Best case for Venture X over CSR:** If you book most travel through the Capital One portal (where the $300 credit applies) and prefer simplicity over category optimization. The transfer partners are narrower but growing.
- **Who gets it:** Someone who wants premium card benefits without the complexity of Chase/Amex category management and credit tracking. A strong CSR alternative.

### The Stack: What to Actually Hold

**Stage 1 — Starting out (no fee stack):**
- Chase Freedom Unlimited — catches all spend at 1.5x, 3x dining
- Citi Double Cash — backup for non-dining/travel spend (2% flat)

**Stage 2 — First premium card:**
- Chase Sapphire Preferred ($95) — unlocks UR transfers, adds dining/travel earn, travel insurance
- Keep CFU — it now earns full UR points instead of just cash back
- Stack: CSP for dining + travel + UR unlocking; CFU for everything else

**Stage 3 — Optimized stack (Romi's current relevant tier):**
- **Chase Sapphire Reserve ($550)** — dining (3x), travel (3x), lounge access, $300 travel credit, primary rental coverage
- **Chase Freedom Unlimited ($0)** — catch-all 1.5x in same UR currency; 3x dining backup
- **Amex Blue Cash Preferred ($95)** — 6x grocery (cash back, separate currency, fills the gap CSR doesn't optimize)
- Optional: **Chase Freedom Flex ($0)** — rotating 5% categories (Amazon, gas, grocery quarters)

This stack generates:
- 3x on dining (CSR)
- 3x on travel (CSR)
- 6% on grocery (BCP — cash back)
- 1.5x on everything else (CFU → UR)
- Airport lounge access (CSR Priority Pass)
- $300 travel credit (CSR)
- Global Entry covered (CSR)
- Full UR ecosystem for hotel/airline transfers

**Stage 4 — If flying for work or high airline spend:**
Add Amex Platinum for the 5x on airfare + Centurion Lounge access. Manage the monthly credits. This only makes sense if you're flying 15+ times/year or have high enough airline spend for 5x to pull ahead.

### Redemption: How to Actually Use Points

Accumulating points is stage one. Redemption is where the real value is.

**Chase UR redemptions, ranked by value:**
1. **Transfer to Hyatt (best):** ~2 cents/point consistently. A Hyatt Category 4 (Park Hyatt, Andaz) is ~25,000 points for a night that costs $250–$350 cash. 1.2–1.5 cents/point minimum; often 2–2.5 cents.
2. **Transfer to United:** Good for international business class via Star Alliance partners. Rare, but 70,000 points for a business class transatlantic that costs $3,000+ cash = 4+ cents/point.
3. **Chase Travel portal at 1.5 cents (Reserve):** Simple, no transfer needed. Book any travel and get 50% more than cash. Good for when transfer partners don't add more value.
4. **Transfer to Southwest:** Good for domestic flights with Companion Pass synergy. Southwest points are ~1.5 cents each — roughly matching portal value but with more flexibility.
5. **Cash back at 1 cent:** The floor. Never do this — you're leaving value on the table vs. the portal.

**Amex MR redemptions, ranked:**
1. **Transfer to Air France/KLM Flying Blue:** Delta flights often bookable via Flying Blue at better rates than Delta's own SkyMiles. 18,000 points for a domestic flight that Delta charges 25,000 SkyMiles.
2. **Transfer to ANA:** Incredible business class value on Star Alliance partners. Niche but high-ceiling.
3. **Transfer to British Airways Avios:** Good for short-haul American Airlines and Alaska Airlines flights (Avios pricing is by distance — under 1,000 miles is cheap).
4. **Amex Travel portal at 1 cent:** Basically cash — the floor for MR. Don't do this.

**Capital One Miles redemptions:**
1. **Transfer to Air Canada Aeroplan or Turkish Airlines:** Star Alliance redemptions; competitive with United MileagePlus.
2. **1 cent against travel charges:** Any travel charge on the statement can be wiped at 1 cent/mile. Simple and flexible — better than Amex's portal floor.

**The meta-principle:** Never redeem for gift cards, merchandise, or cash back unless you've exhausted better options. Points are worth 1–2x more through travel redemption than any other channel.

### Business Credit Cards (Quick Reference)

If you have any self-employment or business income, business cards are a separate opportunity stack with higher signup bonuses and different category multipliers.

**Chase Ink Business Preferred ($95)** — 3x on travel, shipping, internet/cable/phone, social media advertising (up to $150k/year combined); 100,000 UR signup bonus (largest in the Chase ecosystem, typically). Points are full UR. This card alone generates more UR in a year than most consumer cards.

**Chase Ink Business Unlimited ($0)** — 1.5x on everything, business version of CFU. Same UR unlock trick when paired with Ink Preferred or Sapphire.

**Amex Business Platinum ($695)** — 5x on airfare + prepaid hotels via Amex; 1.5x on purchases over $5,000; $200 airline incidental; Priority Pass; Centurion lounge. For high-spend businesses with significant airfare.

**Business cards don't require an LLC.** Any 1099 income, freelance work, or side income qualifies you to apply for a business card as a sole proprietor. Use your SSN and "your name" as the business name. This doubles your available signup bonuses without touching your personal card velocity.

## Key Considerations

**The annual fee math is only real if you use the credits.** A CSR that never uses the $300 travel credit is effectively a $550 card. A Platinum that doesn't use monthly Uber Cash is paying $695 for 5x on airfare and lounges. Map each credit to a habit before applying.

**Chase's 5/24 rule:** Chase will not approve most of its cards if you've opened 5+ credit cards (any issuer) in the past 24 months. If you're building a stack, prioritize Chase cards first — get CSP or CSR before opening Amex, Capital One, or any store cards. Violating this order locks you out of the best cards.

**Signup bonuses are the biggest lever.** The signup bonus on a Sapphire Preferred is typically worth $750–$1,500+ in travel. The year-one return on the card dwarfs the annual earn rate. This is also why the game involves getting cards strategically — not churning, but sequencing.

**Don't open multiple cards at once.** Opening 3 cards in one month signals credit risk behavior and tanks your approval odds. Space applications 3–6 months apart.

**Your credit score.** Premium cards require good to excellent credit (700+, ideally 740+). If your score is below 700: fix it first (pay down utilization to below 10%, dispute errors, let history age), then apply. The rewards math only works if you get approved.

**Authorized users.** Adding a partner as an authorized user on a Priority Pass card gives them lounge access too. On the CSR, each additional authorized user is $75/year — worth it if they travel with you. On Amex Platinum, additional authorized users at lower-tier Platinum get their own Priority Pass card.

## Getting Started

**If you have nothing:**
1. Check credit score (free at creditkarma.com or your bank app)
2. If 700+: Apply for Chase Sapphire Preferred immediately
3. Also apply for Chase Freedom Unlimited (same day is fine — same bank, treated differently)
4. Use CSP for dining and travel; CFU for everything else; pay in full monthly

**If you have a Sapphire Preferred and want to evaluate the Reserve:**
- Count how many flights you take per year and whether you'd use lounges
- If 3+ flights and lounge use: upgrade or product-change to CSR
- If 1–2 flights: keep Preferred, add BCP for grocery

**Immediate action items for Romi:**
1. **Global Entry** — covered by CSR or Venture X. If you don't have PreCheck yet, this alone justifies opening one of these cards.
2. **Ink Business Preferred** — if any freelance/1099 income exists. 100,000 UR signup bonus is the best offer in the ecosystem.
3. **Amex BCP** — if grocery spend is $400+/month. 6% on groceries is high-value with no complexity.

## Common Mistakes

**Paying the minimum or carrying a balance.** A 24% APR erases 2 years of rewards instantly. Points are irrelevant if you're paying interest. This is an absolute prerequisite.

**Getting a premium card and not using the credits.** The credits are what make the math work. Set calendar reminders for monthly credits (Amex Platinum Uber Cash, streaming credits). Missing them is equivalent to paying the fee for nothing.

**Opening Chase cards after non-Chase cards.** The 5/24 rule means if you already have 5+ cards from other issuers, Chase's best cards are inaccessible. Prioritize Chase first.

**Redeeming for cash back or gift cards.** Points redeemed this way are worth 1 cent each. Transfer to Hyatt or airline partners typically gets 1.5–2.5 cents. Never redeem for statement credit on a premium rewards card.

**Applying for multiple cards in a short window.** Damages approval odds and score. Space applications minimum 3 months apart, ideally 6.

**Product-changing instead of canceling to avoid the fee.** If a card's fee is no longer worth it, downgrade it (product-change to the no-fee version) rather than canceling — preserves credit history length and the credit limit.
