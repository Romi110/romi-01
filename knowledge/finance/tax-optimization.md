# Tax Optimization
*Using the tax code as designed — accounts, timing, and structure that legally minimize what you owe over a lifetime*

---

## What Is It

Tax optimization is the practice of structuring your finances to minimize taxes legally, using mechanisms the tax code explicitly provides. It's not evasion — it's using the tools Congress created (retirement accounts, deductions, loss harvesting) as intended.

The basic principle: taxes are a cost, and every dollar of tax you pay unnecessarily is a dollar that stops compounding. Over 35 years, a dollar optimized out of taxes and left invested is worth $7–10 at retirement. The boring structural decisions — which accounts to use, in what order, with what assets — compound into enormous differences.

For account-type basics (what a 401k and Roth IRA are), see [finance/index-fund-investing.md](index-fund-investing.md). This file goes deeper on the optimization layer: how to use them in combination, when to choose which, and what else matters.

## Why It Matters

At Romi's income level in tech in NJ, taxes are likely the largest single expense — larger than rent, food, or any other category. Federal income tax, NJ state income tax (~5–10%), FICA (7.65%), and capital gains taxes together can take 35–45% of income without optimization. The gap between a person who ignores this and one who doesn't, over 30 years, is typically hundreds of thousands of dollars.

This is squarely in the Stoic control bucket: tax rules are set externally, but how you use them is yours.

## Core Concepts

### The Three Tax Buckets

All financial accounts fall into one of three tax treatments:

**Pre-tax (traditional)**: Contributions reduce taxable income now. Invested money grows tax-deferred. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income.
- Examples: Traditional 401(k), Traditional IRA, 403(b), SEP-IRA
- Best when: your current tax rate is higher than your expected retirement tax rate

**After-tax (Roth)**: Contributions made with money already taxed. Invested money grows tax-free. Qualified withdrawals are completely tax-free.
- Examples: Roth 401(k), Roth IRA
- Best when: your current tax rate is lower than your expected retirement tax rate — or when you expect tax rates broadly to rise

**Taxable brokerage**: No tax advantage on contributions. Dividends and realized gains are taxed in the year they occur. Long-term capital gains (assets held >1 year) taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income).
- Best for: savings beyond tax-advantaged limits, money you might need before retirement age

**The central question**: what will your tax rate be in retirement relative to now? At 28 in tech, you're likely in a moderate-to-high bracket now, but your retirement income could also be substantial. In genuine uncertainty, the conventional wisdom is: fill Roth accounts first when income is lower, shift toward traditional as income rises.

### 401(k) Strategy

**Always capture the full match first.** Employer match is the only guaranteed 100% return. If your employer matches 50% on the first 6% of salary, contribute at least 6% before anything else. No investment competes with this.

**Traditional vs. Roth 401(k)**: Many plans offer both. The choice is a bet on future vs. current tax rates. Roth 401(k) has no income limit (unlike Roth IRA), so high earners can access Roth treatment through the 401(k).

**2025 limits**: $23,500 employee contribution limit; $70,000 total (including employer contributions) for those using the mega backdoor Roth (see below).

**Investment options**: Most 401(k) plans offer limited funds, many with high expense ratios. Identify the lowest-cost index funds available (usually an S&P 500 or total market fund). Use those. Ignore the rest.

**Vesting schedules**: Employer match often vests over 3–6 years. If you leave before fully vested, you forfeit unvested match. Factor this into any job-change decision.

**After leaving a job**: Roll the old 401(k) into an IRA (or the new employer's 401(k)). Don't cash out — it triggers income taxes plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty.

### Roth IRA Strategy

The best long-term account for most people under 40 in moderate income brackets:
- All growth is tax-free forever
- No required minimum distributions (unlike traditional accounts)
- Contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn anytime penalty-free — acts as a backstop emergency fund in extreme cases
- 2025 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+)

**Income phase-out**: Roth IRA contributions phase out at $150,000–$165,000 MAGI (single), $236,000–$246,000 (married filing jointly) in 2025. Above these limits, direct Roth IRA contributions are not allowed.

**Backdoor Roth**: When income exceeds the Roth IRA limit:
1. Contribute $7,000 to a Traditional IRA (non-deductible — no tax deduction, but no income limit)
2. Immediately convert it to Roth IRA
3. Pay taxes on any gains (minimal if converted quickly)

This is legal, widely used, and explicitly described in IRS publications. One complication: the pro-rata rule. If you have pre-tax money in any Traditional IRA, the conversion is partially taxable in proportion to your total IRA balances. If you have no pre-tax IRA money, backdoor Roth is clean.

**Mega backdoor Roth**: Available only in plans that allow after-tax contributions and in-service distributions. Allows contributing an additional ~$46,500 (2025) in after-tax 401(k) contributions, then converting them to Roth. Not all plans support this — check your plan documents.

### HSA — The Hidden Retirement Account

The Health Savings Account is the only triple tax-advantaged account in the tax code:
1. Contributions are pre-tax (reduce FICA taxes too, if through payroll)
2. Growth is tax-free
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

Requires enrollment in a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). 2025 contribution limits: $4,300 (individual coverage), $8,550 (family coverage).

**The optimal HSA strategy**: Don't use it for current medical expenses. Pay those out of pocket if possible. Invest the HSA balance in low-cost index funds (most custodians now offer this). Let it grow tax-free for 30+ years. After age 65, withdrawals for any purpose are allowed (taxed as ordinary income — same as a Traditional IRA). Withdrawals for medical expenses remain tax-free at any age.

Save your medical receipts. There's no time limit on reimbursement — you can pay a medical bill today, keep the receipt, and reimburse yourself from the HSA 20 years later (after it has grown). This converts the HSA into a flexible tax-free fund.

**Custodian matters**: Many employer HSAs have limited, high-fee investment options. You can often transfer the HSA to a better custodian (Fidelity HSA is widely considered best — no fees, full investment access) while keeping payroll deduction tax benefits with the employer.

### Tax-Loss Harvesting

Selling an investment at a loss to generate a tax deduction, then immediately buying a similar investment to maintain market exposure.

**How it works**:
- You bought VTI at $220, it's now at $190. You sell — realizing a $30/share capital loss.
- You immediately buy ITOT (iShares total market ETF) — essentially the same exposure, different fund.
- The $30/share loss offsets capital gains elsewhere in your portfolio, or up to $3,000/year of ordinary income.
- Unused losses carry forward indefinitely.

**The wash sale rule**: You cannot buy the "same or substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale. VTI and ITOT are not considered identical (different indexes). VTI and VTSAX are considered identical (same index, different structure) — harvesting one and buying the other would be a wash sale.

**When it matters**: Tax-loss harvesting only applies to taxable brokerage accounts. Losses in tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA) have no tax benefit. The value of harvesting depends on your tax rate — the higher your marginal rate, the more valuable the deduction.

**Don't harvest small losses for complexity.** The administrative overhead and wash sale tracking cost time. Focus on significant positions in significant drawdowns.

### Asset Location — What Goes Where

Different investments produce different types of taxable events. Matching the investment type to the account type minimizes taxes.

**In tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA, HSA)**:
- Bonds and bond funds (interest is taxed as ordinary income — shelter this)
- REITs (high dividends taxed as ordinary income)
- High-dividend stocks
- Actively managed funds with high turnover (frequent capital gains distributions)

**In taxable accounts**:
- Total market index funds (low turnover, mostly qualified dividends, long-term gains)
- Tax-managed funds specifically designed for taxable accounts
- Municipal bonds (interest already tax-exempt, no benefit to sheltering)

**Simple rule**: bonds in tax-advantaged, stocks in taxable. For most people with most of their savings in 401k/IRA, this distinction matters most as wealth grows into the taxable tier.

### NJ-Specific Considerations

New Jersey has its own income tax (1.4%–10.75% graduated) that largely mirrors federal treatment of retirement accounts but with differences:

- **NJ does not allow a state tax deduction** for Traditional IRA contributions (federal allows deduction up to $7,000 for eligible taxpayers). Track your NJ basis — you won't owe NJ tax on those contributions at withdrawal because you already paid it.
- **Roth IRA withdrawals**: NJ taxes Roth conversions in the year of conversion if the original contribution was deductible. Since NJ doesn't allow the IRA deduction, NJ basis tracking is essential to avoid double taxation.
- **Social Security**: NJ does not tax Social Security benefits (unlike the federal government and some states). This is a meaningful advantage at retirement.
- **NJ property tax deduction**: up to $15,000 deduction for property taxes paid, or 18% property tax credit — applies when owning a home.

### The Order of Operations

1. 401(k) up to full employer match (100% instant return)
2. Max HSA if on HDHP ($4,300–$8,550)
3. Max Roth IRA ($7,000) — or backdoor Roth if over income limit
4. Max 401(k) remaining contribution ($23,500 total)
5. Taxable brokerage with any remaining savings

This order maximizes tax-sheltered space before putting money in taxable accounts.

## Key Considerations

**Tax rates will change.** The current tax code is not permanent. Tax-advantaged accounts protect you regardless of rate changes — Roth protects against higher future rates, Traditional protects against lower future rates. Diversifying across tax buckets (some Roth, some traditional) hedges this uncertainty.

**Don't let the tax tail wag the investment dog.** Tax optimization is a second-order concern. Getting the money invested in low-cost index funds is first. Optimizing account type is second. Don't avoid investing because you haven't figured out the optimal account yet.

**Required minimum distributions (RMDs)**: Traditional 401k and IRA accounts require minimum withdrawals starting at age 73. This forces taxable income in retirement even if you don't need the money. Heavy traditional account balances can create an RMD problem. Roth accounts have no RMDs.

**Roth conversion ladders**: In early retirement (before 59½), you can convert traditional IRA money to Roth each year, pay tax at low rates (if income is low), and access the converted money tax-free after 5 years. A useful strategy for early retirement scenarios.

## Getting Started

1. **Confirm you're getting the full employer 401(k) match.** Check your HR portal.
2. **Open a Roth IRA** at Fidelity if you don't have one. Takes 15 minutes. Fund it with $7,000 for 2025.
3. **Check if your employer offers HSA access** and whether your health plan qualifies as HDHP. If yes, enroll.
4. **Identify the lowest-cost funds** in your 401(k) and redirect contributions there.
5. **Track your NJ IRA basis** if making non-deductible Traditional IRA contributions (backdoor Roth).

## Common Mistakes

**Ignoring the 401(k) match.** Equivalent to leaving salary on the table.

**Cashing out old 401(k)s when changing jobs.** Income taxes plus 10% penalty can cost 30–40% of the balance. Roll it over instead.

**Holding high-income bonds in taxable accounts.** Interest taxed as ordinary income — shelter bonds in tax-advantaged space.

**Conflating Roth with "better."** Roth is better when you're in a lower bracket now than you will be later. If you're already in the 32%+ bracket, traditional may be the right call.

**Not tracking NJ IRA basis.** Double taxation risk at withdrawal if NJ non-deductible contributions aren't documented.

**Letting HSA sit in cash.** The triple tax advantage is only realized if the money is invested. Most HSA custodians default to a cash account.
