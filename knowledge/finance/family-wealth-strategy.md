# Family Wealth Strategy
*How all the financial pieces — tax-advantaged accounts, business equity, real estate, insurance, and trusts — layer together and compound over 20–30 years.*

---

## What Is It

A family wealth strategy is an integrated plan that coordinates every financial vehicle available to you into a coherent system. Not individual tactics — a stack. Each layer builds on the previous one: you don't invest in real estate before you have an emergency fund; you don't optimize trust structure before you have assets worth protecting; you don't run an S-corp without a business generating real profit.

The individual pieces exist already in this wiki: [tax-optimization](tax-optimization.md), [trusts](trusts.md), [estate-planning-basics](estate-planning-basics.md), [life-insurance](life-insurance.md), [index-fund-investing](index-fund-investing.md), [investing-beyond-index-funds](investing-beyond-index-funds.md). This file is the map that shows how they connect, sequence, and compound across decades.

The core insight: **wealth compounds when multiple pillars grow simultaneously**. A person who only invests in index funds misses real estate leverage and business equity. A business owner who reinvests everything misses tax-advantaged accounts. The integrated stack grows faster than any single vehicle because the advantages compound on each other — tax savings in one account fund contributions to another; real estate equity funds business expansion; business profits fund the trust that protects everything.

---

## Why It Matters

At 28, with a full-time tech income and business ownership, you're in the highest-leverage decade of wealth building. The compounding math is brutally clear: $100k invested at 28 at 8% real returns is worth $1M by age 58. The same $100k invested at 38 is worth $466k. Every year of delay costs roughly half the terminal value.

The goal of this file: ensure that every dollar is in its highest-leverage vehicle, that the pieces are sequenced correctly, and that you're not leaving systematic money on the table — which most people do, because they optimize one piece without seeing the whole board.

## The Wealth Stack

Think of this as layers. Each layer should be fully funded before aggressively building the next. Within layers, order matters.

### Layer 1 — Foundation (Non-Negotiable)

Before any investment or wealth strategy:

1. **Emergency fund**: 3–6 months of essential expenses in HYSA. Not invested. Not in the market. This is the buffer that prevents you from liquidating investments at bad times.
2. **No high-interest debt**: Anything above ~6% interest is a guaranteed negative return. Pay this off before investing beyond your employer match.
3. **Basic insurance in place**: Health, disability, auto/home. Disability is the most underbought — your income is the engine of all wealth building. A 40% income loss for 2 years sets you back further than any market crash.

### Layer 2 — Tax-Advantaged Accounts (Max These First, Every Year)

These accounts provide guaranteed returns in the form of tax savings. Nothing in investing is more certain than avoiding taxes.

**Order of priority:**

**Employer 401k to the match** — If your employer matches contributions, capture 100% of it before anything else. It's an immediate 50–100% return.

**HSA** (if eligible — high-deductible health plan required) — The only triple-tax-advantaged account: contributions pre-tax, growth tax-free, withdrawals tax-free for medical expenses. After age 65, withdrawals for anything (taxed like traditional IRA). Max in 2025: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family. Invest the balance — don't spend it unless you need to. This becomes a stealth retirement account.

**Roth IRA / Backdoor Roth** — $7,000/year (2025). Tax-free growth forever. No RMDs. At your income, you may be above the direct contribution limit (~$161k single / $240k married). Use the backdoor Roth: contribute to a traditional IRA (no income limit), immediately convert to Roth. See [tax-optimization](tax-optimization.md) for mechanics.

**Max 401k** — $23,500/year (2025, employee contribution). If your business has a solo 401k, you can contribute as both employer and employee — total up to $70,000/year. This is the biggest single-year tax deduction available to most business owners.

**SEP-IRA or Solo 401k (business)** — As a business owner, this is where the real leverage is. Solo 401k lets you contribute 100% of salary up to $23,500 as employee, plus 25% of net self-employment income as employer, up to $70,000 total. SEP-IRA allows 25% of net SE income, up to $70,000. For a business generating $200k+ in net income, these accounts can shelter $40-70k per year from taxes.

**At maximum funding, you're sheltering:**
- $23,500 (401k employee) + $46,500 (solo 401k employer) = $70,000 in a solo 401k
- $7,000 (backdoor Roth IRA)
- $8,550 (HSA)
- **Total: ~$85,550/year in tax-advantaged space**

On a combined household income of $300k with business income, the tax savings on $85k of contributions at a 32% marginal rate is ~$27k/year. Every year. This is Layer 2 doing its job.

### Layer 3 — Real Estate

Real estate adds three things that financial accounts don't: leverage, cash flow, and tax treatment unavailable to paper assets.

**Why real estate in the stack**: A $500k property purchased with $100k down (5:1 leverage) appreciates on the full $500k. At 3% annual appreciation, that's $15k/year — a 15% return on the $100k invested, before rental income. You also get depreciation deductions that reduce taxable income despite the property appreciating in value.

**Primary residence first**: The primary home is the anchor. NJ homeownership gives you:
- Up to $500k (married) / $250k (single) of capital gains excluded at sale
- Property tax deduction (limited post-TCJA, but exists)
- Equity that builds with each payment

**Investment property next**: After establishing primary residence, a rental property creates passive income and the full depreciation benefit. A $600k rental property can be depreciated over 27.5 years (~$21k/year deduction) while appreciating and generating rent. Cost segregation studies can front-load depreciation further.

**The step-up in basis**: This is the generational wealth rule. When you die, the cost basis of appreciated assets resets to fair market value. All capital gains accumulated during your lifetime are forgiven. Real estate held until death passes to heirs with no embedded capital gains tax. See [building-generational-wealth](building-generational-wealth.md).

### Layer 4 — Business Equity

If you own a business, it's potentially your highest-return asset — and the most overlooked in wealth planning.

A business generating $300k net income annually, valued at 3-5x earnings, represents $900k–$1.5M in equity. That equity grows as the business grows, without requiring capital contributions. Unlike a stock portfolio, you have operational control over the return.

**Business equity in the wealth stack:**
- Reinvest profits selectively — not every dollar back into the business when accounts and real estate deserve funding first
- Extract tax-efficiently via S-corp election and owner compensation strategy (see [owner-compensation-strategy](../business/owner-compensation-strategy.md))
- Build to a position where the business is sellable — documented systems, not owner-dependent — even if you don't plan to sell
- Protect via LLC structure and liability insurance; hold business interest in your trust

**Exit as a wealth event**: A business sale at $1.5M after 10-15 years of growth is a significant one-time wealth event. QSB (Qualified Small Business Stock) exclusion under Section 1202 can eliminate federal capital gains on up to $10M of gain from the sale of qualified C-corp stock held 5+ years. Worth structuring for if you anticipate a sale.

### Layer 5 — Insurance as Infrastructure

Insurance protects the stack from catastrophic failure. This is not wealth-building — it's wealth-protecting.

**Disability insurance** — Protects your income (the engine). Get own-occupation disability coverage at 60-70% of income. This is the most important insurance most people don't have. See [insurance-basics](insurance-basics.md).

**Term life insurance** — Protects dependents from loss of your income during the wealth-building years. 20-30 year term at 10-12x annual income. If you have a partner who depends on your income, this is non-negotiable.

**Key man insurance** — If your business depends on you, your business partner or lender may require it. Pays out to the business at your death. Funds a buy-sell agreement or provides operating runway.

**Umbrella liability** — $1-2M umbrella policy over your auto/home. Cheap ($200-400/year). Protects the asset stack from a lawsuit that exceeds underlying policy limits.

### Layer 6 — Trust and Estate Structure

This layer protects and efficiently transfers what you've built.

**Revocable living trust** — Fund your assets into it now. Avoids probate, enables private, efficient transfer at death. See [trusts](trusts.md).

**ILIT** — If you're buying significant life insurance, hold it in an ILIT to keep the death benefit out of your taxable estate. Do this before buying the policy.

**Beneficiary designations** — Keep them current. These override everything else. Reviewed every 3-5 years and at every life event.

**TCJA sunset planning** — The federal estate/gift exemption drops from ~$14M to ~$7M/person after 2025 (absent Congressional action). High-income earners with significant business equity or real estate portfolios should discuss irrevocable transfers with an estate attorney before end of 2025.

## The 30-Year Picture

This is not a prediction — it's an illustration of how the stack compounds if you execute consistently.

**Ages 28–35: Foundation and Launch**
- Emergency fund built, no consumer debt
- Max Roth IRA every year ($7k/yr for 7 years = $49k contributions + growth)
- Max 401k/solo 401k contributions begin (~$23-70k/yr)
- Primary home purchased, equity begins building
- Business generating real profit; S-corp election active
- Term life + disability insurance in place
- RLT established, beneficiaries set

**Ages 35–45: Compounding Kicks In**
- Retirement accounts: 7-10 years of max contributions, compounding hard
- Investment property acquired; cash flow + depreciation + appreciation running
- Business equity substantial; valued at $1-3M
- Real estate + market portfolio + business = three distinct wealth engines
- Trust structure optimized; ILIT in place if life insurance is significant
- Net worth: $1-3M range depending on income and choices

**Ages 45–55: Acceleration**
- Retirement accounts may exceed $2M from 20+ years of max contributions + compounding
- Real estate portfolio: 1-3 properties generating passive cash flow
- Business: peak equity value; possible strategic sale event
- If business sold: reinvest proceeds; consider qualified opportunity zones for deferral
- Estate planning active: annual gifting to heirs, potentially moving assets into irrevocable structures
- Net worth: $3-8M range

**Ages 55–65: Optimization and Transfer**
- Roth conversion ladder: convert traditional to Roth in lower-income years, filling brackets
- Real estate portfolio: hold (step-up in basis at death), sell (1031 exchange), or pass to trust
- Business succession plan executing
- Annual gifting to children/heirs using gift exclusions ($18k/person/year, indexed)
- Net worth: $5-15M+ depending on business outcomes and real estate

## Key Considerations

**Sequence matters more than optimization**: Most people optimize within one layer while ignoring others. A person who picks perfect individual stocks but skips their employer's 401k match is losing guaranteed money to chase uncertain returns. Fill layers in order.

**Tax bracket management is ongoing**: The goal isn't to minimize taxes this year — it's to minimize taxes over your lifetime. This means: contribute to traditional 401k when in high brackets (defer income), contribute to Roth when in lower brackets (pay now, never again), take capital gains in lower-income years, do Roth conversions between retirement and Social Security.

**Asset location**: Different assets belong in different account types. Tax-inefficient assets (bonds, REITs, high-dividend stocks) belong in tax-deferred accounts. Tax-efficient assets (index funds, growth stocks you hold long-term) belong in taxable accounts. Real estate goes in individual name (for depreciation). Business interest goes in trust.

**Liquidity ladder**: Not all wealth is accessible. Retirement accounts have penalties before 59½ (with exceptions). Real estate is illiquid. Business equity is paper until sold. Maintain a liquidity ladder: cash (emergency fund) → taxable brokerage → retirement accounts. Know what you can access without penalty at each stage.

**Diversification across type, not just asset class**: True diversification includes: earned income, business equity (operational), real estate (hard asset), financial investments (liquid), and insurance (protection). Most "diversification" advice only covers the financial investments layer.

## Getting Started

**This month:**
1. Verify you're capturing 100% of employer match (free money)
2. If eligible: open HSA, contribute and invest the balance
3. Backdoor Roth if above income threshold — $7k/year is small but the compounding is permanent
4. Review owner compensation structure if you have a business (is S-corp election in place?)

**This year:**
1. Max 401k/solo 401k
2. Get disability insurance in place if you don't have it
3. Establish RLT and fund it
4. Have an estate attorney review your structure — specifically ask about TCJA sunset timing

**3-year horizon:**
1. Primary home acquired and stable
2. Investment property on the radar
3. Business generating $100k+ net annually — confirm S-corp election and solo 401k are active
4. Net worth tracking: annual snapshot, see all layers

## Common Mistakes

**Treating accounts as separate buckets instead of a system**: Your 401k, your Roth, your taxable brokerage, your real estate, your business — these are one portfolio. Asset allocation, tax location, and liquidity decisions should be made across the whole thing.

**Skipping disability insurance to save premium**: Your ability to earn income is your largest financial asset. Insuring it costs less than insuring your car. This is the most underbuilt layer.

**Letting perfect be the enemy of done**: The ideal trust structure, the perfectly optimized S-corp, the ideal real estate market — none of this matters if you delay for years. A 90% solution in place now beats a 100% solution at 40.

**Not reviewing the stack after life events**: Marriage, kids, business growth, property purchase, income jumps — each of these changes the optimal allocation across the stack. Schedule an annual financial review.
