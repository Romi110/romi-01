# Trusts
*Legal structures that hold assets on behalf of beneficiaries — used for probate avoidance, asset protection, tax reduction, and controlled wealth transfer.*

---

## What Is It

A trust is a legal arrangement where one party (the grantor) transfers assets to a trustee, who holds and manages those assets for the benefit of named beneficiaries according to rules written in the trust document. Unlike a will, a trust is a living structure — it exists during your lifetime and continues operating after your death, without court supervision.

Trusts serve three distinct purposes, and different trust types prioritize different ones:
1. **Probate avoidance** — passing assets directly to heirs without court process
2. **Asset protection** — shielding assets from creditors, lawsuits, or predatory claims
3. **Tax efficiency** — reducing estate tax, gift tax, or income tax

The most important thing to understand: **revocable trusts give you control but no protection; irrevocable trusts give you protection but require giving up control.** Every trust sits somewhere on that spectrum.

---

## Why It Matters

At 28 with a business, a partner, growing assets, and a long time horizon, trusts become relevant sooner than most people think — not for complexity's sake, but because the right structure set up early compounds in value over time.

Specific reasons for Romi's situation:
- **Probate is public**: A will goes through probate court — your asset list becomes public record. A trust doesn't.
- **Partner protection**: NJ doesn't give an unmarried partner automatic inheritance rights. A trust (or beneficiary designation) ensures assets pass correctly without the delay and cost of probate.
- **Business ownership**: If you own a business, it needs a succession plan. Who gets it? How is it valued? A trust or buy-sell agreement answers this.
- **TCJA sunset**: The federal estate/gift tax exemption is scheduled to drop from ~$14M to ~$7M per person after 2025. High-income earners who might accumulate significant wealth have a closing window to move assets into irrevocable structures at the higher exemption.
- **NJ inheritance tax**: NJ taxes inheritances to non-spouse, non-children beneficiaries at 11-16%. Proper trust structure can minimize this for complex family situations.

## Core Concepts

### Revocable Living Trust — The Starting Point

A revocable living trust (RLT) is the most common trust and the most practical starting point. You create it, fund it with your assets, and retain full control. You can change it, revoke it, or take assets back anytime during your lifetime.

**What it does:**
- Avoids probate — assets in the trust pass directly to beneficiaries without court
- Maintains privacy — unlike a will, a trust isn't a public court record
- Provides seamless management if you become incapacitated (your successor trustee steps in)
- Coordinates with your will via a "pour-over" provision (anything not in the trust at death pours into it)

**What it doesn't do:**
- Protect assets from creditors — you control it, so it's reachable
- Reduce estate or income taxes — assets are still "yours" in the IRS's view
- Replace a will — you still need one; the trust handles funded assets, the will handles anything that missed the trust

**Funding is critical**: An unfunded trust is useless. You must retitle assets into the trust's name: bank accounts, brokerage accounts, real estate. Retirement accounts (401k, IRA) don't go into the trust — they stay with beneficiary designations. Life insurance doesn't either, unless you set up an ILIT (see below).

**Cost**: $1,500–$3,000 with an estate attorney in NJ for a complete package (RLT, pour-over will, healthcare proxy, DPOA). More than a basic will alone, but substantially more useful.

**Who this is for**: Everyone with meaningful assets or a partner. Set it up once, fund it, update it at life milestones.

### Irrevocable Trust — Control for Protection and Tax

Once you transfer assets into an irrevocable trust, you give up control. You can't take them back. In exchange, those assets:
- Leave your taxable estate (reducing estate tax exposure)
- Become protected from your personal creditors and lawsuits
- May have different income tax treatment depending on structure

The tradeoff is real: irrevocable means irrevocable. Don't put in assets you might need. This is for assets you're confident you won't need personally — excess wealth, a business interest, a life insurance policy.

**When it makes sense**: You've accumulated assets beyond what you'll need personally, you want to protect a business from personal liability, or you're doing intentional estate planning for heirs.

### ILIT — Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust

An ILIT is an irrevocable trust that owns a life insurance policy on your life. The purpose: keep the death benefit out of your estate.

Life insurance death benefits are income-tax-free but estate-tax-included if you own the policy. If your total estate is large (or will be), this matters. The ILIT owns the policy; you give cash gifts to the trust (using your annual gift tax exclusion — $18,000 per beneficiary in 2025) to pay the premiums; the trustee buys and holds the policy; at death, proceeds go to beneficiaries outside your estate.

**NJ-specific**: NJ has no estate tax, but the federal estate tax applies if your estate exceeds the exemption. At the current ~$14M exemption, this isn't immediately relevant for most 28-year-olds. But if the TCJA sunsets (exemption drops to ~$7M) and you're accumulating aggressively, the math changes.

**Practical for Romi now**: If you're buying a significant life insurance policy (term or whole life) primarily for family protection, talk to an estate attorney about whether ILIT structure makes sense before you buy — because you can't transfer an existing policy into an ILIT without a three-year lookback rule applying.

### Asset Protection Trust (APT)

A domestic asset protection trust (DAPT) lets you create an irrevocable trust in a favorable state (Nevada, South Dakota, Delaware) that protects assets from creditors while still potentially allowing you some discretionary access as a beneficiary. This is the "have your cake and eat it too" structure — in theory.

**NJ doesn't have a DAPT statute** — you'd create the trust in Nevada or South Dakota. Courts in other states sometimes challenge these, especially if the grantor is a beneficiary. The protection is real but has been litigated.

**More relevant for Romi**: An LLC properly structured for your business provides asset protection between the business and your personal assets — which is the practical equivalent and far simpler.

**Offshore APT**: Trusts in the Cayman Islands, Cook Islands, or Channel Islands. Maximum creditor protection, much harder to pierce. Relevant only for very high-net-worth individuals with significant lawsuit exposure (surgeons, developers, entrepreneurs). Complex, expensive, and requires ongoing compliance.

### Bypass Trust / Credit Shelter Trust

A married couple each has a federal estate tax exemption (~$14M per person in 2025, ~$7M if TCJA sunsets). Without planning, an estate passed entirely to a surviving spouse can "waste" the first spouse's exemption.

A bypass trust (also called credit shelter trust, family trust, or B trust) holds assets up to the first spouse's exemption at death, with the surviving spouse able to receive income and limited access. The remainder passes to heirs at the surviving spouse's death without being taxed in the survivor's estate — effectively using both exemptions.

With current high exemptions this matters less. If TCJA sunsets, married couples will care about this again.

### GRAT — Grantor Retained Annuity Trust

A freeze technique for transferring appreciating assets to heirs with minimal gift tax. You put assets into the trust, receive annuity payments back for a set term, and whatever remains at the end (the "remainder interest") passes to heirs. If assets grow faster than the IRS's assumed rate (the "7520 rate"), the excess passes transfer-tax-free.

GRATs work best when: (1) interest rates are low (lower IRS hurdle), (2) you put in assets that will significantly appreciate (stock in a private company, pre-IPO equity, a growing investment portfolio).

A "zeroed-out GRAT" is structured so the annuity payments equal the initial value plus the 7520 rate — meaning the taxable gift is zero. All upside above the IRS rate goes to heirs tax-free.

**Relevant for business owners** who expect their business to grow significantly. You freeze the estate value today; future appreciation passes without estate tax.

### Spendthrift Trust

A trust with provisions preventing a beneficiary from assigning their interest, and protecting the assets from the beneficiary's creditors until distributed. Used when you're leaving assets to someone who can't manage money or has creditor issues.

Not immediately relevant for Romi but worth knowing if you're planning for children or concerned family members.

### Business Succession and Trusts

If you own a business, what happens to it when you die? Options:
1. It passes through your estate (probate, delays, valuation disputes)
2. Your trust owns the business interest — passes directly to named successors
3. A **buy-sell agreement** funded by life insurance lets partners buy out your share at death (common in partnerships/LLCs with multiple owners)

For a sole owner: the trust holds your business interest. Your trust document names who gets control and when. If you want a child or spouse to inherit but not run it, you can separate economic interest from management control.

## Key Considerations

**TCJA sunset timing**: The federal estate/gift exemption was $5M (inflation-adjusted) pre-2017, doubled to ~$14M by TCJA, and is scheduled to revert after December 31, 2025 unless Congress acts. If you're in your late 20s-30s with strong income trajectory and business ownership, the window to make large irrevocable transfers at the high exemption is now. This is a legitimate planning reason to act rather than wait.

**NJ inheritance tax matters for unmarried partners**: If you have a long-term partner and significant assets, and you die without proper structure, they face a 15-16% NJ inheritance tax on anything they inherit (except what passes via beneficiary designation or joint ownership). A trust doesn't eliminate this but careful beneficiary designation + trust structure can route assets around the tax for most accounts.

**Trustee selection**: The trustee has fiduciary responsibility and real authority. For a revocable living trust, you're your own trustee. For irrevocable trusts, you need someone else — a trusted individual, a corporate trustee (bank trust departments), or both (individual + corporate co-trustee for checks and balances). Corporate trustees charge fees (~0.5-1% annually) but provide continuity and professional management.

**Annual trust maintenance**: Trusts need to be reviewed every 3-5 years and after: marriage, divorce, birth of children, death of a trustee or beneficiary, major asset changes, tax law changes. The TCJA sunset is exactly the kind of event that triggers a review.

**Trusts and retirement accounts**: IRAs and 401ks should generally NOT be held in a trust during your lifetime — the trust would be treated as distributing the account, triggering taxes. Instead, name individuals as beneficiaries directly (or the trust as beneficiary only under specific circumstances after consulting a tax attorney).

**NJ probate isn't terrible**: Unlike California (which is notoriously slow and expensive), NJ probate is relatively streamlined. An RLT still makes sense for privacy and incapacity planning, but the probate avoidance benefit alone isn't as compelling in NJ as it is in some other states.

## Getting Started

**Right now (no attorney needed):**
1. Check all retirement account and life insurance beneficiary designations. Update them. This is the highest-leverage estate move you can make in 30 minutes.
2. Read the estate-planning-basics file if you haven't — trusts layer on top of that foundation.

**Within the next 6 months:**
1. Meet with an NJ estate attorney — not a general practice attorney, someone who does estate work regularly. Budget $300-500 for an initial consultation.
2. Discuss whether a revocable living trust makes sense now (it usually does if you have meaningful assets or an unmarried partner).
3. Ask specifically about the TCJA sunset and what (if anything) you should be doing before end of 2025 given your income trajectory and business ownership.

**Questions to bring to the attorney:**
- Does an RLT make sense given our specific asset picture?
- How should my business interest be held (in the trust? in an LLC held by the trust?)
- Is ILIT structure worth setting up for a new life insurance policy?
- What does TCJA sunset mean for someone in my situation?

## Common Mistakes

**Setting up a trust but not funding it**: The unfunded trust is the single most common estate planning failure. People pay for an RLT and never retitle their assets. At death, everything still goes through probate. Fund the trust when you create it.

**Putting retirement accounts in the trust**: IRAs and 401ks held in a trust trigger immediate taxable distribution. These stay outside the trust with individual beneficiary designations.

**Treating revocable trusts as asset protection**: A revocable trust is transparent to creditors — you control it, they can reach it. If you're worried about lawsuit exposure (business owner, professional), this requires an irrevocable structure or LLC, not an RLT.

**Waiting until the estate is "big enough"**: The right time to set up an RLT is when you have a partner, meaningful assets, or a business — not when your estate crosses an arbitrary dollar threshold. The probate avoidance and incapacity protection are valuable regardless of estate size.

**DIY irrevocable trusts**: Revocable living trusts can reasonably be done with quality online tools (Trust & Will, Nolo). Irrevocable trusts involve gift tax returns, complex drafting, and irrevocable decisions — these require an attorney who specializes in this.
