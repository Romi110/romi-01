# Insurance Basics
*What you actually need, what's oversold, and how to think about coverage without getting taken*

---

## What Is It

Insurance is a financial tool for transferring catastrophic risk to someone else in exchange for a predictable premium. The word "insurance" gets applied to everything from health coverage to phone protection plans — but the logic for buying it is the same: pay a known cost to avoid an unknown cost that would be financially devastating.

The mistake most people make is buying insurance for small losses (things they could handle out of pocket) and underinsuring against large ones (disability, liability). The industry's incentive is to sell you more coverage than you need.

This file covers the five types that actually matter at Romi's stage of life.

## Why It Matters

Insurance done right protects the financial foundation you're building. A single uninsured event — a disability, a major lawsuit, a serious illness — can wipe out years of savings and investing. At 28 in tech in NJ, the gaps most likely to hurt are disability and liability, not the ones most people spend time optimizing.

---

## Core Concepts

### How Insurance Works

**Premium**: what you pay to maintain coverage (monthly or annually).

**Deductible**: what you pay out of pocket before insurance pays anything. Higher deductible = lower premium. Choose a deductible you can actually cover from savings.

**Copay / Coinsurance**: your share of costs after the deductible. A copay is a flat fee per visit. Coinsurance is a percentage (you pay 20%, insurance pays 80%).

**Out-of-pocket maximum**: the most you'll pay in a calendar year before insurance covers 100%. This number matters more than the deductible for evaluating health plans.

**Underwriting**: insurance companies price based on risk. Pre-existing conditions, age, location (NJ is expensive for auto and home), and claims history all affect premiums.

**The rule for buying insurance**: insure against losses that would materially harm your financial life. Self-insure (skip coverage or accept high deductibles) for losses you can absorb.

### Health Insurance

The most complex and highest-stakes type. In the US, employer-sponsored plans are usually the best option — the employer subsidy makes them cheaper than individual plans.

**Key decisions:**

**HDHP vs PPO**: High Deductible Health Plans have lower premiums but you pay more before coverage kicks in. If you're healthy and rarely use care, an HDHP paired with an HSA is usually the better financial choice — the premium savings plus HSA tax advantage beats the PPO math for most healthy people. PPOs make sense if you have ongoing prescriptions or specialists.

**HSA**: If you choose an HDHP, fully fund the HSA. It's the triple tax-advantaged account in [finance/tax-optimization.md](tax-optimization.md). A health savings account is also a stealth investment account.

**Out-of-pocket max over deductible**: when comparing plans, look at the out-of-pocket max first. A plan with a $3,000 deductible and $6,000 out-of-pocket max protects you from a $40,000 surgery. A plan with a $1,000 deductible but $9,000 OOP max doesn't protect you better — it just looks friendlier.

**NJ context**: NJ has its own state insurance marketplace with slightly different rules than federal. NJ also expanded Medicaid. If between jobs, check NJ GetCovered before going uninsured even for a month.

**The one non-negotiable**: never go without health insurance. A single ER visit can cost $10,000+. An uninsured hospitalization can exceed $100,000.

### Disability Insurance

The most underinsured and most important coverage for a working professional in their 20s–40s.

**The math**: at 28, you're more likely to become disabled for 90+ days than to die before retirement. A 28-year-old has roughly a 25% chance of being disabled for 90+ days before age 65. Yet most people have no disability coverage beyond a basic employer policy.

**Short-term disability**: typically covers 60–80% of income for 90 days to 6 months. Many employers offer this.

**Long-term disability (LTD)**: kicks in after short-term ends, can cover income until 65. This is the gap most people have.

**What to check:**
- Does your employer offer LTD? At what percentage of income?
- Is employer LTD "own occupation" or "any occupation"? Own occupation pays if you can't do *your* job. Any occupation only pays if you can't do *any* job — a much harder bar.
- Group LTD through employers is often taxable if the employer paid the premium. Individual policies can be structured to pay out tax-free.

**If your employer's LTD coverage is thin** (below 60% of income, "any occupation," or capped at a low dollar amount), supplemental private disability insurance is worth the cost. For a tech salary in NJ, this gap can be enormous.

**How much**: 60–70% of your pre-tax income is the target. Combined between employer and individual policies.

### Auto Insurance

Required in NJ. NJ is a no-fault state, which affects how claims work.

**What NJ requires**: Personal Injury Protection (PIP), liability coverage, and uninsured motorist coverage. NJ minimums are low — don't just carry minimums.

**The coverage worth paying for:**
- **Liability**: at least 100/300/100 ($100k per person, $300k per accident, $100k property). If you cause an accident and injure someone, the minimum limits leave you personally exposed. The cost difference between minimums and 100/300/100 is small.
- **Uninsured/underinsured motorist**: covers you when the other driver has no insurance or not enough. NJ has a significant uninsured driver population.
- **Comprehensive and collision**: worth carrying if the car has meaningful value. Not worth it on a car worth under $3,000–4,000 (the premium cost vs. payout math stops making sense).

**What to skip**: rental car reimbursement and roadside assistance are low-cost add-ons that are cheap to just pay out of pocket. They're priced as convenience, not risk transfer.

**Deductible**: $1,000 deductible is usually the sweet spot — low enough that you'd actually use it for a significant repair, high enough that you're not filing small claims (which raise your premium).

---

### Homeowners / Renters Insurance

**Renters insurance**: cheap ($15–30/month), covers your personal property and personal liability. If you're renting, you have no reason not to have it. The liability coverage alone is worth it — if someone slips in your apartment and sues, renters insurance covers defense costs and judgment.

**Homeowners insurance**: when you own, required by the mortgage lender. The basics are standard — dwelling coverage (the structure), personal property, liability, and additional living expenses if the home becomes uninhabitable.

**NJ-specific considerations:**
- Flood insurance is separate from homeowners and not covered by standard policies. NJ has significant flood risk — check FEMA flood maps for your specific address. The Highlands and coastal areas have different profiles. Many NJ homeowners discover this only after a flood.
- Sewer backup coverage is a common add-on worth having in NJ's older housing stock.

**Replacement cost vs. actual cash value**: replacement cost pays what it costs to rebuild/replace at current prices. Actual cash value pays the depreciated value. Always choose replacement cost — the premium difference is small, the payout difference is enormous after a loss.

**Umbrella insurance** (see below) typically requires underlying homeowners and auto policies at minimum liability levels. Get those right first.

---

### Umbrella Insurance

An umbrella policy provides liability coverage above and beyond your auto and homeowners limits. For $150–300/year, you get $1–2 million in additional liability coverage.

**Why it matters**: if you cause a serious car accident or someone is significantly injured at your property, a judgment can exceed your auto or homeowners liability limits. The excess judgment attaches to your personal assets — savings, investments, future wages. An umbrella policy covers that gap.

**Who needs it**: anyone with meaningful assets or income worth protecting. At a tech salary with growing investments, the cost-benefit is strongly in favor of buying it.

**What it covers**: liability from auto accidents, premises liability (someone hurt at your home), some personal liability (slander, defamation in some policies). Does not cover your own injuries or intentional acts.

**Typical requirement**: most umbrella insurers require underlying auto liability at 250/500 or 100/300 and homeowners liability at $300k. This is another reason to carry adequate underlying limits.

---

### Life Insurance

The least urgent of the five at Romi's current stage — no dependents, no kids.

**The simple rule**: you need life insurance if someone depends on your income. If no one does, term life insurance is low priority.

**When it becomes relevant**: if you have a partner who depends on your income, a mortgage you own together, or children.

**If/when you need it**: term life insurance (not whole life). A 20-year level term policy for 10–12x annual income is the standard recommendation. It's cheap in your 20s and 30s. Whole life, universal life, and variable life products are almost always worse financial instruments than buying term and investing the difference — the insurance industry earns much higher margins on them for a reason.

## Key Considerations

**The order of operations**: health → disability → auto (required) → renters/homeowners → umbrella → life (when relevant). This is roughly the sequence by which an uninsured event would most likely damage your financial life.

**High deductible = self-insuring the small stuff.** Only works if you have the savings to cover it. Your emergency fund is what makes high deductibles viable. See [finance/emergency-fund.md](emergency-fund.md).

**Annual review**: premiums change, life circumstances change. Check all policies annually — what you bought at 25 may not reflect what you need at 30.

**Shopping insurance**: auto and homeowners can be comparison-shopped every 2–3 years. Most people are paying more than they need to because they haven't shopped since they first bought.

**Buying through a broker vs direct**: for disability insurance especially, an independent broker who works with multiple carriers is worth using. They can compare policies and structures that online shopping doesn't surface.

## Getting Started

1. Confirm you have health coverage and understand your plan's OOP max.
2. Check your employer's LTD policy — percentage, definition of disability, benefit cap.
3. Pull your auto declarations page — are liability limits above state minimums? Uninsured motorist coverage on?
4. If renting: get renters insurance if you don't have it.
5. Once you have meaningful assets and income: add an umbrella policy ($150–300/year).

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing low premium with good value.** A low-premium plan with high OOP max or inadequate disability coverage is a bad deal that feels cheap until you need it.

**Skipping disability because you're young and healthy.** Disability happens most often to people who thought they were too young and healthy for it to matter.

**Carrying full coverage on a car worth $3,000.** The annual premium for collision on a low-value car often approaches 30–50% of the car's value. Do the math.

**Buying whole life as an investment.** It isn't. The returns are poor compared to term + index funds, and the fees are high. If an insurance salesperson is excited to sell you something, that's a signal to think slowly.

**Not having flood insurance in NJ.** Homeowners insurance does not cover floods. In NJ, this is a real risk, not a theoretical one.
