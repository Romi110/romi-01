# Investing Beyond Index Funds
*What comes after you've maxed the basics — alternative assets, inflation hedges, and when complexity is actually worth it*

---

## What Is It

Index fund investing in tax-advantaged accounts is the foundation. For most people, it's also the finish line — maximizing 401k, Roth IRA, and HSA is enough to build significant long-term wealth, and adding complexity beyond that often reduces returns through fees, taxes, and bad decisions.

This file is for when the foundation is solid: tax-advantaged accounts are maxed, an emergency fund exists, debt is managed. The question then is: what's actually worth adding, and what's just complexity dressed up as sophistication?

The answer is: very little. But that little is worth understanding.

For the foundation itself, see [finance/index-fund-investing.md](index-fund-investing.md) and [finance/tax-optimization.md](tax-optimization.md).

## Why It Matters

Two real reasons to look beyond index funds:

1. **Inflation protection**: stocks are a real asset but are priced in nominal dollars. In high-inflation environments, certain alternative assets (real estate, commodities, I-bonds) maintain purchasing power differently than equities.

2. **Diversification beyond stocks/bonds**: adding truly uncorrelated assets reduces portfolio volatility. The question is whether the correlation benefit outweighs the cost and complexity of the asset.

Everything else sold as a reason — higher returns, unique access, market inefficiency — is usually marketing.

## Core Concepts

### I-Bonds — The No-Brainer First Alternative

Series I savings bonds from the US Treasury. The interest rate adjusts with inflation (CPI) every 6 months. Currently the simplest, safest alternative to equities for a portion of savings.

**What makes them different:**
- Guaranteed to keep pace with inflation — the rate is CPI + fixed component
- Backed by the US government (no default risk)
- Interest is tax-deferred until redemption; exempt from state and local tax
- Not correlated with stock market

**The constraints:**
- $10,000 limit per person per year (plus up to $5,000 from tax refund)
- 1-year minimum hold — you cannot touch the money for 12 months
- If redeemed before 5 years, forfeit the last 3 months of interest
- Cannot be held in a brokerage account — only through TreasuryDirect.gov

**When to buy**: when your emergency fund is funded and you have cash you won't need for at least a year. They function as a high-yield savings account substitute with inflation protection — better than a savings account in high-inflation periods, comparable in low-inflation periods.

**When not to bother**: if the current I-bond rate is below the yield on a high-yield savings account or money market fund, the liquidity restriction isn't worth it.

### REITs — Real Estate Exposure Without the Landlord Problem

A Real Estate Investment Trust is a company that owns income-producing real estate — apartment buildings, commercial properties, data centers, cell towers, warehouses. By law, REITs must distribute 90%+ of taxable income as dividends.

**Why they're useful:**
- Real estate exposure without buying property, managing tenants, or carrying a mortgage
- Historically low correlation with equities (though this has increased)
- Dividend income — REITs are high-yield by structure
- Inflation hedge — real property values and rents tend to rise with inflation over time

**What they actually are:**
- **Equity REITs**: own and operate properties. The standard type. VNQ (Vanguard Real Estate ETF) holds ~160 equity REITs.
- **Mortgage REITs**: own mortgages, not properties. Much more interest-rate sensitive and volatile. Avoid unless you understand them well.
- **REIT ETFs**: the simplest approach. VNQ or SCHH give broad diversification across property types.

**The tax consideration**: REIT dividends are taxed as ordinary income (not qualified dividends), which is less favorable than stock dividends. Hold REITs in tax-advantaged accounts (Roth IRA is ideal — dividends grow and distribute tax-free) rather than taxable brokerage.

**How much**: a 5–15% allocation to REITs is the common range in portfolios that include them. Adding more doesn't meaningfully reduce risk and increases complexity.

### Farmland — Real Asset, Inflation Hedge, Stoic Investment

Farmland has historically been one of the most inflation-resistant assets: it produces food, which people always need, and the supply is finite. Average historical returns of 10–12% annually (appreciation + rental income) with very low correlation to equities.

**Traditional barrier**: you needed significant capital and connections to buy farmland directly. That's changed.

**Platforms that democratize farmland investing:**
- **AcreTrader**: individual farm deals, typically $15,000–25,000 minimum per deal. You own fractional shares of a specific farm. 3–10 year hold periods. Returns from rent + eventual land sale.
- **FarmTogether**: similar model, slightly different deal structure.
- **Harvest Returns**: lower minimums, broader deal variety.

**What you're actually buying**: fractional ownership in a LLC that holds a specific parcel of farmland, leased to a working farmer. You don't farm it — you own it as a passive investor.

**The honest trade-offs:**
- Illiquid — these are 3–7 year holds with no secondary market
- High minimum — $10,000–25,000 per deal, not suitable for small allocations
- Platform risk — these platforms are young; counterparty risk is real
- Less diversified than a REIT — you own a specific farm, not hundreds of properties

**Why it fits Romi's worldview**: farmland is a real, tangible asset that produces food. It connects the self-reliance and farming knowledge domains to investing. A small allocation ($10,000 in one deal) is a genuine hedge against scenarios where equities fail — which are often scenarios where food security matters.

**Alternative**: REITs that specifically hold farmland exist but are less pure. Farmland Partners (FPI) and Gladstone Land (LAND) trade as public stocks, offer liquidity, but have performed inconsistently.

### Individual Stocks — When It's Worth It

Almost never, for wealth building. The case for index funds — that market prices incorporate available information and consistently beating them is statistically rare — is well-established. Most professional fund managers underperform their index benchmarks over 10+ years.

**The one exception**: if you have deep knowledge of an industry that gives you genuine information edge. Tech professionals sometimes have real insight into which companies are building lasting moats vs. which are burning cash on marketing. This is real but narrow.

**If you hold individual stocks:**
- Cap at 5–10% of portfolio — what you'd be willing to lose
- Treat it as discretionary, not as a replacement for index fund allocation
- Consider tax implications — selling creates taxable events

**What individual stocks are good for**: learning. Owning a few shares of companies you follow closely is a better way to learn business analysis than reading about it. Cap the financial exposure; don't limit the learning.

### Series EE Bonds — The One Specific Use Case

EE bonds are Treasury bonds that double in value if held for exactly 20 years — a guaranteed 3.5% annualized return if held to the 20-year mark.

**Not exciting. One specific use**: if you have a defined financial goal in ~20 years (college fund for a future child, specific large purchase), EE bonds give a guaranteed nominal return for that window. Like I-bonds, state and local tax exempt.

**Skip them if**: you don't have a specific 20-year goal. The return doesn't justify holding them any shorter than the full 20 years.

---

### Commodities — Usually Not Worth It

Commodities (gold, oil, raw materials) are often recommended as inflation hedges. The evidence is mixed.

**Gold**: does not produce income. Stores value over very long time horizons but has had 10–20 year periods of flat real returns. If you want inflation protection, I-bonds and REITs do it better with income.

**Gold ETFs**: GLD, IAU — liquid, no storage. If you want a 3–5% allocation for genuine catastrophe hedging, these are the instrument. Physical gold is a complexity and security problem.

**Commodity ETFs**: high roll costs, tax inefficiency. Rarely worth owning. The farming and energy investments above provide commodity exposure with real productive value underneath.

## The Sequencing — When to Actually Use This

This is the order of operations before anything on this page makes sense:

1. Emergency fund (3–6 months expenses, liquid)
2. 401k up to employer match (free money, take it first)
3. Pay off high-interest debt (>6% return guaranteed)
4. Max HSA if on HDHP
5. Max Roth IRA ($7,000/year)
6. Max 401k remainder ($23,500/year limit in 2025)
7. **Now you're here.** Taxable brokerage with index funds is still the default. Alternatives are optional.

If you haven't maxed steps 2–6, this file is academic. Index funds in tax-advantaged accounts outperform almost everything here after tax drag.

## Key Considerations

**Complexity has a real cost.** More accounts, more platforms, more tax forms (farmland deals often produce K-1s, which complicate taxes). Every added asset class is time and attention. The marginal diversification benefit decreases; the marginal management cost doesn't.

**Illiquidity is a risk, not just a trait.** I-bonds (1 year), farmland (5–7 years), and private REITs (3+ years) tie up capital. Life circumstances change. Only allocate what you genuinely don't need in that window.

**Platform risk is real for new alternatives.** AcreTrader, FarmTogether — they're companies, not government entities. If they fail, the underlying asset (the farm) still exists, but recovery is complex.

**The portfolio that beats most people**: max tax-advantaged accounts, total market index fund + international index fund in taxable, I-bonds for cash reserves. Done. Nothing on this page should displace that if you haven't executed it.

## Getting Started

1. Confirm tax-advantaged accounts are maxed. If not, that comes first.
2. If you have cash earning below inflation in a savings account: buy $10,000 in I-bonds at TreasuryDirect.gov.
3. Add a REIT ETF (VNQ or SCHH) as 5–10% of the taxable portfolio, held in a Roth IRA if possible.
4. If farmland interests you and you can commit $10,000+ for 5+ years: research one deal on AcreTrader or FarmTogether. Read the offering documents.
5. Skip commodities, individual stocks beyond learning positions, and any complex structured product.

## Common Mistakes

**Adding alternatives before maxing tax-advantaged accounts.** The tax advantage of a Roth IRA compounding for 30 years beats almost any alternative investment's return differential.

**Treating I-bonds as fully liquid.** The 1-year lockup and 3-month interest penalty before year 5 make them unsuitable for emergency fund money.

**Holding REITs in a taxable account.** REIT dividends are taxed as ordinary income. In a taxable account this creates unnecessary tax drag. Roth IRA is the right home.

**Chasing farmland returns without reading the risks.** Platform risk, illiquidity, concentration in specific farms, and limited track records on these platforms are real. It's a small allocation, not a cornerstone.

**Buying crypto as "diversification."** Crypto has become increasingly correlated with risk assets (it falls when equities fall). It doesn't provide the diversification benefit the marketing suggests. This file doesn't cover it for that reason.
