# Flying Guide
*Everything about flying well — TSA rules, airport navigation, seat strategy, bags, packing, and getting through it with minimal friction*

---

## What Is It

Flying is a skill. Most people treat it as something that happens to them. A person who understands TSA rules, knows how to pick seats, packs correctly, and has the right bag moves through airports faster, cheaper, and with less friction than someone who wings it every time.

This file covers the full flying experience: before you leave home through landing. Destination research and trip budgeting are in [trip-planning.md](trip-planning.md). Luggage buying decisions are in [considered-luxuries.md](../consumerism/considered-luxuries.md) and [everyday-essentials.md](../consumerism/everyday-essentials.md).

---

## Why It Matters

Flying frequently without a system costs you money (wrong seats, checked bag fees, no loyalty credit), time (security holdups, missed upgrades, gate confusion), and energy (stressed packing, suboptimal positioning in the airport). The system is learnable in one session.

## Core Concepts

### TSA Rules: What Actually Gets You Held Up

The single largest source of avoidable delay is not knowing these rules cold.

**The 3-1-1 Liquids Rule (carry-on):**
- Liquids, gels, aerosols, creams, pastes: each container must be **3.4 oz (100ml) or less**
- All must fit in **one quart-sized clear zip-top bag** — one bag per person
- The bag goes in the bin separately at the checkpoint

What catches people: toothpaste (it's a paste — counts), peanut butter (gel consistency — counts), sunscreen, lip gloss, beard oil. If it flows, squishes, or is aerosol, it counts. Solid versions (solid deodorant, solid shampoo bars, solid sunscreen) go through without restriction.

**Exceptions:**
- Medications in liquid form: exempt from 3-1-1 but must be declared and separated
- Baby formula and breast milk: exempt in reasonable quantities
- Duty-free liquids purchased airside (past security): sealed in the tamper-evident bag with receipt — OK for domestic; check international connecting rules

**Checked bag vs. carry-on — what's only allowed checked:**
- Any blade over 4 inches (knives, large scissors, box cutters, razor blades loose)
- Baseball bats, golf clubs, ski poles (sporting goods)
- Tools over 7 inches
- Firearms (declared, unloaded, in hard-sided locked case — specific airline protocol, always call ahead)

**What's never allowed anywhere (federal law):**
- Explosives, flammable gases, lithium battery banks exceeding 100Wh (must be carry-on, cannot be checked)
- Lithium batteries: the key rule — **carry-on only, not checked**. Laptop batteries, camera batteries, external power banks. Airlines enforce this. A checked bag with a large power bank can be pulled.

**Power bank rules specifically:**
- Under 100Wh: carry-on, no limit
- 100–160Wh: carry-on, up to 2 per person with airline approval (most grant this)
- Over 160Wh: not permitted at all on passenger aircraft
- Most consumer power banks are under 100Wh. A 20,000 mAh bank at 3.7V = 74Wh — fine.

**TSA PreCheck vs. Global Entry:**
- **TSA PreCheck ($85/5 years):** Dedicated shorter lane at most US airports. Shoes stay on, laptop stays in bag, liquids stay in bag, light jacket stays on. Worth it if you fly 3+ times per year. Apply online, fingerprint appointment at enrollment center.
- **Global Entry ($100/5 years):** Includes PreCheck plus expedited US customs re-entry after international flights. Kiosk entry instead of customs officer line. Worth it if you take any international trips. **Start here — Global Entry includes PreCheck**, so it's only $15 more for both.
- Many credit cards reimburse the Global Entry fee ($100 credit): Chase Sapphire Reserve, Amex Platinum, Capital One Venture X, Citi Prestige. If you have any of these, the cost is $0.

**CLEAR ($189/year):** Biometric identity verification (fingerprint/iris) at the CLEAR lane — bypasses the ID check queue, deposits you at the front of the TSA screening line. Works with or without PreCheck. Worth it only if you fly from CLEAR-equipped airports very frequently. Lower priority than PreCheck/Global Entry for most people.

### Security Checkpoint: Moving Fast

This is a skill of preparation, not speed at the bin.

**The belt-to-bin process:**
1. Have your ID and boarding pass ready before you reach the document checker
2. Remove: laptop (its own bin), liquids bag (its own bin), shoes (unless PreCheck), jacket (unless PreCheck), belt, metal-heavy items from pockets
3. Laptop: out of the bag, nothing stacked on top of it in its bin
4. If PreCheck: laptop stays in bag, liquids stay in bag, shoes and light jacket stay on — much faster

**What slows people down:**
- Laptop buried at the bottom of a bag — unpack before you get to the belt
- Liquids not in the designated bag — can result in full bag search
- Forgetting belt or metal in pockets until the scanner — goes back through the line
- Checked bag carry-on packed with ambiguously restricted items — agents may pull the whole bag

**After clearing:** Have a spot planned where you repack — don't do it at the end of the belt where you block everyone. Step aside 10 feet to reassemble.

### Timing: When to Arrive

**Domestic:**
- Non-PreCheck: 90 minutes before departure at major airports; 60 minutes at smaller regional airports
- PreCheck: 60 minutes at major airports; 45 minutes at smaller airports
- Early morning flights (first of the day) have faster security — build up hasn't happened yet

**International:**
- Standard: 3 hours before departure
- If you have Global Entry out and know your terminal: 2.5 hours is usually fine

**Security line real-time data:** The TSA app shows wait times by airport and terminal. Google Maps also shows real-time airport wait estimates. Check before you decide how early to leave.

**The cost of being early:** Airport time is mostly dead time. Being early by 45 minutes usually just means waiting at the gate. PreCheck + real-time wait data lets you calibrate tightly.

### Booking: Seats, Timing, and Price

**Flight timing principles:**
- **First flight of the day** is the most reliable. Delays cascade — a 6 AM flight hasn't been delayed yet because it's the day's first leg. The 6 PM flight may be sitting behind 3 earlier delays on that aircraft's route.
- **Direct > connection** for anything under 6 hours of total travel. The time cost and risk of a connection rarely justify the savings on domestic flights.
- **Tuesday/Wednesday departures** are typically cheapest. Friday and Sunday are peak pricing.

**Seat selection:**
- **Exit row:** More legroom, no cost on many airlines (some charge $15–40). The middle exit seat (row 15 or equivalent) is often skipped — grab the aisle or window there.
- **Bulkhead (first row of cabin):** More legroom, no seat in front to recline into you, but no under-seat storage — everything goes overhead. Good if you have just a small personal item.
- **Aisle vs. window:**
  - Aisle: you move freely, you control the armrest, you deplane faster
  - Window: you lean, you control who crosses you, better for sleeping
  - Middle: never choose voluntarily
- **Avoid last rows:** Last rows don't recline, are near lavatories (noise/smell/line), and deplane last
- **Over the wing:** Smoothest ride in turbulence — near the aircraft's center of gravity

**Seat map tools:**
- **SeatGuru** (now less maintained but still useful): airline and aircraft-specific seat maps with quality ratings. Color-coded: green (good), yellow (caution), red (avoid). Know before you pick.
- **ExpertFlyer** (subscription): real-time seat map access, award availability, upgrade waitlist position — overkill for casual travelers, useful for frequent fliers

**Upgrade mechanics:**
- Most domestic upgrades (on Delta, United, American) go to elite status members first, then upgrade instrument holders (certificates, miles upgrades), then day-of paid upgrades
- **Day-of upgrade pricing** at the gate is often $50–150 for domestic — sometimes worth it for a long flight, price at check-in
- **Elite status fast track:** One airline, as many flights as possible. Mid-tier status (Delta Gold, United Gold, AA Gold) is achievable in a year of regular travel and gives free upgrades, better seat selection, and priority boarding

### Airlines and Loyalty

**The major US carriers:**
- **Delta (SkyMiles):** Generally the most reliable operationally. SkyMiles is less valuable for redemptions than it used to be (no award charts), but status is useful. Strongest hub: Atlanta, JFK, Minneapolis, Detroit, LAX.
- **United (MileagePlus):** MileagePlus miles are actually among the more useful for international redemptions (Star Alliance partners). Hub: Newark (EWR) — your closest major hub from Atlantic County.
- **American (AAdvantage):** Hub: Philadelphia (PHL) — closest major hub to Galloway, most direct NJ routing. AAdvantage miles useful for oneworld partner redemptions.
- **Southwest (Rapid Rewards):** No assigned seats. Points are straightforward — 1.5 cents each roughly. Companion Pass is one of the best deals in travel loyalty if you hit the threshold (earn 135,000 points in a calendar year → a designated companion flies free with you for the rest of that year and all of the next). No change fees ever.

**Atlantic City Airport (ACY):** Galloway's local option. Spirit and limited seasonal routes. Saves the drive to PHL or EWR — worth checking first for Spirit's nonstop routes if cost-sensitive. Security is fast (tiny airport). Parking is cheap.

**PHL vs. EWR for Atlantic County:**
- PHL: 60–75 min drive; American hub; more domestic options
- EWR: 90–100 min drive; United hub; more international options
- For anything international, EWR is usually better routing. For domestic American routes, PHL.

**The credit card layer:**
Chase Sapphire Reserve or Preferred, Amex Platinum, Capital One Venture X — these transfer points to multiple airline programs. More flexible than putting all spend on a single airline card. Principle: accumulate transferable points (Chase Ultimate Rewards, Amex Membership Rewards, Capital One miles), then transfer when you need to book. Don't lock into a single airline's co-branded card unless you have elite status goals.

### The Bag System

**The ideal setup: personal item + carry-on.** No checked bags. Checked bags cost money ($30–40 per leg on most carriers), add 20–30 minutes at baggage claim, and can be lost. One carry-on (overhead) + one personal item (under the seat) is the complete answer for trips up to 10 days.

**Carry-on size limits** (most US carriers):
- Maximum: 22 × 14 × 9 inches (including handle and wheels)
- Hard-sided spinners (Away, Rimowa) are sized to this limit — they fit if the overhead has space
- Enforcement is real on full flights and Spirit/Frontier — on those carriers, oversized bags get gate-checked ($65+)

**Personal item size limits** (under the seat in front):
- Typically 18 × 14 × 8 inches, varies by airline
- Must actually fit under the seat — measure once with your actual bag
- Your laptop bag or backpack is your personal item

**Laptop bag options** (personal item tier — the bag that goes under the seat):

*Best for tech professionals:*
- **Aer Day Pack 2 (~$185):** Already in [everyday-carry.md](../consumerism/everyday-carry.md). The right call if this is your daily bag that also travels. 14.8L, dedicated laptop compartment, passes as personal item on every major airline. Understated — doesn't scream "tech person."
- **Bellroy Transit Backpack (~$250):** Luggage pass-through sleeve. Slightly more polished. Good choice if you want the handle-attachment feature for connecting to a wheeled carry-on.
- **Knomo Beauchamp (~$180):** Slim laptop backpack with a professional silhouette — works as a backpack or a briefcase. 15.6" laptop max. Better for business settings where a backpack reads as informal.

*Dedicated laptop bags (non-backpack):*
- **Waterfield Designs Pro Executive (~$299):** San Francisco-made. Leather and ballistic nylon. Fits 16" MacBook Pro with room for charger, cables, notebook. Shoulder strap or carry handle. Extremely well-built. Not cheap, but functionally BIFL.
- **Tumi Alpha 3 Compact Brief (~$395):** Business-standard. Used by people who are in airports twice a week. Add-a-bag strap for connecting to rolling luggage. Overkill unless you travel for work constantly.
- **Knack Pack Medium (~$199):** Convertible briefcase/backpack, expands for extra capacity. Good for day trips where you need room for a jacket or gym clothes alongside the laptop.

*Budget-conscious:*
- **Everki Titan (~$110):** No-nonsense laptop backpack with a clamshell opening. 18.4" max laptop. Dedicated organization for chargers and cables. Ugly but functional.

*The right choice for Romi:* The Aer Day Pack 2 (if not already owned) or Bellroy Transit Backpack. Daily-use bag that doubles as the travel personal item without adjustment. A second dedicated travel-only bag isn't necessary unless the use case is specifically formal business travel.

**Carry-on luggage options:**
These are well-covered in [considered-luxuries.md](../consumerism/considered-luxuries.md) and [everyday-essentials.md](../consumerism/everyday-essentials.md). Short version:
- Away Carry-On (~$295): The correct answer for most people. Durable, TSA lock, removable battery.
- Rimowa Essential Cabin (~$700): The BIFL version. Only worth it if you fly enough to justify it.
- Osprey Farpoint 40L (~$160): If you prefer a travel backpack over wheels — better for adventure travel, cobblestones, non-flat terrain.

**Packing cubes:** Eagle Creek Pack-It Cubes or Compression cubes. Not optional once you use them. One cube per clothing category — shirts, bottoms, underwear/socks. Compression cubes reduce volume by ~30%. Makes unpacking at the hotel instant and repacking easy.

### What Goes in the Personal Item (Under the Seat)

The under-seat bag should have everything you need during the flight and during the first hour at the destination if luggage is lost. Priority items:

**Always in personal item / on your person:**
- Passport or ID
- Phone, charger, earbuds/headphones
- Laptop + charger
- Wallet (don't put in overhead bin bag)
- Any medication (never in checked bag)
- One outfit change in a packing cube — if checked bag is lost, you have this

**Flight comfort:**
- Noise-canceling headphones — the single biggest quality-of-life upgrade for flying. Sony WH-1000XM5 or Bose QuietComfort 45. Eliminates engine roar, makes a 3-hour flight feel like a library.
- Neck pillow: the U-shaped inflatable ones (Trtl is better — wraps the neck, more compact) if you sleep on planes
- Eye mask — cheap and effective
- Water bottle (empty through security, fill at the fountain airside)

**What NOT to put in overhead bin:**
Anything you'll want mid-flight. Electronics, snacks, medications, wallet — these go under the seat. The overhead is for the carry-on only.

### Packing Strategy: One Carry-On, 7 Days

This is achievable for most climates with discipline.

**The formula:**
- 5 underwear / 5 socks (merino if possible — wearable 2+ days, odor-resistant, quick-drying)
- 3–4 T-shirts (merino wool or technical fabric — they pack small and don't wrinkle)
- 2 pants / 1 shorts (one of the pants is what you wear on the plane — doesn't count in the bag)
- 1 mid-layer or hoodie
- 1 casual button-down (wrinkle-resistant — can dress up)
- 1 pair dress shoes (if needed) — or a single versatile sneaker that works across contexts
- Toiletries in the 3-1-1 bag + anything solid (solid shampoo, bar soap)
- Chargers and cables in a small cable organizer

**What most people overpack:**
- "Just in case" outfits they never wear
- Full-size toiletries (hotel provides shampoo/conditioner; buy deodorant/toothpaste there for trips over 5 days instead of packing full-size)
- Multiple pairs of shoes — one pair that works for walking, one dress shoe maximum

**Packing order:**
- Heaviest items against the back panel of the carry-on (closest to your back when carried)
- Packing cubes fill the main compartment
- Shoes wrapped in a shower cap (hotel freebie) to protect clothes
- Toiletries bag last, near the top — accessible at security

### At the Airport

**Check-in:**
- Online check-in opens 24 hours before most domestic flights; 24–48 hours international
- Check in immediately at the 24-hour mark if you don't have assigned seats (Southwest) — position matters
- Use the airline app — mobile boarding pass is faster than paper; have it loaded before you reach the checkpoint

**Airline app > everything:**
- Gate changes, delays, and upgrade notifications are in the app before they're announced
- Find your gate before going to a restaurant — gates can change

**TSA line selection:**
- The Standard line has multiple lanes — scan them all for length before joining
- The middle lane is often shorter (people default to edge lanes)
- At peak times, lanes with fewer families move faster

**Lounge access:**
- Priority Pass: comes with Chase Sapphire Reserve ($550/year card fee) and Amex Platinum — gives access to hundreds of airport lounges worldwide. Useful if you have layovers. Free food, free drinks, quiet.
- Airline lounges (Delta Sky Club, United Club, American Admirals Club): elite status or day passes ($50–75). Worth it for a long layover, not for a 45-minute connection.

**Gate area:**
- Find an outlet immediately — don't assume the gate has one
- Know your boarding group — boarding early matters if overhead bin space is limited
- Basic Economy and last boarding groups often find full overhead bins on full flights → gate-checked carry-on, which defeats the point

**Boarding strategy:**
- Board as early as your group allows on full flights — overhead space goes fast
- On half-empty flights, board last — you'll get your pick of seats
- Always put your personal item under the seat in front of you before stowing the carry-on — it goes in first, the larger bag goes overhead

### During the Flight

**Turbulence:** Seat belt on whenever seated — moderate turbulence is uncomfortable; serious turbulence with an unbuckled passenger is a hospital trip. It's not a personal choice.

**Noise-canceling headphones + a podcast/audiobook** beats staring at the seatback screen in comfort and battery usage.

**Hydration:** Airplane cabins are extremely low humidity (10–20%). Drink water, avoid alcohol on long flights, use eye drops if you wear contacts. Arriving dehydrated makes jet lag worse.

**Jet lag mitigation (for time zone shifts):**
- Stay awake until local bedtime at the destination — no napping on arrival
- Get sunlight on arrival day — resets circadian rhythm faster than anything else
- Melatonin 0.5–1mg at destination bedtime for the first 2–3 nights (physiological dose, not the 10mg that's commonly sold)
- Avoid alcohol and caffeine near bedtime at destination

### Deplaning and Baggage Claim

**Deplaning:** Let the rows in front of you go first — there's no winning the deplaning race unless you're in the first 5 rows. Stand, get your bag from overhead, and file out.

**If you have a tight connection:**
- Alert the flight attendant before landing — they can announce "passengers with connections to [gate] please allow them to exit first"
- The airline app will show your updated gate as you land
- If the first flight was delayed and the connection is short, the gate agent at deplaning can radio ahead to hold the next flight

**Baggage claim:**
- The airline app shows which carousel before you reach the baggage hall
- Bags take 15–25 minutes after landing on most domestic flights — use this time to get to the carousel, find a bathroom, or handle the car rental queue

**Lost luggage:**
- Report at the airline baggage desk before leaving the airport — file the claim immediately
- Airlines are required to deliver lost bags to your destination address within a reasonable time and to reimburse expenses for delayed bags (clothing, toiletries) — keep receipts
- This is why carry-on only is the right default: it can never be lost

## Key Considerations

**PreCheck / Global Entry first.** If you don't have these, get them before the next trip. Global Entry includes PreCheck, costs $100 (often free with the right credit card), and changes the airport experience permanently.

**The credit card question.** A travel rewards card (Chase Sapphire Preferred at minimum, Reserve if you travel frequently) pays for PreCheck, provides travel insurance, and generates points at a rate that makes flights meaningfully cheaper over time. If you don't have one, getting one before your next trip is the highest-ROI travel action available.

**One carry-on is almost always possible.** The exceptions are travel with a formal suit, ski equipment, and camping gear. For everything else, the packing formula above works for 7 days. Check-in bags are a tax on not learning to pack.

**Noise-canceling headphones are non-negotiable on flights over 2 hours.** Engine noise at 80+ dB for 3 hours is cognitive load. The headphones remove it. Prioritize this over almost every other travel purchase.

**Spirit and Frontier have their own rules.** Budget carriers charge separately for everything — including carry-ons. A Spirit ticket that looks $100 cheaper can cost the same as a mainline carrier once you add a carry-on, seat selection, and no customer service if something goes wrong. Calculate total cost.

## Getting Started

1. Get Global Entry — starts the PreCheck clock and handles international customs. Apply at cbp.gov. Schedule the enrollment appointment; the interview is 10 minutes.
2. Get a travel credit card that reimburses Global Entry and earns transferable points.
3. Buy noise-canceling headphones if you don't have them.
4. Buy packing cubes.
5. Practice one carry-on-only trip — confirm the formula works for your travel style.

## Common Mistakes

**Liquids not pre-packed in the 3-1-1 bag.** Scrambling at the security belt to pull out a Dopp kit, open it, fish out the liquids bag, is the #1 avoidable delay. Pack the liquids bag the night before, top of the bag.

**Laptop buried at the bottom.** Goes in last (or in a dedicated sleeve accessible from the outside), comes out first at security.

**No PreCheck, long security line, almost missed flight.** This is predictable and preventable. Get PreCheck.

**Overpacking into a checked bag.** Checked bags cost $30–40 each way, add 25 minutes at baggage claim, and can be lost. The discipline to pack into a carry-on saves hundreds of dollars per year for a frequent traveler.

**Choosing a middle seat voluntarily.** Even if a window or aisle costs $15 more, pay it. A middle seat on a 3-hour flight is $5/hour to be uncomfortable.

**Not checking the gate app for changes.** Gate changes happen; airports don't always announce them loudly. Check the app after boarding the aircraft at your origin and again upon landing.
