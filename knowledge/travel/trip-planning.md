# Trip Planning
*A repeatable system for researching, booking, and executing trips — from weekend drives to international travel.*

---

## What Is It

Trip planning is the process of turning a destination idea into an actual trip: flights, lodging, itinerary, budget, logistics. Done well, it front-loads the decisions so the trip itself is frictionless. Done poorly, it means scrambling on-site, overpaying, and missing the things worth seeing.

This file is a planning framework. Destination-specific guides live in separate files — see the Connections section and CATALOG.md under Travel.

---

## Why It Matters

Unplanned trips are expensive, rushed, and forgettable. A few hours of upfront research eliminates most of the friction that turns trips into stress. This is self-reliance applied to leisure: don't outsource the thinking.

## Core Concepts

### The Planning Horizon

How far out you plan determines what's available to you.

- **6+ weeks**: Best flight prices, popular hotel availability, restaurant reservations at sought-after spots
- **2–6 weeks**: Still good options, slightly higher prices
- **Under 2 weeks**: Pay a premium or accept limited options — works for road trips and flexible destinations, bad for peak-season cities

For domestic weekend trips: 2–4 weeks is the sweet spot. Book flights Tuesday–Thursday for best prices.

### Budget Framework

Set a total number before you start booking, then allocate:
- **Flights**: Usually 25–40% of total trip cost
- **Lodging**: 30–40%
- **Food**: 15–20%
- **Activities/transport**: 10–15%
- **Buffer**: 10% for the unexpected

Track actuals vs. estimates to calibrate future trips.

### Lodging Strategy

**Hotel**: Predictable, cancellable (book refundable), loyalty points if you travel regularly. Good for short trips and business.

**Airbnb/VRBO**: Better for 3+ nights, groups, or when you want a kitchen. Read reviews for check-in process and host responsiveness — that's where problems surface.

**Location over price**: A cheaper room 45 minutes from everything costs you time and transport. In most cities, being central is worth $30–50/night more.

### Itinerary Design

Build by neighborhood, not by category. Grouping "all museums" regardless of location burns hours in transit. Instead: Day 1 is the north side, Day 2 is downtown, etc.

**The rule of two**: Plan no more than two major activities per day. Leave space — fatigue, spontaneity, and good meals take time.

**Anchors vs. fills**: Identify 2–3 non-negotiable things (the anchor experiences). Build the day's logistics around those, fill gaps with flexible options.

### Research Stack

- **YouTube** — "things to do in [city]" gives a quick visual survey; filter by recent uploads for current info
- **Reddit** — r/[cityname] or r/[cityname]travel; search "first time" or "48 hours" for curated local takes
- **Google Maps** — star places as you research, then cluster them geographically for itinerary building
- **Eater/Infatuation** — restaurant research for cities they cover
- **Atlas Obscura** — unusual, off-the-beaten-path spots worth knowing

## Key Considerations

**Weather is real.** Check seasonal patterns before booking, not just the forecast. A city that's gorgeous in October is brutal in July.

**Reservations are not optional for good restaurants.** In major cities, the places worth eating at book out 2–4 weeks. Decide where you want to eat before you go, not when you're hungry.

**Transit vs. car.** Some cities are car cities (LA, Phoenix, Nashville). Some are not (NYC, Chicago, Boston). Know before you go — renting a car in a transit city creates more problems than it solves.

**Pace over coverage.** A well-paced 3-day trip where you eat well, sleep enough, and see 6 things is better than an exhausting sprint through 20 checkboxes.

## Getting Started

1. Pick the destination and trip length
2. Set a total budget
3. Check flights and set a price alert (Google Flights)
4. Book lodging once flights are confirmed — central location, refundable rate
5. Research the city: Reddit, YouTube, Google Maps starring
6. Build the itinerary by neighborhood; book restaurant reservations
7. Pack light — checked bags add time, cost, and risk

---

## Common Mistakes

**Overscheduling.** The itinerary looks manageable on paper; in practice, transitions take longer, things close, you want to linger somewhere. Build in slack.

**Ignoring neighborhoods.** Booking a hotel in a neighborhood that's far from everything because it's cheap defeats the savings.

**No restaurant plan.** Deciding where to eat while hungry and on-site means picking whatever is visible and convenient — usually tourist traps.

**Packing too much.** One carry-on. It is almost always possible.
