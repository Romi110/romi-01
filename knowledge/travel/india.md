# Traveling to India
*A practical guide to traveling in India — documentation, health, money, transport, and navigating it as both a tourist and someone with family there.*

---

## What Is It

India travel splits into two modes that often get blurred: tourist travel (sightseeing, planned itinerary, hotels) and family-visit travel (staying with relatives, social obligations, less control over the schedule). Most trips for Romi will be some mix of both — a base of family time in one or two cities, with side trips layered in. This file covers the logistics that differ from Western travel: documentation specific to people of Indian origin, health prep, payment infrastructure, and how to move around the country once there.

Destination research for *what to see* lives in [trip-planning.md](trip-planning.md) — this file is the India-specific layer underneath that.

## Why It Matters

India is not a generic "international trip" — it has its own visa category for people born there, a different health-risk profile than Western Europe, a cash-and-card system that's shifted hard toward mobile payments, and a transport system (especially trains) that rewards advance booking and punishes improvisation. Getting the logistics right up front means the trip is about family and place, not about fighting infrastructure.

## Core Concepts

### Documentation: OCI vs. Tourist Visa

Anyone born in India who has taken foreign citizenship is eligible for an **OCI card** (Overseas Citizen of India) rather than a standard tourist visa. OCI is a lifelong, multiple-entry status — no visa renewals, no exit/entry restrictions, and it allows longer uninterrupted stays. If Romi doesn't already hold one, this is worth doing once rather than re-applying for e-visas on every trip.

- **OCI card**: One-time application (processing takes weeks to months — apply early), lifelong validity tied to the passport, re-issuance needed only when the passport is renewed before age 20 or after age 50.
- **e-Visa**: For foreign-born family or friends joining the trip who aren't eligible for OCI — apply online 4–120 days before travel, valid for tourism up to 90 days.
- Carry physical copies of OCI/visa, passport photo page, and onward travel proof — immigration counters in India still ask for paper backups occasionally.

### Health Prep

India's health risks are different from a Western Europe or domestic trip — plan a few weeks ahead, not the week before.

- **Vaccinations**: Routine vaccines up to date, plus Hepatitis A and Typhoid as a baseline for most travelers; Japanese Encephalitis or Rabies if visiting rural areas or doing significant outdoor/animal contact. A travel clinic visit 4–6 weeks out covers this in one appointment.
- **Malaria**: Risk varies heavily by region and season — most major cities are low-risk, but check the specific itinerary. Prophylaxis isn't a blanket requirement.
- **Water and food safety**: Don't drink tap water anywhere, including for brushing teeth — bottled or filtered only. Street food can be excellent and safe if it's hot, fresh, and high-turnover (busy stalls cycle food fast); raw vegetables and unpeeled fruit from unclear water sources are the higher-risk category, not street food per se.
- **Travel insurance with medical evacuation**: Worth having for any India trip — private hospital care is good in major cities but you want the financial buffer and evacuation coverage handled before you need it.

### Money and Payments

India has leapfrogged much of the West on mobile payments — UPI (Unified Payments Interface) is how most transactions happen now, even for street vendors.

- **UPI apps** (Google Pay, PhonePe) require an Indian bank account and phone number to set up fully — not practical to set up cold as a visiting foreigner, though some apps now offer foreign-card UPI onboarding for short trips. In practice: rely on cash and cards, and let family members handle UPI payments when paying together.
- **Cash**: Still essential for small vendors, rickshaws, and rural areas. Withdraw from ATMs at bank branches (lower fraud risk than standalone street ATMs); notify your US bank of international travel and check foreign transaction fees beforehand.
- **Cards**: Visa/Mastercard accepted at hotels, restaurants, and larger retail in cities. Carry a no-foreign-transaction-fee card (Chase Sapphire, Capital One) — see [flying-guide.md](flying-guide.md) for the broader travel credit card framework.
- **Currency**: INR — exchange a small amount at the airport for immediate cash needs, do the bulk of exchange at a bank or licensed exchange in the city for a better rate.

### Connectivity

- **Local SIM**: Airtel or Jio prepaid SIMs are cheap and give excellent 4G/5G coverage in cities — buy at the airport on arrival (passport photo and a passport-size photo required) or have a family member buy one on your behalf in advance.
- **eSIM**: Increasingly viable for short trips — see [travel-tech.md](travel-tech.md) for setup. Good for the first day or two before getting a local SIM; not as cheap as a local SIM for a multi-week stay.
- **WhatsApp is the default communication channel** in India, for everything from coordinating with family to confirming a driver. Have it installed and set up before you land.

### Getting Around

- **Domestic flights**: Cheap and frequent between major cities (IndiGo, Air India, Vistara) — often faster and more comfortable than a long train or road trip for distances over 400km.
- **Trains**: A genuine experience and often the right call for shorter distances or when the journey itself matters. Book through **IRCTC** (the official booking site/app) well in advance — popular routes sell out weeks ahead. AC classes (2AC, 3AC) are the right tier for comfort on overnight journeys; sleeper class is the budget option but skip it for a first trip.
- **Ride-hailing**: Uber and Ola work in most cities — easier and more predictable than negotiating with a street rickshaw, though rickshaws and auto-rickshaws are fine for short, known routes and part of the local experience.
- **Drivers for day trips**: Hiring a car-and-driver for a day or multi-day trip is common, affordable by Western standards, and often less stressful than self-driving — Indian traffic patterns and road conventions are a real adjustment.

### Climate and Regional Variation

India is not one climate. Plan around the specific region and season, not "India weather."

- **Monsoon (June–September)**: Heavy rain across most of the country, especially the west coast and northeast. Travel is still doable but plan for delays and pack accordingly (see packing note below).
- **North India (Delhi, Punjab, Rajasthan)**: Extreme heat May–June (45°C+), cold and foggy winters (December–January) — pack layers for winter trips, this surprises people expecting uniform heat.
- **South India (Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka)**: More moderate year-round, monsoon-heavy on the coast.
- **Hill stations and the Himalayas**: Genuinely cold, a different packing list entirely from the plains.

### Etiquette and Social Navigation

- **Dress modestly at religious sites**: Shoulders and knees covered at minimum; temples and mosques may require head coverings or removing shoes — carry a scarf/shawl as a default solution.
- **Bargaining** is expected in markets and with unmetered transport, not in fixed-price retail, malls, or restaurants. Light, friendly negotiation is normal; aggressive haggling is not necessary or well-received.
- **Family visit dynamics**: Expect a different pace than a planned tourist itinerary — meals, visits, and social obligations will fill more time than expected. Build slack into any side-trip plans rather than scheduling tightly around a family stay.

## Key Considerations

**This is a heritage trip, not a generic international trip.** The research stack in [trip-planning.md](trip-planning.md) (Reddit, YouTube, Atlas Obscura) is less load-bearing here than family knowledge — lean on relatives for restaurant recommendations, safe water sources, and which neighborhoods need extra caution, rather than rebuilding that research from scratch.

**Packing for India-specific conditions**: Lightweight, breathable, modest clothing covers most situations. A rain layer for monsoon season, layers for North Indian winters, modest options for temple visits. The carry-on formula in [flying-guide.md](flying-guide.md) still applies — laundry is cheap and widely available in India, so packing less and washing more is viable even on longer trips.

**Air quality**: Delhi and other North Indian cities have serious air quality issues, especially October–February. Worth checking the AQI before and during the trip if respiratory sensitivity is a concern.

## Getting Started

1. Check OCI status — apply now if you don't have one; it removes visa friction permanently.
2. Book a travel clinic appointment 4–6 weeks before departure for vaccinations.
3. Get travel insurance with medical evacuation coverage.
4. Notify your bank of travel dates, confirm no foreign transaction fees on the card you'll use.
5. Install WhatsApp; plan eSIM for day one and a local SIM for the rest of the trip.
6. If train travel is part of the plan, book IRCTC tickets as soon as dates are fixed.

## Common Mistakes

**Drinking tap water or using it to brush teeth.** The single most common cause of a ruined trip. Bottled or filtered water, every time, including ice in places you're unsure of.

**Underestimating family-visit time.** Treating a family-and-tourism trip like a pure tourism trip leads to an overstuffed itinerary that collides with social obligations. Plan the touristy parts loosely.

**Not booking trains early enough.** Popular routes (especially around holidays) sell out weeks in advance. Waiting until arrival to book is a common and avoidable mistake.

**Packing for "India" as one climate.** A trip spanning Delhi in December and Kerala in the same week needs both warm layers and lightweight breathable clothing — check each leg's actual climate.
