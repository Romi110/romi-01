# Travel Gear
*The small equipment that solves recurring travel problems — security, tracking, laundry, and water — distinct from the bags and luggage covered in flying-guide.md.*

---

## What Is It

Beyond the bag itself (covered in [flying-guide.md](flying-guide.md)) and the digital tools (covered in [travel-tech.md](travel-tech.md)), there's a small set of physical gear that solves specific recurring travel problems: securing a bag, finding it if it's lost, washing clothes on a multi-week trip without checking a bigger bag, and handling water safety in places where tap water isn't reliable. This file is about what to bring and why, not which brand to buy for general use — that buying-decision framing lives in [consumerism/outdoor-gear-bifl.md](../consumerism/outdoor-gear-bifl.md) and related Consumerism files.

## Why It Matters

Most of this gear is small, cheap, and solves a problem that otherwise costs real money or time mid-trip — a lost bag with no tracker, a missed laundry day forcing an unplanned shopping trip, a stomach illness from bad water. None of it is optional gear for serious travelers; it's the difference between handling these problems proactively versus reactively at the worst time.

## Core Concepts

### Bag Security

**TSA-approved locks** on checked bags let TSA open and re-lock the bag for inspection without cutting the lock off — a non-TSA lock that blocks an inspection gets cut, no exceptions. Cheap (~$10) and worth using on any checked bag with anything you'd mind a stranger handling.

**Slash-resistant bag material or a cable lock** for carry-ons used in crowded transit (markets, trains) — less about airport security, more about deterring opportunistic theft on the ground at the destination.

### Tracking

**Apple AirTag (or Tile for Android)** in a checked bag is the single highest-value addition to the bag system. If a bag is lost or delayed, you know its exact location instead of waiting on the airline's tracking system, which is often slower and less precise. A second AirTag in the carry-on or personal item is reasonable for genuinely high-value gear (camera body, laptop) inside it.

### Luggage Scale

A **portable digital luggage scale** (~$10–15, clips onto the handle) prevents the at-the-airport surprise of an overweight checked bag fee. Weigh the packed bag at home before leaving — most airlines cap checked bags at 50lbs (23kg), and the fee for going over is disproportionate to the $10 scale that prevents it.

### Compression

**Compression sacks** (not the same as the packing cubes covered in [flying-guide.md](flying-guide.md)) squeeze bulky soft items — a packable jacket, a sleeping bag if camping is part of the trip — down to a fraction of their size. Packing cubes organize; compression sacks reduce volume. Most carry-on trips don't need both, but multi-week or cold-climate trips benefit from at least one compression sack for the bulkiest layer.

### Travel Laundry

For trips longer than a week of packed clothing (or any trip following the one-carry-on discipline in [flying-guide.md](flying-guide.md)), washing clothes mid-trip beats packing more.

- **Laundry detergent sheets or sachets** (Tide Eco-Box travel packs, or dedicated travel sheets) — flat, leak-proof, TSA-liquid-rule-free since they're not liquid.
- **A universal sink stopper** — many hotel and guesthouse sinks lack a working stopper; a flat rubber universal stopper (~$5) turns any sink into a usable wash basin.
- **A travel clothesline** (twisted/braided elastic line, no clothespins needed — items wedge into the twist) — packs flat, dries clothes in a bathroom overnight.
- **Quick-dry fabrics** (merino, technical synthetics — already the packing recommendation in [flying-guide.md](flying-guide.md)) make this whole system work; cotton takes too long to air-dry overnight.

### Water Safety (International / Adventure Travel)

For destinations where tap water isn't reliable — much of South and Southeast Asia, parts of Latin America and Africa — relying entirely on purchased bottled water is the default (see [india.md](india.md)), but a backup matters for remote areas or to cut down on plastic waste.

- **Steripen (UV purifier)**: Treats a liter in about a minute using UV light, no waiting, no taste change. Needs batteries/charge — not a backup for when power is the constraint.
- **LifeStraw or Sawyer Squeeze (filter straw/bottle)**: No power needed, filters bacteria and protozoa (not viruses) — good backup or primary for areas without reliable bottled water access, like trekking.
- **Purification tablets (iodine or chlorine dioxide)**: Lightest, longest shelf life, slowest (30 minutes to a few hours wait) — the right backup-to-the-backup, not the primary method.

For most trips with reliable bottled water access (most of urban India, for example), none of this is necessary — it's specifically for remote/rural legs of a trip or genuine adventure travel.

### First Aid, Travel-Sized

A small kit beyond what's in a home first-aid kit (see [skills/first-aid-basics](../skills/first-aid-basics.md) for the general framework): anti-diarrheal (Imodium), oral rehydration salts, any personal prescriptions in original labeled containers with a doctor's note for controlled substances, motion sickness medication if relevant, and basic wound care (bandages, antiseptic wipes). Sized for the trip's actual risk profile — a weekend domestic trip needs almost none of this; international travel to a region with different food/water risk profiles needs more.

## Key Considerations

**This gear is small and cheap — the failure mode is forgetting it, not overspending on it.** Almost everything here is under $20; the discipline is having a standing travel gear pouch that gets restocked after each trip rather than re-buying or forgetting items every time.

**Match the kit to the trip.** A domestic long weekend needs a luggage scale and maybe an AirTag. An international multi-week trip to a region with unreliable water needs the full water-safety and laundry kit. Don't carry adventure-travel gear for a city weekend.

**Liquids rules still apply.** Laundry detergent sheets and purification tablets are solid-form specifically to avoid the 3-1-1 liquid restrictions covered in [flying-guide.md](flying-guide.md) — check that any liquid-form alternative (liquid detergent, liquid purification drops) fits the carry-on liquid limit if checked bags aren't part of the plan.

## Getting Started

1. Buy a TSA-approved lock and a luggage scale if you don't have them — cheap, permanent additions to the travel kit.
2. Add an AirTag to the primary checked or carry-on bag.
3. Build a small "travel gear pouch" — detergent sheets, sink stopper, travel clothesline — that lives packed and ready between trips.
4. For the next international trip to a region with water-safety concerns, add a Sawyer Squeeze or purification tablets as backup to bottled water.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Buying gear per-trip instead of maintaining a standing kit.** Re-buying detergent sheets or a sink stopper every trip because the last batch isn't in a known location wastes money and time. Keep a dedicated, restocked pouch.

**Skipping the luggage scale and eating an overweight fee.** A $50–100 airport fee for a bag that's 3lbs over the limit is preventable for the cost of a $10 scale used once at home.

**Treating water purification gear as necessary for every international trip.** Most travel destinations with developed tourist infrastructure are fine with bottled water alone — the filter/UV/tablet layer is for remote or rural legs, not blanket precaution everywhere.
