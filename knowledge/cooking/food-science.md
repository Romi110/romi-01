# Food Science
*The chemistry and physics behind cooking technique — why things work, not just what to do*

---

## What Is It

Food science is the application of chemistry, physics, and biology to understand what happens to food during cooking. When an onion goes translucent in a hot pan, when bread rises, when yogurt sets, when an emulsion breaks — these aren't mysteries. They're predictable chemical and physical events with specific causes.

Understanding food science doesn't make you a better cook automatically. But it turns failure from random misfortune into diagnosed cause-and-effect. It also lets you adapt — if you understand *why* a baking recipe works, you can substitute ingredients intelligently. If you understand *why* an emulsion breaks, you can fix it.

---

## Why It Matters

Most cooking knowledge is procedural: do this step, then this step. Food science gives you the *why*. Combined with [cooking/flavor-theory.md](flavor-theory.md), it makes cooking a system of principles rather than a collection of recipes. Romi's cooking approach — from scratch, building deep skill — benefits most from understanding mechanisms.

## Core Concepts

### The Maillard Reaction

The most important chemical reaction in cooking. Named after French chemist Louis-Camille Maillard (1912), who described it but didn't fully understand it. Modern food science has identified it produces hundreds of distinct flavor compounds in combination.

**What it is**: a non-enzymatic browning reaction between amino acids (from proteins) and reducing sugars (glucose, fructose, lactose — not sucrose until it's hydrolyzed). Above ~285°F/140°C in a dry environment.

**What it produces**: the brown color and complex, roasted flavors of:
- Seared bread crust
- Browned butter and ghee
- Roasted coffee and cocoa
- Caramelized onions (partial Maillard + caramelization)
- Crispy dosa edges
- Roasted vegetables

**The conditions required:**
1. **Temperature**: above 285°F/140°C. Water boils at 212°F — while food is wet, you can't reach Maillard temperatures. This is the critical constraint.
2. **Dry surface**: wet food steams rather than browns. Pat food dry before searing. Don't crowd a pan (lowering temp causes moisture release).
3. **Protein and sugar presence**: pure sugar caramelizes; pure starch doesn't Maillard effectively. Both protein and reducing sugar need to be present.
4. **Alkalinity helps**: this is why pretzel dough is dipped in lye (sodium hydroxide) solution before baking — alkaline conditions accelerate Maillard. Baking soda (alkaline) added to onions while caramelizing speeds browning significantly.

**Practical applications:**
- **Don't crowd the pan**: crowding drops temperature and traps moisture → steaming instead of browning.
- **Pat food dry**: critical for tofu, paneer, vegetables, anything you want crust on.
- **High heat, short time**: for Maillard without overcooking the interior. A very hot pan and short contact time produces crust; a medium pan produces grey, soft, steamed food.
- **Deglaze the browned bits**: the fond (browned residue on the pan) is concentrated Maillard flavor. Deglaze with wine, stock, water, or acid to dissolve it into the dish.

### Caramelization

The thermal decomposition (pyrolysis) of sugar molecules when heated above their melting points. Distinct from Maillard — no protein required, just sugar and heat.

**Temperature thresholds** (approximate, vary by sugar type):
- Fructose: ~230°F/110°C
- Glucose: ~320°F/160°C
- Sucrose (table sugar): ~320–360°F/160–182°C — first hydrolyzes into glucose and fructose, then caramelizes

**What happens**: sugar molecules break apart and recombine into hundreds of new compounds — caramel color (brown) and caramel flavor (sweet, bitter, nutty, butterscotch). The bitterness increases with darker caramelization.

**Caramelizing onions**: a common point of confusion. Recipe instructions saying "caramelize onions in 5 minutes" are wrong. Real caramelization takes 40–60 minutes of low, patient heat as the sugars in the onions (sucrose, glucose, fructose) slowly convert. The transformation is total — raw onion sharpness becomes sweet, complex depth. Adding a pinch of baking soda (alkaline) accelerates both Maillard and caramelization.

**Dry vs. wet caramel:**
- **Dry caramel**: melt sugar directly in a pan without water. Faster, but edges burn while center is still solid — requires constant movement.
- **Wet caramel**: dissolve sugar in water first, then boil off the water. More control, more forgiving — the water equalizes temperature. Once the water is gone, caramelization begins.

### Protein Denaturation and Coagulation

Proteins are long chains of amino acids folded into specific 3D shapes. Heat (and acid, and salt) breaks the bonds holding those shapes — **denaturation**. The unfolded proteins then rebond to each other — **coagulation** — forming a solid structure.

**Eggs**: egg white proteins (primarily albumin) denature and coagulate between 140–185°F/60–85°C. Yolk proteins coagulate between 149–158°F/65–70°C. The window between runny white and hard-cooked yolk is narrow — temperature control determines texture entirely. Sous vide exploits this: hold at 145°F/63°C for precisely cooked eggs.

**Gluten**: flour proteins (glutenin and gliadin) hydrate and form gluten when mixed with water. Gluten is elastic and extensible — it traps gas bubbles in bread dough and provides chew. Heat (baking) denatures gluten proteins, setting the bread structure. See [cooking/baking-fundamentals.md](baking-fundamentals.md) for gluten development.

**Dairy**: milk proteins (casein) destabilize when acid is added — they unwind and clump together. This is how paneer and chèvre are made: heat milk, add acid (lemon juice or vinegar), casein curdles, strain. Yogurt uses the same principle but with bacterial acid production instead of direct addition.

**Meat and vegetable proteins**: similar denaturation applies. Over-cooking vegetables makes them mushy because cell walls break down and proteins coagulate beyond the optimal texture. The goal in vegetable cookery is usually to cook *to* the denaturation point without going through it.

### Starch Gelatinization

Starches are long carbohydrate chains packed into granules. When heated in water above a threshold temperature, granule walls rupture, starch chains hydrate and swell, and the mixture thickens — **gelatinization**.

**Common gelatinization temperatures:**
- Wheat starch: ~140–160°F/60–71°C
- Corn starch: ~144–162°F/62–72°C
- Potato starch: ~130–140°F/54–60°C (lower — very efficient thickener)
- Tapioca: ~130–140°F/54–60°C

**Why roux thickens sauces**: flour (wheat starch + protein) dispersed in fat first, then combined with liquid. The fat coats the starch granules individually, preventing clumping when liquid is added. When heated, the starch gelatinizes in place, thickening the sauce evenly.

**Retrogradation**: cooled gelatinized starch partially recrystallizes, expelling water — this is staling. Day-old bread is stale because the gelatinized starch has retrograded. Reheating reverses it temporarily. Potato starch retrogrades most, which is why cold cooked potatoes form resistant starch (helpful for gut health; a dietary positive).

**Cornstarch vs. flour thickening:**
- Cornstarch: pure starch, no protein. Thickens at lower temperature, produces a clearer, glossier sauce. Loses thickening power if simmered too long or reheated (starch chains break). Best for clear sauces, glazes.
- Flour: starch + protein. Thicker, more stable, tolerates simmering. Produces opaque sauces. Requires cooking out the raw flour taste.
- Arrowroot: similar to cornstarch but doesn't withstand high heat. Use at the very end. Produces silky, clear sauces.

### Emulsification

An emulsion is a stable mixture of two immiscible liquids — oil and water. Left alone they separate. With an emulsifier, they stay suspended.

**How emulsifiers work**: emulsifier molecules have a hydrophilic (water-loving) end and a hydrophobic (fat-loving) end. They position themselves at the oil-water interface, surrounding oil droplets and keeping them dispersed in water (or vice versa). This prevents separation.

**Common emulsifiers in cooking:**
- **Egg yolk lecithin**: the emulsifier in mayonnaise, hollandaise, béarnaise. One egg yolk can emulsify up to a cup of oil if added slowly enough.
- **Mustard**: contains mucilage compounds that emulsify. A teaspoon of Dijon in a vinaigrette produces a more stable emulsion than shaking alone.
- **Garlic**: raw garlic cells contain alliinase and other compounds that can act as weak emulsifiers. Garlic aioli is more stable than oil + water.
- **Honey**: contains proteins and sugars that emulsify.

**Oil-in-water vs. water-in-oil:**
- Oil-in-water: oil droplets dispersed in water. Mayonnaise, milk, cream, vinaigrette. The majority.
- Water-in-oil: water droplets dispersed in oil. Butter (water droplets in butterfat).

**Making mayonnaise**: add oil extremely slowly to egg yolk while whisking. The yolk's lecithin coats each oil droplet as it's added. Adding oil too fast produces droplets too large for the lecithin to coat — the emulsion breaks, and you get greasy, separated mayo.

**Fixing a broken emulsion**: if mayonnaise or hollandaise breaks (separates), start with a fresh yolk in a clean bowl and slowly whisk the broken emulsion into it. The fresh lecithin coats the existing oil droplets.

**Vinaigrette stability**: basic vinaigrette is a temporary emulsion (shaken) that separates quickly. Adding mustard, honey, or a little garlic creates a more stable emulsion that lasts. The ratio is approximately 3 parts oil to 1 part acid for taste; the emulsifier amount determines stability.

### Acids and Bases in Cooking

The pH of cooking environments changes flavor, texture, color, and chemical reactions.

**Acids (low pH):**
- Denature proteins (ceviche — acid "cooks" fish without heat)
- Preserve bright green chlorophyll pigment if added before cooking; destroy it if cooked with — the acid catalyzes chlorophyllin breakdown
- Preserve red/purple anthocyanins (red cabbage stays red with acid; turns blue-grey without)
- Activate baking soda (baking soda is alkaline; acid + baking soda → CO2 bubbles for leavening)
- Slow starch gelatinization
- Strengthen gluten (bread dough gets tighter in acidic sourdough)

**Alkaline environments (high pH):**
- Speed Maillard reaction (pretzels, adding baking soda to onions)
- Weaken gluten (too much baking soda in bread dough)
- Turn green vegetables dull and mushy (old trick of adding baking soda to peas — don't)
- Saponify fats (what happens in soap-making)

**Baking soda vs. baking powder:**
- **Baking soda**: pure sodium bicarbonate. Alkaline. Requires an acid in the batter (buttermilk, yogurt, lemon juice, brown sugar, honey) to react. Produces CO2 immediately when combined with acid + moisture. Use when batter has acid.
- **Baking powder**: baking soda + dry acid (cream of tartar or sodium aluminum sulfate) + starch. Self-contained reaction — requires only heat and moisture. Double-acting baking powder reacts twice: once when wet, once when heated. Use when batter has no significant acid.

### Fermentation

Microorganisms converting sugars into acids, gases, or alcohol. The science behind yogurt, bread, pickles, cheese, and more.

**Lactic acid fermentation**: bacteria (Lactobacillus) consume sugars and produce lactic acid. Lowers pH, preserves food, and develops tangy flavor. Yogurt, sour cream, buttermilk, kimchi, sauerkraut, sourdough starter.

**Alcoholic fermentation**: yeast consume sugars and produce ethanol and CO2. Bread rises because yeast CO2 is trapped in gluten network. Beer and wine produce the same reaction but in liquid form.

**Why fermented food tastes complex**: fermentation produces hundreds of flavor compounds — acids, esters, aldehydes — that don't exist in the raw ingredients. Sourdough bread has more complex flavor than yeasted bread for this reason.

**Kombucha SCOBY and sourdough starter**: mixed cultures of bacteria and yeast working together. The bacteria produce acid that the yeast tolerate (most organisms can't). They're stable ecosystems maintained by feeding and temperature.

## Key Considerations

**Understanding failure modes changes cooking.** Maillard failure: pan too cool, food too wet, pan overcrowded. Emulsion failure: oil added too fast, temperature too high (proteins over-cook). Gluten overdevelopment: over-mixing muffin batter. Every cooking failure has a physical cause — diagnosing it correctly means fixing it, not repeating it.

**Water is the limiting factor in browning.** If food is browning too slowly (boiling in its own liquid), increase heat, crowd less, or dry it better. If it's browning too fast, add moisture or drop heat. The relationship between water content and browning temperature is the governing variable.

**Small amounts of salt, acid, and sugar change chemistry.** Salt in bread dough controls yeast activity and strengthens gluten. Acid in milk causes curdling. Sugar slows starch gelatinization and delays Maillard (by competing for water). The chemistry explains why recipes are specific — changing one ingredient changes several reactions.

## Getting Started

1. **Run the browning experiment**: cook identical pieces of tofu or paneer — one patted very dry, one wet. Compare the browning. The mechanism is immediate and visible.
2. **Make emulsified vinaigrette**: compare a shaken oil-and-vinegar dressing to one with a teaspoon of Dijon added. The stability difference is the emulsifier at work.
3. **Read McGee's chapter on your current weakness**: *On Food and Cooking* (Harold McGee) is organized by ingredient and technique. Don't read it cover to cover — use it as a reference.

## Common Mistakes

**Crowding the pan.** Temperature drops, moisture accumulates, Maillard doesn't happen. This is the most common reason home-cooked food doesn't taste restaurant-quality.

**Adding emulsifying ingredients too fast.** Oil into mayonnaise, butter into a butter sauce — the rate matters. Fast addition overwhelms the emulsifier's capacity to coat droplets.

**Baking soda where baking powder is needed.** Without acid in the batter, baking soda doesn't react properly — metallic flavor, poor rise. Always check whether the recipe has acid.

**Treating coagulated proteins as reversible.** Once eggs are scrambled hard, they don't uncook. Once gluten is developed in muffins, you can't un-develop it. Protein coagulation is one-way.
