# Indian Cooking Basics
*The logic behind the cuisine — spice work, tadka, dal, roti, rice — techniques first, recipes second*

---

## What Is It

Indian cooking is a system of techniques applied to a set of core ingredients. Once you understand the system — how spices work, what tadka does, how a curry base is built — any dish becomes a variation on a pattern you already know, not a new set of instructions to memorize.

This file teaches the technique logic. Individual recipes are applications of these patterns. Learn the patterns; the recipes follow.

The fundamentals of heat control, seasoning, and mise en place are in [cooking/vegetarian-cooking-fundamentals.md](vegetarian-cooking-fundamentals.md) — read that first if you haven't.

## Why It Matters

For Romi: Indian cooking is the cuisine you grew up with, the one that maps best to a vegetarian diet, and the one where your protein staples (dal, paneer, chickpeas, soy chunks) live. Cooking it well means eating better protein, spending less on food, and being less dependent on meal prep variability.

It also directly connects to the farming knowledge domain — understanding what you're growing (lentils, chickpeas, tomatoes, leafy greens, spices) and how to cook it is the complete loop from soil to plate.

## Core Concepts

### Spice Logic — The Most Important Thing to Understand

Indian spices don't work the way most people think. You don't just add spices to food and stir. The technique of how and when spices are added determines the entire character of the dish.

**Whole vs ground spices:**
- **Whole spices** (cumin seeds, mustard seeds, cardamom pods, cloves, bay leaves): used in fat, at high heat, to extract fat-soluble flavor compounds. They bloom — the fat carries the flavor into the dish.
- **Ground spices** (coriander powder, cumin powder, turmeric, chili, garam masala): added later, usually with tomato or water. They need moisture to release their water-soluble compounds and time to cook through. Raw ground spices taste harsh and bitter.

**The sequence:**
1. Fat in hot pan
2. Whole spices bloom in fat (30 seconds to 2 minutes)
3. Aromatics (onion, ginger, garlic) cook down
4. Ground spices added and cooked with the aromatics (2–3 minutes) — this is critical, don't rush it
5. Tomato or liquid added, further cooking
6. Main ingredient added
7. Garam masala and fresh herb finish at the end

Garam masala is almost always a finishing spice — added near the end. Adding it at the beginning destroys the volatile aromatics that give it its character.

**The core spice pantry (the 10 you need):**

| Spice | Form | Primary use |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Cumin | Seeds + ground | Almost everything |
| Coriander | Ground (seeds optional) | Dal, sabzi, curry base |
| Turmeric | Ground | Color, mild flavor, health properties |
| Chili | Ground (Kashmiri for color, hot for heat) | All dishes |
| Mustard seeds | Whole | South Indian tadka, pickles |
| Garam masala | Ground blend | Finishing spice |
| Cardamom | Pods + ground | Rice, chai, desserts |
| Cumin seeds (jeera) | Whole | Tadka, raita, rice |
| Asafoetida (hing) | Powder | Dal tadka, replaces garlic/onion |
| Dried red chili | Whole | Tadka |

**Hing (asafoetida)**: a small amount (1/8 tsp) added to hot oil creates a garlic-onion depth without either. Used in Jain cooking, and excellent in dal tadka. Transformative in very small quantities.

### The Base (Masala) — The Technique Behind Every Curry

Virtually every North Indian curry and sabzi uses the same base construction:

**Fat → Whole spices → Onion → Ginger-garlic → Ground spices → Tomato → Main ingredient**

The quality of the base is the quality of the dish. Two failures that ruin dishes:

**1. Undercooked onions.** Onions need 12–18 minutes on medium heat to properly cook down and sweeten. Rushed onions (transparent but not golden) taste raw and sharp. Golden-brown onions create a sweet, deep base. Whenever a recipe says "cook until golden," believe it.

**2. Undercooked ground spices.** Ground spices added with wet ingredients (tomato, water) and not given 2–3 minutes to cook out will taste raw and harsh in the final dish. You can tell by smell — raw spices have a sharper, almost chemical note. Cooked spices smell rounded and integrated.

**The tomato stage:** once tomatoes go in, cook until the oil separates from the tomato-spice mixture and pools at the edges. This is called "bhunoing" — sautéing and stirring while the water evaporates. The oil separation is the signal that the base is cooked properly. Rushing past this stage produces acidic, watery curries.

### Tadka — Tempering

Tadka (also called chaunk or tarka) is the technique of heating whole spices in fat until they pop and bloom, then pouring this directly onto the finished dish. It's most often done at the end of cooking dal, raita, or rice — as a finishing step.

**Why it works:** heat rapidly extracts fat-soluble compounds from whole spices, creating an intense burst of flavor. Added hot to the dish at the end, it sits on top without being fully integrated — creating a concentrated hit of flavor in the first few bites, then distributing through the dish as it's stirred.

**Classic dal tadka:**
- Heat 1.5 tbsp ghee in a small pan until shimmering
- Add cumin seeds (they sizzle and pop), dried red chili, and a pinch of hing
- Optionally: sliced garlic (it turns golden quickly, watch it), or mustard seeds
- Once fragrant and slightly colored (30–60 seconds), pour directly onto the finished dal
- Cover the pot immediately — the steam captures the aroma

The ghee matters here more than in other applications — it carries and amplifies the flavor. Neutral oil produces a flat tadka.

**Other tadka applications:**
- Raita: cumin + chili in ghee, pour over yogurt-based raita
- Poha: mustard seeds + curry leaves + dried chili in oil, the starting step
- Khichdi: cumin + bay leaf + clove + cardamom at the start, sets the base

### Dal — The Most Important Dish to Master

Dal is the cornerstone of vegetarian Indian cooking. It's the primary protein vehicle, infinitely variable, cheap, stores well, and gets better with practice. A cook who makes excellent dal doesn't need much else.

**The basic types:**

| Dal | Character | Cooking time | Best for |
|-----|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Masoor (red lentil) | Earthy, breaks down fast | 15–20 min | Quick dal, silky texture |
| Moong (split mung) | Mild, digestible | 20–25 min | Khichdi, light everyday dal |
| Toor/arhar (pigeon pea) | Rich, needs pressure cooker | 20 min PC, 45+ min open | Classic dal tadka |
| Urad (black lentil) | Creamy, heavy | Long cook | Dal makhani (slow cooked) |
| Chana (split chickpea) | Nutty, holds shape | Long cook | Dal chana, thick curries |

**The pressure cooker is not optional for most dal.** Open pot cooking works for masoor and moong. For toor, chana dal, and whole lentils, a stovetop pressure cooker (or Instant Pot) reduces 45 minutes to 20. Buy one if you cook dal regularly.

**The ratio:** 1 cup dry dal : 3–4 cups water for pressure cooker, 1:5 for open pot. Skim the foam that rises in the first minutes of open-pot cooking — it's not harmful but makes the dal murky.

**Building a dal from scratch:**

1. Wash dal 2–3 times until water runs clear
2. Soak masoor/moong 20 min (not required but faster); soak toor/chana 2–4 hours
3. Cook dal until soft and broken down
4. While dal cooks: build the base (oil → cumin → onion → ginger-garlic → turmeric + chili → tomato → cook down)
5. Combine base with cooked dal, simmer 10 min to integrate
6. Finish with tadka, lemon juice, fresh coriander
7. Consistency: pourable but not watery. If too thick, add water; if too thin, simmer uncovered.

### Roti — The Skill That Takes a Month

Roti (chapati) is a flatbread made from atta (whole wheat flour) and water. The technique is simple; the execution takes practice.

**The dough:** atta + water + a pinch of salt. Ratio by weight: 2:1 flour to water approximately, but you adjust by feel. The dough should be soft — softer than you think, slightly tacky. Stiff dough produces tough roti. Knead 5–8 minutes until smooth; rest 20–30 minutes.

**The cook:** medium-high to high heat. A tawa (flat iron pan) is ideal; a cast iron skillet works. Roll thin (2–3mm), flour the rolling surface, keep it round (it comes with practice; don't worry about it at first). Cook 30–40 seconds per side until brown spots appear, then optionally finish directly on the flame for puffing.

**Why roti fails:**
- Dough too stiff: tough, crackers
- Pan not hot enough: no browning, dry texture
- Rolled too thick: doesn't cook through
- Not rested: dough tears while rolling

**The fix if you can't make roti yet:** frozen paratha or packaged whole wheat roti while you practice. Don't avoid Indian food because you can't make roti; roti is a practice skill.

### Rice — The Absorption Method

For everyday white rice (basmati or long grain):

1. Rinse 3–4 times until water runs clear — removes surface starch, prevents mushiness
2. Soak 20–30 minutes (for basmati — makes grains longer and more distinct)
3. Ratio: 1 cup rice : 1.5 cups water for stovetop
4. Bring to boil with a pinch of salt, reduce to lowest heat, cover tightly
5. Cook 12–15 minutes without lifting the lid
6. Remove from heat, rest covered 5 min, fluff with fork

The most common mistake: lifting the lid during cooking. The steam is doing the work — releasing it extends cook time and produces uneven results.

**Jeera rice (cumin rice):** blooms cumin seeds in ghee in the pot before adding washed rice and water. One step, transforms plain rice into something worth eating on its own.

**Brown rice:** 1:2 ratio, 40–45 minutes. Same method. Needs longer soak (1 hour) and longer cook.

### The Flavor Finishers

These go on at the end, not during cooking:

**Fresh coriander (cilantro):** added after the heat is off. Loses its volatile flavor compounds when cooked. Non-negotiable garnish for dal, sabzi, raita.

**Lemon/lime juice:** always at the end. Acid added during cooking loses brightness. Squeeze onto the finished dish.

**Garam masala:** a small pinch (1/4 tsp) stirred through at the end rounds out the dish with warmth and complexity. More than a small amount tastes perfumey.

**Kasuri methi (dried fenugreek leaves):** rub between palms before adding to release aroma. Added at the end to butter chicken, paneer masala, any cream-based dish. Creates the "restaurant dal makhani" quality that's hard to replicate without it.

## Key Considerations

**Smell is a more reliable guide than timers.** When aromatics are cooked, they smell sweet not sharp. When spices are properly cooked in, they smell integrated not raw. When dal is ready, it smells like dal. Cook by smell and taste, use timers as backup.

**The spice base can be made in bulk and frozen.** Onion-ginger-garlic-tomato base cooked down (before adding the main ingredient) freezes in portions. This is the biggest time-saver in Indian cooking — defrost a cube, add cooked dal or paneer, have a meal in 10 minutes.

**Fresh vs dried spices.** Ground spices lose potency after 6–12 months. If your coriander and cumin powder are from two years ago, your dishes are underperforming. Small quantities, replaced regularly.

**Ghee vs oil.** Ghee has a higher smoke point than butter and more flavor than neutral oil. Use it for tadka and finishing. Use neutral oil (mustard oil or vegetable) for base cooking where you're cooking aromatics for 15+ minutes at medium heat.

## Getting Started

1. Master one dal (start with masoor — fastest, most forgiving). Make it 5 times, varying the tadka.
2. Learn the base sequence once with a simple sabzi (aloo jeera or palak paneer). Understand when the base is done by sight and smell.
3. Add roti practice separately — it's its own skill that takes repetition. Don't make it harder by combining it with a new curry.
4. Build your spice pantry to the 10 essentials. Fresh, small quantities.
5. Once dal + sabzi + rice/roti work reliably: expand to chickpea dishes (chole), then paneer dishes.

## Common Mistakes

**Adding ground spices with the liquid.** Ground spices need fat and heat to cook through. Added to water, they don't bloom — they just stew and taste raw.

**Not cooking the onions long enough.** This ruins more Indian dishes than anything else. Golden-brown is the target.

**Skipping the tadka.** The dal is fine without it. With it, it's great. Takes 2 minutes. Don't skip it.

**Over-spicing to compensate for technique errors.** More spice doesn't fix undercooked aromatics or a rushed base. Fix the technique; the spice level will seem correct.

**Using old ground spices.** If you can't smell them clearly from 6 inches away, they're not doing anything in your food.
