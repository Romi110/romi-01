# Kitchen Budgeting
*Practical grocery math for scratch cooking: calculate real food cost, optimize bulk buying, manage pantry rotation, and decide when to make versus buy.*

---

## What Is It
Kitchen budgeting is the process of treating your kitchen like a small business: track the real cost of ingredients, understand how much each dish actually costs to make, and use that information to make smarter grocery and cooking decisions. It is not about frugality for its own sake. It is about making the cheapest path also the best one for flavor, nutrition, and time.

## Why It Matters
The grocery budget is one of the few monthly expenses you can change quickly and repeatedly. For a vegetarian kitchen, a modest improvement in food cost compound into hundreds of dollars saved each year. Better budget discipline also keeps your scratch-cooking habit sustainable: once you know which ingredients earn the savings and which are false economy, you stop wasting money on expensive convenience foods or pantry clutter.

## Core Concepts

### Cost per Serving
The most useful number in kitchen budgeting is cost per serving, not total bill. Calculate it by dividing the total purchase cost by the usable servings after accounting for waste.
- dry lentils, rice, and flour typically have the lowest cost per serving
- produce costs must account for trim and spoilage
- ready-made sauces and packaged convenience foods may look cheap until you compare them to homemade equivalents

### Bulk Buying
Bulk buying is the single biggest lever for lowering per-unit cost.
- buy dried legumes, whole spices, grains, and flour in larger bags when you can use them before they go stale
- stock onions, potatoes, carrots, and squash in the quantity you will eat in 10–14 days
- compare the unit price (per 100g or per liter) and ignore package size or branded marketing

### Pantry, Fridge, and Freezer Economics
Different storage zones have different real costs.
- pantry goods are low-cost per serving and low spoilage, making them the baseline of the budget
- fridge goods are higher cost and higher risk; use them quickly or turn them into cooked components
- freezer goods are the best place for batch-made stock, cooked legumes, and portioned meals

The kitchen budget is about moving value from the shelf to the fork before it spoils.

### Make versus Buy
Decide whether to make an item from scratch or buy it ready-made based on three factors:
- true cost of ingredients and time
- quality and flavor difference
- frequency of use

If you make a masala paste once a week and use it in five meals, it is almost always cheaper and better than buying a small jar. If you only need a single serving of a specialty condiment, buying it may be the better option.

### Meal Plan Discipline
Budgeting is not just arithmetic. It is also a planning system.
- write the week’s menu before shopping
- shop to the list, not to the name of the store
- use a “buy once, cook twice” mindset for ingredients that appear in multiple meals

### Equipment Cost and Total Cost of Ownership
The tool layer matters too. A good scale, a sharp knife, and a reliable pot reduce waste and make scratch cooking faster.
- buy the right cookware once and keep it; poor tools increase prep time and lower the value of the system
- compare the hours saved by a better tool against the cash spent on it

## Key Considerations

- local prices vary. The exact cost numbers should be your own, not a spreadsheet from someone else.
- seasonality is a budget ally: cheaper tomatoes, greens, and squash in season are the most powerful cost savings.
- bulk buying only works if you can use or preserve the item before it spoils; otherwise it is waste, not savings.
- the cost of your time matters. A weekly hour spent cooking a masala base is worthwhile only if it is reused several times.
- avoid “kitchen efficiency theater”: buying more gadgets or organizing containers without actually lowering meal costs.

## Getting Started

1. track one month of spending.
   - write down the cost of every grocery purchase, broken into staples, fresh produce, pantry, and opportunistic buys
2. build a simple unit-price table.
   - note price per 100g for lentils, rice, flour, tomatoes, onions, potatoes, yogurt, paneer, and spices
3. calculate cost per serving for your most common meals.
   - dal + rice, paneer curry, chole, khichdi, overnight oats
4. identify the cheapest repeats.
   - what will you make again next week because it is both cheap and worth eating?
5. adopt one budget rule.
   - batch cook one base each week, rotate inexpensive legumes, or buy only one fresh vegetable that can be used in 3 meals

## Common Mistakes

- ignoring unit prices. The package on the shelf can hide the real price per serving.
- not accounting for waste. Buying a large bag of greens is not cheaper if half the bag is trimmed away.
- overbuying bulk. A larger package only saves money if you use it before spoilage.
- paying for convenience disguised as savings. Pre-cut vegetables, ready-made sauces, and single-serve snacks often have the highest cost per serving.
- treating kitchen budgeting as a one-time audit. Prices change, and a budget system must be revisited every few months.
