# Motherbatching
*Budget-first batch cooking system for DIY vegetarian kitchens: one weekly base and reusable meal components that cut grocery expense, minimize waste, and make every meal faster to assemble.*

---

## What Is It
Motherbatching is the practice of cooking one large multi-purpose base each week and using it as the foundation for different meals instead of making each dish from scratch every time. The mother batch is not a finished meal. It is a set of reusable components — spice pastes, cooked legumes, stock, grains, sauces, yogurt, roasted vegetables — that turn inexpensive raw ingredients into five meal variations without buying convenience food.

## Why It Matters
For a vegetarian kitchen, the biggest cost is not the occasional splurge; it is the daily friction that pushes you toward expensive packaged food and takeout. Motherbatching shifts the work from multiple small cooking sessions into one efficient focused session while also lowering ingredient waste and making the fridge ready for real meals. It makes food spending predictable, reduces impulse grocery buys, and keeps home cooking practical instead of a weekend hobby.

## Core Concepts

### The Mother Batch
A mother batch is the prepared base every meal is built around. Typical components include:
- a spice base: onion-garlic-ginger aromatics, tomato paste, masala, curry paste, green chutney
- a liquid base: vegetable stock, coconut milk with water, unsalted broth
- cooked legumes: dal, chickpeas, rajma, soy chunks
- cooked grains: rice, quinoa, bulgur, barley
- a sauce or curry base: tomato-coconut sauce, sesame-soy dressing, red curry paste
- preserved accents: yogurt, pickles, roasted nuts, fried onions

Make two mother batches if you want variety: one savory base plus one bright or fresh element.

### Reuse, Not Repetition
The point is to reuse the base in different ways, not to eat the same dish five times.
- dal becomes soup, curry, khichdi, or dal fritters
- cooked rice becomes fried rice, stuffed peppers, or grain bowls
- tomato-coconut sauce becomes curry, shakshuka-style eggs, or vegetable stew

This is the practical difference between messy batch cooking and a system that stays useful all week.

### Cost Leverage
Motherbatching saves money in three ways:
- buy ingredients in bulk instead of paying for individual convenience items
- use low-cost staples (lentils, rice, onions, tomatoes, spices) as the meal engine
- reduce waste by turning scraps into stock, using stems in chutney, and freezing excess portions

A large pot of dal or a batch of sauce costs a fraction of the same meal from a restaurant or grocery meal kit.

### Component Assembly
Think of the week as building blocks, not finished plates.
- base block: dal, curry, stew, stock
- starch block: rice, roti, quinoa, roasted potatoes
- texture block: paneer, tofu, roasted chickpeas, fried onions
- freshness block: greens, yogurt, pickles, herbs

Every meal should combine 2–3 blocks. That is the practical assembly rule.

### Storage and Preservation
A mother batch only works if you store it correctly.
- fridge: fresh cooked dal, tempered sauces, marinated vegetables, yogurt
- freezer: stock, spice paste, cooked legumes, grain balls, dough
- pantry: dried spices, canned tomatoes, whole grains, pickling ingredients

Label containers with the date and contents. The system collapses when you cannot tell whether a container is 2 days old or 6 days old.

## Key Considerations

- fridge space is the real constraint. Use the containers you already own and choose components that fit them.
- refresh each meal with texture and acid. A dollop of yogurt, a squeeze of lemon, or a handful of herbs makes the same base feel different.
- stronger, fattier bases store better than delicate emulsions. Save fresh dressings and chopped herbs for last-minute assembly.
- whole spices and grinding your own are worth it if you will actually use the spice mix before it loses aroma.
- on a tight budget, prioritize cooked legumes and grains; consider premium ingredients like paneer or imported sauces as accents, not the foundation.

## Getting Started

1. plan one motherbatch day.
   - choose one base: dal with tadka, tomato-masala sauce, or green chutney
   - choose one starch: rice, quinoa, or roti dough
   - choose one texture provider: paneer, tofu, roasted chickpeas, or nuts
2. shop to a short list.
3. cook one large pot of the base, one grain batch, and one texture item.
4. portion the base into 3–4 fridge containers and freeze 1–2 smaller portions.
5. each meal, assemble: base + grain + fresh element + garnish.
6. on day 4 or 5, repurpose leftovers into a new format: soup, bowl, wrap, or stir-fry.

## Common Mistakes

- making too many different dishes in one session. Limit yourself to 2–3 core components and reuse them.
- trying to prep salads, cooked vegetables, and snacks all at once. The mother batch is the foundation; fresh elements should stay fresh.
- storing everything in one giant container. Portioning prevents waste and makes reheating faster.
- assuming a meal is done once the base is cooked. Texture and acid finish the plate.
- not labeling containers. The weekly system fails when you cannot tell how old the food is.
