# Egg Cookery
*Eggs as a technique subject — the science of temperature, emulsion, and structure that applies far beyond breakfast*

---

## What Is It

Egg cookery is the study of how to cook eggs precisely — and the science underneath each technique. Eggs are unusual: no other single ingredient requires you to understand protein coagulation, emulsification, aeration, and temperature control all at once. Master eggs and you've learned fundamental cooking science that applies to custards, sauces, baked goods, and dozens of other preparations.

This isn't a file about recipes. It's about understanding what eggs do and why, so every preparation — from a soft-boiled egg to a hollandaise — is reproducible and correctable.

---

## Why It Matters

Eggs are one of the most nutrient-dense, versatile, and affordable ingredients in a vegetarian kitchen. They're complete protein (all essential amino acids), high in choline, B12, fat-soluble vitamins, and healthy fats. For a vegetarian without meat, eggs are a high-value protein source that can anchor a meal or add richness to an otherwise incomplete dish. The ability to cook them well — in all their forms — is a foundational kitchen skill.

## Core Concepts

### The Science of Egg Proteins

An egg contains two distinct protein systems:

**Egg white (albumen)**: primarily water (~88%) and protein (~10%). The main proteins — ovalbumin, conalbumin, ovomucin — are coiled chains that unfold (denature) and then bond together (coagulate) with heat. This is what makes the white go from clear and liquid to white and solid.

- White proteins begin denaturing at **140°F/60°C**
- White proteins fully coagulate at **185°F/85°C**
- Between these temperatures, whites go from runny → silky → just set → rubbery → tough and dry

**Egg yolk**: fat, protein, water, and lecithin (a phospholipid — the emulsifier). Yolk proteins are different from white proteins and coagulate at a lower temperature range:
- Yolk begins thickening at **149°F/65°C**
- Yolk fully sets at **158°F/70°C**

**The implication**: the yolk solidifies at a *lower* temperature than the white fully sets. A hard-boiled egg cooked too long overcooks the yolk (grey-green ring = iron sulfide forming at the yolk surface when hydrogen sulfide from whites reacts with iron in yolks — caused by high temperature, long time, or both). The goal for most preparations is to hit the yolk window *before* the white goes rubbery.

**Temperature is everything.** This is why sous vide changed egg cookery — you can hold an egg at exactly 145°F/63°C for an hour and get a custard-set yolk with a barely set white, impossible to achieve with boiling water.

### Soft-Boiled and Hard-Boiled Eggs: The Technique

**Starting with cold vs. boiling water:**
- **Cold water start**: eggs heat gradually, more even cooking throughout. Time starts from cold → boil.
- **Boiling water start**: eggs go into already-boiling water. More abrupt heat, crack risk is higher (especially if eggs are cold from refrigerator). But timing from the moment of boiling is more precise. Most professional preference: boiling water start.

**Boiling water start timing** (large eggs, straight from refrigerator):
- **5 minutes**: very runny white, warm runny yolk. Barely soft-boiled.
- **6 minutes**: set white, fully liquid yolk. Classic jammy soft-boiled for ramen.
- **7 minutes**: set white, yolk set on outside, liquid in center. The sweet spot for most uses.
- **8 minutes**: fully set white, yolk mostly set with slight softness in center. Jammy but cohesive.
- **10 minutes**: firm white, chalky but not yet grey yolk. Medium-boiled.
- **12+ minutes**: hard-boiled. Firm throughout. Stop at 12 to avoid the grey ring.

**The ice bath**: stop cooking immediately by plunging into ice water for 1–2 minutes. Prevents carryover cooking, makes peeling easier (the cold water contracts the egg slightly from the shell).

**Peeling soft-boiled eggs**: tap the wide end (air pocket), peel under running cold water. Fresh eggs are harder to peel than older eggs (the membrane clings to the white in very fresh eggs — CO2 hasn't had time to escape through the shell). Use week-old eggs for boiled preparation.

### Poached Eggs: Controlled Submersion

Poaching produces a delicate, sauce-like white surrounding a runny yolk — impossible with a pan.

**The technique:**
1. Water at **180–190°F/82–88°C** — simmering, not boiling. Boiling agitates too much and shreds the white.
2. Add a splash of vinegar (1 tsp white vinegar per quart) — the acid accelerates white protein coagulation, helping the white hold together before it disperses.
3. Create a gentle swirl in the water — the rotation wraps the white around the yolk as the egg enters.
4. Crack the egg into a small bowl first, then slide into the water gently.
5. Cook **3–4 minutes** for a runny yolk.
6. Remove with a slotted spoon; drain on a towel.

**Fresh eggs poach better**: the white of a fresh egg is thicker and holds together more tightly than an old egg's thinner white. For poaching, use the freshest eggs you have.

**Batch poaching**: poach eggs 90 seconds short of done, transfer to cold water, refrigerate. Reheat in hot water for 60 seconds when needed. Used in restaurants to serve poached eggs to order. Works at home for meal prep.

### Scrambled Eggs: Temperature and Patience

The most common egg preparation and the one most often done wrong. Overcooked scrambled eggs are rubbery, wet, and grey. Properly cooked scrambled eggs are silky, soft, and barely set.

**The key variable**: temperature and time. High heat → eggs seize quickly, produce large curds, become rubbery. Low heat + patience → slow coagulation, small curds, silky texture.

**Gordon Ramsay method (low and slow):**
1. Crack 3 eggs into a cold pan. Add cold butter (tablespoon).
2. On medium-low heat, stir constantly with a rubber spatula, moving eggs in figure-8s.
3. Eggs will very slowly begin to thicken. Pull the pan off heat periodically — residual heat will continue cooking; put back on if needed.
4. When curds are small, barely set, and shiny — still looks slightly underdone — pull off heat.
5. Season with salt, add crème fraîche or a little cold butter for richness, stir through.
6. Serve immediately — they continue cooking off heat.

**American diner style**: higher heat, faster, larger curds, still not dry. Season *after* cooking (salt draws moisture out of raw eggs if added before). Butter the pan generously.

**Salt timing for scrambled eggs**: salt added to raw beaten eggs before cooking draws water out of the proteins (osmotically), producing a slightly wetter, more tender curd. This is actually a feature, not a bug — pre-salting by 15 minutes produces noticeably more tender eggs. Heavily contested among cooks; the scientific mechanism is real.

**The French omelet**: the most technically demanding egg preparation. A thin, rolled egg sheet with no color (no browning), barely set, filled and rolled tightly. Made quickly in a very hot pan with constant agitation, then rolled with the wrist. Requires practice. The point is controlling the temperature to set but not brown the eggs while moving the pan. It's the technique that proves egg control.

### Fried Eggs: Heat and Fat

**Sunny-side up**: cook on one side only, low-medium heat, covered (steam from covered lid sets the white without flipping). Yolk remains runny. Whites should be fully set — no translucent patches.

**Over easy / over medium / over hard**: flip briefly. Easy = yolk runny. Medium = yolk partially set. Hard = yolk fully set. Brief cooking on the second side.

**Basted eggs**: high heat, spoon hot butter continuously over the white and yolk as it cooks. Sets the white on top without flipping. Produces a thin, white film over the yolk from the hot fat. Restaurant technique for speed and control.

**The crispy-bottomed fried egg**: high heat, generous oil, drop egg into the hot fat. The white immediately sets and crisps at the edges while the yolk remains runny. Popular in Spanish (huevo frito), Indian, and Southeast Asian cooking. The crisped edges are a texture contrast — Maillard reaction on the protein of the egg white.

### Eggs as Emulsifier: Mayonnaise and Hollandaise

The lecithin in egg yolks is one of the most powerful natural emulsifiers. See [cooking/food-science.md](food-science.md) for emulsification mechanics.

**Mayonnaise**: yolk + acid (lemon juice or vinegar) + oil, added very slowly while whisking. The yolk lecithin coats each oil droplet, creating a stable oil-in-water emulsion. One egg yolk can emulsify up to 1 cup of oil. If you add oil faster than the lecithin can coat the droplets, the emulsion breaks — greasy, separated.

**Fixing broken mayo**: add a teaspoon of water or mustard to a clean bowl. Drizzle the broken emulsion in slowly while whisking — the water provides a new continuous phase, and the existing lecithin can re-emulsify.

**Hollandaise**: butter emulsified into egg yolks with lemon. The yolks are first cooked gently over a double boiler to thicken slightly (begin denaturation — creating more protein surface area for fat attachment), then clarified butter is whisked in slowly. Temperature matters: too cool and the emulsion won't form; too hot and the eggs scramble.

**Blender hollandaise**: a shortcut that works. Blend yolks + lemon, then drizzle in hot clarified butter with blender running. The blender provides the agitation that whisking does manually. Faster and more forgiving.

### Eggs as Structure: Custard and Quiche

When eggs are combined with dairy and heated slowly, they form a gel — this is custard. The egg proteins coagulate slowly in the presence of the dairy's water, creating a smooth, silky, set structure that holds its shape.

**Ratio**: a simple baked custard is approximately 1 egg (or 2 yolks) per ½ cup liquid. More egg = firmer set. More yolk = richer, creamier.

**Baking temperature**: always low. 325°F/165°C or lower. The dairy dilutes the egg protein concentration, requiring longer, gentler heat to coagulate without curdling. An oven too hot produces custard that weeps liquid (protein contracts too quickly, squeezing out water — syneresis).

**Water bath (bain-marie)**: baking custard in a pan of hot water moderates the temperature, preventing the edges from setting before the center. Essential for crème brûlée, flan, and baked cheesecake.

**Quiche**: a savory custard baked in a pastry shell. Standard ratio: 3 eggs + 1 cup cream per 9-inch tart. Fillings should be pre-cooked and dry — wet fillings release water and produce watery quiche. Bake at 350°F until just barely set at the center — it will firm as it cools.

### Eggs as Aeration: Meringue and Soufflé

Egg whites can be beaten into foam — protein films surround air bubbles, trapping them. Heat sets the foam into structure.

**Meringue stages:**
- **Soft peaks**: whites are opaque and hold a peak that falls over. For folding into soufflé or pavlova.
- **Medium peaks**: peak just barely holds. For most meringue uses.
- **Stiff peaks**: glossy, hold a firm peak when whisk is lifted. For meringue cookies or Italian meringue.
- **Over-beaten**: dry, grainy, starts separating. Unrepairable. Add another egg white and start folding.

**What inhibits foaming**: any trace of fat (yolk, greasy bowl, oily spatula). Fat breaks down the protein film that stabilizes the bubbles. Clean bowl, clean whisk, no yolk contamination.

**Cream of tartar** (tartaric acid): stabilizes egg white foam by lowering pH, which increases protein denaturation rate and foam stability. ⅛ teaspoon per egg white.

**French soufflé**: béchamel or pastry cream base (flavor carrier) + egg yolks folded into stiffly beaten whites. The whites provide lift; the base provides flavor. Bake immediately in a hot oven — the steam + air expansion puffs the soufflé. Serve immediately; it falls within 2–3 minutes as the steam condenses.

## Key Considerations

**Freshness matters differently for different preparations.** Fresh eggs: best for frying and poaching (tighter white). Week-old eggs: better for hard-boiling (easier peeling). Very old eggs float in water (air pocket expanded) — don't use.

**Room temperature vs. cold eggs**: for baking (cakes, custards), room temperature eggs incorporate more easily and produce a better emulsion. For boiling, cold eggs from the refrigerator need an extra minute of cooking time. For scrambled, cold eggs on cold fat (Gordon Ramsay method) give you control.

**Egg size matters in baking.** Recipes are calibrated for large eggs (US). Extra-large or medium eggs will change liquid/fat ratios in baked goods. Weight matters more than size — a large egg is approximately 50g, a medium 44g, extra-large 56g.

## Getting Started

1. **Soft-boil an egg precisely**: boiling water, 7 minutes, ice bath, peel, taste. Adjust timing by 30 seconds until you find your preferred texture. This builds the temperature intuition.
2. **Make mayo from scratch**: one yolk, teaspoon of Dijon, squeeze of lemon, drizzle in ¾ cup oil while whisking. The control required forces you to understand emulsification physically.
3. **Practice French scrambled eggs**: 3 eggs, cold pan, low heat, constant stirring. The exercise is in resisting the urge to increase heat.

## Common Mistakes

**High heat for scrambled eggs.** High heat = large, rubbery curds. The scramble cooks faster but worse. Lower heat, more patience, better result.

**Overcooking hard-boiled eggs.** Twelve minutes maximum from boiling water start for large eggs. The grey ring is overcooked — lower the time.

**Dropping cold eggs into boiling water without care.** The thermal shock cracks shells. Either bring eggs to room temperature first (10 minutes on the counter), or lower them in with a spoon.

**Greasy equipment for meringue.** Any fat contamination collapses the foam. Wipe the bowl and whisk with a cut lemon or vinegar first.
