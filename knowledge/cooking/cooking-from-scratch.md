# Cooking From Scratch
*Stocks, sauces, doughs, ferments — the base skills that make everything else easier and better*

---

## What Is It

Cooking from scratch means making the foundational elements yourself rather than buying them pre-made — stocks instead of bouillon cubes, bread instead of packaged loaves, yogurt instead of store-bought, ferments instead of jarred pickles. These are the skills one layer below recipe cooking.

The benefit is not purity or frugality (though both apply). It's that understanding how foundations are made lets you fix failures, substitute intelligently, and improve everything built on top of them. A cook who knows how to make stock understands why soup has depth. A cook who has made bread dough understands gluten and why some doughs are sticky.

This file covers the five foundational skill areas: stocks, sauces, doughs, ferments, and condiments. The general technique layer is in [cooking/vegetarian-cooking-fundamentals.md](vegetarian-cooking-fundamentals.md). The cuisine-specific applications are in [cooking/indian-cooking-basics.md](indian-cooking-basics.md).

## Why It Matters

The four compounding benefits of scratch cooking:

1. **Quality ceiling rises.** A dish is only as good as its worst ingredient. Store-bought stock is mostly salt and flavoring. Homemade stock is gelatin, minerals, and real flavor — it changes the texture and depth of anything you cook in it.

2. **Waste drops significantly.** Vegetable scraps make stock. Overripe fruit makes jam. Stale bread makes breadcrumbs or panzanella. Scratch cooking is what closes the loop between produce and plate.

3. **Connects to the farming system.** Growing food means you have fresh produce, herbs, and excess in season. Scratch cooking — fermentation, preservation, stocks — is how you use all of it. This is the cooking-farming loop.

4. **The skills compound.** Making one bread dough teaches you how all doughs work. Making one ferment teaches you how all fermentation works. The tenth thing is much easier than the first.

## Core Concepts

### Stocks and Broths — The Flavor Infrastructure

Stock is liquid extracted by long simmering of vegetables, aromatics, and optionally bones (not covered here) in water. It is the base of soups, stews, braises, sauces, and grain cooking. Cooking rice in vegetable stock instead of water produces a completely different dish.

**Vegetable stock:** the simplest and fastest stock. Uses what you have.

*Standard aromatics for vegetable stock:*
- Onion or leek (halved, no need to peel)
- Carrot
- Celery
- Garlic cloves (smashed)
- Bay leaf, peppercorns, thyme, parsley stems
- Mushroom stems (optional — add umami significantly)

*What not to add:* brassicas (broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts) — they turn the stock bitter and sulfurous after long cooking. Starchy vegetables (potato) — they cloud the stock. Beets — they turn everything red.

**Method:**
1. Roughly chop vegetables — no need for precision, this all gets strained
2. Sauté aromatics in a small amount of oil for 5 minutes (optional, adds depth)
3. Add cold water, bring to a gentle simmer — never a rolling boil (boiling makes stock cloudy and bitter)
4. Simmer uncovered 45–60 minutes
5. Strain through a fine mesh strainer; press gently on solids
6. Season lightly — salt after straining, not during

**The scrap bag method:** keep a bag in the freezer for vegetable scraps — onion skins, carrot peels, celery tops, parsley stems, mushroom stems. When full, make stock. Zero waste, near-zero effort, continuous supply.

**Storage:** 5 days refrigerated, 3 months frozen. Freeze in 2-cup portions (the amount most recipes call for).

### Sauces — Building Blocks, Not Just Accompaniments

Classical French cooking organizes sauces into "mother sauces" — five foundations from which hundreds of sauces derive. Most Indian and Asian cooking has analogous base structures. Understanding the logic lets you build sauces without recipes.

**The five structures (simplified for practical use):**

**1. The cooked tomato sauce**
Fat + aromatics + tomatoes + time. The base for pasta sauce, shakshuka, Indian curry base, shakshuka, tomato soup. Variations: more garlic (Italian), ginger + spices (Indian), harissa + cumin (North African). One technique, every cuisine's tomato.

**2. The emulsification sauce (vinaigrette/aioli logic)**
Fat + acid + an emulsifier (mustard, egg yolk, lecithin from garlic). Oil and water don't mix — an emulsifier bridges them. A salad dressing, a mayo, a hollandaise are all emulsification variants. The skill: adding oil slowly while whisking.

**3. The reduction sauce**
Cooking liquid down concentrates flavor and thickens through evaporation. Deglaze a pan after sautéing with wine, stock, or tamarind water, then reduce. Works in Western and Indian cooking. The pan fond (browned bits) dissolves into the sauce and is the best part.

**4. The dairy sauce (béchamel logic)**
Butter + flour roux, cooked out, then milk added and whisked smooth. The foundation of mac and cheese, white sauce pasta, gratin. Swap butter for ghee, add spices, and you have the base of several Indian cream curries.

**5. The puréed sauce**
Vegetables or legumes cooked until soft, then blended. Roasted red pepper sauce, puréed dal, blended soup. The technique: cook until very soft, blend while hot (with a lid held down firmly — hot liquid expands in a blender), season after.

### Doughs — Flour + Water + Understanding

All doughs are flour, water, fat, and sometimes leavening. The differences between roti, pizza dough, pasta, and puff pastry are ratios and technique, not separate skills.

**The two key variables in dough:**
- **Gluten development**: gluten forms when flour proteins are hydrated and worked. More kneading = more gluten = chewier, more elastic. Minimal mixing = less gluten = tender, crumbly.
- **Hydration**: more water = more extensible, stickier, harder to handle, better final texture (higher hydration bread has more open crumb). Less water = stiffer, easier to handle, denser.

**The three doughs worth mastering:**

**Roti/chapati dough** (from [cooking/indian-cooking-basics.md](indian-cooking-basics.md)):
- Atta + water + salt. Soft, slightly tacky. Low fat, unleavened.
- Low gluten development needed — 5 minutes kneading. Rest 20–30 min.

**Basic yeast bread / pizza dough:**
- Flour + water + yeast + salt + oil.
- Ratios (baker's percentages): 100% flour, 65–70% water, 2% salt, 1% yeast, 3% oil (pizza)
- Active dry yeast: bloom in warm water (105–110°F) with a pinch of sugar first. Instant yeast: add directly to flour.
- Knead 8–10 minutes (by hand) until smooth and passes the windowpane test (stretch a small piece thin — it should be nearly transparent without tearing).
- First rise: 1–2 hours until doubled. Shape. Second rise: 30–45 min. Bake.
- The one variable to control first: don't kill the yeast with hot water (>115°F kills it; <95°F doesn't activate it well).

**Pasta dough (egg pasta):**
- Flour + eggs. 100g flour : 1 large egg, per serving. No water usually.
- Mix, knead 8–10 min until smooth and elastic. Rest wrapped 30 min minimum.
- Roll thin (pasta machine or rolling pin). Cut to shape.
- Eggs provide fat and protein; no yeast, no leavening.

### Fermentation — The Living Food Foundation

Fermentation is controlled microbial transformation: bacteria, yeast, or fungi convert sugars and starches into acids, alcohols, and CO2. The results are probiotic, preserved, and more nutritious than the raw ingredients.

This file covers home fermentation fundamentals. The farming application (kombucha, kimchi, lacto-fermentation at scale) is in [farming/fermentation.md](../farming/fermentation.md).

**Yogurt — the starter ferment:**
Yogurt is the simplest ferment: milk + live culture + warmth + time. Making it at home produces better yogurt for less money, and teaching yourself this teaches you how all fermentation works.

*Method:*
1. Heat milk to 180°F (just before boiling) — kills competing bacteria, changes protein structure
2. Cool to 105–115°F — the temperature range where lactobacillus thrives but isn't killed
3. Add 2 tbsp live-culture yogurt (the starter) per quart of milk. Stir thoroughly.
4. Keep warm and undisturbed for 6–8 hours. Options: wrap in a towel, use the oven with just the light on (maintains ~100°F), use an Instant Pot's "yogurt" setting.
5. Refrigerate to set firm. The longer it incubates, the tangier it gets.

The culture perpetuates itself — save 2 tbsp of each batch as the starter for the next. One purchase starts an indefinitely self-sustaining supply.

**Lacto-fermented vegetables (the everything technique):**
Vegetables + salt + time. No heat, no vinegar. The naturally occurring lactobacillus on vegetable surfaces consume sugars and produce lactic acid, preserving the vegetables and producing probiotics.

*Brine method (for whole or large-cut vegetables):*
- 2% brine: 20g salt per liter of water
- Pack vegetables in a jar, submerge in brine, weigh down so everything stays below the surface (a zip-lock bag filled with brine works as a weight)
- Ferment at room temperature 3–7 days (faster in summer, slower in winter)
- Taste daily after day 3. Move to refrigerator when it tastes right.

*Direct salt method (for shredded vegetables — kimchi, sauerkraut):*
- Shred cabbage, add 2% of its weight in salt, massage until liquid releases
- Pack tightly into a jar; the liquid should cover the cabbage
- Ferment 5–14 days, pushing vegetables below liquid daily

**Sourdough starter (the commitment ferment):**
A living colony of wild yeast and lactobacillus maintained in flour and water. Used for bread, pancakes, flatbreads. Makes bread with real flavor, better digestibility, and long shelf life.

*Starting one:*
- Day 1: mix 50g flour + 50g water in a jar. Cover loosely.
- Days 2–7: discard half daily, feed with fresh 50g flour + 50g water. Keep at room temperature.
- After 5–7 days: the starter is active if it's bubbly, rises after feeding, has a sour-yeasty smell, and doubles in 4–8 hours after feeding.

*Maintaining:* if baking weekly, feed twice a day at room temperature. If baking occasionally, store in the refrigerator and feed once a week.

The discard (the half you remove daily) can be used in pancakes, crackers, flatbreads — it doesn't rise but adds flavor and is not waste.

### Condiments — Make These, Stop Buying Them

A few condiments are dramatically better homemade and take very little time:

**Ghee:**
Simmer unsalted butter on low heat, skim the foam, cook until the milk solids settle and brown at the bottom (20–30 min). Strain through cheesecloth. The result has no dairy solids — higher smoke point than butter, longer shelf life (months unrefrigerated in a cool place), and a nutty depth that store-bought often doesn't have. Making it at home from good butter costs less than specialty store ghee.

**Garam masala:**
Toast whole spices (cumin, coriander, cardamom, cloves, cinnamon, black pepper, bay leaf) in a dry pan until fragrant, cool, grind in a spice grinder. Takes 10 minutes. Lasts 3–6 months. Freshly ground garam masala tastes nothing like the pre-ground version — the volatile aromatics are alive.

**Pickles (quick fridge pickles):**
Vegetable + 1:1 vinegar to water + salt + sugar + heat = fridge pickle in 30 minutes. No canning required. Sliced cucumbers, onions, daikon, green chilies. Lasts 2–4 weeks refrigerated.

## Key Considerations

**The scrap-bag approach prevents waste and builds stock habits.** The moment you start keeping a scrap bag in the freezer, you stop throwing away onion skins and carrot peels and start seeing them as future stock.

**Fermentation is more forgiving than cooking.** You can't under-boil a stock by 2 degrees and ruin it the way you can overcook a sauce. Fermentation has a wide margin — your yogurt will be fine if incubated at 108°F instead of 110°F. Start here if cooking feels overwhelming.

**Dough knowledge transfers between cuisines.** Roti, pita, naan, pizza, focaccia, and tortillas are all variations of the same flour-water-salt-fat-leavening logic. Learn one well; the others are modifications.

**The time investment is front-loaded.** Making stock takes 90 minutes but 10 minutes of active work. Making yogurt takes 8 hours but 5 minutes of active work. Batch production — large quantities made at once — is what makes from-scratch sustainable with a full-time job.

## Getting Started

1. Start a yogurt batch — it's the fastest and most useful skill here. Buy one container of full-fat plain yogurt with live cultures; use it as your first starter.
2. Start a scrap bag in the freezer. When it's full, make stock.
3. Make one lacto-fermented vegetable (cucumbers or cabbage). Let it ferment for 5 days. Taste it and understand what fermentation does.
4. Learn the basic yeast dough for pizza (or bread). Make it twice in one month to understand how yeast behaves.
5. Grind one batch of fresh garam masala. Compare it to store-bought.

## Common Mistakes

**Boiling stock instead of simmering.** Rapid boiling makes stock cloudy, bitter, and less clean-tasting. Low, gentle heat extracts flavor without extracting harsh compounds.

**Killing yeast with hot water.** The most common bread failure. Use a thermometer until you can judge temperature by touch.

**Fermenting without keeping vegetables submerged.** Exposed to air, vegetables mold rather than ferment. Weight everything below the brine.

**Starting sourdough and abandoning it after two days.** It looks like nothing is happening for 2–3 days. It takes 5–7 days for wild yeast to colonize and become active. Commit to the full week before concluding it isn't working.

**Making large batches of dough you don't use.** Fresh pasta dough should be made in the quantity you'll use that day. Bread dough can be refrigerated for several days (in fact, cold fermentation improves flavor). Know which doughs hold and which don't before scaling.

## Resources

- *Salt, Fat, Acid, Heat* — Samin Nosrat. The sauce sections are the best explanation of sauce logic for home cooks.
- *Tartine Bread* — Chad Robertson. The definitive home sourdough reference. Start here after you have an active starter.
