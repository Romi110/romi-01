# Infusion Bases
*A practical DIY flavor system for batch cooking: alcohol and vinegar infusions, flavored oils, spice pastes, and the reusable bases that make meal assembly faster and cheaper.*

---

## What Is It
Infusion bases are concentrated flavor preparations made ahead of time and stored for later use. They are the cooking equivalent of a software library: one prepared base can be pulled into many dishes. The most useful infusion bases are alcohol- or vinegar-based extracts, oil infusions, and spice pastes.

## Why It Matters
A major friction point in home cooking is building flavor from scratch every time. Infusion bases remove that friction by letting you add depth to a dish with a single spoonful. They also extend the value of inexpensive ingredients: a batch of chili-garlic oil or a jar of ginger-garlic paste multiplies into many meals, reducing both time and grocery waste.

## Core Concepts

### What Counts as an Infusion Base
Useful bases are concentrated, storable, and versatile.
- oil infusions: chili oil, garlic oil, toasted sesame oil, cumin oil
- vinegar infusions: citrus zest vinegar, ginger vinegar, herb vinegar
- spice pastes: ginger-garlic paste, green chutney, curry paste, harissa
- taste extracts: vanilla extract, cardamom extract, coffee extract

### Storage and Shelf Life
Different bases have different lifespans.
- alcohol extracts: indefinite if topped up and kept sealed
- oil infusions: 1–2 weeks in the fridge for fresh aromatics; 1 month if made from dried herbs and toasted spices
- vinegar infusions: 2–3 months in a cool dark place
- spice pastes: 1–2 weeks refrigerated, or freeze in tablespoon portions for longer storage

Storage matters more than the recipe. The same garlic oil will be safe and flavorful only if kept cold and used before it turns rancid.

### Flavor Leverage
Infusion bases concentrate flavor into a format that is easy to use across meals.
- a spoonful of chili oil improves stir-fries, soups, and roasted vegetables
- a spoonful of curry paste turns plain cooked lentils into a meal
- a splash of citrus vinegar brightens sauces, salads, and grain bowls
- a jar of ginger-garlic paste saves 10–15 minutes on every savory Indian-style dish

This is the practical leverage: one batch makes many meals.

### Building a Pantry System
The goal is not to have every base, but to have the right few.
- start with one neutral base: ginger-garlic paste or green chutney
- add one spicy base: chili oil or harissa
- add one bright base: citrus vinegar or herb-infused vinegar
- add one sweet/aromatic base: vanilla extract or cinnamon oil

Combine them with cooked grains, legumes, roasted vegetables, and fresh greens.

### Making It Practical
Do not confuse batch cooking with batch hoarding. A successful infusion base system keeps only what you will use in 2–4 weeks.
- make enough for two to three meals of real use
- freeze small portions of pastes so they do not spoil before use
- rotate bases with the season: citrus vinegar in summer, warm spice paste in winter

## Key Considerations

- choose your storage containers carefully. Glass is best, and a small funnel makes bottling easier.
- keep a list of bases on the fridge door. If you cannot name what is in the jar, you will not use it.
- match bases to your cooking style. If you cook mostly Indian food, invest in spice pastes. If you cook more salads and bowls, invest in vinegar and oil infusions.
- do not make a batch of every base at once. One or two good bases per week is enough.
- understand the safety rules for oil infusions. Keep refrigerated, and use acid or dried aromatics when you are not using the oil quickly.

## Getting Started

1. choose one base to make this week.
   - ginger-garlic paste, chili oil, or citrus vinegar are the best first projects.
2. gather ingredients and jars.
   - 1–2 small glass jars with tight lids
   - neutral oil, vinegar, fresh aromatics, whole spices
3. make the base and store it properly.
4. plan three meals that will use the base.
5. use or freeze the leftover portion within the storage window.

## Common Mistakes

- making too much and letting it spoil. Smaller batches are better than large jars that sit unused.
- using raw garlic in oil and storing it at room temperature. This is a food-safety risk if the oil is not properly acidified or refrigerated.
- assuming all infusions are interchangeable. Chili oil is not the same as curry paste; use each base for its intended purpose.
- never labeling jars. If you do not know what it is, you will not use it before it goes bad.
- treating the jar as the product. The value is in the meals it completes, not the weight of the jar.
