# Galaxies
*Gravitationally bound systems of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter — the fundamental building blocks of the universe's large-scale structure*

---

## What Is It

A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system containing stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas and dust, and dark matter. The numbers are staggering by design: the observable universe contains an estimated 2 trillion galaxies. A typical galaxy contains hundreds of billions of stars. The Milky Way, our home, holds between 200 and 400 billion.

Galaxies are not static objects. They form, evolve, collide, merge, and eventually die — on timescales of billions of years. Understanding them means understanding the large-scale architecture of everything that exists: how matter organized itself from a nearly uniform early universe into the structured, clustered cosmos we observe today.

## Why It Matters

This is not practical knowledge in the way farming or finance is. It is a different kind of essential.

Stoics practiced the *view from above* — Marcus Aurelius repeatedly returned to the smallness of human affairs when seen from a cosmic vantage. Modern astronomy makes this literal. When you understand what a galaxy actually is — the distances, the timescales, the number of stars — the weight you assign to ordinary human anxieties shifts. Not because the problems become unreal, but because the frame of reference changes. This is what Stoics were reaching for with metaphor; astronomy gives you the actual fact.

There's also this: the atoms in your body were forged in stars. The carbon, oxygen, iron — all synthesized in stellar interiors and distributed by supernovae across galaxies over billions of years. Understanding galaxies is, in a literal sense, understanding your own deep history.

## Core Concepts

### Types of Galaxies

**Spiral galaxies** — Flat rotating disks with a central bulge and spiral arms extending outward. The arms are not fixed structures — they're density waves, like a traffic jam that moves through the disk, triggering star formation as gas compresses while passing through. The Milky Way is a barred spiral (SBbc in the Hubble classification), meaning it has a central bar structure connecting to its arms.

Spirals are rich in gas and dust — the raw material for new star formation. Young, hot, blue stars dominate the arms. Older, cooler, red and yellow stars populate the central bulge.

**Elliptical galaxies** — Smooth, featureless, roughly egg-shaped or spherical systems. Little to no gas or dust remaining; almost no new star formation. Composed mostly of old, red stars. Range from giant ellipticals (the largest galaxies in the universe, sometimes containing trillions of stars) to small dwarf ellipticals. Often found at the centers of galaxy clusters, formed by repeated mergers.

**Lenticular galaxies** — Intermediate between spirals and ellipticals. Have a disk and bulge like spirals but no spiral arms and little star formation. Think of a spiral that has used up its gas.

**Irregular galaxies** — No regular shape. Often the result of gravitational disruption by a nearby larger galaxy. The Large and Small Magellanic Clouds, dwarf satellite galaxies of the Milky Way, are examples.

**Dwarf galaxies** — Not a shape category but a size one. Contain millions to a few billion stars (vs. the hundreds of billions in large spirals). The most common type of galaxy by number. Most exist as satellites orbiting larger galaxies; the Milky Way has dozens.

### The Milky Way — Our Galaxy

You are located about 26,000 light-years from the center of a barred spiral galaxy roughly 100,000 light-years in diameter. The Sun sits in a minor arm called the Orion Arm, somewhat between the center and the edge.

**Structure:**
- **Central bar**: a bar-shaped concentration of stars through the galactic center, 27,000 light-years long
- **Spiral arms**: four major arms (Norma, Scutum-Centaurus, Perseus, Carina-Sagittarius), plus minor arms
- **Central bulge**: dense region of older stars and gas surrounding the galactic center
- **Galactic halo**: a roughly spherical cloud of older stars, globular clusters, and dark matter extending far beyond the disk
- **Sagittarius A\***: the supermassive black hole at the center, approximately 4 million solar masses. First directly imaged by the Event Horizon Telescope in 2022.

The Milky Way orbits within the Local Group — a collection of about 54 galaxies dominated by the Milky Way and Andromeda (M31). The Local Group itself is part of the Virgo Supercluster, which is part of the Laniakea Supercluster — a structure 520 million light-years across containing about 100,000 galaxies.

The Milky Way and Andromeda are on a collision course. In approximately 4.5 billion years they will begin to merge, producing a new elliptical galaxy (sometimes called Milkomeda). Most stars won't collide — the distances between individual stars within galaxies are vast — but the gravitational interaction will reshape both completely.

### Supermassive Black Holes and Active Galactic Nuclei

Nearly every large galaxy contains a supermassive black hole at its center, ranging from millions to tens of billions of solar masses. In most galaxies (including ours currently), this black hole is relatively quiet — it's not actively consuming large amounts of material.

When a supermassive black hole is actively feeding — accreting gas and dust from the surrounding material — it becomes an **active galactic nucleus (AGN)**. As material spirals inward, friction heats it to extreme temperatures, producing enormous amounts of radiation — often outshining the entire rest of the galaxy by factors of 100 or more.

**Quasars** (quasi-stellar objects) are the most luminous AGN. Observed mostly at high redshift (i.e., in the early universe, meaning we see them as they were billions of years ago), quasars are powered by the most actively feeding supermassive black holes. The most luminous quasars produce more energy than 1,000 Milky Ways combined — from a region smaller than our solar system.

The co-evolution of supermassive black holes and their host galaxies is one of the active areas of galactic research. The correlation between black hole mass and galaxy bulge mass suggests they regulate each other's growth in ways not yet fully understood.

### Dark Matter — The Invisible Majority

In the 1970s, astronomer Vera Rubin measured the rotation curves of spiral galaxies. Problem: the outer regions of galaxies rotate at nearly the same speed as the inner regions. By Newtonian mechanics, if the visible matter were all there was, the outer stars should rotate more slowly (as planets in our solar system do). They don't.

The explanation: there is far more mass in galaxies than can be seen. **Dark matter** — which does not emit, absorb, or reflect light — makes up approximately 85% of all matter in the universe. It interacts gravitationally but not electromagnetically. We have never directly detected a dark matter particle. We have only observed its gravitational effects.

Dark matter doesn't just make up the outer halos of galaxies — it is the scaffolding on which galaxies formed at all. In the early universe, dark matter's gravitational pull created the density fluctuations that eventually collapsed into the large-scale structure: filaments, walls, nodes, and voids that constitute the cosmic web.

What dark matter actually is remains one of the largest open questions in physics.

### Galaxy Formation and Evolution

The leading model: **hierarchical structure formation**. Small structures form first and merge into larger ones over time.

Early universe (first ~400 million years): hot, dense plasma cools, hydrogen and helium form, dark matter clumps through gravity. Gas falls into dark matter halos and begins to collapse. First stars form — massive, short-lived, seeding the universe with the first heavy elements via supernova.

Early galaxies were smaller, denser, and more chaotic than today's large spirals. Over billions of years: galaxies merge, grow, and settle into more organized forms. Gas-rich mergers can trigger massive bursts of star formation. Eventually, gas is consumed, star formation slows, and galaxies "quench" — settling into the passive elliptical phase.

**Lookback time** is the key concept for observing this history. Because light travels at a finite speed, looking farther away means looking further back in time. The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST), launched 2021, observes galaxies as they existed 300–500 million years after the Big Bang — and has already found galaxies more massive and more organized than models predicted for that era, suggesting the early universe's story is more complex than the current model accounts for.

### The Cosmic Web and Galaxy Clusters

Galaxies are not distributed randomly. They cluster along vast filaments and sheets, with enormous voids between them — a structure called the **cosmic web**, seeded by quantum fluctuations in the very early universe and shaped by gravity over 13+ billion years.

**Galaxy clusters** are the largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe — collections of hundreds to thousands of galaxies, bound by dark matter and hot intergalactic gas. The Virgo Cluster, ~65 million light-years away, is the nearest large cluster. The Coma Cluster, one of the densest, contains over 1,000 galaxies.

Clusters are extreme environments. Galaxies that fall in often get stripped of their gas by the hot intracluster medium — quenching star formation. Smaller galaxies get consumed by larger ones. The giant ellipticals at cluster centers are the product of billions of years of this cannibalism.

### How We Observe and Measure Galaxies

**Redshift** — As the universe expands, light from distant galaxies is stretched to longer wavelengths (redshift). The amount of redshift gives you the galaxy's distance and the era it belongs to (lookback time). The entire observational history of the universe is built on interpreting redshift.

**The distance ladder** — Measuring distances across cosmological scales requires a chain of methods, each calibrated to the previous: parallax (nearby stars) → Cepheid variables (nearby galaxies) → Type Ia supernovae (distant galaxies) → Hubble's Law (very distant galaxies via redshift). Each rung has uncertainties that compound. The current tension between different distance-ladder measurements (the Hubble Tension) is an active problem.

**Multi-wavelength astronomy** — Galaxies look radically different depending on the wavelength. Optical light shows stars. Infrared reveals dusty star-forming regions hidden from optical. Radio shows gas and AGN jets. X-ray reveals hot gas in galaxy clusters and accretion around black holes. The full picture of a galaxy requires all of these.

**JWST** — The James Webb Space Telescope observes in infrared, cutting through dust and reaching unprecedented distances. Since 2022, it has produced the deepest and sharpest infrared images ever taken and has already complicated models of early galaxy formation.

## Key Considerations

The timescales of galactic processes dwarf anything human intuition can genuinely grasp. The Sun has completed about 20 orbits of the Milky Way in its 4.6 billion year lifetime. The galactic center, Sagittarius A*, has been relatively quiet for millions of years. The Andromeda collision is 4.5 billion years away — longer than Earth has existed. Any human engagement with these scales is necessarily abstract, which is part of why they're valuable: they force a different kind of thinking.

The field is actively evolving. JWST is producing observations that challenge existing models almost monthly. Cosmology is not settled science — it's the frontier of what's known.

Dark matter and dark energy (which drives the accelerating expansion of the universe) together constitute ~95% of the universe's energy content. Neither is understood. The confidence of modern cosmology coexists with fundamental ignorance about most of what exists.

## Getting Started

1. Download Stellarium (free, open-source planetarium software) and identify what you can see from Galloway, NJ on a clear night. Knowing what you're looking at in the sky is where the abstract becomes concrete.
2. Find a dark sky site within driving distance (the Pine Barrens is one of the best dark-sky areas in NJ — the Pinelands preserve limits light pollution). Take a look at the Milky Way band on a clear moonless night. That's the plane of our galaxy viewed from inside.
3. Look up the JWST image gallery. The "First Deep Field" image is the best single demonstration of what a galaxy actually is — thousands of them in a patch of sky the size of a grain of sand held at arm's length.
4. *Cosmos: A Personal Voyage* (Sagan, 1980) or the 2014 *Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey* (Tyson) — either is a good orientation to scale.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing the Milky Way band with the whole galaxy.** What you see on a dark night is only the disk of the galaxy — the plane we're embedded in. The full galaxy, including halo, is roughly 200,000 light-years across and invisible to the naked eye as a totality.

**Treating galaxy distances as static.** The universe is expanding. When we say a galaxy is "13 billion light-years away," that's the lookback time — how long the light traveled. The galaxy itself is now much farther away due to expansion. Cosmological distances require careful definition.

**Assuming dark matter = known matter we can't see.** Dark matter is not dim stars, rogue planets, or black holes. We know this because gravitational lensing surveys have constrained how much "ordinary" invisible matter exists. Dark matter is something genuinely different from any known particle.

## Resources

- *Cosmos* — Carl Sagan. The classic accessible entry point. More philosophical and humanistic than technical, but the perspective it provides is irreplaceable.
- *Astrophysics for People in a Hurry* — Neil deGrasse Tyson. 200 pages. Covers dark matter, dark energy, the Big Bang, and galaxies at the right depth for a first pass.
- *The Milky Way: An Insider's Guide* — William Waller. Focused on our own galaxy specifically — more detailed than most general astronomy books.
- JWST Image Gallery (webbtelescope.org) — the actual data. Best visual demonstration of what the field is currently finding.
