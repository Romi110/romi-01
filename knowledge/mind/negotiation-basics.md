# Negotiation Basics
*BATNA, anchoring, interests vs. positions — the principles that govern every negotiation you'll ever have*

---

## What Is It

Negotiation is the process by which two or more parties with different preferences reach an agreement. It happens constantly — salary, contracts, purchases, conflict resolution, resource allocation, relationship decisions. Most people negotiate poorly because they treat it as a battle (where someone wins and someone loses) rather than a problem-solving exercise (where the goal is finding agreements that work well for both sides).

The foundational frameworks come from the Harvard Negotiation Project's Getting to Yes (Fisher, Ury, Patton), supplemented by practical work on the psychology of negotiation. This file covers the principles that govern every negotiation regardless of context.

---

## Why It Matters

A single well-negotiated salary negotiation, done correctly, compounds into hundreds of thousands of dollars over a career. A poorly negotiated lease or contract costs money and optionality for years. Beyond money: understanding negotiation dynamics determines how you resolve conflict, advocate for yourself, and navigate disagreement without damaging relationships.

Self-reliance includes the ability to represent your own interests effectively without a lawyer or agent doing it for you.

## Core Concepts

### Positions vs. Interests — The Central Distinction

**Position**: What you say you want. "I want a $110k salary." "I won't accept less than 3 weeks vacation." Positions are the opening demands people state.

**Interest**: Why you want it — the underlying need, concern, or motivation. The position "I want $110k" might reflect interests in financial security, being valued at market rate, and being able to afford a specific lifestyle. The position "3 weeks vacation" might reflect interests in time for family, recovery from burnout, or flexibility.

**Why this matters**: Two parties' positions often conflict directly while their underlying interests can often be simultaneously satisfied. The classic example (from Getting to Yes): two sisters fighting over an orange. One wants to eat it; one wants the peel for baking. Both claim the whole orange, but they could each get exactly what they need if interests were surfaced. Positional bargaining fights over the positions; interest-based negotiation finds solutions that satisfy the underlying needs.

**How to surface interests**: Ask "why" questions. "What matters to you about that?" "What would having that give you?" "What's driving the concern on this point?" Most people will reveal their interests if asked directly and respectfully.

### BATNA — Your Most Important Asset

**BATNA**: Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. What you'll do if this negotiation fails. Your walk-away option.

Your BATNA is the source of your negotiating power, not your position, your needs, or how eloquently you argue. A weak BATNA (you have no good alternative) gives the other party leverage. A strong BATNA (you have an excellent alternative) gives you leverage — you can walk away credibly.

**The key rule**: Never accept an agreement worse than your BATNA. That's the minimum threshold. Anything below it, you're better off walking away.

**Improving your BATNA before negotiating**: Before a salary negotiation, get competing offers. Before a lease negotiation, have other apartments you'd accept. Before any significant negotiation, invest in developing alternatives — this is often the highest-leverage preparation you can do. The negotiation starts before the conversation begins.

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)**: The range where both parties prefer an agreement to their respective BATNAs. If your BATNA is $90k (you have a competing offer) and the minimum you'd accept is $95k, and the employer's maximum they'd offer is $105k, the ZOPA is $95k–$105k. Deals happen within the ZOPA; negotiations that reveal no ZOPA should end without agreement.

**Never reveal a weak BATNA voluntarily**: If your BATNA is weak, don't advertise it. The other party's leverage comes from knowing you have no good alternative. Keep weak BATNAs private; strong BATNAs can often be revealed or implied.

### Anchoring — Who Sets the Reference Point

The first number named in a negotiation acts as an anchor — all subsequent discussion gravitates toward it. This is a cognitive bias (see [cognitive-biases.md](cognitive-biases.md)) with direct practical applications.

**Anchor high (when selling) or low (when buying)**: If you're negotiating salary, name your number first and set it at the top of the reasonable range. If you're buying a car, offer first and offer low. The anchor constrains the final agreement.

**How high/low**: Extreme anchors that are clearly unrealistic backfire — they signal bad faith and can end negotiations. Anchors at the outer edge of the plausible range work best. Do your research so you know what "plausible" looks like.

**Counter-anchoring**: When faced with an unreasonable anchor, don't start from it. Explicitly challenge the anchor as a reference point: "Let's set that aside and look at what this is actually worth." Then introduce your own anchor. Accepting the other party's extreme anchor as a starting point concedes ground before the negotiation has really started.

**When not to anchor first**: If you don't know the range well and the other party likely does, let them anchor first — you'll learn information you need. If they anchor reasonably, you've lost nothing; if they anchor extremely, you know their position.

### Creating Value — The Non-Zero-Sum Frame

Most people treat negotiation as distributive: a fixed pie to be divided, where more for you means less for me. In many negotiations, this is wrong — the pie can be expanded before dividing.

**Log-rolling (trading across issues)**: When parties have different priorities across multiple issues, they can trade: you give on what matters less to you, they give on what matters less to them, and both sides end up ahead of a pure positional split on each issue.

Example: Job negotiation involves salary, title, start date, remote work, and vacation. You care most about title and remote work; they care most about salary and start date. A deal where you get your title and remote work in exchange for accepting a slightly lower salary and flexible start date can be better for both than splitting every issue down the middle.

**Identifying different priorities**: "What matters most to you in this deal?" Direct question. Most people will answer. The answer reveals where trades are possible.

**Contingent agreements**: When parties have different expectations about the future, make the agreement contingent on outcomes rather than fighting about predictions. "If the revenue reaches X by Q3, the bonus kicks in" resolves a disagreement about whether revenue will reach X without requiring either party to be proven right in advance.

### Tactics and Responses

**Good cop / bad cop**: One party is agreeable; the other plays hardball, claiming constraints the good cop "can't override." Recognize this as a tactic. Engage the actual decision-maker or ask the good cop to check with the bad cop and come back.

**The nibble**: After reaching agreement on the main points, one party asks for a small additional concession. Works because the other party doesn't want to re-open the whole negotiation. Counter: don't mentally close the deal until it's in writing. Respond with a counter-nibble on something you want.

**Deadline pressure**: "This offer expires Friday." Creates artificial urgency and activates loss aversion. Ask whether the deadline is real. Often it isn't, or it can be extended. Real deadlines do exist — if you believe it's real, factor it in; if you suspect it's artificial, call it.

**The limited authority move**: "I'd love to say yes, but I have to check with my manager." Creates a buffer and a delay. Useful as a tactic when you need time. When used against you, ask to speak directly with whoever has authority, or give your counter and let them take it back.

**Silence**: Most people fill silence. After making an offer or concession, stop talking. The first person to fill the silence often makes the next concession. Practice being comfortable with silence — it's one of the most effective and underused negotiating tools.

### The Relationship Dimension

Negotiations exist in context. A one-shot negotiation (buying a car from a dealer you'll never see again) has different dynamics than an ongoing relationship (negotiating with your employer, your landlord, a key partner).

In ongoing relationships, the quality of the long-term relationship has value that a short-term win can destroy. Being right isn't the same as getting the best outcome — a deal that leaves the other party feeling taken advantage of produces a counterparty who finds ways to get even later.

**Separate people from the problem**: The negotiation is about the issue, not about whether you like or dislike each other. Being hard on the substantive issue while being respectful of the person makes it possible to disagree firmly without damaging the relationship.

**Face-saving**: In many negotiations — especially across cultures — allowing the other party to claim they "won" something is worth more than the substance of the concession. A small concession that lets the other party tell their boss they got something can enable an agreement that pure positional bargaining would have prevented.

### Salary Negotiation — The Most Consequential Application

Salary negotiations are often the highest-stakes negotiations most people engage in and the ones people are most reluctant to have. Key specifics:

**Research first**: Know the market rate before the conversation. Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, industry surveys. Your number should be defensible by reference to market data, not just what you want.

**Never give a number first if you can avoid it**: "What's your salary expectation?" — "I'm focused on finding the right role. What's the budgeted range for this position?" Most recruiters will give you the range or a number. You've anchored them.

**When pressed for a number**: Give a range, with the bottom of your range at what you'd actually accept. They'll typically land somewhere in the range — usually toward the bottom. Set the bottom high enough.

**Negotiate the whole package**: Base salary, bonus structure, equity/stock, PTO, remote work, start date, title, professional development budget. Benefits with dollar value are negotiable. You may have more flexibility on some of these than on salary.

**Never accept on the spot**: "I'm very excited about this — can I have until [specific date] to review and get back to you?" Virtually always granted. Use the time.

**Counter even when the first offer is good**: Counter at least once. First offers almost always have room. The cost of a polite counter is essentially zero; the upside is real. "I'm very interested in this role. Based on my research and experience, I was hoping for X. Is there flexibility?"

## Key Considerations

**Preparation is the leverage multiplier**: The party that has done more homework — on their BATNA, the other side's likely BATNA, market conditions, the range of issues in play — has a structural advantage. Preparation compounds information asymmetry in your favor.

**Emotion is information, not a tool**: Genuine emotion in a negotiation communicates what matters to you. Performed emotion (feigned anger, theatrical offense) erodes trust. Read the other party's emotion as information about their interests and limits.

**In repeated games, reputation matters**: In a world where people talk, a reputation for dealing in good faith is a negotiating asset. A reputation for extracting every last cent is a liability — people avoid you, give you worse deals, or underperform on their obligations.

**The zone of fairness**: Both parties have a sense of what's fair. Agreements that violate that sense feel bad even if they're technically correct — and people find ways to exit or underperform on commitments they feel forced into. Aim for agreements both parties feel are reasonable.

## Getting Started

1. **Know your BATNA before every significant negotiation**: What will you do if this fails? Make it concrete. Develop alternatives before you need them.
2. **Practice asking "what matters most to you?"**: In low-stakes conversations first. Notice how readily people share their interests when asked directly.
3. **Next purchase involving negotiation (car, rent, service contract)**: Do your market research first, anchor first, and counter at least once. Track results.
4. **Salary**: If you haven't negotiated in your current role or haven't researched market rate recently, do it now. The research takes an afternoon; the conversation takes 5 minutes.

## Common Mistakes

**Negotiating against yourself**: Immediately conceding before the other party even pushes back. Make an offer and let them respond. Silence is not rejection.

**Revealing your BATNA unnecessarily**: "I really need this job" or "this is the only apartment I liked" eliminates your leverage. Keep your alternatives private.

**Fighting over positions instead of surfacing interests**: Spending an hour arguing about two positions that are in conflict, when a five-minute conversation about underlying interests would reveal a mutually acceptable solution.

**Accepting the first offer**: In most negotiation contexts, first offers have room. The other party expects a counter. Not countering leaves value on the table and often signals inexperience.

**Making it personal**: Treating the other party as an adversary rather than a counterpart solving a shared problem activates defensiveness and reduces the information flow that good agreements depend on.

## Resources

- *Getting to Yes* — Fisher, Ury, Patton: The foundational text. Short, clear, still the best general framework.
- *Never Split the Difference* — Chris Voss: FBI hostage negotiator's tactical approach. Particularly good on using empathy and tactical listening. Complements rather than replaces Getting to Yes.
- *Negotiation Genius* — Malhotra and Bazerman: More rigorous than the above two. Harvard Business School framework. Worth reading after the first two.
