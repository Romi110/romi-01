# Decision-Making (Practical Frameworks)
*The tools for thinking through hard choices clearly — before bias, ego, and urgency corrupt the analysis*

---

## What Is It

Decision-making frameworks are structured ways of thinking that catch the errors your unaided brain makes automatically. The brain is fast, pattern-matching, and motivated to feel certain — none of which produce accurate decisions. Frameworks slow the process, force explicit reasoning, and make the hidden assumptions visible.

This file covers the practical toolkit: the specific techniques to apply to real decisions. The Stoic philosophical layer underneath these tools is in [philosophy/decision-making.md](../philosophy/decision-making.md). These two files are complementary — the philosophy tells you *what to value*; the frameworks tell you *how to think clearly about it*.

## Why It Matters

Every major life decision — career moves, investment choices, relationship calls, what to build next — will be distorted by the same cognitive failures unless you have a process. Most of those failures are predictable and correctable. The frameworks here are not theoretical — they're the ones that actually catch the most expensive mistakes.

For Romi: self-reliance means being able to reason through hard problems yourself, not outsourcing judgment. That requires a toolkit, not just good intentions.

## Core Concepts

### Second-Order Thinking

**What it is**: Thinking past the immediate consequence to the consequences of the consequences.

First-order thinking: "If I take this job offer, I get a higher salary." Second-order thinking: "If I take this job offer, I get a higher salary — which means I'll need to move cities — which means I lose my current community — which may cost me more in time, loneliness, and rebuilding than the salary gains."

**How to use it**: For any significant decision, explicitly ask: *And then what?* Run the chain two or three levels deep. Most negative outcomes from decisions are visible at level two — they're just uncomfortable to look at.

**When it matters most**: Any decision with a lag between action and consequence — investments, career moves, relationship decisions, lifestyle changes. The short-term outcome and long-term outcome are often in opposite directions.

### Inversion

**What it is**: Instead of asking "how do I succeed at X?", ask "what would guarantee I fail at X?" Then avoid those things.

Charlie Munger: "Invert, always invert." The idea is that it's often easier to identify the failure conditions than the success conditions, and clearing those is more reliable than optimizing toward an ideal.

**How to use it**:
1. Name the goal.
2. List everything that would guarantee failure.
3. Design your approach to avoid those things.

Example: "How do I build a consistent writing practice?" → Invert: "What would guarantee I never write consistently?" Answer: No fixed time, no minimum floor, waiting for inspiration, keeping no record of streaks. Now you know exactly what not to do.

**Why it works**: Success is rarely one path; failure is often a small set of avoidable conditions. Clearing failure conditions gives you much more reliable traction than optimizing toward the ideal version.

### Pre-Mortem

**What it is**: Before committing to a decision, mentally project forward to a point where the decision has failed. Then work backwards to identify why.

The standard post-mortem happens after failure. The pre-mortem happens before commitment, when you can still change the plan.

**How to run one**:
1. Assume it is 12 months from now. The decision was made, the plan was executed, and it failed completely.
2. Ask: *Why did it fail? What went wrong?*
3. Write down every reason you can think of — without defending the plan.
4. Use those failure modes to either kill the decision, redesign around the risks, or at minimum know what to watch for.

**What it catches**: Overconfidence. The planning fallacy (underestimating time, cost, difficulty). Hidden assumptions. Risks that felt too uncomfortable to name in the enthusiasm phase.

The pre-mortem works because it gives you *permission* to identify problems — in normal planning, naming risks can feel like being negative about the team's idea. In a pre-mortem, finding problems is the whole point.

### Reversibility Test (1-Way vs 2-Way Doors)

**What it is**: Classify decisions by how easily they can be undone.

Jeff Bezos's framing: **2-way door** decisions can be reversed if they're wrong — iterate quickly, decide with less analysis. **1-way door** decisions are hard or impossible to undo — slow down, apply the full framework.

**How to apply it**:
- 2-way door (reversible): Take the job, try it for 6 months, leave if it's wrong. Launch the product, get feedback, pivot. Start the habit, drop it if it's not working.
- 1-way door (irreversible): Having a child. Signing a 30-year mortgage. Burning a professional relationship. Selling an asset you can't rebuy.

Most decisions that feel like 1-way doors are actually 2-way. Most people spend the wrong amount of time on each category — over-deliberating reversible decisions (which builds decision fatigue) and under-deliberating irreversible ones (because the path felt inevitable).

### Opportunity Cost

**What it is**: Every choice is a choice against every alternative. The cost of any decision includes the value of the best option you didn't take.

This is not just financial. Time is the primary currency. Every hour of work on one project is an hour not available for another. Every commitment is a cost imposed on everything you didn't commit to.

**How to apply it explicitly**:
- Name the best alternative to the option you're considering.
- Ask: "Is what I'm choosing actually better than that alternative?"
- When evaluating a new commitment: "What is this displacing?" The answer is often something you'd choose over the new commitment if you named it.

The most common failure mode: evaluating options against an implicit baseline of "nothing" instead of against the best actual alternative. This makes too many things look attractive.

### The Outside View

**What it is**: Instead of asking "how will this go for me, specifically?" — ask "how does this usually go for situations like mine?"

The inside view is your intuition about your specific situation. It tends toward optimism and uniqueness-bias ("my situation is different"). The outside view is the base rate — what actually happens to people doing this kind of thing.

**How to use it**:
1. Identify the reference class: "People starting this kind of business / making this type of investment / attempting this kind of career change."
2. Find the base rate: How many succeed? Over what time frame? What are the common failure modes?
3. Apply that to your situation before adjusting for factors that genuinely make your case different.

**Why it matters**: Starting a restaurant feels different when you know that ~60% fail in the first year and 80% in five. Starting a business in your area of professional expertise has a meaningfully different base rate. The outside view forces you to start with reality instead of enthusiasm.

### First Principles vs. Analogy

**What it is**: Two modes of reasoning. Analogy says "this is like X, therefore I should do what worked for X." First principles says "strip this down to what's actually true, then build up from there."

Analogy is fast and useful. It's also how you inherit other people's assumptions, including their wrong ones.

First principles thinking: break the problem into its components. Ask what's actually true at the base level. Reason up from there, even if the conclusion contradicts conventional wisdom.

**When to use each**:
- Use analogy for routine decisions — it's efficient and usually correct.
- Use first principles when the conventional answer feels wrong, when you're in a domain with rapidly changing conditions, or when you're trying to innovate rather than copy.

**The key question for first principles**: "What do I know is actually true about this situation, and what am I just assuming because it's convention?"

### The 10/10/10 Rule

**What it is**: For a decision you're emotionally reactive about, ask: How will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years?

Designed by Suzy Welch, useful for separating acute emotion from considered judgment. The decision that seems obviously right at the 10-minute scale often looks different at 10 years, and vice versa.

**Best use case**: Interpersonal decisions driven by short-term discomfort — avoiding a hard conversation, reacting to criticism, making a commitment you'll regret to avoid awkwardness now.

## Key Considerations

**Apply frameworks to match the decision size.** Running a full pre-mortem and outside view on whether to change your workout schedule is overkill. Running nothing on a career change is negligent. Match the depth of the process to the stakes.

**Frameworks are not algorithms.** They surface information and reduce bias — they don't output the right answer. The decision is still yours.

**Track your decisions.** Write down significant decisions, the reasoning, and the expected outcome. Review periodically. This is the only way to learn whether your judgment is improving or just becoming more confident. The Stoic evening reflection is one mechanism; a dedicated decision log is another.

**Distinguish between "I don't know" and "I'm uncomfortable."** Both feel like uncertainty. One is a data problem (get more information). The other is a risk problem (you have the information and don't like what it says). Treating discomfort as ignorance leads to analysis loops that don't resolve.

## Getting Started

For any decision that's been sitting unresolved:

1. Write the decision as a one-sentence question.
2. Apply reversibility: is this a 1-way or 2-way door? If 2-way, make the call faster.
3. Apply inversion: what would guarantee failure here?
4. Apply the outside view: what's the base rate for this type of situation?
5. Run the 10/10/10 if you're emotionally reactive.
6. Set a decision date. Decide.

Don't run all frameworks on every decision. Pick the one that's most likely to catch the relevant bias for this particular choice.

## Common Mistakes

**Using frameworks to delay.** Analysis has diminishing returns. Past a certain point, more deliberation is avoidance with intellectual cover.

**Ignoring the outside view because "my situation is different."** It usually isn't different enough to ignore base rates.

**Second-order thinking only in one direction.** Running second-order analysis on the risks of acting, without running it on the risks of not acting, produces a systematic bias toward inaction.

**Not writing the decision down.** Mental decisions can be quietly revised in hindsight to look wiser than they were. Written decisions hold you accountable and produce actual learning.

**Applying inversion as pessimism.** Inversion is a design tool, not an argument for not trying. "This could fail in these ways" → "here's how I'll avoid those failure modes." Not "here's why I won't try."

## Resources

- *Thinking in Bets* — Annie Duke. Poker player turned decision researcher. Practical, evidence-based. Excellent on the difference between decision quality and outcome quality — the most important distinction in this whole topic.
- *The Great Mental Models (Vol. 1)* — Shane Parrish / Farnam Street. Covers many frameworks in this file and others in more depth. Well written and dense. The companion blog (fs.blog) is consistently high quality.
- *Superforecasting* — Philip Tetlock. How to make accurate predictions and calibrate uncertainty. Directly applicable to the outside view and base rates.
