# Critical Thinking
*The skill of evaluating arguments and claims by examining their structure, evidence, and reasoning — not just their conclusions.*

---

## What Is It

Critical thinking is the disciplined practice of analyzing how conclusions are reached, not just whether you agree with them. It asks: is this argument well-constructed? Does the evidence actually support the claim? What's being assumed without being stated?

Informal logic is the practical side of this — the study of how real arguments work (and fail) in natural language, outside formal mathematical proofs. Where formal logic deals in symbols and strict rules, informal logic deals with the arguments people actually make in conversations, articles, decisions, and debates.

Together, these give you a framework for not being fooled — by others, by bad sources, and by yourself.

---

## Why It Matters

Every consequential decision Romi makes rests on reasoning: about what's true, what to do next, who to trust, what the evidence shows. Weak reasoning produces confident wrong answers. Critical thinking is the meta-skill — it sharpens every other skill that depends on interpreting information.

It's also a Stoic concern. Epictetus was relentless about examining impressions before assenting to them. The discipline of not jumping to conclusions, of demanding that your own reasoning hold up to scrutiny — that's not just intellectual hygiene, it's character work.

## Core Concepts

### Argument Structure

An argument is a claim (the **conclusion**) supported by reasons (the **premises**). This sounds obvious, but most poor thinking fails here — people assert conclusions without premises, or give premises that don't connect to the conclusion.

**Example:**
> P1: Regular sleep deprivation impairs cognitive function.
> P2: I regularly sleep fewer than 6 hours.
> C: My cognitive function is likely impaired.

Recognizing this structure in natural language is the first skill. People rarely label their premises. Train yourself to ask: *what's the actual claim, and what's being offered as support for it?*

### Deductive, Inductive, and Abductive Reasoning

Three modes of inference — each appropriate in different contexts.

**Deductive**: The conclusion is guaranteed if the premises are true. If all men are mortal and Socrates is a man, Socrates is mortal — necessarily. Used in math, formal logic, and tight procedural arguments. A valid deductive argument cannot have true premises and a false conclusion.

**Inductive**: The conclusion is probable, not certain. "Every raven I've observed is black, therefore ravens are probably black." No amount of confirming observations proves it absolutely — one white raven refutes it. Most empirical reasoning is inductive. Scientists, doctors, and investors all work inductively.

**Abductive**: Inference to the best explanation. You observe something (the grass is wet) and reason backward to the most plausible cause (it rained). Used constantly in diagnosis, debugging, and everyday life. The conclusion is the hypothesis that best explains the evidence — not the only possible one.

Knowing which mode you're using matters because each has different failure modes and different standards of proof.

### Validity and Soundness

Two separate properties of deductive arguments:

- **Valid**: The logical structure is correct — *if* the premises were true, the conclusion would have to be true. Validity doesn't require the premises to actually be true.
- **Sound**: Valid *and* all premises are actually true. Sound arguments guarantee true conclusions.

This distinction is useful when arguing. Someone might have a valid argument (structure is fine) built on false premises (unsound). Or an argument can be invalid even if the conclusion happens to be true — the conclusion got there by bad reasoning.

### Common Informal Fallacies

Fallacies are errors in reasoning — some are deceptive, some are genuine mistakes. Recognizing them lets you identify where an argument actually breaks down.

**Ad hominem**: Attacking the person making the argument instead of the argument itself. Dismissing an inconvenient truth because you dislike the source.

**Straw man**: Misrepresenting someone's argument in a weakened form, then refuting that weaker version. Common in debate and media. The tell: the rebuttal doesn't actually address what was claimed.

**Appeal to authority**: Treating someone's credentials or reputation as a substitute for evidence or reasoning. Experts can be wrong; their authority is evidence, not proof.

**False dichotomy**: Presenting two options as if they're exhaustive when more exist. "You're either with us or against us" is the crude version; subtler versions appear constantly in strategic planning.

**Slippery slope**: Claiming that one step will inevitably lead to an extreme outcome without showing why the intermediate steps are inevitable. Sometimes valid (some slopes are genuinely slippery), often not.

**Post hoc ergo propter hoc**: "After this, therefore because of this." Confusing correlation or temporal sequence with causation. A classic bad reasoning pattern, especially with health and market claims.

**Begging the question**: The conclusion is smuggled into the premises. The argument is circular — it assumes what it's trying to prove.

For cognitive biases that distort reasoning before arguments are even formed, see [cognitive-biases.md](cognitive-biases.md).

### Burden of Proof

Who is responsible for providing evidence? By default, the burden falls on whoever makes a positive claim. "God exists" and "God doesn't exist" are both claims — both carry some burden. "Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence" (Sagan) is a reasonable heuristic: the stronger the claim, the higher the evidentiary bar.

In practice: when someone asserts something confidently, ask what the evidence is. If they shift the burden to you ("prove it isn't true"), that's a fallacy — absence of disproof is not proof.

### Steelmanning vs. Strawmanning

Strawmanning (above) is the failure mode. **Steelmanning** is the discipline: reconstruct the strongest version of the opposing argument before responding to it. If you can't articulate the other side's position in a way they'd agree with, you don't understand it well enough to refute it.

This is not about being nice. It's about not wasting time arguing against positions no one actually holds — and about finding the real fault lines in disagreements.

## Key Considerations

**Language is imprecise.** Most arguments in real life are made in ambiguous language. The same word means different things to different people. A huge proportion of apparent disagreements dissolve when terms are defined carefully.

**Reasoning and conclusion can be wrong independently.** You can reach a true conclusion via bad reasoning (lucky), and you can reason soundly to a false conclusion (if your premises were wrong). The goal is good reasoning consistently — not just being right this time.

**Most fallacies are not intentional deception.** People commit fallacies in their own thinking constantly. The point of knowing them isn't to catch others — it's to catch yourself.

**Emotional and motivated reasoning are the harder problem.** The formal fallacies are the easy part. The real battle is noticing when you're reasoning toward a conclusion you already want. That's where [cognitive-biases.md](cognitive-biases.md) picks up.

## Getting Started

1. **Learn to identify premises and conclusions.** Take any article or argument you read this week. Strip it down: what's the conclusion? What are the premises offered? Is the connection valid? Five minutes of this daily builds the habit fast.

2. **Memorize the ten most common fallacies.** Ad hominem, straw man, false dichotomy, appeal to authority, post hoc, slippery slope, begging the question, appeal to ignorance, hasty generalization, red herring. Recognize them in real arguments, including your own internal monologue.

3. **Practice steelmanning one position you disagree with per week.** Write it out in two paragraphs. If you can't, read more until you can. This exposes where you've been reasoning against a straw man.

4. **Ask "what would change my mind?"** For any belief you hold strongly, identify what evidence would cause you to revise it. If the answer is "nothing," that's a flag — the belief is held on grounds other than evidence.

## Common Mistakes

**Winning the argument instead of finding the truth.** The goal of evaluating reasoning should be accuracy, not scoring points. When the goal shifts to winning, motivated reasoning takes over and critical thinking shuts down.

**Applying scrutiny asymmetrically.** Demanding high standards of proof from claims you dislike, low standards from claims you agree with. This is the primary failure mode for intelligent people — the intelligence goes into rationalizing, not reasoning.

**Confusing complexity with depth.** A long, technical-sounding argument is not necessarily a good one. Complicated language often hides weak premises. Ask for the simple version.

**Stopping at "that's a fallacy."** Identifying a fallacy doesn't automatically refute an argument — the conclusion could still be true via different reasoning. Fallacy-spotting is the beginning, not the end.

## Resources

- **"Thinking, Fast and Slow" — Daniel Kahneman**: The best accessible treatment of how reasoning actually works vs. how we think it works. Covers System 1/2 thinking and many of the biases that corrupt reasoning. Long, but chapters are self-contained.
- **"A Rulebook for Arguments" — Anthony Weston**: Short (under 100 pages), dense, practical. The best quick reference for argument structure and how to construct or evaluate one.
- **"The Demon-Haunted World" — Carl Sagan**: A case for skeptical thinking applied to extraordinary claims. The "baloney detection kit" chapter alone is worth the read.
