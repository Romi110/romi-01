# Reading Deeply
*Reading as a skill — active comprehension, retention, physical setup, and book selection*

---

## What Is It

Most people treat reading as passive — eyes move, pages turn, nothing sticks. Reading deeply is the opposite: it's an active conversation with a text, where you push back, ask questions, and force understanding before moving on.

There are two completely different activities both called "reading." Skimming for information is one. Understanding an argument, internalizing a framework, or changing how you think is another. This file is about the second. The first is just Googling with extra steps.

---

## Why It Matters

Books compress decades of thinking into hours. Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus, Seneca — their complete frameworks are sitting in your hands for $15. The leverage is absurd. But only if you read to understand, not to consume.

For someone building a personal philosophy, learning self-reliance skills, and compounding knowledge intentionally — reading well is the highest-ROI skill in this system. Every hour of active reading compounds differently than an hour of passive scrolling.

## Core Concepts

### Active vs Passive Reading

Passive reading: you read every word and feel like you read the book. Ask yourself a week later what it said — nothing.

Active reading requires engagement at every level:
- **Before**: What do I already know? What do I expect this to argue?
- **During**: Agree? Disagree? What's missing? What connects to something else I know?
- **After**: Can I summarize the core argument in 3 sentences? What changes because I read this?

The transition from passive to active isn't effort — it's method. The tools below make it structural.

### Adler's Four Levels

From *How to Read a Book* (Mortimer Adler) — the best framework for matching reading depth to purpose:

1. **Elementary** — decoding words. Assumed.
2. **Inspectional** — skim to understand what kind of book this is and what it's arguing. Read the table of contents, intro, first/last chapter, random sections. 20 minutes max. Tells you whether the book deserves a full read.
3. **Analytical** — full engagement. Read the whole book, identify the central question, the author's thesis, the key arguments. Mark it up. This is the default level for any book you're reading to learn from.
4. **Syntopical** — reading multiple books on the same topic comparatively. Useful when mastering a domain. You stop being a student of one author and become a student of the topic.

For most books: inspectional first, then decide if it earns analytical.

### Annotation (Marginalia)

Marking up a book is not disrespecting it — it's using it. The goal of annotation is to make rereading 10x faster and to force engagement while reading.

What to mark:
- **Underline** or **highlight**: The core claim of a paragraph or argument. Not interesting sentences — the *load-bearing* claims.
- **Stars / circles**: Most important passages — the ones you'd cite to explain the book's thesis
- **Margin notes**: Your response — "this contradicts X", "apply to habit design", "wrong because...", "see Stoicism file"
- **Question marks**: Unclear, unresolved, or disagreed-with passages
- **Brackets**: Sections you want to return to

The test: after finishing, can you flip through and reconstruct the book's argument from your marks alone? If yes, you annotated well.

For ebooks (Kindle/Kobo), highlights and notes sync to a file. Export them. Kindle exports to `My Clippings.txt`; third-party tools like Readwise automate this.

### Retention Systems

Reading without retention is entertainment. Three mechanisms that actually work:

**The Feynman test**: After a chapter, close the book. Explain the core idea out loud or on paper as if teaching it to someone who's never heard of it. Where you stall out, you don't actually understand — go back.

**Spaced repetition**: Review key ideas at increasing intervals. 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks. Flashcards (Anki) for facts. For frameworks and arguments, re-reading your own margin notes is enough.

**Writing it out**: The act of writing knowledge files *is* the retention system in this repo. Writing a file forces you to synthesize, not just recall. If you can't write the file, you didn't understand the book.

### Physical vs Digital

Both have trade-offs. Choose based on use case, not preference.

**Physical books:**
- Easier to annotate freely (pens, tabs, margin scrawl)
- Easier to flip around non-linearly — important for syntopical reading
- No eye strain, no notifications, no battery
- Better for philosophy, dense non-fiction, books you'll reread
- Resale/lending possible

**E-readers (Kindle Paperwhite / Kobo Libra H2O):**
- Highlight + note export is automatically structured — searchable, exportable
- One hand readable (great for commuting, lying down)
- Adjustable font/size for reading comfort
- Instant access to any book
- Kobo has better annotation export workflow and OverDrive library integration (free books via public library)
- Better for: light reading, travel, books you won't keep, fiction

**Recommendation**: Physical books for philosophy, dense frameworks, books in your core domains. Kindle/Kobo for everything else. The library card is free — Libby app + OverDrive = free ebooks on Kobo without DRM friction.

### Book Selection

The biggest reading bottleneck isn't speed — it's selection. Most books waste your time. Useful filters:

**Primary sources over summaries**: Read Epictetus directly, not a book about Epictetus. Summaries flatten nuance and often introduce the author's interpretation as fact.

**Old books over new books**: A book that has been read for 50 years has survived selection pressure. A book published this year hasn't. Apply this especially to self-help and business.

**One book per domain at a time**: Reading five business books simultaneously doesn't build a framework — it builds confusion. One book per area, finish it before starting another in that domain.

**The 50-page rule**: Give every book 50 pages. If it hasn't earned your attention by then, abandon it without guilt. There is no obligation to finish a bad book.

### Reading Environment and Accessories

Environment determines reading quality. Most people underinvest here.

**Lighting**:
- Natural light is ideal — position so it comes over your left shoulder (for right-handers)
- Dedicated reading lamp beats overhead lighting: BenQ e-Reading Lamp (halo-style, no glare, wide color range) is the standard recommendation for serious readers. More useful than it sounds.
- For e-readers: built-in warm light, reduce to ~10% at night

**Seating**:
- Not a couch — posture degrades attention within 20 minutes. Use a proper chair with back support, or a floor setup with a back-jack chair (frameless floor chair).
- Ergonomics matter here — see `posture-and-ergonomics.md` when written

**Book stands**:
- For analytical reading of heavy reference books, a reading stand keeps the book flat and your neck neutral. Matte black wooden stands (Bamboo Radius) are the standard. Cheap and lasts forever.
- Adds friction to passive reading, which is intentional

**Annotation tools**:
- Pencil over pen — erasable, no bleed-through. Palomino Blackwing or any decent mechanical pencil
- Staedtler triplus fine-liner for color coding if you want a system (e.g., yellow = core claim, blue = disagreement)
- Sticky tabs for pages you'll return to. Post-it 1/2" tabs — not full bookmarks

**Timers**:
- Pomodoro-style sessions (25 min read, 5 min reflect) work better than marathon sessions for retention. Use a physical timer, not your phone.

**Notebooks**:
- A dedicated reading notebook for syntopical reading — where you're building a map across multiple books on one topic
- Leuchtturm1917 A5 (dot grid) is the standard — fits in most bags, durable binding

**E-readers (if buying)**:
- **Kobo Libra Colour**: Best library integration (Libby/OverDrive), no ads, better annotation export, color e-ink now available. Recommended over Kindle if you use the library.
- **Kindle Paperwhite Signature Edition**: Best hardware if you're already in Amazon ecosystem. Readwise integration is excellent for note export.
- Both are fine. Skip the base Kindle (too small, front-light is worse).

## Key Considerations

**Reading speed is mostly irrelevant.** Speed reading destroys comprehension. The goal is the slowest speed at which you still understand. For dense philosophy or science, that might be 10 pages/hour with full stops. For light narrative non-fiction, 40-50 pages/hour is fine.

**Page count is a vanity metric.** "I read 50 books last year" means nothing if you can't explain 10 of them. One book read analytically beats twenty read passively.

**The book you don't finish taught you something.** If you hit 50 pages and the book hasn't earned more time, dropping it is good judgment, not laziness.

**Rereading is underrated.** The Stoics re-read Meditations constantly. Seneca re-read Epicurus. The first read plants seeds; the reread harvests them. Plan to reread any book that made it into your marginalia heavily.

**Audiobooks have a specific role.** They don't replace analytical reading — retention is lower, annotation impossible, and you can't flip around. But they're excellent for: commuting, narrative non-fiction you'd otherwise skip, fiction, re-listening to books already annotated. Audible credits are best saved for books where the narrator is exceptional (e.g., *Meditations* narrated by Duncan Steen).

## Getting Started

1. Pick one book you already own that you haven't finished or haven't understood well. Don't buy anything new yet.
2. Do an inspectional read first: table of contents, intro, last chapter. 20 minutes. What is this book actually arguing?
3. Start annotating from page 1. Underline the load-bearing sentences. Write one margin note per section.
4. After each chapter: close the book, write 3 sentences summarizing the chapter's core claim. Put it in a notebook or a file.
5. When done: write the knowledge file. That file is the proof of reading.

For accessories: start with just a good pencil and sticky tabs. Add a lamp if you read at night. The e-reader can wait unless you're commuting.

## Common Mistakes

**Highlighting everything.** Highlights are a signal; if every line is highlighted, nothing is. Force yourself to highlight at most 10% of any page. This requires you to decide what matters — which is the skill you're trying to build.

**Reading without a question in mind.** Walk into every book knowing what you want to get from it. "What does this say about self-discipline?" is better than "let's see what happens."

**Never abandoning bad books.** Sunk cost. Drop it at 50 pages if it hasn't earned more.

**Treating a summary as a substitute.** Blinkist and summaries are useful for deciding whether to read something, not for replacing reading it. The thinking happens in the full text, not the extract.

**Reading in bed, lights off, horizontal.** You will fall asleep. Reading requires posture that keeps you alert. Bed reading is for fiction before sleep, not analytical work.

**Not writing after.** If you read without producing a knowledge file, a good notebook page, or at least a written summary, your retention will be ~20% at 2 weeks. Writing forces synthesis.

## Resources

- *How to Read a Book* — Mortimer Adler. The foundational text on analytical and syntopical reading. Published 1940, still the best framework. Read Chapter 20 (syntopical) last.
- *The Pleasures and Sorrows of Work* — Alain de Botton. Not a reading guide — but models what a deep, curious mind does with a subject. Shows what it looks like to think through a book's ideas rather than just report them.
- Readwise (readwise.io) — If you use a Kindle or Kobo heavily, Readwise syncs all your highlights and resurfaces them on spaced repetition schedules. $7.99/month. Worth it only if you're highlighting actively.
- Libby app + public library card — Free access to ebooks and audiobooks through Kobo or any device. The NJ library system is well-stocked. This alone justifies a library card.
