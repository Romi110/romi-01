# Systems Thinking
*A way of seeing the world as interconnected feedback loops rather than linear cause-and-effect — the framework that explains why things resist change, why fixes backfire, and where leverage actually lives.*

---

## What Is It

Systems thinking is a way of understanding complex situations by focusing on structure rather than events. Instead of asking "what happened?" it asks "what is the system that keeps producing this outcome?"

A system is any set of things that are interconnected in ways that produce patterns of behavior over time: a body, a garden, a habit, a business, a economy. Systems thinking gives you a vocabulary and a set of tools for understanding why these patterns persist, what drives them, and where you can actually change them.

The foundational text is Donella Meadows' *Thinking in Systems* (2008). Everything in this file comes from that framework.

The practical payoff: you stop being surprised by outcomes that seem counterintuitive, you stop blaming people for problems that are structural, and you find intervention points that actually work instead of applying fixes that make things worse.

---

## Why It Matters

Most problems that persist despite good effort are systems problems, not execution problems. The soil that won't improve despite adding amendments. The habit that keeps breaking. The organization that keeps producing the same dysfunction despite leadership changes. The financial pattern that recurs regardless of income.

Meadows' key insight: **the structure of a system determines the behavior it produces.** If the structure doesn't change, the behavior won't change, regardless of who's in charge or how hard people try.

For Romi specifically: this framework connects to everything — soil systems in farming, habit loops in discipline, compounding in finance, feedback in training, organizational behavior in tech work. Once you see systems, you can't unsee them.

## Core Concepts

### Stocks and Flows

A **stock** is anything that accumulates or depletes over time: water in a tank, money in an account, soil carbon, trust in a relationship, knowledge in your head, inventory in a warehouse.

A **flow** is the rate of change of a stock — what fills it or drains it: water flowing in or draining out, income and spending, organic matter added or decomposed, interactions that build or erode trust.

Every system is built from stocks connected by flows. This sounds obvious but it has non-obvious implications:

1. **Stocks change slowly.** Flows change the rate, but the stock itself lags. You can turn off a faucet but the bathtub doesn't empty instantly. You can eat poorly for a week but your health stock doesn't collapse overnight. This explains why systems have inertia and why change always takes longer than expected.

2. **Stocks act as buffers.** They absorb shocks and create stability. A savings account buffers income shocks. Fat reserves buffer caloric deficit. Topsoil buffers bad farming seasons. When you see a system that's fragile, look for depleted stocks.

3. **You can only change flows directly.** You can't directly move a stock — you can only adjust the inflows and outflows, then wait. This is frustrating but it's how every system works.

### Feedback Loops

A feedback loop occurs when a change in a stock feeds back to affect the flows that change it. There are two types:

**Reinforcing (positive) loops** amplify change in the same direction — a virtuous or vicious cycle.
- More savings → more interest → more savings
- More soil carbon → better water retention → better plant growth → more organic matter → more soil carbon
- More fitness → more energy → more capacity to train → more fitness
- More debt → higher interest payments → less money to repay principal → more debt

Reinforcing loops produce exponential growth or collapse. They are the engine of compounding. Left unchecked, they either explode or crash — they always require a balancing loop to stabilize.

**Balancing (negative) loops** resist change and push toward a goal or equilibrium.
- Thermostat: room temperature below target → heater turns on → temperature rises → heater turns off
- Hunger: energy below threshold → eat → energy restores → hunger drops
- Body weight: deviation from set point → metabolic/hormonal adjustment → return toward set point
- Soil pH: too acidic → plants struggle → less organic matter added → less decomposition acids

Balancing loops create stability and resistance to change. They are why systems are hard to shift — the system pushes back. When you try to change something and it keeps reverting, you've hit a balancing loop.

Most real systems contain both. A reinforcing loop makes something grow; a balancing loop eventually limits it.

### Delays

Delays between action and feedback are one of the most disruptive elements in systems. They cause oscillation, overshoot, and the failure of well-intentioned interventions.

Classic example: the shower. You turn the handle toward hot. Nothing happens immediately. You turn it more. More nothing. You turn it further. Now the water is scalding. You overcorrect toward cold. Oscillation.

In real systems:
- **Soil fertility**: you improve practices today, but soil carbon takes years to build. Farmers who don't see results quit before the system responds.
- **Weight loss**: caloric deficit → metabolic adaptation has a delay. The body catches up slowly, which is why weight loss plateaus seem to appear from nowhere.
- **Antibiotic resistance**: overuse today → resistance emerges over years → crisis arrives when cause seems distant.
- **Training**: you stress a muscle → 24-48 hour delay → adaptation occurs. Ignoring the delay leads to overtraining.

The response to delays is almost always wrong: people either give up (the improvement didn't work) or escalate (the fix isn't working fast enough, apply more). Both are rational responses to a system with a delay you don't see.

The correct response: **reduce the delay if possible, or slow down your response rate to match it.**

### Leverage Points

Meadows identified a hierarchy of places to intervene in a system, ranked by how much change they produce. Counterintuitively, the high-leverage points are often the least obvious ones. In order of increasing effectiveness:

**Low leverage (common interventions, weak effect):**
- Numbers: change a parameter (tax rate, subsidy amount, dosage). Rarely changes system behavior fundamentally.
- Material flows: add more infrastructure to an already-strained system. Often makes the system more rigid.

**Medium leverage:**
- Feedback loop strength: make a balancing loop faster (better monitoring) or weaker (reduce response lag). Meaningful change but working within the existing structure.
- Information flows: change who gets what information when. Often neglected but very effective. "Simply by changing the information structure, you can dramatically change system behavior."
- Rules: change the incentives and constraints. More powerful than adjusting parameters.

**High leverage:**
- Self-organization: the system's ability to change its own structure. Preserve and protect this — diversity, options, redundancy.
- Goals: what the system is optimizing for. Changing the goal changes everything downstream.
- **Paradigms**: the shared ideas and assumptions the system is built on. Changing the underlying mental model changes which goals get set, which rules get made, which information gets measured. This is the highest leverage point — and the hardest.

The practical implication: most people fight over numbers (budget amounts, policy parameters). That's low leverage. The high-leverage questions are: what is the system actually optimizing for? What information is missing? What feedback loop is broken?

### Archetypes — Common System Patterns

Certain feedback loop structures appear repeatedly across domains. Recognizing them speeds up diagnosis.

**Fixes that fail**: A problem symptom → apply a fix → symptom improves → underlying problem worsens over time → symptom returns worse. Examples: painkillers masking an injury, subsidies masking market inefficiency, motivational tactics masking missing systems.

**Shifting the burden**: A fundamental solution exists but is slow and costly. A symptomatic fix is quick and easy. Over time, the symptomatic fix is used more, the fundamental capacity to solve atrophies, dependency on the symptomatic fix grows. Classic: alcohol to manage stress, medication to manage what exercise and sleep would manage, outsourcing a skill you should own.

**Tragedy of the commons**: A shared resource (stock) is depleted by individual users each acting rationally in their own interest. No individual has incentive to restrain — the benefit of restraint is shared while the cost is individual. Overfishing, overgrazing, aquifer depletion.

**Limits to growth**: A reinforcing loop drives growth → growth erodes a constraining stock or triggers a balancing loop → growth slows or reverses. The limiting factor is often not what you expect. Addressing the wrong limit (adding more inputs when the constraint is something else) wastes resources and delays recognition of the real bottleneck.

**Eroding goals**: When achieving a goal is hard, lower the goal rather than improve performance. Produces a gradual decline that's barely visible year to year. Common in organizations and habits under stress.

## Key Considerations

**You are always inside a system.** Systems thinking is not just for analyzing things "out there." Your habits, your attention, your relationships — all systems. The question is whether you're seeing the structure or just reacting to events.

**Behavior follows structure.** Before blaming people (including yourself) for poor outcomes, look at the system structure. If the same problem keeps recurring despite good-faith effort, it's almost certainly structural, not motivational.

**Counterintuitive interventions**: High-leverage interventions often feel wrong. Slowing down during a crisis (to avoid overshoot). Adding more of something that seems to be the problem (increasing inventory when stockouts are causing panicked over-ordering). Trusting a balancing loop and doing less. Systems thinking explains why experienced people sometimes do the opposite of what looks right.

**The hardest part is identifying the goal**: Every system has an actual goal — what it's structured to produce — which may differ from the stated goal. If a diet plan keeps producing weight regain, its actual goal (homeostasis) is winning over the stated goal (fat loss). If an organization's stated goal is customer service but its metrics are call volume, the actual goal is call throughput. Align structure with actual desired outcomes, not stated ones.

## Getting Started

1. **Read *Thinking in Systems* by Donella Meadows.** It's short (~200 pages), readable, and covers every concept in this file with better examples. No other systems thinking book is as clear or as complete.

2. **Pick one recurring problem in your life and diagram it.** Identify: what's the stock? What are the flows? What feedback loops are maintaining the current state? Where's the delay? Do this on paper with actual arrows.

3. **For any intervention you're about to make, ask:** Am I changing a number (low leverage), or am I changing a feedback loop, goal, or information flow (higher leverage)?

4. **Notice "fixes that fail" and "shifting the burden" in your own life.** These two archetypes cover a huge fraction of recurring personal problems.

## Common Mistakes

**Confusing events with structure.** Events are what happened. Systems thinking is interested in the pattern of events over time and the structure underneath that produces the pattern. Don't stop at the event level.

**Seeing only linear chains.** "A caused B" is usually an oversimplification. A caused B, which fed back to reinforce A, with a delay that made it look like C caused B. Look for loops, not lines.

**Trying to optimize the wrong variable.** Improving a metric that isn't the actual goal produces systems that look better by the measurement while getting worse in reality. Common in organizations, common in personal health tracking. First confirm you're measuring the right thing.

**Impatience with delays.** The most common response to a delayed system is escalation — applying more of the intervention because it "isn't working." This causes overshoot. Patience is a structural response to a delayed system, not a passive one.

**Missing the limiting stock.** When growth stops, it stopped because something ran out or got depleted. Identifying the limiting stock is the key to restoring growth. Adding more inputs to a different stock doesn't help.
