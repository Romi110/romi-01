# Mental Models
*The 20 most useful lenses for thinking clearly — how to see situations accurately and reason without common failures*

---

## What Is It

A mental model is a compressed representation of how something works. You already use them constantly — "supply and demand," "compound interest," "feedback loop" — these are mental models. The goal of building a deliberate library is to have accurate, named models available when you need them, instead of defaulting to the ones you picked up accidentally.

Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's partner, made the case most clearly: "You need a latticework of mental models in your head... and you hang your experience on this latticework of models." The alternative — reasoning from first principles every time, or defaulting to whatever pattern your brain happens to reach for — is slower and error-prone.

This is a reference file. It is not meant to be read in sequence — it's a library to return to. The models are grouped by what kind of thinking they improve.

## Why It Matters

You're making decisions and interpreting situations constantly. Every one of those interpretations runs through a mental model whether you name it or not. Unnamed models are invisible, which means you can't test them, update them, or notice when you're misapplying them. Naming them makes them legible.

For Romi: this compounds with the decision-making frameworks in [decision-making.md](decision-making.md). The frameworks are the process; the mental models are the lenses you apply within that process.

## Core Concepts

### Thinking Clearly About the World

#### First Principles Thinking
Break a problem down to what's fundamentally true — the base facts — and reason up from there, rather than from analogy or convention. Most conventional thinking is chains of analogy from prior art. First principles cuts through that.

*Use it*: When the conventional answer feels wrong. When you're in a fast-changing domain. When you want to build something genuinely different rather than a copy.

*Watch out for*: Reinventing wheels that didn't need reinventing. Not everything deserves first-principles treatment — it's expensive in time.

#### The Map Is Not the Territory
Your mental model of something is not the thing itself. It's an approximation, always. The danger is treating the map as accurate when the territory has changed or when the map was wrong to begin with.

*Use it*: Whenever you catch yourself arguing about how something "should" be based on your model, when reality is showing you something different. The evidence wins; the model updates.

*In practice*: "My model says this should work, but it isn't working" — investigate the gap rather than defending the model.

#### Occam's Razor
Among competing explanations, prefer the simpler one — the one that requires fewer assumptions. Not because simpler is always right, but because every additional assumption is an additional way to be wrong.

*Use it*: When evaluating competing explanations for what's happening. When troubleshooting systems.

*Limit*: "Simpler" doesn't mean "familiar." Sometimes the accurate explanation is counterintuitive. Occam's Razor is a starting presumption, not a final verdict.

#### Hanlon's Razor
Never attribute to malice what can be explained by incompetence (or ignorance, or accident).

*Use it*: When someone's action hurt you. Before escalating a conflict. The vast majority of the time, people aren't trying to sabotage you — they're distracted, uninformed, or making their own mistake.

*Limit*: Applies to individuals, not systems. Systemic patterns of harmful behavior can be malicious even when individual actors are each "just following the rules."

#### The Lindy Effect
For non-perishable things (ideas, books, technologies, practices), the expected remaining lifespan is proportional to the current age. Something that has survived 100 years will likely survive another 100. Something that launched last year may not survive next year.

*Use it*: Evaluating what to learn and invest time in. The Stoics, compound interest, basic nutrition science — these are Lindy-robust. Most trending frameworks and tools are not.

*In practice*: Before investing deeply in something new, ask whether anything in the same category has lasted 20+ years. If not, hold lightly.

### Thinking About Systems and Causation

#### Second-Order Thinking
Every action produces consequences. Those consequences produce further consequences. Second-order thinking means explicitly working through the chain: *And then what?*

*Use it*: Major decisions, policy thinking, any situation with a significant lag between action and outcome. See [decision-making.md](decision-making.md) for applied framework.

#### Feedback Loops
A system where outputs become inputs. Positive feedback loops amplify (compounding, viral spread, habit reinforcement). Negative feedback loops stabilize (a thermostat, market price correction).

*Use it*: Understanding any dynamic system — markets, habits, ecosystems, software. Ask: what are the feedback loops here? Are they accelerating or stabilizing the behavior?

#### Compounding
Small consistent inputs produce disproportionately large outputs over time. Works for money, knowledge, reputation, skill, and habits — in both directions (compound gains and compound damage).

*Use it*: Long-range planning. Any decision with cumulative effects. The question to ask: "If I do this consistently for 10 years, what does the compounded result look like?"

*The key insight*: The compounding curve looks nearly flat for a long time, then bends sharply upward. This makes it psychologically hard to sustain — you don't feel the returns until late.

#### Mean Reversion
In systems subject to random variation, extreme outcomes tend to be followed by outcomes closer to the average. A company with an unusually great year will likely have a more ordinary year next. An athlete who has an unusually bad game will likely play more averagely next game.

*Use it*: Interpreting performance data. Hiring decisions. Investment evaluation. Before concluding that an extreme outcome represents a permanent new state, ask whether it's likely mean reversion.

*Common failure*: Hiring a new manager after a terrible quarter and crediting them when the next quarter is better (the company was reverting to mean regardless). Or firing a manager after a great quarter when the next quarter is worse.

### Thinking About Evidence and Bias

#### Survivorship Bias
You see the winners, not the full population. The successful businesses, the famous books, the people who "made it." The ones that failed are invisible — they didn't survive to be studied.

*Use it*: Any time you're learning from success stories. Ask: where are the failures? What happened to the people who tried this and didn't make it? Success rates matter more than success stories.

*Classic example*: "This style of investing made these famous investors rich" — but how many people tried the same approach and failed? You never hear from them.

#### Confirmation Bias
The tendency to seek, notice, and remember information that confirms what you already believe, while filtering out information that doesn't.

*Use it as a diagnostic*: When you're researching something you already have an opinion on, actively seek the strongest counterargument. Not to change your mind necessarily — to test whether your position survives contact with the best objection.

#### The Planning Fallacy
Humans systematically underestimate how long tasks take and how much they'll cost. This applies even when people have experience doing similar tasks. The inside view feels accurate; the base rate (how long these things usually take) is more reliable.

*Use it*: Any time you're estimating time or cost for a project. Apply a multiplier to your initial estimate — typically 1.5–2x for familiar work, higher for novel work. Use the outside view: how long did similar things actually take?

#### Sunk Cost Fallacy
Past investment (money, time, effort) is not a reason to continue a failing course of action. The past investment is gone regardless of what you do next. The only relevant question: what does the best future look like from here?

*Use it*: When you catch yourself justifying continued investment with "but I've already put so much in." That phrase is a sunk cost signal. Ignore the past investment and evaluate the future-only options.

#### Dunning-Kruger Effect
At low competence levels, people overestimate their ability (they don't know enough to know what they don't know). As competence grows, the estimate drops (you see how complex it really is). At high competence, estimates rise again (but now they're accurate).

*Use it*: Calibrating confidence — both your own and others'. The most confident voice in the room is often the least competent. The expert often expresses the most uncertainty.

*In practice*: When you're new to something, actively distrust your confidence. Seek feedback from people who are deep in it.

### Thinking About Trade-offs and Choices

#### Opportunity Cost
Choosing anything means not choosing the best alternative. The true cost of any decision includes what you gave up. This applies to time as much as money.

*Use it*: Evaluating any new commitment. "What is this displacing?" If the answer is your best alternative, and the new thing isn't better, it's not worth doing.

#### Circle of Competence
You have areas where your judgment is reliable (your circle) and areas where it isn't (outside your circle). Most disasters come from making confident decisions outside the circle, especially under the mistaken belief that you're inside it.

*Use it*: Before making a decision, ask honestly: is this inside or outside my circle? Acting outside it isn't always wrong — but you should know when you're doing it and adjust confidence accordingly.

#### Via Negativa
Subtraction often improves more than addition. Removing what's harmful, inefficient, or distracting produces more benefit than adding new things. The first-order question is usually "what should I add?" The better question is often "what should I remove?"

*Use it*: When a system isn't performing well. When you're overwhelmed. When habits aren't sticking. Often the bottleneck is not missing a good thing — it's the presence of a bad one.

*Examples*: Removing alcohol improves health more than adding supplements. Removing notifications improves focus more than buying a focus app. Removing a toxic obligation frees more time than optimizing everything else.

#### Margin of Safety
Build in buffer beyond what you calculate you need. Your calculation is based on assumptions that may be wrong, or on a world that may change. The margin of safety absorbs the gap between your model and reality.

*Use it*: Engineering. Finance (don't invest borrowed money in volatile assets). Planning (add 30% to project estimates). Emergency preparedness (hold 3 months expenses, not 1).

#### Skin in the Game
Trust information, advice, and recommendations more when the person providing them bears the consequences of being wrong. A doctor who would take the same medication, an investor who holds the same position, an advisor whose fee depends on outcomes — these are more credible than those whose recommendations cost them nothing.

*Use it*: Evaluating advice and sources. Before taking someone's word on something high-stakes, ask: what happens to them if they're wrong? If the answer is "nothing," apply more skepticism.

## Key Considerations

**A model used wrong is worse than no model.** Survivorship bias applied backward (assuming everything successful was inevitable) is worse than not knowing the model. Learn the failure modes for each model alongside the model itself.

**Models are for thinking, not winning arguments.** The goal is accurate perception, not having a clever label to attach to whatever the other person is doing.

**Build depth in a few, not breadth in many.** Knowing 20 models at surface level is worth less than knowing 5 well enough to apply them reliably under pressure. Start with the models that address your most common failure modes.

**Track misapplications.** When a model led you wrong, note it. What was the misapplication? This is how the library becomes calibrated rather than just large.

## Getting Started

Identify your top failure mode in thinking. If it's:
- Overconfidence on new topics → start with Dunning-Kruger and circle of competence
- Making decisions that look good short-term but hurt long-term → second-order thinking and opportunity cost
- Holding on to things that aren't working → sunk cost fallacy and via negativa
- Learning from wrong sources → survivorship bias and skin in the game

Pick two or three. Apply them deliberately for 30 days before expanding. Breadth-first collection of models produces shallow pattern matching, not genuine thinking.

## Common Mistakes

**Naming models instead of applying them.** Knowing the term "survivorship bias" and actually correcting for survivorship bias in your analysis are different things. The model only counts when it changes the analysis.

**Using models to sound smart.** A well-placed "that's just the planning fallacy" in a meeting is not the point. The point is accurate decisions.

**Assuming one model explains everything.** Most situations require multiple lenses. A company's failure is usually survivorship bias *and* sunk cost *and* planning fallacy *together*. Single-model analysis is usually incomplete.
