# Cognitive Biases
*15 biases that distort judgment: how they work, when they activate, and how to catch them*

---

## What Is It

Cognitive biases are systematic errors in thinking — predictable patterns in which human judgment deviates from rational analysis. They're not random mistakes; they're consistent, reliable distortions that arise from the heuristics the brain uses to make fast decisions. Most of the time, these heuristics work well. In specific situations, they fail in predictable ways.

This file covers the 15 most consequential biases — the ones that distort financial decisions, relationships, work, and self-assessment. Not the full Kahneman catalog, but the ones that actually show up in decisions that matter.

---

## Why It Matters

You can't think your way out of a bias you don't know you have. The first defense is knowing the patterns — recognizing when you're in the conditions where a specific bias tends to activate. The second defense is building decision processes that force the considerations the bias would suppress.

This knowledge transfers to everywhere decisions get made: hiring, negotiating, investing, assessing yourself and others, making plans.

---

## Core Concepts

### Foundational Framework: System 1 and System 2

Kahneman's model (from *Thinking, Fast and Slow*): the brain operates in two modes.

**System 1**: Fast, automatic, unconscious. Pattern matching, intuition, heuristics. Effortless but error-prone in complex situations. Produces most of your mental activity most of the time.

**System 2**: Slow, deliberate, effortful. Logic, analysis, planning. Requires attention and energy. Prone to fatigue and shortcuts.

Most cognitive biases are System 1 errors that System 2 fails to catch — either because System 2 isn't engaged, is too tired, or confirms what System 1 already concluded.

### The 15 Biases

**1. Confirmation Bias**

*What it is*: The tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs, while ignoring or dismissing contradicting evidence.

*How it operates*: Not just about ignoring contrary evidence — it's subtler. You ask different questions when you want a particular answer. You remember confirming examples more readily than disconfirming ones. You interpret ambiguous information as supporting your view.

*When it activates*: Any situation where you have a prior belief — an opinion about a person, a theory about why something is failing, a hypothesis you've already formed.

*Catch it*: Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Ask: "What would need to be true for me to be wrong about this?" Steelman the opposing view before dismissing it.

---

**2. Anchoring Bias**

*What it is*: Over-reliance on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making estimates or decisions. Subsequent information is adjusted from the anchor insufficiently.

*How it operates*: The anchor biases all subsequent estimates toward it, even when the anchor is arbitrary or irrelevant. A high asking price for a car anchors the negotiation even if the car is objectively worth far less.

*When it activates*: Negotiations, price assessments, salary discussions, estimates of quantity or time. Any context where a number is offered first.

*Catch it*: Generate your own estimate before encountering any anchor. In negotiations, name your number first (set the anchor) or immediately challenge the anchor as irrelevant: "Let's set aside that number and work from the actual value."

---

**3. Availability Heuristic**

*What it is*: Judging the likelihood of events by how easily examples come to mind — not by actual frequency.

*How it operates*: Plane crashes feel more common than car accidents because they're more vivid and media-covered, even though car accidents kill far more people. A recent bad experience with a contractor makes you think all contractors are unreliable.

*When it activates*: Risk assessment, probability estimation, any time you're asked "how common is X?"

*Catch it*: Ask for base rates. Look for data rather than relying on recalled examples. Ask: "How many times has this actually happened, not just how many times can I easily remember it happening?"

---

**4. Sunk Cost Fallacy**

*What it is*: Continuing an endeavor because of previously invested resources (time, money, effort) rather than evaluating future prospects on their own merits.

*How it operates*: You've spent $40,000 on a business that's clearly failing, so you invest another $20,000 because "I've already put too much in to quit." The prior investment is gone regardless of what you do next — it should not affect the forward decision.

*When it activates*: Any decision where prior investment creates emotional attachment — projects, relationships, careers, investments.

*Catch it*: Frame the decision prospectively: "If I had not invested anything already, would I start this today?" If no, that's your answer. The sunk cost is sunk — it's not recoverable and shouldn't be a factor.

---

**5. Loss Aversion**

*What it is*: Losses loom larger than equivalent gains — psychologically, losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good (roughly 2:1 ratio). This asymmetry leads to overly risk-averse decisions when losses are framed, and under-diversification in portfolios.

*How it operates*: The pain of a loss is felt more acutely than the pleasure of an equivalent gain. This is adaptive (losses in ancestral environments often meant death), but it leads to poor expected-value decisions in modern contexts.

*When it activates*: Financial decisions, negotiations, any time the framing includes potential losses ("you'll lose X if you don't" vs. "you'll gain X if you do").

*Catch it*: Reframe decisions in terms of expected value, not loss/gain framing. If the expected outcome is positive, the aversion to downside is irrational. Distinguish recoverable from unrecoverable losses — the bias matters most when the loss is painful but recoverable.

---

**6. Dunning-Kruger Effect**

*What it is*: At low competence levels, people tend to overestimate their ability (lack the knowledge to know what they don't know). At high competence levels, people tend to underestimate their relative ability (they know how much there is to know).

*How it operates*: Beginners often feel confident; experts often feel uncertain. The least competent people have the most inflated self-assessments. This is not a knock on intelligence — it's a structural feature of incomplete knowledge.

*When it activates*: Self-assessment of any skill, domain, or situation where you're early in learning.

*Catch it*: Actively seek feedback from people more expert than you. Be most skeptical of your confidence when you're newest to a domain. The "I've read 3 articles about this" phase of learning is the peak Dunning-Kruger risk zone.

---

**7. Attribution Errors**

*What it is*: Two related biases in how we explain behavior:
- *Fundamental attribution error*: When others do something, we attribute it to their character ("he's lazy"). When we do the same thing, we attribute it to circumstances ("I was tired").
- *Self-serving bias*: We attribute successes to our own qualities and failures to external circumstances.

*When it activates*: Any judgment of behavior — hiring, performance reviews, conflict, blame assignment.

*Catch it*: Ask: "What situational factors might explain this behavior?" before concluding it's a character trait. For self-serving bias: apply the same attribution standard to your own successes and failures that you'd apply to others.

---

**8. Overconfidence Bias**

*What it is*: Systematic overestimation of the accuracy of your own beliefs and predictions. Studies consistently show people are wrong more often than they believe they'll be — calibrated confidence is rare.

*How it operates*: When people say they're 90% confident in a fact, they're typically right about 70–75% of the time. Predictions about the future are especially poorly calibrated.

*When it activates*: Predictions, estimates, plans, negotiation, expert judgment in complex domains.

*Catch it*: Track predictions and score them. Explicitly widen confidence intervals — your 90% confidence interval should be about twice as wide as your intuition suggests. Use pre-mortem thinking: "Assume this failed — what went wrong?"

---

**9. Planning Fallacy**

*What it is*: The systematic tendency to underestimate the time, costs, and risks of future projects and to overestimate their benefits. Closely related to overconfidence.

*How it operates*: Project estimates almost universally come in over budget and behind schedule, across domains ranging from construction to software to personal projects. The optimism comes from focusing on the best-case scenario and ignoring the reference class of similar projects.

*When it activates*: Any project planning, time estimation, budgeting.

*Catch it*: Reference class forecasting: instead of planning from the inside view (our specific project), look at the outside view (how long do similar projects actually take?). Add a 50–100% buffer to initial estimates for anything novel. Track your past project timelines vs. estimates and apply your personal correction factor.

---

**10. Status Quo Bias**

*What it is*: Preference for the current state of affairs, even when change would produce better outcomes. The status quo becomes the reference point; deviations from it are perceived as losses.

*How it operates*: Combined with loss aversion, the status quo feels safe even when it demonstrably isn't. People keep bad investments longer than they should, stay in bad situations longer than makes sense, and choose default options over potentially better alternatives.

*When it activates*: Any decision involving change — career transitions, ending relationships or commitments, portfolio rebalancing.

*Catch it*: Reframe: "If I were starting fresh, would I choose the current situation?" Use the outside view on the current state: would you recommend this status quo to a friend? Explicitly evaluate the cost of inaction — the status quo has costs too.

---

**11. Social Proof (Herding)**

*What it is*: Using the behavior of others as evidence of the correct behavior, especially in situations of uncertainty.

*How it operates*: Adaptive in genuine uncertainty (others may have information you don't). Maladaptive when used to justify bad decisions ("everyone is doing it"), in financial bubbles (everyone's buying this stock), or in groupthink dynamics.

*When it activates*: Uncertainty, social situations, financial markets, product choices.

*Catch it*: Ask: "Would this be a good decision if I couldn't observe what others were doing?" Separate the fact that others are doing something from evidence that it's the right thing. Crowds are often wrong, especially at extremes.

---

**12. In-Group Bias**

*What it is*: Favoring members of your own group — by nationality, company, tribe, ideology, or any other marker — over out-group members, often without conscious awareness.

*How it operates*: Trust, attribution, hiring, information interpretation all bend toward in-group members. Work produced by in-group members is judged more favorably; the same work from out-group members is judged more harshly.

*When it activates*: Any evaluative context where group membership is salient — hiring, negotiations, political information processing, conflict.

*Catch it*: Blind evaluations where possible. Ask: "Would my assessment change if this person were from my tribe/company/country?" Check for consistency in standards applied to in-group vs. out-group.

---

**13. Framing Effect**

*What it is*: The same information presented differently produces different choices. "90% survival rate" and "10% death rate" describe identical situations but produce different responses.

*How it operates*: Positively framed options feel better than negatively framed equivalents. Losses are felt more strongly than gains, so negative framing activates more caution. Skilled communicators and marketers exploit this constantly.

*When it activates*: Medical decisions, policy choices, negotiations, advertising, any time information is being presented.

*Catch it*: Rephrase the same choice in multiple framings and see if your preference changes. If the preference changes with framing but the underlying reality hasn't, the framing is influencing you irrationally.

---

**14. Hindsight Bias**

*What it is*: After learning the outcome, believing you "knew it all along" — the outcome feels inevitable in retrospect even though it wasn't predictable in advance.

*How it operates*: This distorts learning from experience ("I knew that would fail") and overestimates predictability of complex events. It also leads to unfair blame — judging past decisions by information that wasn't available at the time.

*When it activates*: Post-hoc analysis of any outcome — business decisions, sports, market moves, relationship failures.

*Catch it*: Record predictions and reasoning before outcomes are known — journals, decision logs. When analyzing a failure, reconstruct what was actually known at the time the decision was made, not what's known now. Ask: "Was this decision reasonable given the information available then?"

---

**15. Recency Bias**

*What it is*: Over-weighting recent events relative to the broader historical distribution. Recent experience feels more representative than it actually is.

*How it operates*: Investors who lived through a crash assume crash conditions will continue; investors in a bull market assume returns will always be strong. Recent hires are rated more favorably than those from 18 months ago. Whatever just happened looms too large.

*When it activates*: Performance evaluation, market timing, risk assessment, planning.

*Catch it*: Look at longer time series rather than recent snapshots. Ask: "What's the historical base rate, not just the recent experience?" Build in explicit review of trends over multiple time periods.

## Key Considerations

**Knowing biases doesn't fully inoculate you**: Studies show that awareness of a bias slightly reduces its effect but doesn't eliminate it. The stronger defense is process — building decision procedures that force relevant information to be considered regardless of what System 1 wants to do.

**Biases compound**: Loss aversion + confirmation bias + overconfidence in a single investment decision is a particularly bad combination. They interact and reinforce each other.

**Biases are sometimes adaptive**: These heuristics exist because they often work. Availability heuristic produces reasonable risk assessments in most normal situations. Confirmation bias supports efficient learning in stable environments. The goal is not to eliminate them but to catch them when the conditions cause them to fail.

**The bias blind spot**: People recognize biases in others more readily than in themselves. Believing you're less biased than average is itself a bias (illusory superiority). Calibrate toward assuming you're as biased as the research says people are.

---

## Getting Started

1. **Keep a decision journal**: Write down your reasoning and predictions before outcomes are known. Review periodically — this builds calibration and exposes your personal bias patterns.
2. **Pre-mortem thinking on important decisions**: Before committing, assume the decision failed spectacularly — what went wrong? This forces consideration of the scenario System 1 would suppress.
3. **Identify your three highest-risk biases**: Which of the 15 are most relevant to your current decisions (career, finance, relationships)? Focus on the ones with highest stakes.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Applying bias-spotting only to other people's arguments**: The bias blind spot makes this the default. Apply the same scrutiny to your own positions.

**Thinking about biases in the abstract, not in active decisions**: The time to catch a bias is in the moment of decision — not in retrospect. Build a habit of brief structured reflection before significant choices.

**Conflating heuristics with biases**: A heuristic that produces a correct answer in a given situation isn't a bias — it's working. The bias is the systematic error produced when the heuristic is applied outside its appropriate domain.
