# Yoga Fundamentals
*The complete Indian practice system — not just stretching; a coherent framework for body, breath, and attention.*

---

## What Is It

Yoga is a 2,500-year-old Indian system of physical, mental, and spiritual practice. In the West, "yoga" almost always means asana (physical postures) — one of eight limbs described by Patanjali in the Yoga Sutras (~200 CE). The full system is larger: asana prepares the body; pranayama regulates the nervous system through breath; dharana and dhyana develop sustained attention. Most Western classes engage only with asana and sometimes breath.

The physical practice is most useful when it connects to breath and attention — which is also what separates yoga from generic flexibility work. A pose held without breath awareness is a stretch. The same pose held with coordinated ujjayi breath and focused attention is a different kind of training.

## Why It Matters

At 28 with a strength training background: yoga fills gaps that kettlebell and mace work don't cover — end-range control, spinal mobility in all planes, single-leg balance, and hip external rotation. It's not redundant with [mobility-and-flexibility.md](../health/mobility-and-flexibility.md); it's a practice system that develops those same joint ranges within a moving form rather than isolated drills.

The Stoic connection is legitimate. Patanjali's first definition: *chitta vritti nirodhah* — yoga is the stilling of the fluctuations of the mind. Holding a difficult position correctly demands exactly the kind of present-moment focus Stoics cultivated through contemplation. Distraction breaks form immediately.

## Core Concepts

### The Eight Limbs
Patanjali organized yoga as eight stages:

1. Yama — ethical restraints (non-harming, truthfulness, non-possessiveness)
2. Niyama — personal observances (cleanliness, contentment, discipline, self-study)
3. **Asana** — physical postures
4. **Pranayama** — breath control
5. Pratyahara — withdrawal of the senses
6. Dharana — concentration
7. Dhyana — meditation
8. Samadhi — integration, absorption

Most Western classes engage only with 3 and 4. Understanding the map clarifies what you're actually working with. Asana without breath integration stays physical therapy. Together they're a coherent training system for the body and attention simultaneously.

### Asana Categories
Each category develops different capacities:

- **Standing poses** (warrior sequences, triangle, crescent) — leg strength, hip flexibility, balance. Backbone of Vinyasa classes.
- **Balancing poses** (tree, warrior III, half-moon) — single-leg stability, proprioception, focused attention. A shaky balance pose reveals mental distraction as much as physical weakness.
- **Backbends** (cobra, bridge, wheel) — thoracic extension and shoulder mobility. Counter to prolonged desk sitting. Most neglected category for desk workers.
- **Seated and supine** (forward folds, twists, reclined poses) — hamstring and hip length, spinal rotation, decompression. Primary domain of Yin practice.
- **Restorative** — bolster-supported positions held 5–20 minutes. Not stretching. Parasympathetic activation. The body releases in a way that passive stretching alone doesn't achieve.

### Styles — Different Purposes

- **Vinyasa** — movement synchronized with breath, flow-based, physically demanding. Good for conditioning and breath-movement integration.
- **Yin** — passive holds for 3–5 minutes targeting connective tissue (fascia, ligaments). Deeply uncomfortable. The most complementary to strength training — active recovery that doesn't compete with lifting.
- **Restorative** — bolster-supported, 5–20 minute holds. Pure nervous system recovery. Even more passive than Yin.
- **Ashtanga** — fixed sequence, same postures every time. Highly disciplined; the primary series takes 90+ minutes. Not a beginner starting point.
- **Hatha** — slower paced, individual poses with holds. Best for learning alignment.

For someone strength training: **Yin 1–2x/week** (recovery, connective tissue), **Vinyasa 1x/week** (physical challenge, breath integration).

### Alignment Principles

Alignment is structural, not cosmetic. Misaligned poses load the wrong tissue and miss the intended adaptation:

- **Ground through all four corners of the foot** in standing poses (heel, ball of big toe, ball of little toe, outer heel). Activates the arch, creates stable base.
- **Hinge from the hips in forward folds**, not the lower back. Same principle as a deadlift setup.
- **Distribute backbends across the thoracic spine** — not collapsing into the lumbar where it already moves too much.
- **Shoulder packing in overhead positions** — scapulae depressed and set, not elevated. Same principle as in kettlebell overhead work.
- **Breath as alignment feedback** — if you can't breathe smoothly in a pose, something is working against itself. Back off.

### Breath in Yoga
In Vinyasa, movement is cued to breath — inhales for expansion and lift, exhales for compression and folding. The yogic breath technique (ujjayi) uses a slight throat constriction to create an audible hiss — Darth Vader, gently — which regulates breathing rate and creates a focal point.

Pranayama (breath control) is the fourth limb and has its own practice outside of asana. For the physiological basis of breath coordination and parasympathetic activation, see [breathwork.md](../health/breathwork.md) — the mechanisms are the same; yoga provides the movement context.

## Key Considerations

**Class quality varies enormously.** A good teacher gives alignment cues and modifications. A bad one demonstrates and plays music. Find instruction that actually teaches technique — in person or online (Yoga with Adriene for home practice is genuinely good).

**Yin is not comfortable.** The long holds target fascia. The discomfort starts at 2–3 minutes — that's when the work begins. Practitioners who leave thinking it was easy didn't hold long enough.

**Don't force range.** Most yoga injuries (hamstring tears, sacroiliac dysfunction) come from competing with the pose. The edge is mild-to-moderate sensation. Pain means stop.

**Yoga and strength training stack cleanly.** Yin and Restorative recover tissue; Vinyasa is a training session. Schedule accordingly.

## Getting Started

1. **Start with Yin**, one 60-minute class per week for a month. Targets the hip and spinal restrictions most people who lift will have.
2. **Online first to reduce friction** — Yoga with Adriene (Foundations series) is free and technically sound.
3. **Add Vinyasa once Yin feels manageable.** One class per week alongside Yin.
4. **Focus on hips and thoracic spine first.** Highest-leverage areas for someone with a strength training background.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Treating it as stretching only** — missing the breath and attention components misses most of the practice
- **Comparing to others in class** — every body's available range is different; injury comes from copying someone else's pose
- **Skipping Savasana** — the 5-minute closing rest integrates the session; leaving early wastes the parasympathetic effect
- **Jumping to advanced poses** — handstands and splits require years of foundation; people who chase them early plateau and get hurt

## Resources

- *Light on Yoga* — BKS Iyengar. The canonical asana reference. Dense, comprehensive, unmatched on alignment detail. Use as a reference, not a read-through.
- *The Yoga Sutras of Patanjali* — Edwin Bryant translation. The source text with commentary. Understand what yoga actually is before the studios filtered it.
- Yoga with Adriene (YouTube) — genuinely good entry-level instruction. "Foundations" and "30 Days of Yoga" series are structured and alignment-conscious.
