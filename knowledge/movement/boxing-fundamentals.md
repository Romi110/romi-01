# Boxing Fundamentals
*Stance, punching, defense, and the training system — boxing as a complete physical practice, not just a fight sport.*

---

## What Is It

Boxing is a combat sport and complete physical training system built around punching and movement. The sport version is well-known; what's less appreciated is boxing as a training methodology — one of the most comprehensive systems for building cardiovascular conditioning, coordination, timing, mental composure under stress, and full-body explosive power.

The training apparatus has a clear hierarchy: jump rope → shadowboxing → heavy bag → speed bag → mitts → sparring. Each layer develops something distinct. You can run a full training session with just a heavy bag and a rope. That self-contained completeness is unusual.

## Why It Matters

Boxing develops physical qualities that lifting doesn't. Lifting builds force capacity. Boxing trains force expression under fatigue, coordination under stress, and full-body power integration — the ability to generate force from the floor through the legs, hips, torso, shoulder, and into the punch. That kinetic chain transfers to any athletic movement requiring whole-body power.

The conditioning is severe and specific. Three-minute rounds with one-minute rest are deliberate — they replicate fight work-to-rest ratios and target the glycolytic and phosphocreatine energy systems. For the framework behind energy system training, see [strength-and-conditioning.md](../sports/strength-and-conditioning.md).

The mental component is more direct than most physical training. Sparring practices composure under real, if controlled, physical threat. In Stoic terms: boxing confronts exactly the distinction between what feels threatening and what actually is. A jab is both.

## Core Concepts

### Stance and Footwork

**Orthodox stance** (left foot forward) for right-handed fighters; southpaw (right foot forward) for left-handed. The structure:

- Feet shoulder-width apart, staggered; lead foot angled 45° toward opponent, rear foot roughly perpendicular
- Weight 60% front foot at rest, shifting with movement
- Lead hand at eyebrow level, rear hand at chin; elbows tucked covering the body
- **Chin down, eyes up** — chin tucked toward chest, looking through the brow; shortens the chin target and keeps vision high
- Knees slightly bent — never flat-footed

**Footwork principles:**
- Always move the foot closest to the direction of travel first; never cross the feet
- **Step-and-slide**: lead foot steps, rear foot follows; stance width maintained throughout
- **Pivoting**: rotate on the lead foot to change angle; creates offensive positions from the side
- **In-and-out**: lead foot enters, rear foot exits; the basic offensive entry and escape

Footwork is what beginners forget under pressure, the first thing to drill, and the last thing to master.

### The Four Punches

**Jab (1)** — lead hand straight punch. The most important punch in boxing. Not a power punch — a ranging tool, a set-up, a way of controlling distance and creating openings. Snaps out and returns faster than it extended. Mechanics: push off the rear foot, rotate the shoulder, return the hand to guard immediately.

**Cross (2)** — rear hand straight punch; the power punch. The rear heel rises as the hip pivots through, shoulder rotates fully, elbow stays horizontal. Power comes from the kinetic chain, not the arm.

**Hook (3)** — lead or rear hand, arc punch to the head or liver. Elbow parallel to the floor, arm bent ~90°, entire torso rotates. A liver hook (rear hook to the body) ends fights cleanly.

**Uppercut (4)** — vertical punch driving upward under the chin or into the body. Requires closing distance. Drop the shoulder slightly, then drive upward with legs and hip rotation.

Power in every punch comes from the floor up: feet → legs → hips → torso → shoulder → arm. Arm strength is the last factor.

### Defense

**Guard** — default position; hands protect the face, elbows cover the body. Block punches on the forearms and gloves rather than absorbing them with the face or ribs.

**Slip** — rotate the torso to move the head offline without moving the feet. A slip outside a jab positions you for a counter cross.

**Roll** — rotate under a hook by dropping the center and rolling the head under the punch's arc. Come up inside, ready to counter to the body.

**Parry** — deflect a punch off its track with the lead hand. Requires timing; doesn't work against power shots.

Hierarchy for beginners: keep your guard up first. Slipping and rolling only once the guard is automatic.

### Training Hierarchy

Each layer has a distinct purpose:

1. **Jump rope** — footwork timing, conditioning, coordination. 5–15 minutes every session.
2. **Shadowboxing** — full-speed technique with no bag. The only place where you choose and sequence your own combinations. 3-minute rounds.
3. **Heavy bag** — power and endurance. Heavy bag masks your mistakes; it doesn't move or punch back. Don't mistake hitting it hard for competence.
4. **Speed bag** — rhythm and hand-eye coordination. Not power training.
5. **Mitts** — partner holds focus pads; you hit on cue. Develops timing, accuracy, reaction. A skilled mitt holder accelerates development faster than any other tool.
6. **Sparring** — live training with a partner, controlled intensity. Nothing replaces it. Not the place to perform; the place to apply technique with real feedback.

## Key Considerations

**Find a real boxing gym, not a fitness boxing class.** Cardio boxing builds cardio but no technical skill. A coach watching and correcting your form is the entire difference. The gym matters less than the instruction.

**Don't rush to sparring.** Technique needs to be installed before it's tested. 3–6 months of fundamentals first — footwork, the four punches, combinations, bag work. Sparring with bad technique trains bad technique under pressure.

**Head movement is the most important skill nobody practices.** Everyone drills offense. The distinguishing mark of a skilled boxer is defensive — moving the head so punches miss, not just blocking them.

**Protect your hands.** Wraps before gloves, every session. Proper technique (first two knuckles making contact, wrist aligned) and hand wraps prevent the fractures that end training months.

## Getting Started

1. Find a boxing gym with coaches who give technical feedback. Try 2–3 before committing; the instructor matters far more than the equipment.
2. First 2 months: stance, jab, cross, hook, footwork. Shadowboxing and bag only.
3. 3 sessions per week: 10 min jump rope → 3 rounds shadowboxing → 3–4 rounds bag → conditioning.
4. Mitts when the coach decides you're ready. Controlled sparring once mitts feel consistent.

## Common Mistakes

- **Dropping the guard after punching** — hands return to face on every punch; drill this from day one, it erodes under pressure
- **Holding the breath** — exhale sharply with each punch; holding breath causes the inevitable gasp and destroys rhythm
- **Arms only** — throwing punches without hip rotation generates almost no power; the arm is the last link in the chain
- **Hunting for knockouts** — beginners want to hit hard; boxing is accuracy and timing first; power follows technique
- **Avoiding sparring indefinitely** — real feedback from a live partner is irreplaceable; controlled sparring with a partner who's not trying to hurt you is the only way to develop composure

## Resources

- Precision Striking (YouTube, coach Kostya) — best freely available technical boxing instruction; combination structure, footwork mechanics, defense drills.
- *Boxing: The Complete Guide to Training and Fitness* — Ian Oliver. Practical and comprehensive; covers the full training system clearly.
