# Stoicism

## What Is It?
A practical philosophy founded in ancient Greece and Rome. Not about suppressing emotions or being cold — it's about understanding what you control, acting with virtue, and not being jerked around by things outside your power. The Stoics were soldiers, emperors, slaves, and merchants. It was built for real life, not classrooms.

## Why It Matters to Me
- It directly addresses my consistency problem: do the work regardless of how you feel
- It aligns with self-reliance: depend on your own character, not external circumstances
- It's a daily practice, not just ideas — fits perfectly with this journaling system
- It teaches you to respond instead of react, which matters at work, in relationships, everywhere

## The Core Ideas

### The Dichotomy of Control
The most fundamental Stoic concept. Everything falls into two categories:
- **What I control**: My actions, my effort, my judgments, my responses, how I spend my time
- **What I don't control**: Other people's opinions, outcomes, the economy, the weather, the past

The practice: pour energy into the first category. Release attachment to the second. This doesn't mean don't care — it means don't let things you can't change destroy your peace or stop you from acting.

### The Four Virtues
Stoics organized a good life around four virtues:
1. **Wisdom** — seeing things clearly, knowing what matters and what doesn't
2. **Courage** — doing the right thing even when it's hard or uncomfortable
3. **Justice** — treating others fairly, contributing to your community
4. **Temperance** — self-control, moderation, not being driven by impulses

### Amor Fati (Love of Fate)
Don't just tolerate what happens — use it. Every setback is training. Lost your momentum on fitness? That's practice in starting again. Had a bad day at work? That's practice in responding well under pressure. Nothing is wasted if you choose to learn from it.

### Memento Mori (Remember You Will Die)
Not morbid — motivating. Time is finite. Knowing that makes you stop wasting it on things that don't matter. It makes you show up for the things that do. You're 28. You have time, but not unlimited time. Act accordingly.

### Premeditatio Malorum (Premeditation of Adversity)
Imagine what could go wrong — not to be anxious, but to be prepared. If you lose your job, what's your plan? If your car breaks down, can you handle it? This connects directly to your emergency preparedness and self-reliance goals.

## The Three Main Stoic Teachers

### Marcus Aurelius (121–180 AD)
Roman Emperor. Wrote "Meditations" — a private journal never meant to be published. It's basically a man reminding himself every day to be better. The most relatable Stoic text because it's raw and personal. **Start here.**

### Epictetus (50–135 AD)
Born a slave. Became one of the most influential philosophers in history. His core teaching: "It's not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters." Wrote "Discourses" and "Enchiridion" (a short handbook — also a great starting point).

### Seneca (4 BC–65 AD)
Roman statesman and writer. His "Letters to a Friend" are practical wisdom on time, anger, grief, and living well. Very readable and direct.

## Where to Start Reading
1. **"Meditations" by Marcus Aurelius** — the Gregory Hays translation is the most modern and readable
2. **"The Enchiridion" by Epictetus** — short, punchy, you can read it in an afternoon
3. **"Letters from a Stoic" by Seneca** — pick and choose letters by topic
4. **"The Daily Stoic" by Ryan Holiday** — one page per day, 366 entries, good for building a reading habit

## Daily Stoic Practices
These fit directly into your daily log:

1. **Morning intention**: Before the day starts, remind yourself what's in your control today. What's the one thing you'll focus on?
2. **Evening reflection**: What went well? Where did I react instead of respond? What can I do better tomorrow? (This IS your daily journal entry.)
3. **Negative visualization**: Briefly imagine losing something you value — your health, your partner, your job. Not to be depressed, but to feel gratitude for what you have right now.
4. **The view from above**: When stressed, zoom out. Will this matter in a year? Five years? On your deathbed?

## How This Connects to Everything Else
- **Consistency problem** → Stoicism says: you don't need motivation, you need discipline. Do it because it's the right action, not because you feel like it.
- **Self-reliance** → The Stoics were obsessed with self-sufficiency. Not isolation — but being capable.
- **Fitness** → Your body is the one thing you live in. Taking care of it is a discipline, not a hobby.
- **Learning** → Wisdom is the first virtue. Ignorance is voluntary. You're choosing to fix that.
