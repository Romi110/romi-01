    Preparing for a Declining US Empire¶

    What history says about how empires fall, what the honest indicators look like today, and the practical steps worth taking

    What Is It¶

    Every dominant empire in recorded history has eventually declined. The question is not whether the United States will decline — it will, as every great power does — but at what pace, through what mechanisms, and what that means for an ordinary person's life.

    This file is not about doomsday prepping or apocalyptic collapse. The historical record shows that most imperial decline is slow, uneven, and survivable — even livable — for most people who prepared thoughtfully. It looks like rising prices, institutional dysfunction, reduced global influence, and gradual erosion of living standards over a generation — not a single catastrophic event.

    The honest framing: you are probably not witnessing the end of America. You are probably witnessing a long transition from unchallenged global dominance to one major power among several — the same transition Britain made between 1900 and 1960. Most British people lived through that transition without catastrophe. But those who understood what was happening made different financial and practical choices than those who didn't.

    Why It Matters¶

    The specific combination of Romi's goals — self-reliance, food production, energy independence, financial literacy, practical skills — is exactly the right preparation for this environment whether or not any particular prediction about decline comes true. These aren't hedge-your-bets moves. They're worth doing anyway. The decline scenario makes them more urgent, not different.

    The Stoic framing applies precisely here: macro geopolitical trajectories are not in your control. Your financial positioning, practical skills, and local resilience are. Focus on the latter.

    Core Concepts¶

    The Historical Pattern — How Empires Actually Decline¶

    Ray Dalio's research on reserve currency cycles (The Changing World Order) and the broader historical pattern identify consistent dynamics. The arc, compressed:

    1. Rise: A power develops productive capacity, military strength, and trade networks. It accumulates wealth and extends its influence.
    2. Dominance: The power's currency becomes the global reserve — the medium of international trade and savings. This creates enormous structural advantages: you can print money that the world accepts, borrow cheaply, and run persistent deficits.
    3. Overextension: Military commitments expand beyond productive justification. Domestic inequality rises as financial interests extract more than productive ones create. Political polarization increases.
    4. Debt accumulation: The reserve currency advantage enables chronic borrowing. Debts accumulate to levels where they can't be repaid in real terms — only through inflation (printing money to devalue the debt).
    5. Debasement: The currency is inflated to manage the debt burden. This slowly erodes the purchasing power of savings and wages.
    6. Loss of reserve status: Over decades, other powers develop alternatives. The reserve currency's dominance erodes gradually, then faster.
    7. Decline: Living standards fall relative to rising powers. Global influence contracts. Internal conflict often increases as the shrinking pie is fought over.

    How long does this take? The British Empire's decline from peak dominance (1900) to secondary power status (1960s) took roughly 60 years. The Ottoman decline lasted 150 years. The Soviet collapse, from structural overextension to actual dissolution, took 40 years. These are generational processes, not events. For most of those transitions, daily life remained functional — sometimes very functional — for significant periods.

    The critical variable: whether decline is managed (like Britain — relatively graceful handoff to American dominance, institutional continuity, welfare state built during the decline) or catastrophic (like Rome — institutional collapse, fragmentation, loss of security). The managed version is survivable and even prosperous for well-positioned individuals. The catastrophic version is a different problem.

    Where the US Actually Stands¶

    This is where honest assessment matters more than either cheerleading or catastrophism. The indicators worth taking seriously:

    Debt and fiscal trajectory - US national debt: ~$36 trillion, roughly 120%+ of GDP (as of 2025–26) - Federal government runs structural deficits of $1–2 trillion annually even outside recessions - Interest payments on the debt now exceed the defense budget — a meaningful threshold - The only resolution paths: grow out of it (historically rare at this debt level), inflate it away (gradual purchasing power destruction), or restructure (dramatic and destabilizing) - This is not alarmism — the CBO and Federal Reserve themselves project that the current trajectory is unsustainable without significant policy change

    Dollar reserve status — under pressure, not ending - ~58% of global foreign exchange reserves are in USD (down from ~70% in 2000) - BRICS nations (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa + new members) are actively developing dollar alternatives for bilateral trade - China's yuan is growing as a trade settlement currency, particularly in commodity markets (oil priced in yuan in some bilateral deals) - The dollar is not being displaced quickly — there is no credible replacement that matches the dollar's depth, liquidity, and institutional backing. But the direction of travel is toward a multipolar currency world over the next 20–40 years - Implication for you: a slow, long-term erosion of dollar purchasing power is more likely than a sudden collapse

    Manufacturing decline and trade dependency - The US imports ~80% of manufactured goods in many categories - Supply chain vulnerabilities became visible during COVID: PPE, semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, rare earths — all sourced primarily from geopolitical rivals or fragile supply chains - The reshoring trend (CHIPS Act, IRA subsidies) is real but early-stage - Implication: external shocks to global trade disrupt American daily life more than they used to

    Political and institutional erosion - Trust in major institutions (Congress, media, courts, healthcare, education) has declined sharply and steadily over 20+ years — across both parties - Political polarization in Congress (measured by voting patterns) is at the highest level since Reconstruction - This is a weak leading indicator of harder institutional dysfunction ahead, but it is the right direction for a declining empire - Note: institutional erosion is reversible. It's also been predicted to accelerate for 20 years and hasn't catastrophically broken yet

    What the US still has - The deepest, most liquid capital markets in the world - Dollar still dominates global trade and reserves despite declining share - Still the world's largest economy by several measures - Military power still without peer - Energy independence achieved (net oil exporter since 2019) - Demographic advantage over China, Japan, and Europe due to immigration - Innovation ecosystem (AI, biotech, software) that remains unmatched globally

    Honest summary: The US is in relative decline from peak dominance — not absolute collapse. The risks are real over a 20–40 year horizon: inflation, reduced purchasing power, institutional dysfunction, supply chain vulnerability. The risks are not: sudden currency collapse, loss of basic governmental function, imminent military defeat.

    Plan for the real risks, not the dramatic ones.

    Financial Preparation¶

    The financial moves that make sense given this picture — most of which are good financial practice regardless:

    Reduce dollar concentration - If all your savings are in USD-denominated assets, you have full currency risk. This was fine when the dollar was 70% of global reserves and rising. It's worth diversifying as that trend reverses. - International equity exposure: a global index fund (VXUS covers total non-US world market) or a total world fund (VT) alongside domestic holdings. Many financial advisors recommend 20–40% international allocation. - This is already addressed in finance/investing-beyond-index-funds.md

    Hard assets and inflation hedges - Real estate: historically one of the best inflation hedges. A home is a real asset that doesn't depend on dollar purchasing power maintaining its value - I-Bonds and TIPS: government bonds indexed to inflation. I-Bonds especially (covered in finance/investing-beyond-index-funds.md) - Commodities exposure (through funds, not physical storage for most purposes) - Precious metals: gold has preserved purchasing power across every major currency debasement in history. Not a growth asset, not an income asset — a preservation asset. 5–10% of portfolio as inflation/chaos insurance is reasonable. Physical gold or gold ETFs (IAU, GLD)

    Skills over currency - This is the most underrated financial preparation. Skills — medical, mechanical, agricultural, construction, electrical — are inflation-proof and depression-proof. They're valuable regardless of what currency does. - Someone who can fix what breaks, grow food, manage their own health, and generate income independently is more resilient than someone with $200k in savings and no practical capabilities - The entire farming, energy, skills, and health sections of this wiki are financial preparation in this sense

    Reduce debt - In an inflationary environment, fixed-rate debt at below-inflation rates is actually advantageous (you're repaying with cheaper dollars). Variable-rate debt is dangerous — costs rise with inflation. - Avoid consumer debt. Fixed-rate mortgage at a low rate in an inflationary environment is one of the better positions to be in

    Income diversification - Multiple income streams are resilience. Side projects, skills that can be monetized, a partner who earns — all reduce dependency on any single employer or sector - See career/side-projects-to-income.md

    Physical Self-Reliance Preparation¶

    The farming, energy, and skills work in this wiki is exactly the right preparation for this scenario. Specifically:

    Food production - Producing even a fraction of your own food reduces grocery price exposure, supply chain dependency, and provides a skill that's valuable in disrupted conditions - Even a serious garden — 1,000 square feet with proper planning — can produce significant calories and most of a family's vegetables - See farming/self-sustainable-farming.md, farming/starting-from-scratch.md - Food preservation skills extend this: canned, fermented, and dehydrated food creates a buffer against supply disruptions and price spikes - See farming/food-preservation.md

    Energy independence - Distributed solar + battery storage removes your dependence on grid stability and utility pricing - Grid disruptions during extreme weather events are already more frequent and longer than a generation ago - A modest solar setup can power essentials (refrigeration, communication, medical equipment, lighting) through grid outages - See energy/diy-renewable-energy.md and related energy files

    Water - Municipal water supply is generally reliable in the US but is infrastructure that ages and can fail - Basic rainwater collection, filtration capability, and knowledge of nearby groundwater sources is low-cost insurance - See farming/water-management.md

    Medical and first aid knowledge - Healthcare costs are already among the most financially destabilizing events for American families - Basic first aid, emergency response, and understanding of common conditions reduces dependency on an increasingly expensive system - See the planned skills/first-aid-basics.md

    Home repair and mechanical skills - The ability to fix what breaks — plumbing, electrical, structural, mechanical — is directly inflation-resistant and reduces dependency on service providers whose labor costs rise with inflation - See skills/home-repair-basics.md

    Community and Location¶

    Community is the most underrated preparation. Individual prepping — stockpiling goods, building bunkers, withdrawing entirely from society — is both less effective and less enjoyable than building genuine community networks. Historical evidence from actual collapse events (Argentina 2001, Soviet collapse, Great Depression) consistently shows that social capital was more protective than individual stockpiles. People with strong community ties fared better — they had mutual aid, shared information, and could specialize in ways that isolated individuals couldn't.

    Practical community building: - Know your neighbors genuinely, not just by name - Participate in local institutions — civic organizations, local government, neighborhood networks - Develop relationships with people who have different complementary skills - The local politics and civic involvement file (civics/local-politics-civic-involvement.md) is relevant here — local government is where resilience is actually built

    Location considerations for New Jersey / Galloway area: - NJ is high cost-of-living and property tax state — financial vulnerability if tax revenues decline - The Northeast corridor has dense population and supply chain infrastructure — not ideal for extreme scenarios but very functional in moderate ones - The Pine Barrens region of NJ (where Galloway sits) is unusual: large undeveloped land area, groundwater access (the Cohansey Aquifer beneath the Pinelands is one of the largest unconfined aquifers in the US), low population density relative to surrounding areas, agricultural land, relatively dark skies - Downside: proximity to major metro areas (Philadelphia, NYC) means population pressure in severe disruption scenarios - The realistic scenario (not collapse, but gradual disruption) makes the current location fine — especially if farming, energy independence, and community roots are being built

    The "move to rural Montana" question: This is the prepper fantasy. For the realistic scenarios (inflation, supply chain disruption, institutional dysfunction over 20 years), moving away from your job, community, and support network to a remote location is usually more costly than it's worth. The more useful move is building resilience where you are.

    What to Ignore¶

    Hyperinflation / dollar collapse narratives: Gold bugs and doomsday financial media predict imminent dollar collapse every year. This narrative has been wrong for decades. Dollar debasement is real and ongoing — but it happens over decades, not in a sudden event. Positioning for it is rational; panic-selling is not.

    Imminent US collapse: The US has significant structural problems. It also has enormous structural advantages that countries typically in late-stage decline don't have. It's not Rome in 400 CE.

    The "right" safe haven country to relocate to: Every country has risks. The countries that look safe from America (Portugal, New Zealand, Costa Rica) have their own problems, require uprooting everything that makes your life functional now, and are usually not worth the cost in realistic scenarios. Possible in extreme scenarios; rarely worth it for the probable ones.

    Cryptocurrency as the primary hedge: Crypto is a high-volatility speculative asset, not a store of value in the sense gold is. Some allocation is fine as part of a speculative portfolio — not as your inflation hedge.

    Key Considerations¶

    The time horizon matters enormously. For a 5-year horizon: the US dollar remains dominant, most of your preparations should be about financial buffers (emergency fund, I-bonds, modest international diversification) and skill-building. For a 20–40 year horizon: the structural trends are meaningful and worth positioning against more deliberately.

    The preparation itself is the point. Building self-reliance skills, reducing financial fragility, growing food, generating your own energy, knowing your neighbors — these make your life better in any scenario. The decline thesis makes the work more urgent; it doesn't change its value.

    Stoic application: The macro trajectory of the American empire is not in your control. What's in your control: your balance sheet, your skills, your community, your land. The Stoic move is not anxiety about what you can't change — it's thorough, calm preparation in the areas where your action matters.

    Getting Started¶

    1. Financial audit first. What percentage of your assets are in USD-denominated accounts? Add VXUS or VT to your portfolio for international exposure. Add I-bonds for inflation protection.
    2. Get the first food production season going. Even a small raised bed garden is the starting point. See farming/starting-from-scratch.md.
    3. Assess your energy situation. Is solar feasible at your property? What would a basic setup cost and cover? Start with energy/sizing-a-solar-system.md.
    4. Build one practical skill this year. Home repair, first aid, food preservation — something that reduces your dependence on external services.
    5. Get to know three neighbors. Community isn't built in a crisis. Start now.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Conflating preparation with pessimism. Preparing for disruption doesn't mean believing in collapse. It means reducing fragility in your own life. Stoicism is not fatalism.

    Over-indexing on dramatic scenarios. People prepare for grid-down zombie apocalypse and miss the actual risk: inflation eroding purchasing power over 20 years while supply chains grow more fragile. Prepare for the likely scenario, not the dramatic one.

    Individual prepping over community building. A 6-month food stockpile with no community network is less resilient than a 1-month stockpile and ten solid relationships with neighbors who have different skills.

    Doing nothing because the timing is uncertain. The uncertainty argument is the reason to act now, not later. Every year you build skills, diversify assets, and grow food is a year of compounding preparation.

    Resources¶

    • The Changing World Order — Ray Dalio. The most rigorous data-driven analysis of reserve currency cycles and great power transitions. Dense but foundational. The accompanying documentary on YouTube is a good free version.
    • The Fourth Turning — Strauss & Howe. Generational cycle theory of American history — contested as deterministic but identifies real patterns. Read skeptically; the historical pattern analysis is better than the predictions.
    • Antifragile — Nassim Taleb. Not about empire decline specifically but the most important framework for thinking about resilience, optionality, and preparing for unknown disruptions without needing to predict the specific form they'll take.
    • How to Survive the End of the World as We Know It — James Wesley Rawles. The serious prepper's manual. Useful for specifics even if the framing is more extreme than warranted. Read for practical information, not the tone.
