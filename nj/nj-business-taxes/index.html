    NJ Business Taxes¶

    Every tax a retail business in New Jersey owes, when it's due, and how to minimize it legally

    What Is It¶

    NJ taxes a retail business at multiple levels simultaneously: sales tax collected from customers, income tax on profits, payroll taxes on wages, and entity-level fees. Understanding all of them matters because missing any one creates penalties, and because the choices you make about business structure legally determine how much income tax you pay.

    This file covers the full NJ + federal tax picture for a retail LLC or S-Corp operating in New Jersey.

    For the operational and legal side of running the business, see nj-retail-overview.md.

    Why It Matters¶

    NJ has one of the more complex tax environments for small business in the country: a 6.625% sales tax with a non-trivial exemption structure, a Corporation Business Tax, a Pass-Through Business Alternative Income Tax (BAIT) that's a genuine planning opportunity, and payroll taxes with multiple components. Getting these wrong is expensive — the sales tax penalty alone runs 5% per month on unpaid tax. Getting them right is tractable once you have the full picture.

    Sales Tax¶

    The Rate and What It Applies To¶

    NJ sales tax rate: 6.625% (reduced from 7% in 2018, where it has stayed).

    Urban Enterprise Zones (UEZ): select NJ cities have a reduced rate of 3.3125% (half the standard rate) for qualified purchases. If your retail location is in Asbury Park, Camden, Trenton, Newark, Elizabeth, or one of the ~30 UEZ municipalities, you may charge the reduced rate. Verify at nj.gov/njbusiness/uez.

    What Is Taxable¶

    Taxable in NJ retail: - Most tangible personal property - Prepared food and beverages (restaurant meals, ready-to-eat food, hot beverages) - Clothing and footwear at or above $110 per item (items under $110 are exempt) - Digital products sold to NJ customers (streaming, software downloads) - Most services related to tangible goods (installation, repair, alterations over $110 threshold)

    Exempt in NJ: - Groceries and most unprepared food (significant: no tax on most grocery sales) - Clothing and footwear under $110 per item — this is NJ-specific and counterintuitive to many retailers - Prescription and over-the-counter drugs - Newspapers and magazines - Sales to NJ-registered nonprofits with a tax-exempt certificate (Form ST-5)

    The clothing exemption is the most commonly misunderstood. If you sell apparel, a $109 shirt is exempt; a $111 shirt is taxable on the full $111 (not just the amount above $110).

    Certificate of Authority¶

    Required before your first taxable sale. Apply via NJ Division of Taxation at njtaxation.org. Free. Once issued, you receive a Certificate of Authority — it must be visibly displayed in your retail location. Operating without one: back taxes + 5% per month penalty + interest.

    Sales Tax Remittance Schedule¶

    NJ assigns filing frequency based on your annual sales tax liability:

    Annual Tax Liability Filing Frequency
    Less than $500 Annual (January 20)
    $500 – $30,000 Quarterly (20th of month after quarter end)
    Over $30,000 Monthly (20th of following month)

    Most small retailers start quarterly. As volume grows, NJ automatically bumps you to monthly filing. You can file online via NJ's TELE-FILE or through the Division of Taxation portal.

    Late filing penalty: 5% of tax due per month, up to 25%. Plus interest at the prime rate + 3%. Sales tax is collected money you hold in trust for the state — missing remittance is treated seriously.

    Nexus and Online Sales¶

    If you sell online to NJ customers (even from outside NJ), you owe NJ sales tax if you have economic nexus: $100,000 in NJ sales or 200 transactions to NJ customers in the prior year. Most retail businesses with a physical NJ location already have nexus by default. Shopify, Square, and most e-commerce platforms now handle sales tax calculation and filing automatically — use them rather than doing this manually.

    Keeping Records¶

    Keep all sales tax records for a minimum of 4 years (NJ statute of limitations for audit). Records should include: daily sales by category, exempt sales with customer certificates, returns, and remittance receipts. Your POS system should handle categorization if set up correctly at launch.

    NJ Corporation Business Tax (CBT)¶

    Who It Applies To¶

    Every corporation (including S-Corps) incorporated in NJ or doing business in NJ owes CBT. LLCs taxed as partnerships or single-member LLCs (disregarded entities) do not pay CBT — their income flows to the owner's personal NJ Gross Income Tax return.

    CBT Rates (2024)¶

    NJ Allocated Net Income Rate
    $0 – $100,000 6.5%
    Over $100,000 9.0%

    Minimum CBT tax: - LLCs: None (if treated as partnership or disregarded — see below) - S-Corps: $375/year minimum regardless of income - C-Corps: $500/year minimum

    NJ's 9% CBT rate is one of the highest in the region. This is a significant reason why most small retail businesses structure as LLCs (pass-through) rather than C-Corps.

    S-Corp in NJ¶

    An S-Corp election at the federal level does not automatically mean S-Corp treatment in NJ. You must separately file Form CBT-2553 with the NJ Division of Taxation to elect NJ S-Corp status. If you forget this step, NJ taxes your S-Corp as a C-Corp.

    NJ S-Corps still pay CBT on income allocated to NJ at the standard rate on income above the shareholder's reasonable salary — one of the few states that does this. This reduces (but doesn't eliminate) the S-Corp advantage in NJ compared to other states.

    CBT Filing Deadline¶

    Due 30 days after the federal corporate tax return: April 15 for calendar-year corporations (with a 6-month extension available). Extension extends the filing deadline, not the payment deadline.

    NJ Gross Income Tax (Pass-Through)¶

    For an LLC taxed as a pass-through, profit flows to the owner's personal NJ Gross Income Tax return. NJ has a progressive rate structure:

    NJ Taxable Income NJ GIT Rate
    $0 – $20,000 1.4%
    $20,001 – $35,000 1.75%
    $35,001 – $40,000 3.5%
    $40,001 – $75,000 5.525%
    $75,001 – $500,000 6.37%
    $500,001 – $1,000,000 8.97%
    Over $1,000,000 10.75%

    Business income is added to all other NJ income (wages, investment income, etc.) before applying these rates. A tech professional with a W-2 plus retail business income will hit the 6.37% bracket quickly.

    Quarterly Estimated Payments¶

    Once your combined NJ income tax liability exceeds $400/year from pass-through business income, you must make quarterly estimated payments to avoid underpayment penalties.

    NJ estimated payment due dates: - Q1: April 15 - Q2: June 15 - Q3: September 15 - Q4: January 15 (following year)

    Pay via njtaxation.org. The safe harbor: pay either 100% of prior year's NJ tax liability or 90% of current year's — whichever is lower — to avoid penalties.

    Pass-Through Business Alternative Income Tax (BAIT)¶

    What It Is and Why It Matters¶

    The BAIT is one of the most significant NJ tax planning opportunities for pass-through business owners and is widely underused.

    Background: The 2017 federal tax law (TCJA) capped the federal deduction for state and local taxes (SALT) at $10,000. This hurt high-income NJ residents who previously deducted full NJ income taxes on their federal return. The BAIT is NJ's legal workaround.

    How it works: - The LLC or S-Corp elects to pay NJ income tax at the entity level (the BAIT) instead of the owner paying it individually - The business gets a full federal deduction for the BAIT payment as a business expense (not subject to the $10,000 SALT cap) - The owner gets a credit on their personal NJ GIT return for the BAIT paid, so they don't pay it twice - Net effect: the NJ income tax on business income is federally deductible again

    BAIT rates (2024):

    Distributive Share Rate
    Up to $250,000 5.675%
    $250,001 – $1,000,000 6.52%
    $1,000,001 – $5,000,000 9.12%
    Over $5,000,000 10.9%

    The mechanics: The LLC files Form PTE-100 and pays the BAIT on allocated NJ income. The owner claims a credit on Form NJ-1040, line 47 (for single-member) or on their K-1 allocation (for multi-member). The business deducts the BAIT payment as a state tax expense on the federal return, reducing federal taxable income.

    Example for a sole-member LLC in the 32% federal bracket with $150,000 of business income: - Without BAIT: pay $8,512 in NJ GIT on business income with no federal deduction (SALT cap already used by property taxes) - With BAIT: LLC pays $8,512 BAIT, deducts it federally, saves ~$2,724 in federal tax - Net tax savings: ~$2,724 per year with no downside

    Elect annually. The BAIT election is made year-by-year (Form PTE-100 due March 15 for calendar-year entities). Miss the election and you lose the benefit for that year.

    Federal Income Tax¶

    Self-Employment Tax¶

    For a sole proprietor or single-member LLC owner not paying themselves as an S-Corp: - SE tax: 15.3% on net earnings up to $168,600 (2024 SS wage base) + 2.9% Medicare above that - This is the equivalent of both sides of FICA — you pay the employee and employer share - Deductible: 50% of SE tax is deductible on Schedule 1

    S-Corp strategy to reduce SE tax: Pay yourself a reasonable salary (FICA applies to salary only) and take remaining profit as a distribution (no FICA). On $150K net profit, splitting into $80K salary + $70K distribution saves ~$10,000/year in SE taxes. The IRS looks for unreasonably low salaries — "reasonable" means market rate for what you actually do.

    QBI Deduction (Section 199A)¶

    Pass-through business owners may deduct up to 20% of qualified business income (QBI) from federal taxable income. This is not available to C-Corps.

    Eligibility for retail: - Most retail is a "qualified trade or business" (QTB) — retail is not a Specified Service Trade or Business (SSTB), which has income limits - For 2024: full deduction available if taxable income is below $191,950 (single) or $383,900 (MFJ). Above those thresholds, W-2 wage limitations apply.

    Practical effect: On $100K of retail business income with taxable income below the threshold, the QBI deduction reduces federal taxable income by $20K. At a 22% federal rate, that's a $4,400 tax reduction.

    Section 179 and Bonus Depreciation¶

    Section 179: Deduct the full cost of qualifying equipment and property in the year purchased, up to $1,160,000 (2023 limit, adjusts annually). For retail: POS systems, fixtures, computers, equipment — all eligible. Instead of depreciating a $10,000 shelving system over 7 years, deduct it entirely in year one.

    Bonus Depreciation: 60% bonus depreciation available in 2024 (phases down: 40% in 2025, 20% in 2026). Applies to new and used property. Stacks with Section 179.

    Startup costs: Up to $5,000 of business startup costs (before opening) deductible in year one; remainder amortized over 180 months. Includes legal fees, licenses, market research, initial advertising.

    Payroll Taxes¶

    See nj-retail-overview.md for the employer obligations. Tax rates:

    Tax Employee Employer Wage Base (2024)
    Federal SS (FICA) 6.2% 6.2% $168,600
    Federal Medicare 1.45% 1.45% Unlimited
    Additional Medicare 0.9% employee only above $200K — —
    FUTA (federal unemployment) — 6.0% (net ~0.6% after credit) $7,000
    NJ SUI (unemployment) — 0.5%–5.4% $42,300
    NJ SDI (disability) 0.14% 0.1% $161,400
    NJ FLI (family leave) 0.09% — $161,400

    NJ SUI rate starts new employers at 2.8% and adjusts based on unemployment claims history. The rate is assigned annually by NJ Department of Labor.

    Payroll tax deposits: - Monthly depositors (< $50K annual liability): deposit by the 15th of the following month - Semi-weekly depositors (>$50K annual liability): deposit Wednesday/Friday following payroll - Quarterly Form 941 (federal) and NJ-927 (state) regardless of deposit frequency

    Use Gusto or a payroll service — manual payroll tax compliance for even one employee in NJ is complex enough to warrant it.

    Annual Filing Calendar¶

    Date What's Due
    Jan 15 Q4 NJ estimated GIT payment (prior year)
    Jan 20 NJ sales tax annual return (if annual filer)
    Jan 31 W-2s to employees; 1099s to contractors
    Feb 28 W-2s and 1099s to NJ Division of Taxation
    Mar 15 BAIT election deadline (Form PTE-100) for calendar-year entities
    Mar 15 S-Corp / partnership federal return (Form 1120S / 1065)
    Apr 15 Personal federal return (Form 1040) + NJ GIT return (Form NJ-1040)
    Apr 15 Q1 NJ estimated GIT payment
    Apr 15 / May 15 CBT return for S-Corps (30 days after federal)
    Apr 20 Q1 NJ sales tax return (if quarterly filer)
    Jun 15 Q2 NJ estimated GIT payment
    Jul 20 Q2 NJ sales tax return (if quarterly filer)
    Sep 15 Q3 NJ estimated GIT payment
    Oct 20 Q3 NJ sales tax return (if quarterly filer)
    Jan 20 (next year) Q4 NJ sales tax return (if quarterly filer)

    Mark all of these in a recurring calendar at business formation. Missing estimated payments triggers underpayment penalties regardless of whether you pay in full at year-end.

    Tax-Reduction Strategies¶

    Retirement Accounts (the most powerful tool)¶

    As a business owner, you can contribute far more to retirement accounts than as a W-2 employee. These contributions reduce both federal and NJ taxable income.

    • SEP-IRA: Contribute up to 25% of net self-employment income, max $69,000 (2024). Simple to set up; one account, no employee contribution required. Best for solo or very small operations.
    • Solo 401(k): If you have no full-time employees other than a spouse. Employee contribution: up to $23,000 ($30,500 if 50+). Employer contribution: up to 25% of compensation. Total: up to $69,000. More powerful than SEP for people who want to maximize contributions on lower net income.
    • SIMPLE IRA: For businesses with employees. Employee defers up to $16,000; employer matches 3% of compensation. Lower admin burden than a full 401(k).

    The compounding effect: A $30,000 SEP-IRA contribution reduces federal taxable income by $30,000. At a combined 32% federal + 6.37% NJ rate, that's a ~$11,500 tax reduction today on money that grows tax-deferred.

    Home Office Deduction¶

    If you do any business administration from home (accounting, ordering, email), the home office deduction applies to that dedicated space. - Simplified method: $5/sq ft, up to 300 sq ft ($1,500/year max) - Actual method: prorate home expenses (mortgage interest, utilities, insurance) by percentage of home used for business. Higher deduction but more documentation.

    NJ conforms to the federal home office deduction for pass-through entities.

    Vehicle Use¶

    If you use a vehicle for business (deliveries, supply runs, bank runs, trade shows), track miles and deduct: - 2024 standard mileage rate: 67 cents/mile for business use - Or deduct actual vehicle expenses pro-rated by business use percentage - Requires a contemporaneous mileage log. Apps: MileIQ, Everlance. The IRS requires date, destination, purpose, and miles per trip.

    COGS and Inventory Deduction¶

    Cost of goods sold is fully deductible — reduces gross revenue to arrive at gross profit. For retail, this includes: wholesale cost of merchandise, freight in, import duties, and packaging costs directly associated with goods sold. Good bookkeeping that separates COGS from operating expenses is worth real tax savings.

    Working With an Accountant¶

    A CPA who specializes in NJ small business is worth the fee at roughly $1,500–$3,000/year for a simple retail LLC. What justifies it: - BAIT election and filing (most accountants know about it; many business owners don't) - Entity structure review as income grows (when to elect S-Corp) - Sales tax compliance and audit representation - Estimated payment calculations to avoid underpayment penalties

    What to ask when hiring: 1. Do you have NJ retail clients specifically? 2. Do you prepare and file the BAIT (PTE-100) for pass-through clients? 3. What's your process for quarterly estimated payments? 4. How do you handle sales tax questions?

    Do not use a national chain (H&R Block, TurboTax) for a retail business with employees. The complexity warrants a local NJ CPA.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Not electing the BAIT. It's free money for most NJ pass-through business owners above ~$50K in business income. The election has to be made by March 15 each year. Miss the deadline, lose the benefit.

    Collecting sales tax but not remitting it. The most expensive mistake. Sales tax collected belongs to the state from the moment of sale — keeping it (even temporarily) triggers the 5%/month penalty.

    Not making quarterly estimated payments. Most new business owners discover this problem at tax time when they owe a large balance plus underpayment penalties. Model your expected annual profit, calculate expected tax, and pay quarterly.

    Treating inventory purchases as expenses. Inventory is an asset until sold, at which point it becomes COGS. Expensing unsold inventory in the purchase year overstates expenses and understates taxable income — a red flag in an audit and incorrect accounting.

    Missing the NJ S-Corp election. Electing S-Corp treatment at the federal level (Form 2553) without separately filing the NJ election (Form CBT-2553) means NJ taxes you as a C-Corp. Two separate elections required.

    • NJ Division of Revenue & Enterprise Services (njportal.com/dor) — Entity formation, Business Registration Certificate, annual reports.
    • IRS Small Business and Self-Employed Tax Center (irs.gov/smallbiz) — Federal side: SE tax, 199A, Section 179, payroll deposits.
