    NJ Laws You Should Know¶

    Unique, counterintuitive, and high-stakes NJ laws every resident gets surprised by — before the surprise costs you

    What Is It¶

    New Jersey has a collection of laws that differ from federal defaults and from most other states in ways that aren't obvious and that have real consequences when ignored. Some are quirks (mandatory full-service gas); others are serious financial and legal traps (inheritance tax on siblings, contractor licensing requirements, near-impossible gun carry).

    This file is the "things you didn't know you didn't know" reference — organized by domain, with enough detail to act on each.

    Why It Matters¶

    Most people learn NJ law by getting burned. The contractor who does $15,000 of work without an HIC registration loses the right to be paid. The sibling who inherits a house doesn't know there's a 16% inheritance tax. The person transporting a legal firearm incorrectly gets arrested. These aren't edge cases — they're the common failures of people operating in NJ without knowing the rules.

    Driving and Roads¶

    Full-Service Gas: It's the Law¶

    NJ is the only state in the US that prohibits self-service gasoline by law (N.J.S.A. 34:3A-1 to -17). Pumping your own gas in New Jersey is illegal for consumers. Gas stations are required to pump for you.

    The law was passed in 1949 and has survived every repeal attempt. The rationale cited (fire safety, jobs) is debated; the law is real. If you move to or from NJ, this adjustment is jarring in both directions.

    Practical: In remote areas (farm supply stores, marinas), self-service diesel is sometimes available. For regular gasoline, you wait and a station attendant pumps it.

    Hands-Free Driving Enforcement¶

    NJ prohibits handheld cell phone use while driving. Technically true in most states, but NJ enforcement and fines are aggressive:

    • First offense: $200–$400
    • Second offense: $400–$600 + possible license suspension
    • Third+ offense: $600–$800, 90-day suspension, possible surcharge points

    Mount your phone before driving. Holding it at a red light still qualifies as a violation.

    Right Turn on Red Restrictions¶

    NJ does not have a blanket right-turn-on-red rule. In many urban intersections and certain designated locations, right on red is prohibited. Signs are posted but easy to miss.

    Standard practice: look for the "No Turn on Red" sign at every intersection. Default to stopping when unclear.

    NJ MVC and the Points System¶

    NJ operates a driving points system. Accumulate too many points and face surcharges ($150/year for 6 points, escalating), insurance premium spikes, and license suspension.

    Point restoration: Completing an NJ-approved defensive driving course removes 3 points and prevents insurance surcharge for 2 years. The course costs ~$50 online and is worth doing after any moving violation.

    Insurance Surcharges (SCDCA): The NJ Motor Vehicle Commission charges annual surcharges for DWI, reckless driving, and excess points — separate from and in addition to court fines. These run $100–$1,000/year for 3 years. They surprise people who think paying the fine ends the matter.

    Firearms¶

    NJ gun laws are among the strictest in the country. This is not political commentary — it's practical information for anyone who owns or plans to own a firearm in NJ.

    Firearms ID Card (FID)¶

    To purchase any firearm (rifle, shotgun, or handgun) or purchase handgun ammunition in NJ, you must have:

    • Firearms Purchaser Identification Card (FID) — Required for rifles, shotguns, and all ammunition purchases. Application through your local police department or NJ State Police. ~$50 fee. Includes fingerprinting and background check. No expiration once issued, but you only have one.

    • Permit to Purchase a Handgun (PPH) — Required for each handgun purchase (not one for all handguns — one permit per handgun). Applied for through your local PD. Valid 90 days from issue; extendable once. 30-day waiting period applies.

    Timeline: The initial FID application in NJ legally must be processed within 30 days (60 days for non-residents). In practice, many departments take 3–6 months due to backlog. Plan accordingly.

    Carry Permit (Permit to Carry a Handgun)¶

    NJ requires a permit to carry a handgun in public — open or concealed. Prior to the 2022 Bruen Supreme Court decision, NJ required applicants to demonstrate "justifiable need," making permits nearly impossible for civilians. Post-Bruen, NJ converted to a "shall issue" standard but immediately passed new restrictions designating most public places as sensitive locations where carry is prohibited even with a permit.

    Currently prohibited carry locations (even with permit): Healthcare facilities, libraries, museums, schools, government buildings, casinos, entertainment venues, bars, restaurants that serve alcohol, parks, beaches, boardwalks, recreational facilities, public transportation, and private property without explicit permission from the owner.

    Practical effect: A carry permit in NJ is difficult to use legally. This doesn't affect home storage or transport under safe transport rules.

    Transport of Firearms¶

    NJ has strict requirements for transporting firearms even for licensed owners:

    • Firearms must be unloaded and in a locked container separate from ammunition
    • For handguns: in a locked case in the trunk (not the passenger compartment)
    • For rifles/shotguns: unloaded, in a case, in the trunk
    • Transport is permitted between: your home, a range, a gun shop (for service), hunting grounds (with license), or when moving residences

    Do not transport a firearm in NJ without following these rules. Violation is an unlawful possession charge, which carries serious criminal penalties.

    Prohibited Weapons¶

    NJ prohibits: - Assault weapons as defined by NJ statute (includes specific named models and features-based tests — AR-15 style rifles with certain features are prohibited) - High-capacity magazines — Magazines over 10 rounds are prohibited. This was reduced from 15 rounds in 2018. Out-of-state owners who bring legal-in-other-states 30-round magazines into NJ are committing a crime. - Hollow point ammunition — Technically legal to own at home and transport to a range; illegal to carry in public. A nuanced rule that catches people off guard. - Stun guns / tasers — Legal in NJ as of 2019 (court ruling); previously banned. No permit required. - Suppressors (silencers) — Illegal in NJ regardless of NFA tax stamp.

    Inheritance Tax¶

    NJ Is One of 6 States with an Inheritance Tax¶

    NJ imposes an inheritance tax separate from and in addition to the federal estate tax. It is paid by the beneficiary (the person receiving the inheritance), not the estate.

    No tax (Class A beneficiaries): Spouses, domestic partners, civil union partners, children, grandchildren, great-grandchildren, stepchildren, parents, grandparents

    High tax (Class C beneficiaries): Siblings, children-in-law, daughters/sons-in-law, surviving spouses of deceased children - First $25,000: exempt - $25,001–$1,100,000: 11% - $1,100,001–$1,400,000: 13% - $1,400,001–$1,700,000: 14% - Over $1,700,000: 16%

    High tax (Class D beneficiaries): Everyone else (friends, cousins, non-married partners not in a civil union) - First $500: exempt - Over $500: 15% up to $700,000; 16% above $700,000

    Example: A sibling inherits a $500,000 house. After the $25,000 exemption, they owe 11% on $475,000 = $52,250 in NJ inheritance tax. Unexpected if they didn't know this existed.

    Practical planning: If you have siblings or non-spouse partners you want to leave assets to, NJ inheritance tax is a major consideration in estate planning. Life insurance proceeds paid to a named beneficiary are exempt from NJ inheritance tax — a key planning tool. Trusts structured correctly can also mitigate the tax. See finance/estate-planning-basics.md and finance/trusts.md.

    Contractor Licensing¶

    Home Improvement Contractor (HIC) Registration¶

    Anyone performing home improvements in NJ for compensation must register as a Home Improvement Contractor with the NJ Division of Consumer Affairs. This includes: general contractors, painters, landscapers, pool companies, HVAC, plumbers (who also need a trade license), and virtually anyone who works on a residential property.

    Registration requirements: - Registration fee: $110/year for individuals, $220 for firms - Certificate of liability insurance required (minimum $500,000) - Must provide the registration number on all contracts and advertising - Registration number format: 13VHXXXX

    Why this matters to a homeowner: - Any home improvement contract over $500 must be in writing (N.J.S.A. 56:8-151) - The contract must include: contractor's name, address, registration number, estimated start/completion dates, total price - An unregistered contractor cannot enforce payment in NJ. If a contractor does $10,000 of work without being registered, they may be barred from collecting what they're owed in NJ courts. - You can verify any contractor's registration at newjersey.gov/dca.

    Trade licenses (separate from HIC): - Licensed plumber, licensed electrician, licensed HVAC technician — each requires state examination, continuing education, and a separate license - A master electrician or master plumber must supervise and pull permits for their respective work - Always ask for both the HIC registration AND the applicable trade license before hiring

    Permit requirements: - Virtually all structural work, electrical work, plumbing, HVAC, and roofing requires a permit from your municipality's building department - The permit is pulled by the contractor (or homeowner for owner-occupied single-family DIY work) - Work done without required permits creates title and insurance issues when you sell - Unpermitted work can require demolition and re-do at the homeowner's expense if discovered during inspection

    NJ Consumer Fraud Act (NJCFA)¶

    The NJCFA is one of the strongest consumer protection laws in the country. It provides for treble damages (3x the actual loss) plus mandatory attorney fee recovery against any merchant or contractor who engages in unconscionable commercial practices, misrepresentations, or violations of specific consumer protection laws.

    In practice: a contractor who abandons a job after taking a deposit, or who lies about materials, faces exposure far beyond the actual loss. This is the law that gives homeowners leverage against bad contractors.

    Landlord-Tenant Law¶

    Security Deposits¶

    NJ limits security deposits to 1.5 months' rent (N.J.S.A. 46:8-21.2). No more. A landlord who charges more can be sued for double the excess plus attorney fees.

    Deposits must be held in a separate interest-bearing account, and the landlord must notify the tenant annually of the bank, account number, and interest earned. The interest belongs to the tenant (or can be credited toward rent by agreement).

    Return of Security Deposit¶

    A landlord must return the security deposit (minus legitimate deductions) within 30 days of lease termination. If the landlord fails to return within 30 days and cannot document legitimate deductions, the tenant is entitled to double the deposit plus attorney fees.

    Legitimate deductions: unpaid rent, damage beyond normal wear and tear. Not legitimate: routine cleaning, repainting for normal wear.

    Just Cause Eviction (Anti-Eviction Act)¶

    NJ requires just cause to evict any residential tenant — landlords cannot evict a tenant simply because the lease expires or because they want the unit back. Valid grounds include: - Non-payment of rent (most common) - Violation of lease terms (with notice to cure) - Disorderly conduct - Destruction of property - Owner wants to convert to personal use (specific requirements apply)

    This significantly complicates tenant removal. Even month-to-month tenants have just-cause protections in NJ. A bad tenant requires documented grounds, proper notices, and a court process.

    Anti-lockout: Locking out a tenant, removing their belongings, or shutting off utilities to force a tenant out is illegal and creates civil liability independent of the eviction process.

    Pinelands and Environmental Restrictions¶

    The Pinelands Commission¶

    The NJ Pinelands — 1.1 million acres in southern NJ — is governed by the Pinelands Comprehensive Management Plan (CMP), a federally recognized regulation that controls land use in ways that supersede local zoning in many respects.

    If your property is in the Pinelands: - Development, clearing, farming, and construction are regulated by the Pinelands Commission, not just your municipality - Septic systems, well placement, and impervious coverage limits are stricter than standard NJ rules - The Pinelands designation reduces what you can do with raw land but provides significant environmental protection - Farmland in the Pinelands may qualify for different agricultural exemptions than elsewhere

    Check whether you're in the Pinelands: The Pinelands Commission has an online mapping tool at pinelands.nj.gov. Parts of Atlantic County, including areas near Galloway, fall within Pinelands boundaries.

    Highlands Region¶

    The NJ Highlands — 800,000+ acres in northwestern NJ — is similarly protected. Less relevant for Atlantic County but important if you own or buy land in Bergen, Morris, Passaic, Somerset, Hunterdon, Warren, or Sussex counties.

    Business and Consumer Laws¶

    NJ Non-Compete Landscape¶

    Addressed fully in nj-employment-law.md. Short version: NJ courts are skeptical of non-competes. Narrow, specific agreements protecting legitimate business interests have some enforceability; broad "don't work in this industry" clauses generally don't.

    Contractor Payment Laws¶

    The NJ Prompt Payment Act (for commercial construction) and similar provisions require timely payment to contractors and sub-contractors. Failure to pay triggers interest penalties at 12%/year. If you're hiring contractors for commercial projects (not residential), understand these payment timing rules.

    NJ Consumer Product Safety¶

    Products sold in NJ must comply with NJ-specific safety requirements that can exceed federal standards (particularly for children's products, lead paint, and certain chemicals). NJ DPSA has ongoing enforcement actions — relevant if you operate retail and source any consumer products.

    Health and Personal Rights¶

    NJ Paid Sick and Family Leave (Employee Perspective)¶

    Addressed from the employer side in nj-employment-law.md. From the employee side:

    • Earned Sick Leave: Your employer must provide 1 hour per 30 hours worked, up to 40 hours/year. You are entitled to this regardless of company size.
    • FLI (Family Leave Insurance): You can take up to 12 weeks of paid leave (85% of wages, capped) to bond with a new child or care for a family member. Apply through the NJ DOL — your employer is not the gatekeeper.
    • TDI (Temporary Disability Insurance): If you can't work due to non-work illness, injury, or pregnancy, TDI pays up to 85% of wages (capped) for up to 26 weeks. Apply through NJ DOL.

    These benefits are funded by your payroll deductions. If you never claim them when eligible, you paid for benefits you didn't use.

    NJ Advance Directives and Healthcare Proxy¶

    NJ has specific rules for healthcare directives: - A healthcare proxy (healthcare power of attorney) designates someone to make medical decisions if you're incapacitated. Requires two adult witnesses; neither can be the designated proxy. - A living will specifies your preferences for end-of-life care. - Both are recognized in NJ and travel with you to NJ hospitals. Federal HIPAA allows designated agents access to your medical records. - See finance/estate-planning-basics.md for setup details.

    NJ Recreational Cannabis (Legal Since 2021)¶

    NJ legalized recreational cannabis for adults 21+ in 2021. Key rules: - Purchase: Licensed dispensaries only (NJRC licensees). Purchasing from unlicensed sources remains illegal. - Possession: Up to 6 oz of cannabis, 17g of concentrate, or 1,000mg THC in edibles — legal for adults 21+. - Public use: Prohibited. Cannabis can only be consumed in private. - Driving: DUI law applies to cannabis impairment. NJ uses roadside Drug Recognition Evaluators and blood tests. The "how high is too high to drive" question has no legal bright line — impairment is impairment. - Employment: Employers can still maintain drug-free workplace policies. NJ law has some worker protections for off-duty use, but employers in safety-sensitive industries retain significant latitude.

    Miscellaneous High-Value Laws¶

    NJ No-Fault Auto Insurance¶

    NJ requires Personal Injury Protection (PIP) coverage on all auto policies — minimum $15,000. PIP covers your medical expenses (and your passengers') regardless of who caused the accident.

    NJ offers two options: - Standard policy: Full tort rights — you can sue for any injury - Limitation on Lawsuit (Verbal Threshold): Cheaper, but limits your ability to sue unless injuries are severe (fractures, disfigurement, permanent injury)

    Many people choose the cheaper verbal threshold option without understanding the trade-off. Verify which you have on your current policy.

    NJ Lemon Law¶

    NJ's Lemon Law covers new vehicles with significant defects that the manufacturer cannot fix within 3 attempts (or the car is out of service for 20+ cumulative days) within the first 24,000 miles or 2 years.

    A valid lemon law claim entitles you to a full refund or replacement vehicle. The process: give the manufacturer written notice, go through the NJ dispute resolution process. This is a real protection — use it if you have a chronically defective new vehicle.

    Sunday Alcohol Sales (Blue Laws — Mostly Gone)¶

    NJ's blue laws (prohibiting Sunday sales) have been largely repealed. Liquor stores can sell on Sundays in most NJ municipalities. Car dealerships, however, are still prohibited from selling cars on Sundays — one of the last surviving blue laws. If a NJ dealership is open Sunday, they can show you a car; they cannot complete the sale paperwork.

    NJ Notary Requirements¶

    NJ notaries cannot perform real estate closings — that requires a licensed NJ attorney. NJ is an attorney-state for real estate: both buyer and seller must be represented by a licensed NJ attorney at closing. Out-of-state title companies that handle closings without an attorney are operating illegally. Budget for attorney fees in any real estate transaction.

    Getting Started¶

    1. If you're a homeowner: Verify your property's Pinelands status, understand the annual ANCHOR filing deadline, and confirm your assessment against recent comparable sales.
    2. If you're a business owner: Ensure all contractors you hire have HIC registration. Verify any home improvement contract is in writing with the registration number included.
    3. If you have firearms: Confirm your transport protocol is correct. If you're unfamiliar with NJ's carry restrictions, assume you cannot carry unless you've verified the specific location.
    4. If you're estate planning: Account for NJ inheritance tax in your plan for any beneficiaries outside Class A.
    5. If you're an employee: Know your FLI and TDI benefits exist and how to access them through the NJ DOL when needed.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Hiring an unregistered contractor. Check newjersey.gov/dca before any work begins. The HIC registration takes 30 seconds to verify.

    Not accounting for NJ inheritance tax in estate planning. If you have assets going to siblings or non-spouse partners, this is significant and solvable with proper planning — but only if you know it exists.

    Transporting a firearm without following the unloaded/locked container rules. People moving between states or going to a range in ways that seem reasonable under other states' rules are committing serious crimes under NJ law.

    Assuming your NJ lease can be terminated by not renewing it. The Anti-Eviction Act requires just cause. Month-to-month doesn't mean easy removal.

    Never filing for FLI/TDI when eligible. These are funded by your payroll deductions throughout your working life. When you have a new child or a non-work illness, the benefit is there — but you have to file for it.
