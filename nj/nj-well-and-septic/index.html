    NJ Well and Septic¶

    Private water and wastewater systems in NJ — how they work, how to inspect them, what fails, and what it costs

    What Is It¶

    Rural and semi-rural properties in NJ — including most of Atlantic County and the Pinelands region — are typically not connected to municipal water or sewer systems. Instead, they rely on a private well for water and a septic system for wastewater treatment. Both are entirely your responsibility to maintain, repair, and replace.

    This changes the math and the risk profile of a rural property purchase significantly. A municipal water and sewer hookup costs you a line item in taxes and a monthly utility bill. A private well and septic system are capital assets with a lifespan, maintenance requirements, and failure modes that can cost $5,000–$50,000+ to address.

    Why It Matters¶

    For Romi's trajectory toward land ownership in Atlantic County — particularly in or near the Pinelands, where municipal infrastructure is limited — understanding well and septic is not optional. It's due diligence. You need to know: - What condition they're in before you buy - What the regulatory requirements are (NJ has specific law here) - How to maintain them so they last - What failure looks like and what it costs

    A failing septic system at closing is a negotiating opportunity. Discovering it six months after closing is a financial emergency.

    Core Concepts¶

    Private Wells: How They Work¶

    A private well is a borehole drilled into the ground to reach the groundwater table (aquifer). In Atlantic County, wells are typically drilled wells (not dug wells), ranging from 50 to 300+ feet deep depending on local geology and aquifer depth. In the Pinelands, the Kirkwood-Cohansey aquifer is large, shallow, and generally high-yield — most Pinelands wells reach it at 50–150 feet.

    Well components: - Casing: Steel or PVC pipe lining the borehole; prevents collapse and surface contamination - Submersible pump: Electric pump sitting at the bottom of the well, pushes water up - Pressure tank: Maintains water pressure between pump cycles; usually located in the basement or utility room - Wellhead: Protective cap above ground; should be elevated, sealed, and undamaged

    Lifespan: The well itself (borehole) can last indefinitely. The pump typically lasts 10–15 years ($800–$2,000 to replace). The pressure tank lasts 10–20 years ($300–$800 to replace). Inspect these components, not just the water quality.

    NJ Private Well Testing Act (2002)¶

    NJ is one of the few states with a mandatory well testing law. The Private Well Testing Act requires that a water quality test be performed and the results disclosed before the transfer of title on any property with a private well. This is not buyer-optional — it's required by law.

    What must be tested (standard panel under the Act): - Total coliform and E. coli (bacteriological contamination) - Nitrates and nitrites - pH - Iron and manganese (not health hazards but affect taste and appliances) - Lead - Volatile organic compounds (VOCs)

    NJ-specific contaminants to add: - Arsenic: Naturally occurring in NJ bedrock, particularly in certain geological formations. NJ's Maximum Contaminant Level (MCL) for arsenic is 5 µg/L (stricter than the federal 10 µg/L). Arsenic contamination in Atlantic County is relatively low compared to northern NJ, but test. - PFAS (perfluoroalkyl substances): "Forever chemicals" from firefighting foam, industrial sites, military facilities. Atlantic County has PFAS concerns in certain areas, particularly near Joint Base McGuire-Dix-Lakehurst and surrounding municipalities. NJ's MCL for PFOA and PFOS is among the strictest in the nation (14 ppt). If the property is within 10 miles of a known PFAS source, add this to the panel. - Radon in water: NJ has elevated radon in groundwater in some regions. Radon in water can be released as gas during shower/use. Test separately from air radon.

    Who does the test: A NJ-certified laboratory. Your real estate attorney or the buyer's agent typically arranges this, but you can also hire an environmental testing firm directly. Cost: $150–$400 for a standard panel; $400–$800 with PFAS.

    Pinelands water character: The Kirkwood-Cohansey aquifer is naturally acidic (pH 5.5–6.5) and low in minerals, which is unusual for groundwater. It often contains elevated iron and manganese. Homes in this region typically have water treatment systems (acid neutralizers, iron filters, water softeners). Expect to maintain these — they're not optional luxuries but practical necessities for the plumbing and appliances.

    Well Inspection at Purchase¶

    Beyond the water quality test (which is chemistry), get a physical well inspection by a licensed well driller or pump contractor. This covers: - Pump function and age (request the original drilling log if available — well drillers file these with the municipality in NJ) - Pressure tank condition and pressure settings - Wellhead condition and sealing - Casing above ground level and condition - Any signs of surface water intrusion

    Cost: $200–$400. Worth every dollar. A pump that's 14 years old and making unusual sounds is about to fail. Knowing this before closing means you negotiate; discovering it 3 months later means you pay full replacement price in an emergency.

    Septic Systems: How They Work¶

    A conventional NJ septic system has three components:

    1. Septic tank: A buried concrete or fiberglass tank (typically 1,000–1,500 gallons for a residential home) that receives all wastewater from the house. Solids settle to the bottom (sludge), grease floats to the top (scum), and the liquid layer (effluent) exits to the leach field. Bacteria in the tank break down solids over time.

    2. Distribution box (D-box): Distributes effluent from the tank evenly to the leach field pipes.

    3. Leach field (drain field): A network of perforated pipes buried in gravel trenches. Effluent seeps out of the pipes and is filtered through the soil. Soil treatment removes pathogens and contaminants before the water reaches the groundwater.

    Lifespan: A properly maintained system lasts 25–40 years. A neglected one fails in 10–15 years. Failure means sewage backing up into the house or surfacing in the yard. Either is a health hazard and a regulatory violation.

    NJ Septic Regulations (NJDEP)¶

    Septic systems in NJ are regulated under the Individual Subsurface Sewage Disposal System (ISSDS) rules, administered by the NJDEP and implemented by county health departments.

    Key rules: - New or replaced septic systems require a permit from the county health department - A perc test (percolation test) measures how quickly water drains through the soil — required for any new system design or replacement. Sandy Pinelands soils percolate quickly; clay soils in northern NJ percolate slowly and require engineered alternatives. - Minimum setbacks from wells: 50 feet between a septic tank and a potable well; 100 feet between a leach field and a potable well (NJ rule — some counties enforce stricter setbacks) - Minimum setbacks from property lines and waterways — important for small lots

    Pinelands overlay: In the Pinelands, NJDEP rules are stricter. Impervious coverage limits affect septic system placement, and the Pinelands CMP may require alternative system designs in some management areas. See nj-pinelands-regulations.md.

    Types of Septic Systems in NJ¶

    Conventional (standard): The three-component system described above. Works in soils with adequate percolation and sufficient lot size. Most common in rural NJ.

    Mound system: Built when the soil's water table is too high or percolation is too slow for a conventional leach field. The leach field is elevated above grade in a constructed mound of sand and soil. More expensive ($15,000–$30,000 new) and requires more land area.

    Peat filter / drip irrigation: Alternative treatment systems used in environmentally sensitive areas (including parts of the Pinelands) where nutrient loading (particularly nitrogen) is a concern. Higher cost, requires regular maintenance and monitoring.

    Holding tank: A sealed tank with no leach field — must be pumped regularly. Not a treatment system, just storage. Very rare for permanent residences; more common for seasonal properties near water.

    Septic Inspection at Purchase¶

    NJ does not have a statewide mandatory septic inspection law (unlike the mandatory well test), but county health departments may require one for certain transactions, and it is standard practice to request one in any offer.

    What a septic inspection involves: 1. Locate and expose the tank access cover (professional locators use probes; some tanks are mapped) 2. Pump the tank (this happens anyway during routine maintenance; $300–$500) 3. Inspect tank condition, inlet/outlet baffles, and signs of cracking or leakage 4. D-box and distribution pipe inspection 5. Leach field probe or observation — look for signs of saturation, surfacing effluent, or failure

    A good inspection takes 2–3 hours and costs $400–$800 including pumping. It will tell you: - Current condition - Estimated remaining life - Any immediate failures or code violations

    What to look for / ask: - Age of the system (recorded in municipal records when the permit was pulled) - Date of last pumping (should be every 2–3 years) - Any prior repairs, replacements, or failures - Whether the system was designed for the current number of bedrooms (increasing bedrooms later requires an upgraded system)

    Maintenance: What You Do Annually¶

    Well: - Test water quality annually (at minimum coliform and nitrates; full panel every 3–5 years) - Inspect wellhead annually for damage, settling, or cracking - Maintain water treatment equipment per manufacturer schedule (filter changes, media replacement) - Shock chlorinate the well after any flooding, work near the well, or coliform positive test

    Septic: - Pump the tank every 2–3 years (less if you're disciplined about what goes in; more if you use a garbage disposal) - Never flush: wipes (including "flushable"), feminine hygiene products, medication, cooking grease, paint, bleach in large quantities - Do not park vehicles or plant deep-rooted trees over the leach field - Divert surface water runoff away from the leach field area

    Cost of maintenance: Well: $50–$150/year in water testing, occasional filter costs. Septic: $300–$500 every 2–3 years for pumping. This is cheap compared to repair.

    Failure: What It Costs¶

    Well pump failure: $800–$2,000 including parts and labor. Emergency replacement (water is out) runs to the high end.

    Pressure tank failure: $300–$800.

    Well contamination requiring treatment: A UV system handles bacterial contamination ($600–$1,000 installed). Reverse osmosis handles arsenic and nitrates ($500–$1,500 installed). PFAS remediation requires more complex systems ($2,000–$5,000+).

    Septic tank failure (cracked, collapsed): $3,000–$8,000 for tank replacement.

    Leach field failure: $10,000–$25,000 for a conventional replacement. Mound system replacement: $20,000–$40,000. In the Pinelands, alternative system requirements can push costs higher.

    Total system replacement (new construction level): $20,000–$50,000 depending on system type, soil conditions, and county requirements.

    Key Considerations¶

    The NJ Private Well Testing Act test is required at sale but covers only a minimum panel. Add PFAS and arsenic if near any military installation or industrial site. Add radon-in-water if the air radon test is elevated.

    Septic age + maintenance history is the key question. A 20-year-old system that's been pumped regularly and has no signs of failure may have 15 years left. A 10-year-old neglected system may be starting to fail. The history matters more than the age alone.

    Negotiate repairs before closing, not after. A failed or aging system discovered during inspection is leverage. Request a price reduction, seller credit at closing, or seller-funded repair/replacement with a licensed septic contractor. After closing, it's your problem.

    Leach field location limits everything else. The leach field takes up significant lot area and has setback requirements from wells, property lines, and waterways. If you're planning to expand the footprint of a home or add structures, confirm that the existing leach field location doesn't conflict with those plans.

    Water treatment systems add maintenance. Pinelands properties with acidic, iron-rich water almost always have treatment equipment. This equipment needs regular maintenance and media replacement. Factor it into the cost of ownership.

    Getting Started¶

    For a purchase: 1. Request disclosure of well age, pump age, last water test results, and last septic pumping date from the seller 2. Pull the original well drilling log from the municipal records (filed when the well was drilled) 3. Commission the mandatory water quality test under the Private Well Testing Act — add PFAS and arsenic if warranted 4. Schedule a septic inspection with tank pumping — separate from the home inspection 5. Inspect the wellhead, pressure tank, and any water treatment equipment during the home inspection

    For existing ownership: 1. Test water quality annually (coliform minimum; full panel every 3–5 years) 2. Pump the septic tank every 2–3 years; keep records 3. Map your leach field location (sketch + measurements) so you know where it is

    Common Mistakes¶

    Relying only on the mandatory well test. It's the legal minimum. It doesn't test for PFAS, arsenic, or radon-in-water. Add those for the area and geology.

    Skipping the physical well and septic inspection. The water quality test tells you what's in the water. It doesn't tell you if the pump is about to fail or the septic tank is cracked. These are separate inspections.

    Not pumping the septic for 5+ years. The solids layer builds up and eventually enters the leach field, clogging the soil. Once the leach field is clogged, it cannot be unclogged — only replaced. Pumping every 2–3 years is the entire preventive maintenance program.

    Using the garbage disposal heavily with a septic system. Ground food waste significantly increases the solid loading in the tank, requiring more frequent pumping and accelerating leach field failure. Compost food scraps instead.

    Planting trees near the leach field. Tree roots follow water. They will find the perforated leach field pipes and collapse them. Keep deep-rooted trees 30+ feet from the leach field.
