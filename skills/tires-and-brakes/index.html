    Tires and Brakes¶

    The two systems that determine whether the car stops and stays on the road

    What Is It¶

    Tires and brakes are the primary safety systems on any vehicle. Tires are the only contact between a 4,000-pound car and the road. Brakes convert kinetic energy to heat and bring the car to a stop. Both are wear items that require regular monitoring and periodic replacement — and both fail gradually enough that people drive on compromised versions far longer than they should.

    Why It Matters¶

    Worn tires in NJ rain (South Jersey roads aren't known for excellent drainage) or worn brakes on any road are not abstract risks — they're concrete reductions in stopping distance and traction that determine whether you avoid an accident. These are not optional maintenance items.

    Core Concepts¶

    Tires — Pressure¶

    Tire pressure is the single most neglected maintenance item. Most drivers haven't checked their tire pressure in months.

    Why it matters: Underinflated tires run hot, wear faster on the edges, reduce fuel economy, and have reduced handling responsiveness. Overinflated tires wear in the center, reduce contact patch size, and ride harshly.

    Where to find the correct pressure: The sticker inside the driver's door jamb — NOT the maximum pressure molded on the tire sidewall. That's the tire's maximum, not the vehicle's recommendation. Typical passenger car pressures: 32–36 PSI cold. SUVs/trucks: 35–45 PSI.

    "Cold" pressure: Check pressure when the car has sat for at least 3 hours or driven under 1 mile. Driving heats the tires and increases pressure by 4–6 PSI — checking after driving gives a false high reading.

    How to check and inflate: 1. Remove the valve cap from the valve stem (the small rubber or metal stem on the wheel) 2. Press a tire pressure gauge firmly onto the valve stem 3. Read the pressure. Digital gauges are more accurate than stick gauges. 4. At a gas station air pump: set the target pressure on digital pumps. For dial pumps, add air in short bursts and recheck. 5. If overinflated: use the gauge's release valve or a pen to depress the valve stem pin. Release slowly — it's easy to over-release.

    TPMS (Tire Pressure Monitoring System): All cars sold in the US after 2008 have TPMS — a dashboard light that illuminates when a tire is 25% below recommended pressure. The light coming on means you're already significantly underinflated. Use it as an emergency alert, not a maintenance indicator. Check pressure manually monthly.

    NJ seasonal pressure changes: Cold air contracts. NJ winters drop tire pressure roughly 1 PSI per 10°F temperature drop. A tire at 35 PSI in October may be at 28–30 PSI in January — check every month in winter.

    Tires — Tread Depth and Wear¶

    Tread depth determines wet-weather performance. New tires have approximately 10/32" of tread. The legal minimum in NJ is 2/32". Most tire performance degrades significantly below 4/32".

    The quarter test: Insert a quarter into the tread groove with Washington's head pointing down into the groove. If you can see the top of his head, you're at 4/32" or less — time to replace. The penny test (Lincoln's head) checks the 2/32" legal minimum, not the practical safety threshold. Use the quarter.

    Wear indicators: Tires have built-in wear bars — small rubber bridges in the tread grooves that become visible when tread reaches 2/32". If you can see these, the tires are at the legal limit and must be replaced.

    Wear patterns diagnose problems: - Center wear (faster in the middle): Chronically overinflated - Edge wear (faster on both edges): Chronically underinflated - One-edge wear: Alignment problem — front tires wearing on the inside or outside edge indicates camber misalignment - Cupping/scalloping (wavy wear): Worn shocks or struts — the wheel is bouncing rather than maintaining contact - Feathering (one edge of each tread block worn): Toe alignment issue

    When to replace: Before 4/32" on any tire, or sooner if you drive in rain. As a set (all four) or in pairs (front pair, rear pair) when possible — mixing significantly different tread depths creates handling imbalances, especially in the rain.

    Tires — Rotation¶

    Why rotate: Front and rear tires wear at different rates due to different loads (front-wheel drive puts drive, steering, and braking load on the fronts). Rotating tires evenly distributes wear across all four, extending the life of the set.

    Interval: Every 5,000–7,500 miles. Convenient to do with every oil change.

    Rotation patterns: - Directional tires (with an arrow indicating rotation direction): Can only move front-to-rear on the same side. Not cross-rotatable. - Asymmetric tires (different inner/outer tread): Can move front-to-rear but must stay on the same side of the car (inner and outer patterns are different). - Standard non-directional tires: Can rotate in an X-pattern (front-left to rear-right, etc.) for maximum wear equalization.

    DIY feasibility: Possible with a floor jack and jack stands, but requires correctly placed jack points (owner's manual) and proper jack stand placement. The risk is high if done incorrectly — a car falling off jack stands causes serious injury. If unsure, pay a shop to rotate — it's cheap ($20–30) and not worth improvising.

    Brakes — How They Work¶

    Disc brakes (standard on all four wheels of most modern cars): A caliper squeezes brake pads against a rotor (steel disc attached to the wheel hub). Friction slows the wheel. The pads wear over time; the rotor can also wear or develop heat damage.

    Drum brakes: Found on the rear wheels of some budget cars and older vehicles. Curved brake shoes press outward against a drum. Less efficient than discs but still common.

    Brake pad material: - Organic: Soft, quiet, gentle on rotors. Worn faster by heat. Standard on most passenger cars. - Semi-metallic: More heat-resistant, better stopping power, harder on rotors, noisier when cold. - Ceramic: Low dust, quiet, good heat resistance. Premium option.

    Brakes — Monitoring Wear¶

    Brake pads have wear indicators: A metal tab that contacts the rotor when pads wear thin. The result is a high-pitched squeal when braking. This is not failure — it's the designed alert. When you hear it, get the brakes inspected within the next few weeks, not months.

    Grinding sound: Metal-to-metal contact. The pads are gone. This is damaging the rotor with every stop. Address immediately.

    Vibration when braking: Indicates warped rotors. Rotors can be machined (resurfaced) if enough material remains, or replaced.

    Visual inspection without removal: Turn the wheel to full lock (steering wheel fully left or right). Look through the wheel spokes. You can see the outer brake pad pressed against the rotor. If the pad material is less than ¼ inch thick, it's time for replacement.

    Pad thickness measurement: A simple way to read brake life: - New pads: ~10–12mm of friction material - Replace at: 3–4mm or less - Wear indicator contact: ~2–3mm

    Brakes — Replacement (DIY Assessment)¶

    Brake pad replacement is one of the more accessible DIY maintenance tasks. The process: 1. Jack the car, remove the wheel 2. Inspect the caliper, pads, and rotor 3. Compress the caliper piston back into the caliper (using a C-clamp or brake piston tool) 4. Remove the caliper bolts, swing the caliper aside (hang it — don't let it hang by the brake line) 5. Remove old pads 6. Install new pads (with caliper hardware if included) 7. Reinstall caliper, torque bolts to spec 8. Repeat the process with the opposite wheel (brakes should always be replaced in axle pairs) 9. Pump the brake pedal several times before driving — the pedal will be soft until the piston re-seats against the new pads

    When to also replace rotors: If the rotor has deep grooves, scoring, or heavy rust, or if you're hearing grinding — replace pads and rotors together. Rotor replacement adds $100–150 in parts but is done simultaneously during pad replacement. A shop often recommends this even when unnecessary — measure rotor thickness against the minimum spec stamped on the rotor edge before agreeing.

    Brake bleeding: If you're replacing calipers or opening any brake line, bleeding is required to remove air from the system. This is more advanced and requires either a vacuum bleeder or a helper. Don't improvise this.

    Key Considerations¶

    Tires and brakes are not places to delay: Worn tires and worn brakes don't fail gracefully — they fail in emergency situations when you're relying on them most.

    NJ roads and conditions: South Jersey roads have significant standing water after rain. Tread depth directly affects hydroplaning resistance — the minimum legal tread (2/32") has dramatically reduced wet-weather performance compared to 5/32". Don't run tires to the minimum in a state with frequent rain.

    Buy tires in sets: Mismatched tread depths front-to-rear create handling asymmetry and can make the car unpredictable at the limit. If one tire is damaged beyond repair, replacing it alone is acceptable short-term; replace the set or pair as soon as practical.

    Getting Started¶

    1. Check tire pressure this week — all four plus the spare. Record the correct PSI from your door jamb sticker.
    2. Do the quarter test on all four tires — know exactly where you are on tread depth.
    3. Listen for brake sounds and visually inspect the pads through the wheel spokes on your next parking lot walk.
    4. Buy a tire pressure gauge — keep it in the car. Digital gauges are more accurate than stick gauges.

    Common Mistakes¶

    Only checking pressure when a light comes on: The TPMS light means you're 25% low — already significantly underinflated. Monthly manual checks prevent this.

    Replacing only front brakes: Brakes should be replaced in axle pairs. Front-only replacement leaves significantly different stopping capacities at front and rear.

    Ignoring brake sounds "because the car still stops": The squeal is the indicator. Waiting until it grinds means you're paying for rotors you wouldn't have if you'd responded to the squeal.

    Inflating to the maximum tire pressure: The max pressure on the tire sidewall is for maximum load, not for normal driving. Use the door jamb sticker pressure.
